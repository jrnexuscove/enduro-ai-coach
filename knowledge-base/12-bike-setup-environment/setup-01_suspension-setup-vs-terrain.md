# Suspension Setup vs Terrain

## 1. OVERVIEW

Suspension setup is one of the highest-leverage changes a rider can make without actually improving their riding skill. A correctly set-up suspension system transforms an aggressive, unpredictable bike into a compliant, confidence-inspiring tool. A poorly set-up suspension — too stiff, too soft, or with mismatched compression and rebound — actively fights the rider on every terrain change, requiring constant compensatory inputs that fatigue both rider and machine. For most production enduro bikes, the factory setup is calibrated for an average rider weight (typically 75-80kg) on moderate terrain. Riders significantly lighter or heavier than this baseline, or riding terrain significantly harder or softer than moderate, will benefit enormously from deliberate setup work. This topic covers the key setup parameters — sag, compression damping, rebound damping — and how to optimise each for different terrain types.

## 2. CORE PRINCIPLES

### Principle 1: Sag Is the Foundation — Get This Right First
Sag is the amount the suspension compresses under the rider's weight before any additional force is applied. It determines the bike's baseline geometry and how much suspension travel is available for upward and downward movement. Setting sag correctly is the most important suspension setup step — everything else is adjusted on top of a correct sag baseline. Static (rider) sag for enduro forks: 30-35mm. Static sag for the rear shock: 25-30mm. Free sag (no rider, bike on stand): 5-10mm front and rear. These are starting targets — actual optimal values depend on specific bike, spring rate, and rider weight.

### Principle 2: Compression Damping Controls How Fast the Suspension Collapses
Compression damping resists the fork and shock from compressing when a force is applied — a rock hit, a jump landing, a compression at the bottom of a descent. Too little compression damping: the suspension dives and bottoms out on moderate hits, and the bike feels wallowy and imprecise. Too much compression damping: the suspension feels harsh and rigid, transmitting impacts directly to the rider, and wheel traction suffers because the wheel cannot follow terrain contours. Most modern enduro forks separate compression into high-speed compression (HSC, for big sharp impacts like rock hits) and low-speed compression (LSC, for rider-induced weight shifts and slower-moving compressions like braking). Both are adjustable on premium forks.

### Principle 3: Rebound Damping Controls How Fast the Suspension Returns
After a compression event, the suspension returns (rebounds). Rebound damping controls the speed of this return. Too slow rebound (too much rebound damping): the suspension "packs down" on consecutive hits — each hit happens before the previous compression has fully recovered, so the bike progressively rides lower and lower on subsequent impacts. Over a rock garden or whoops section, the bike ends up fully compressed with no travel left. Too fast rebound (too little rebound damping): the suspension "kicks back" after each hit, flicking the bike upward and unsettling the chassis, causing the rider to be bucked off a balanced position.

### Principle 4: Clicker Reference Point — Always Count From Full Hard
Suspension clicker settings are measured from full hard (fully clockwise) in clicks. This is the only consistent reference because the total number of clicks varies between manufacturers and models. When documenting a setup, always record clicks from full hard, not from full soft. Full hard is zero; the number increases as you turn counter-clockwise (softer). Typical enduro clicker ranges: compression 4-18 clicks from full hard; rebound 4-16 clicks from full hard. Baseline starting point for most enduro setups: approximately 10-12 clicks from full hard on both compression and rebound — in the middle of the adjustment range.

### Principle 5: Spring Rate Determines the Setup's Effective Range
Damping adjustments can only optimise what the spring rate allows. If the spring rate is wrong for the rider's weight, no amount of clicker adjustment will produce a correctly functioning suspension. Spring rate is typically measured in N/mm. A correctly rated spring for the rider's weight means the sag target can be achieved with the preload ring in its normal range. If you need maximum preload to achieve correct sag, the spring is too soft. If the spring is fully slack and you still have too little sag, the spring is too hard. Spring rate selection requires knowing the rider's combined weight with gear (typically add 10-15kg to rider's body weight).

### Principle 6: Setup Changes Must Be Made One Variable at a Time
The suspension system is an interconnected system — a change to front compression affects the bike's balance and therefore how the rear behaves, and vice versa. The cardinal rule of suspension setup is to change one setting at a time, test it with consistent test-route riding, then evaluate. Changing multiple settings simultaneously makes it impossible to identify which change produced which effect. Keep a written log of changes and their effects.

## 3. TECHNIQUE BREAKDOWN BY LEVEL

### 3a. Beginner Level
**Goal:** Learn to set static sag correctly. Understand what "too stiff" and "too soft" feel like on the trail. Use the baseline clicker settings as a starting point.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Measuring and setting static sag: 30-35mm front, 25-30mm rear
- Identifying packing down (rebound too slow) and harsh single hits (compression too stiff) by feel
- Starting with the manufacturer's baseline clicker settings as the foundation
- Not changing multiple things simultaneously

**What Good Looks Like:**
The beginner rider can measure their sag correctly using a tape measure and makes the appropriate preload adjustments. They ride the baseline setup, notice "the bike feels harsh over small rocks" (suggesting compression too stiff or spring rate too hard), note it, and make a single small change (2-3 clicks softer on compression) before testing again.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Adjusting clickers without first checking sag:** Clicker adjustments on incorrect sag produce unpredictable results. → Always verify sag first.
- **Making large adjustments (6+ clicks at once):** Changes are too large to isolate the effect. → Move 2-3 clicks at a time, test, then adjust further.

### 3b. Intermediate Level
**Goal:** Develop a systematic terrain-specific setup process. Understand the difference between HSC and LSC adjustments. Make appropriate setup changes for different event types.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Developing a standard test route for setup evaluation — same terrain for all changes
- Understanding HSC vs LSC: HSC handles sharp rock hits (adjust for rocky vs smooth terrain), LSC handles rider-induced compression (adjust for rider weight and riding style)
- Terrain-specific baseline setups: rocky/enduro (harder compression), sand/soft (softer compression, faster rebound), mud (similar to sand but slightly more rebound control)
- Setting up for a specific event type before the event

**What Good Looks Like:**
Before a rocky enduro event, the intermediate rider reviews their setup: adds 2 clicks of compression (HSC) to handle the square-edge rock hits, leaves rebound unchanged (they've tested this and it's correct for their style). They have a written record of this setup and can return to their standard baseline after the event.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Running the same setup for all terrain types:** A sand riding setup on a rocky trail will pack down and feel wallowy. → Maintain terrain-specific setups in a written log.
- **Over-relying on clicker adjustment when spring rate is wrong:** 20 clicks of compression change cannot fix an incorrectly rated spring. → Confirm spring rate is appropriate for your weight before extensive clicker work.

### 3c. Advanced Level
**Goal:** Fine-tune setup for specific event profiles, understand the interaction between bike geometry and suspension, and make informed decisions about component upgrades.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Understanding how sag changes affect geometry (more sag = shallower head angle = slower steering)
- The relationship between fork and shock setup: matched rebound speeds for balanced front-rear feel
- Identifying setup-vs-technique: is the bike struggling because of setup or because of rider error?
- Aftermarket suspension components: when the OEM system cannot achieve the required performance envelope

**What Good Looks Like:**
An advanced rider at a hard enduro event notices their bike is deflecting unpredictably on high-speed square-edge hits. They systematically investigate: sag is correct, rebound is set correctly, but HSC is 2 clicks too fast — they add 2 clicks HSC, test, and the deflection problem resolves. They know the change is valid because they made it systematically on a known test section.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Attributing technique problems to setup:** If the rider is stiff-arming and not absorbing, the bike will feel harsh regardless of setup. → Always check technique before setup changes.
- **Chasing perfection to the point of constant adjustment:** Excessive micro-adjustment wastes time and obscures the relationship between changes and feel. → Find a solid baseline and ride it for full sessions before further adjustment.

## 4. DIAGNOSTIC CUES (AI COACHING ENGINE REFERENCE)

### Visual Cues — Correct Technique
- Fork and shock compressing smoothly on rock hits, recovering to ride height between impacts
- Bike maintaining consistent height across consecutive rocky sections — not packing down
- No visible bottoming (fork or shock at full compression with harsh metal-on-metal feel communicated through rider body language)
- Rider able to maintain consistent standing position — bike not bucking or wallowing

### Visual Cues — Error Indicators
- Bike visibly riding progressively lower across a whoops or rock garden section: rebound too slow (packing)
- Harsh, abrupt deflections of the front or rear wheel on individual rock hits: compression too stiff
- Excessive chassis pitching fore-aft on rock section: mismatched front-rear damping
- Rider visibly jarred on routine impacts — body not absorbing because the suspension isn't absorbing
- Fork appearing to dive excessively on braking: compression too soft or spring rate too low

### Audio Cues
- Metallic clank from fork or shock: bottoming event — suspension has reached full compression
- Harsh impact sounds transmitted to the frame on routine trail features: suspension too stiff for current terrain
- Repetitive "slap" sound from the fork — uncontrolled rebound (rebound too fast, suspension bouncing)

### Sensor Cues
- Accelerometer data showing consistent high-amplitude vertical spikes at handlebars: harsh compression — setup too stiff
- Accelerometer showing high-frequency low-amplitude chatter: rebound too fast — wheel tracking poorly
- Travel sensor (if fitted) showing consistent near-zero return between consecutive hits in a rock garden: packing down — rebound too slow

## 5. COACHING CUES & LANGUAGE

### Positive Reinforcement
- "Your bike is working with you today — I can see the fork tracking the ground rather than deflecting off it. That is correct compression for this terrain."
- "Your sag is spot on. The bike is sitting in the right part of its travel and the geometry feels balanced."
- "The setup change you made before this session worked — the bike is more planted through the rocky section than last week."

### Corrective Feedback
- "Your bike is packing down across that rock garden — I can see it getting lower on each hit. Your rebound is too slow. Wind it out 3 clicks (counter-clockwise) and test it again."
- "You're getting bucked off every 10 metres in those whoops — the fork is kicking back. Your rebound is too fast. Add 3 clicks (clockwise) and see how it feels."
- "Your sag looks short — the bike is riding high and turning slowly. Check the measurement. I'd expect about 10mm less than what you've got."

### Progression Prompts
- "Keep a suspension notebook. Every time you change a setting, write the date, the change, the terrain, and the outcome. You'll build a personal setup database that's worth more than any generic guide."
- "Develop a 5-minute test route you use for every setup change — same hills, same rough section, same corner. Consistency in testing gives you meaningful data."
- "Next session, ride the first 20 minutes paying attention only to how the suspension feels — not your riding. At the end, rate compression feel (too stiff/right/too soft) and rebound (kicking/right/packing). That's your setup direction."

## 6. DRILLS & PRACTICE EXERCISES

### Drill 1: Sag Measurement and Setting
**Purpose:** Ensure the most important suspension baseline is correctly established before any other setup work.
**Setup:** Bike on a firm, level surface. Tape measure or dedicated sag tool. Riding gear and helmet on (rider's weight in riding configuration).
**Execution:**
1. Measure free sag: bike on stand, no rider. Measure the distance from a fixed point on the subframe to the rear axle. Record as Measurement A.
2. Remove the bike from the stand, rider sits on the bike in riding position, rider's feet on pegs. Have an assistant measure the same point to axle. Record as Measurement B.
3. Static (rider) sag = A minus B. Target: 25-30mm rear, 30-35mm front (measure front from lower triple clamp to the bottom of the dust seal).
4. Adjust preload ring (rear shock) or fork preload (if applicable) to achieve target sag. Clockwise = more preload = less sag.
**Success Criteria:** Sag measurements are within 2mm of target on both ends.
**Duration/Reps:** 30 minutes for initial setup. 5 minutes for verification checks between sessions.
**Progression:** After learning to set sag, experiment with ±5mm of target and note the handling effect on a test route.

### Drill 2: Rebound Adjustment Test
**Purpose:** Learn to feel and identify rebound-related symptoms, and to adjust rebound systematically.
**Setup:** A section with 5-10 consecutive moderate hits: small whoops, a root section, or a constructed rhythm section.
**Execution:**
1. Set rebound to full stiff (full clockwise) — deliberately wrong.
2. Ride the section and note the packing-down feeling.
3. Return to baseline (10-12 clicks from full hard).
4. Ride the section and note the improvement.
5. Set rebound to full soft (full counter-clockwise) — deliberately wrong.
6. Ride the section and note the kicking/bouncing feeling.
7. Return to baseline. Adjust from baseline in small increments (2 clicks) and test until optimal.
**Success Criteria:** Rider can accurately identify packing (too slow) and kicking (too fast) by feel, and knows which direction to adjust.
**Duration/Reps:** One session dedicated to this exercise.
**Progression:** Test the same rebound settings on different terrain types to understand how terrain changes the optimal rebound setting.

### Drill 3: Terrain-Specific Setup Log
**Purpose:** Build a personal setup database that enables rapid, informed setup decisions for different terrain types.
**Setup:** A notebook (physical or digital) maintained across sessions.
**Execution:**
1. After each session, record: date, terrain type (rocky/sandy/muddy/mixed), front and rear sag, front and rear compression settings (clicks from full hard), front and rear rebound settings (clicks from full hard), and a brief note on handling feel.
2. After 10 sessions, review: what settings correlated with the best handling descriptions? Are there patterns by terrain type?
3. Develop a "setup card" with baseline settings for 3 terrain types: hard pack rocky, sand/soft, mixed.
**Success Criteria:** After 10 sessions, rider can reference the log and set up the bike for a known terrain type in under 10 minutes.
**Duration/Reps:** 5-10 minutes post-session. Maintained across the full season.
**Progression:** Share the log with a more experienced rider or coach for input on the setup decisions.

### Drill 4: One-Change-At-A-Time Sensitivity Session
**Purpose:** Train systematic approach to suspension adjustment by deliberately changing a single variable and identifying the specific feel change it produces.
**Setup:** A consistent test route: one rocky section, one smooth section, one corner. Same each time.
**Execution:**
1. Ride the test route with baseline settings. Note overall feel on each section (scale 1-5 for confidence and comfort).
2. Change one setting only: e.g., add 3 clicks of rear compression.
3. Ride the test route again. What changed? Only the rear compression. Document the change in feel.
4. Return to baseline. Change a different single setting.
5. Repeat across 4-5 individual settings in one session.
**Success Criteria:** Rider can identify the specific feeling change produced by each single variable adjustment.
**Duration/Reps:** One dedicated session per setting type. Total: 4-5 sessions.
**Progression:** Begin making multi-variable changes (informed by individual sensitivity knowledge) and predicting the outcome before testing.

## 8. TERRAIN & CONTEXT VARIATIONS

### Hard-Pack Rocky Terrain
Recommended direction: slightly more HSC (2-4 clicks harder than baseline) to resist the sharp square-edge hits without excessive bottoming. Rebound: medium-slow (slightly more than baseline) to prevent kicking on consecutive hits. Spring rate becomes more important here — if spring rate is borderline, rocky terrain will expose it through frequent bottoming.

### Sand and Soft Terrain
Recommended direction: slightly less compression (2-3 clicks softer than baseline) to allow the wheel to absorb the softer, more yielding surface. Faster rebound (2-3 clicks from baseline) because the lower forces allow quicker recovery between hits. Note: sand compacted by rain behaves differently — can be quite hard and may suit closer-to-baseline settings.

### Mud
Similar to sand in compression direction (slightly softer). Rebound can be slightly slower than sand because mud sections tend to have heavier hits with more debris involvement. The bike will be heavier with mud accumulation — this adds load to the suspension and can make it feel stiffer. A slightly softer setup can compensate for the additional weight.

### High-Speed Enduro (Fast Straights, Big Berms)
Higher-speed riding increases the force of compression events — hits that feel moderate at 40 km/h are much harder at 70 km/h. Increase HSC to match. Rebound may need to be slightly slower to prevent instability at high speed. Air pressure in air forks (if applicable) may need 1-2 PSI increase for high-speed events.

### Extreme/Hard Enduro
Slow, technical extreme enduro requires maximum traction — softer settings overall allow the wheel to conform to the terrain. However, there is more risk of bottoming on large individual hits. Setting: softer than standard baseline, with careful attention to HSC to prevent catastrophic bottoming on individual big hits (rock steps, log overs). This setup will feel wallowy at normal trail speeds.

## 9. EXPERT INSIGHTS & SOURCES

### Key Insights
- **Paul Friedrichs** (suspension engineer, WP/KTM): notes that 80% of suspension problems on enduro bikes are actually sag problems — riders trying to fix a sag issue with clicker adjustments. Setting correct sag first eliminates a large proportion of handling complaints before any clicker work is needed.
- **Enduro21 technical guides**: document that typical enduro riders run significantly too stiff a setup — fear of bottoming leads to over-damped, over-sprung bikes that have poor traction and increased arm pump as a result. Under-springing and under-damping for the terrain is more common than over.
- **Suspension tuning philosophy** (common across professional enduro coaching): "Set sag. Set rebound. Set compression. In that order." The sequence matters because each setting builds on the previous one.
- **KTM Factory Racing setup philosophy** (documented in team technical reports): Factory enduro riders often run significantly softer setups than recreational riders expect — prioritising traction and wheel tracking over the immediate comfort of a stiff, composed feel. Comfort comes from the rider, not the bike.
- **WP and Öhlins technical manuals**: both manufacturers document starting baseline settings from full hard for their specific units — these manufacturer baselines are a better starting point than the common "middle of the range" advice because they account for the specific internal valve design.
