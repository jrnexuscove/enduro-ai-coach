# Gearing Setup vs Riding Style

## 1. OVERVIEW

Gearing determines the relationship between engine output and wheel speed — it is the mechanical translator between the engine's power delivery characteristics and the actual force and speed available at the rear tyre. Getting gearing right for the terrain and riding style is a high-value setup decision that costs relatively little (a sprocket change is inexpensive and quick) but affects everything from how tractable the bike feels in technical terrain to how much top speed is available on fast sections. Most production enduro bikes come geared for a compromise between motocross and hard enduro applications that suits neither perfectly. Understanding how to adjust gearing, what the trade-offs are, and how to match gearing to a specific event profile is a valuable tool for every enduro rider.

## 2. CORE PRINCIPLES

### Principle 1: Gear Ratio — The Fundamental Relationship
The overall gear ratio in any given gear is determined by: the gearbox ratio (fixed) multiplied by the final drive ratio (front sprocket to rear sprocket). The final drive ratio = number of rear sprocket teeth / number of front sprocket teeth. A 13-tooth front sprocket with a 50-tooth rear sprocket = 50/13 = 3.85:1. For every 3.85 rotations of the engine output shaft, the rear wheel rotates once. Higher ratio = more torque, less speed; lower ratio = less torque, more speed.

### Principle 2: Front vs Rear Sprocket — Effect Per Tooth Differs
Changing the front sprocket has a larger proportional effect than changing the rear: one tooth on the front sprocket is equivalent in effect to approximately 2.5-3 teeth on the rear sprocket (the exact ratio depends on current tooth counts). This means a 1-tooth front sprocket change produces a more dramatic effect than a 1-tooth rear change. Recommendations: use rear sprocket changes for fine-tuning, front sprocket changes for more significant ratio changes.

### Principle 3: Typical Enduro Gearing Reference Points
- **Standard enduro 250cc-350cc:** 13/50 or 13/51 — primarily technical enduro use
- **Cross-country enduro (more speed needed):** 14/50 or 14/48
- **Hard enduro / extreme:** 13/52 or even 13/53 — maximum torque for very slow technical
- **Motocross-derived gearing for enduro:** 13/48 or 14/48 — faster but less torque
- **Standard 450cc enduro:** 13/48 or 14/48 (the larger engine provides more torque, allowing taller gearing)
These are starting references — the correct gearing for any specific rider depends on their riding style, terrain, and how they use the powerband.

### Principle 4: Engine Braking Changes With Gearing
Engine braking is a function of engine compression working against forward wheel movement. Shorter gearing (smaller front or larger rear) increases the engine braking effect at any given speed — the engine has to turn faster to maintain the same wheel speed, so its compression resistance per wheel rotation is higher. This has significant handling implications: riders who rely on engine braking for speed management on descents will feel more braking effect with shorter gearing. Riders who prefer to trail brake may find shorter gearing too aggressive on descent entries.

### Principle 5: Event Profile Drives Gearing Decision
The optimal gearing for a specific event depends on the ratio of technical slow-speed to fast open terrain:
- Events with long fast liaisons and open sections: taller gearing (14 front or 48-50 rear)
- Events with prolonged technical terrain and many slow sections: shorter gearing (13 front, 50-52 rear)
- Mixed enduro: a compromise, typically 13/50 on a 250cc or 14/50 on a 450cc
Reviewing course descriptions and elevation profiles helps predict what the event demands.

### Principle 6: Countershaft (Front) Sprocket Life and Chain Wear
Smaller front sprockets (12T or below) increase the chain's articulation angle at the sprocket, accelerating chain wear significantly. Running a 12T front for extended periods is hard on both chain and front sprocket. The 13T front is generally the smallest practical long-life front sprocket for enduro. Chain and sprocket wear are also linked: a worn chain on new sprockets, or new chain on worn sprockets, both cause accelerated wear of the newer component. Replace chain and sprockets together for maximum component life.

## 3. TECHNIQUE BREAKDOWN BY LEVEL

### 3a. Beginner Level
**Goal:** Understand the gearing concept and how to identify whether current gearing is appropriate. Know the correct direction of change (more teeth = shorter gearing = more torque) and how to make a basic sprocket change.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Reading the current sprocket tooth counts on the bike
- Identifying whether the bike runs out of top gear too quickly (needs taller gearing) or feels sluggish in technical (needs shorter gearing)
- Understanding that gearing changes are reversible — experimenting is low-risk

**What Good Looks Like:**
The beginner rider finishes a session noticing they are hitting the rev limiter in third gear on flat open sections. They correctly identify this as a need for taller gearing and make a front sprocket change from 13T to 14T (or remove 2 teeth from the rear), testing the result on the next session.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Confusing gearing problems with engine problems:** Running out of revs in top gear does not mean the engine is underpowered — it often means the gearing is too short. → Evaluate gearing before assuming an engine issue.
- **Changing too many teeth at once:** Going from 13/50 to 14/48 is a large change and may overshoot the target. → Change one component at a time, typically 1-2 teeth.

### 3b. Intermediate Level
**Goal:** Select terrain-appropriate gearing before each session or event. Understand the interaction between gearing, engine braking, and terrain management. Maintain a basic gearing log.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Matching gearing to the event profile from the course description
- Understanding the engine braking effect of different ratios and how it affects descent technique
- Carrying a spare rear sprocket (±2 teeth) to events for quick adjustment
- Tracking chain and sprocket wear and replacing as a set

**What Good Looks Like:**
Before a mountain enduro event with significant steep technical descents and fast liaison roads, the intermediate rider selects 13/50 — technical enough for the climbs, tall enough for the liaison roads. They know from experience that the 13/50 has slightly more engine braking than the 14/50 they use for faster events, which suits the technical descent management.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Never changing gearing and accepting whatever the bike came with:** Factory gearing is often sub-optimal for specific terrain types. → Treat gearing as a tunable parameter, not a fixed setting.
- **Ignoring chain condition:** A stretched chain loses effective ratio — what was 13/50 effectively becomes slightly taller as chain wear progresses. → Check chain stretch regularly with a chain wear gauge.

### 3c. Advanced Level
**Goal:** Use gearing as a precision tool for specific event and terrain optimisation. Understand the interaction between gearing choice and engine mapping. Make strategic gearing decisions for multi-day events.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- The interaction between gearing and traction control/engine maps on modern fuel-injected bikes: shorter gearing can make traction control feel more intrusive (more wheel speed change per throttle input)
- Multi-day events: a single gearing choice must work across varied day profiles — finding the best compromise
- Precise powerband use: gearing should place the engine in its optimum power range for the most common terrain sections of the event
- Timing of sprocket changes: carrying tools for mid-event adjustments in longer events

**What Good Looks Like:**
An advanced rider preparing for a 3-day enduro with day 1 fast (open cross-country), day 2 technical (mountain enduro), and day 3 mixed. They choose 13/50 as a compromise and accept that they will be slightly tall on day 2 and slightly short on day 1. They note this trade-off explicitly and decide the day 2 technical terrain is the higher risk day, so they optimise for it.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Over-optimising gearing for one day at the expense of another:** A significant gearing change between days takes time and may not be available in remote event settings. → Choose the best compromise gearing for multi-day events.
- **Ignoring the powerband in gearing decisions:** Gearing should place the engine in its power band for the most demanding sections, not just be adjusted for speed range. → Consider where in the rev range the bike will be running most of the time.

## 4. DIAGNOSTIC CUES (AI COACHING ENGINE REFERENCE)

### Visual Cues — Correct Technique
- Rider using the full rev range of their gearbox without constant gear changes: gearing matched to terrain speed range
- Smooth power delivery in technical sections without clutch-slipping or wheelie management: appropriate ratio providing tractable torque
- Rider not running into top gear limiter on longest fast section

### Visual Cues — Error Indicators
- Rider constantly in lowest gear with engine screaming: gearing too short for terrain speed
- Rider constantly in top gear with engine barely running: gearing too tall, powerband not accessible
- Frequent clutch slipping in technical terrain: gearing too tall, rider compensating with clutch to reduce wheel speed
- Wheelie management required constantly in lower gears: gearing too short, too much torque per gear at typical terrain speed

### Audio Cues
- Consistent rev-limiter hitting in top gear: gearing too short
- Engine lugging (very low RPM, stuttering throttle response) in the lowest gear the rider is comfortable using: gearing too tall
- Clutch slip sound (high-frequency engagement slip) in technical terrain: gearing too tall for required torque delivery

### Sensor Cues
- GPS/speed vs throttle position data: if speed is very low but throttle is very open, gearing may be too tall for the terrain
- RPM data showing consistent peak-rev engagement in the most-used gear: gearing matched — engine in its range
- RPM data showing engine never reaching peak power range in any gear: gearing too tall for the terrain speed

## 5. COACHING CUES & LANGUAGE

### Positive Reinforcement
- "Your gearing is working well today — I can see you using most of the gearbox across this route and the engine is in its power range when you need it."
- "Good call fitting the 50 for today. The extra torque shows in these rocky sections — you're not having to slip the clutch nearly as much."
- "You matched the gearing to the event profile and it's paid off. Less clutch work, cleaner exits from slow corners."

### Corrective Feedback
- "You're slipping the clutch for the first 20 metres of every technical section because the gearing is too tall for this speed range. Try a 51 or 52 on the rear and see if you can engage the clutch cleanly without the slip."
- "You're hitting the rev limiter every time you get any speed up. You need one more tooth on the front or two less on the rear for this kind of terrain."
- "Your chain is stretched well beyond 1% — at that point you're running an effective ratio that's 2-3% taller than your sprockets say. Replace the chain before you blame the gearing."

### Progression Prompts
- "For the next three sessions, ride your current gearing and note where you're in the wrong ratio the most — too long or too short. That data will tell us exactly what to change."
- "Pick up a spare rear sprocket in the size above and below your current one. Before the next event, decide which one fits the event profile better."
- "Learn to change the rear sprocket yourself before an event. It takes 10 minutes with the right tools and it is worth having the skill."

## 6. DRILLS & PRACTICE EXERCISES

### Drill 1: Gearing Impact Test
**Purpose:** Feel the direct difference between a shorter and taller gearing ratio on the same terrain to build personal reference points.
**Setup:** A trail section with both slow technical and a brief fast section. Two different rear sprockets (±2 teeth from current).
**Execution:**
1. Ride the section with current gearing. Note: what gear are you in most? Do you hit the limiter? Do you need to slip the clutch?
2. Fit the smaller rear sprocket (taller gearing). Ride the section. Note the differences.
3. Fit the larger rear sprocket (shorter gearing). Ride the section. Note the differences.
4. Choose the sprocket that felt best for this terrain type and document it.
**Success Criteria:** Rider can articulate clear differences between gearing choices by feel, not just by specification.
**Duration/Reps:** One session per sprocket. Three sessions total.
**Progression:** Add a fourth sprocket size. Build a personal "gear map" for different terrain types.

### Drill 2: Engine Braking Awareness Drill
**Purpose:** Develop sensitivity to how gearing ratio affects engine braking behaviour on descents.
**Setup:** A consistent descent with known grip level.
**Execution:**
1. Descend with taller gearing (fewer rear teeth), relying partially on engine braking. Note the braking feel.
2. Fit shorter gearing (more rear teeth). Descend the same section. Note the increased engine braking effect.
3. On the shorter gearing, attempt to modulate speed using engine braking alone (no wheel brake). Note where this is comfortable and where it isn't.
**Success Criteria:** Rider can accurately predict and describe the engine braking effect of a gearing change.
**Duration/Reps:** 30-minute session on one descent.
**Progression:** Apply the learning to descents with varying grip levels to understand how gearing-engine braking interacts with surface conditions.

### Drill 3: Chain and Sprocket Condition Check
**Purpose:** Establish a routine mechanical check that maintains gearing accuracy and prevents accelerated component wear.
**Setup:** The bike, a chain wear gauge (or ruler), and good lighting.
**Execution:**
1. Measure chain stretch: use a chain wear gauge or measure 24 inner links — new chain should measure 304.8mm (12 inches). At 1% stretch (307.8mm) replace chain.
2. Inspect front sprocket: hook teeth are worn — the leading face of each tooth should be straight, not concave like a hook. Hooked teeth = replace.
3. Inspect rear sprocket: shark-fin profile (pointed, asymmetric teeth) = replace. Even if gently worn, mismatched chain and sprocket wear accelerates damage.
4. Document when last replaced.
**Success Criteria:** Rider can perform the full condition check in under 10 minutes and make correct replacement decisions.
**Duration/Reps:** Before every event. Monthly during regular training.
**Progression:** Learn to replace chain and sprockets as a single operation — this is the most cost-effective approach.

### Drill 4: Event Gearing Decision Framework
**Purpose:** Build a systematic pre-event gearing decision process rather than defaulting to whatever is on the bike.
**Setup:** Event information (course description, GPS profile if available), current sprocket selection.
**Execution:**
1. Review the event profile: what percentage of the course is technical/slow (below 30 km/h), moderate, and fast (above 60 km/h)?
2. Calculate approximate gearing preference: predominantly technical = shorter gearing (larger rear), predominantly fast = taller gearing (smaller rear or larger front).
3. If mixed profile: identify the highest-risk terrain type (usually technical) and optimise for it, accepting a minor compromise elsewhere.
4. Document the decision and the reasoning. After the event, review: was the gearing choice correct? What would you change?
**Success Criteria:** Rider makes a deliberate, reasoned gearing decision before every event rather than defaulting to factory settings.
**Duration/Reps:** Applied pre-event. Takes 15 minutes of preparation.
**Progression:** Build a database of event profiles and gearing outcomes across a full season to refine the decision process.

## 8. TERRAIN & CONTEXT VARIATIONS

### Extreme / Hard Enduro
Shorter gearing than standard enduro: 13/52 or even 13/53 on a 250cc two-stroke. The terrain speed is very low (walking pace in many sections), and maximum torque at minimum wheel speed is the priority. Engine braking on this gearing is substantial — riders must account for this on descents.

### Cross-Country Enduro (Long Liaisons)
Taller gearing than technical enduro: 14/50 or 14/48 on a 250cc. Allows comfortable cruising on fire roads and tarmac liaisons between special stages without excessive engine RPM. The technical stages on this gearing require more clutch management — a known trade-off accepted for the liaison sections.

### Two-Stroke vs Four-Stroke Gearing Philosophy
Two-strokes typically run shorter final drive ratios than equivalent four-strokes because the two-stroke powerband is narrower — the bike needs to stay in its power range more carefully, requiring more frequent gear changes on a taller ratio. Four-strokes have broader powerbands and can tolerate taller gearing with less precise rev management.

### High Altitude
At high altitude, engine power decreases (see SETUP-10). Shorter gearing partially compensates for reduced power by increasing torque at the rear wheel. This is not a fix for altitude power loss but can partially mitigate it. A 13/50 at altitude may feel similar in character to a 13/48 at sea level.

### Sand / Desert
Taller gearing than technical enduro: sand riding is typically higher speed and the loose surface requires momentum rather than torque. Shorter gearing in deep sand produces excessive wheel spin — counter-productive. 14/48 or 14/46 is appropriate for desert/sand racing.

## 9. EXPERT INSIGHTS & SOURCES

### Key Insights
- **Graham Jarvis** (extreme enduro): has been documented running 13/52 and even 13/53 setups for the most extreme Rocky Mountain events — extreme short gearing for maximum traction in near-zero speed sections. The same rider uses taller gearing for cross-country enduro events.
- **KTM Factory Racing gearing guide**: documents that EXC enduro models leave the factory with gearing appropriate for moderate mixed terrain — explicitly recommending shorter ratios for technical events and taller ratios for predominantly fast events.
- **Two-stroke vs four-stroke gearing difference** (widely documented in enduro technical literature): 250cc two-strokes typically run 2-3 teeth more on the rear than equivalent 250cc four-strokes, reflecting the narrower powerband and need to stay in the power delivery range more precisely.
- **Chain wear and sprocket replacement economics**: industry consensus is that running a stretched chain on new sprockets destroys the sprockets in approximately 30% of the time it would take a matched set. Replacing chain and sprockets as a set every 60-80 hours (depending on terrain abrasiveness) is the most cost-effective maintenance approach.
- **Countershaft guard importance**: smaller front sprockets are harder on the countershaft seal — the increased chain angle at small tooth counts can cause premature seal wear. Fitting a countershaft guard that redirects chain debris protects the seal.
