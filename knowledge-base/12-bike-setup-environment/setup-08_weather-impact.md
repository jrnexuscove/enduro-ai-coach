# Weather Impact (Wet/Dry Transitions)

## 1. OVERVIEW

Weather fundamentally changes the grip contract between tyre and terrain. The same section that offers confident, predictable traction in dry conditions can offer near-zero grip when wet. Riders who understand how different terrain types respond to rain — and specifically how they change in the transitional periods after rain — are far better prepared for the wide range of conditions that enduro riding involves. The most dangerous scenario is often not the obviously wet session but the transitional period: the surface that looks dry but is not, the trail that appears the same but where the conditions have fundamentally changed beneath a deceptive dry crust. This topic covers how the five primary terrain types (hard-pack, sand, roots, rock, and mud) respond to rainfall and subsequent drying, and how to adjust both setup and technique in response to weather changes.

## 2. CORE PRINCIPLES

### Principle 1: Wet Hard-Pack — Immediate Grip Reduction
Water on hard-pack creates a thin lubricating layer between the tyre rubber and the soil. Grip reduction is immediate — the moment rain contacts the surface, traction decreases. The degree of reduction depends on the surface composition: clay-rich hard-pack becomes extremely slippery (almost zero grip), decomposed granite hard-pack retains reasonable grip longer, and compacted gravel provides moderate grip even when wet. Practical implication: when rain starts during a session, immediately reduce speed on hard-pack sections and increase following distance behind other riders.

### Principle 2: Dry-After-Wet — The Grip Illusion
One of the most dangerous conditions in enduro: after rain has dried, a thin layer of dry dust forms on top of a still-slightly-damp base. The surface looks dry. It may even sound dry under the tyres. But the thin dry layer sits on a slippery damp base, and as soon as the tyre penetrates the dust layer (under braking, hard cornering, or wheel spin), the underlying slipperiness is exposed suddenly. This transition from apparent grip to sudden loss is the mechanism behind many early-lap crashes in events held shortly after rain. Riders who know this condition exists approach corners cautiously for the first 2-3 laps until the condition has been verified.

### Principle 3: Wet Roots — Near-Zero Grip Regardless of Tyre
Roots, particularly smooth bark-on roots and polished exposed roots, offer near-zero grip when wet. The smooth, non-porous root surface simply cannot be penetrated or gripped by tyre rubber — water sits on the surface as a complete lubricating film. Soft compound tyres provide marginally more grip than hard compounds on wet roots, but the difference is small compared to the overall grip reduction. The key adaptation is not to try to find traction on wet roots — it is to minimise the time the tyre is on the root under any load. Cross roots at 90 degrees where possible. Do not brake or accelerate on root contact — neutral throttle only.

### Principle 4: Wet Rocks — Zero Available Grip
Wet rock (particularly granite and sandstone) has a coefficient of friction approaching 0 on a smooth surface. Even aggressive tyre knobs cannot penetrate smooth wet rock — the knob sits on the water film, not the rock surface. Textured or rough rock (fractured, angular faces) retains some grip when wet because the sharp edges can cut through the water film. Wet smooth rock sections should be treated as ice — speed set before entry, no steering corrections on the rock, no braking on the rock, and exit planned before entry. The setup change that helps most: maximum weight on the traction tyre (rear tyre, forward weight distribution) to minimise front tyre load on wet rock.

### Principle 5: Sand Behaviour After Rain
Sand responds to rain differently from other terrain types — rain compacts sand, initially improving traction. Freshly rained-on compact sand can offer better grip than dry loose sand. However, this improvement is temporary: as traffic passes through and the surface dries, the compacted layer breaks up. Heavily trafficked wet sand also becomes churned and soft, losing the compaction benefit. Deep wet sand is significantly heavier than dry sand — the bike sinks more and requires more momentum to maintain progress.

### Principle 6: Brake Performance in Wet — Specific Considerations
Wet brakes (disc and pad wetted by rain or water crossings) provide initially reduced braking force — water on the rotor temporarily reduces friction between pad and rotor. Braking force recovers within 3-5 brake applications as the heat from braking evaporates the water. Riders crossing water features should apply the brakes briefly and repeatedly for 50-100 metres after the crossing to dry the rotors. In sustained rain, disc brakes remain functional but response time is slightly extended — begin braking earlier on wet descents to account for the additional rotor-drying application time.

## 3. TECHNIQUE BREAKDOWN BY LEVEL

### 3a. Beginner Level
**Goal:** Understand the five most important weather-terrain interaction rules. Know to reduce speed on all surfaces immediately when rain starts or when transitioning from clearly wet to apparently dry conditions.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Immediate speed reduction when rain starts on any terrain type
- The wet roots rule: cross at 90 degrees, neutral throttle
- The wet rock rule: set speed before, no corrections on the surface
- Drying brakes after water crossings

**What Good Looks Like:**
Rain begins during a session. The beginner rider immediately reduces pace by 20-30%. They approach a root section that they were navigating confidently in the dry: they cross each root at the nearest possible right angle, with neutral throttle, and avoid any braking on root contact. They maintain these reduced expectations for the remainder of the session.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Maintaining dry-weather pace immediately after rain starts:** "It's only light rain — I'll be fine at the same speed." → Even light rain changes grip immediately on hard-pack and roots. Reduce pace immediately.
- **Not drying brakes after water crossings:** The first hard brake application after a water crossing may be less effective than expected. → Dry the brakes before you need maximum braking.

### 3b. Intermediate Level
**Goal:** Distinguish between the different post-rain transition states and adapt setup and pace accordingly. Understand compound and pressure adjustments for wet conditions.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Reading the dry-after-wet transition visually: looking for the dull, powdery surface appearance that indicates the grip illusion condition
- Tyre pressure adjustment for wet conditions: reduce 1-2 PSI below dry baseline on hard-pack for larger footprint
- Compound awareness: wet traction on hard surfaces requires softer compound — identify when compound choice is limiting grip
- Sequential session management: first laps after rain are the highest-risk, regardless of how dry the surface appears

**What Good Looks Like:**
Arriving at a trail that had rain overnight and is now "drying out." The intermediate rider observes the surface: it looks mostly dry but the corners show a dull, chalky appearance rather than the usual dusty-dry look. They recognise the dry-after-wet condition, reduce their corner entry speeds by 25%, and test each corner with a conservative entry for the first lap before building pace back if the surface proves consistent.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Assuming "mostly dry" means "mostly same as dry":** The dry-after-wet transition can be dangerous until the surface has dried completely through, not just on the surface. → Treat any post-rain session as wet-condition riding until the surface has been verified across multiple corners.
- **Not adjusting tyre pressure for wet conditions:** Correct pressure in wet is lower than dry — more contact area matters more on slippery surfaces. → Lower pressure before a wet session.

### 3c. Advanced Level
**Goal:** Anticipate weather-induced conditions from forecast information before arrival at the trail. Adjust session planning, event strategy, and setup based on weather forecasting.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Weather forecasting literacy: using mountain weather forecasts (which differ from valley forecasts for altitude enduro)
- Anticipating terrain conditions from the forecast: "overnight rain drying by 9am means I need to plan for dry-after-wet conditions in the first hour"
- Start order strategy for events with uncertain weather: positioning relative to expected dry-out time
- Pre-event tyre compound choice based on forecast

**What Good Looks Like:**
Before a mountain enduro event, the advanced rider checks the forecast: 8mm rain overnight, clearing by 8am, 12°C maximum temperature. They predict: the start section (exposed ridge) will dry quickly; the forest section (north-facing, shaded) will remain wet through the whole event; the rock sections will be near-zero grip all day. Their tyre choice is soft compound for maximum wet grip. They plan for dry-after-wet conditions on the exposed sections during the first stage.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Using valley weather forecasts for mountain events:** Mountain conditions can be significantly different from valley conditions — temperature, humidity, and precipitation vary substantially with altitude. → Use elevation-specific mountain weather apps (e.g., Mountain Forecast, Meteo Blue) rather than standard weather apps.
- **Believing "it'll dry by race time":** Rain-soaked terrain takes hours to dry completely, especially on shaded or clay-rich sections. A 7am rain stop does not mean dry conditions for a 10am start in technical terrain. → Be conservative with "dry by race time" predictions.

## 4. DIAGNOSTIC CUES (AI COACHING ENGINE REFERENCE)

### Visual Cues — Correct Technique
- Rider reducing pace on wet terrain relative to observed dry-session baseline
- 90-degree root crossings maintained in wet conditions: feet visible positioned for correct crossing angle
- No steering or braking inputs visible during rock section crossing in wet conditions
- Rider applying brakes briefly after water crossings to dry rotors
- Evidence of tyre pressure adjustment: tyre contact patch wider than in dry sessions

### Visual Cues — Error Indicators
- Rider maintaining dry-weather pace on visually wet terrain
- Diagonal root crossing in wet: high crash risk — tyre contact at acute angle to wet root
- Braking mid-rock-section in wet conditions: wheel lock-up on zero-grip surface
- Rider falling on surface that appeared dry (grip illusion): post-fall surface inspection reveals damp underlay
- Rear wheel spin on wet clay-rich hard-pack where wheel spin on dry was not observed: inappropriate throttle application unchanged from dry conditions

### Audio Cues
- Tyre squeal on wet hard-pack corners: tyre sliding — grip is inadequate for the speed and input being applied
- Brake squeal on wet rotors: normal on initial post-rain application; should resolve within 3-5 applications
- High-frequency wheel spin sound in wet conditions where none occurred dry: throttle needs reduction for wet conditions

### Sensor Cues
- Lateral slip angle data (if available) significantly higher than dry-session baseline on identical corners at lower speed: grip reduction consistent with wet conditions
- Braking distance data longer than dry baseline at equivalent entry speed: brake efficiency reduced by wet conditions
- ABS activation data: more frequent ABS triggers in wet = reduced traction as expected; unexpected ABS triggers on sections that appeared dry = grip illusion condition

## 5. COACHING CUES & LANGUAGE

### Positive Reinforcement
- "Good pace management in the wet. You came down your speed appropriately and you're riding with the conditions, not against them."
- "Perfect root crossing — 90 degrees, neutral throttle. That is the correct approach in wet conditions."
- "You read the dry-after-wet condition on those corners and treated them with appropriate caution. That first-lap conservatism prevented a slide."

### Corrective Feedback
- "You braked on that wet rock and the wheel locked up immediately. On wet rock you have essentially no grip — set your speed before you get on the rock and do not adjust it while you're on it. Plan ahead."
- "Your dry-weather pace on a wet trail is producing slides. You're fighting for grip instead of working with what the conditions offer. Reduce pace by 30% and you'll be surprised how much more controlled it feels."
- "That root section is going to put you on the ground if you keep crossing it diagonally in the wet. Cross every root as close to 90 degrees as you can. Perpendicular crosses offer the most grip on wet roots."

### Progression Prompts
- "After your next rain session, assess: which sections were most affected by the wet conditions? Which were surprisingly grippy? Build a mental map of your local terrain's weather response."
- "Before the next event with uncertain weather, check a mountain-specific weather forecast and write down three specific adaptations you'll make if conditions are as predicted."
- "Next session after rain: deliberately ride the first lap as an assessment lap — no pace targets, just observations of every section's condition. That information is worth more than a fast lap 1."

## 6. DRILLS & PRACTICE EXERCISES

### Drill 1: Deliberate Wet Terrain Assessment Lap
**Purpose:** Build the habit of using the first lap after any rain as a deliberate condition assessment, not a pace lap.
**Setup:** A session starting after rain (or during rain).
**Execution:**
1. Commit to riding lap 1 at 60% of normal pace.
2. On every section, actively assess: what is the grip level? Is this the expected wet level, or the dry-after-wet transition? Is the surface confirming or surprising expectations?
3. On the sections where grip confirmed expectations: return to moderate pace on lap 2.
4. On the sections where grip surprised (better or worse than expected): adjust pace up or down on lap 2 accordingly, then verify.
**Success Criteria:** Rider completes lap 1 as an assessment, builds an accurate wet-condition terrain map, and applies the map on lap 2 for more confident riding.
**Duration/Reps:** Applied as standard practice for any post-rain session start.
**Progression:** Develop a personal terrain map (which local sections are most/least affected by rain) over multiple wet sessions.

### Drill 2: Wet Root Crossing Practice
**Purpose:** Build the specific skill of 90-degree root crossings and neutral throttle technique in wet conditions.
**Setup:** A section of wet roots — either natural after rain or in a shaded section that stays damp.
**Execution:**
1. Walk the root section first to identify the angle of each major root.
2. Plan your crossing path: where can you cross each root at closest to 90 degrees?
3. Ride slowly (walking pace) through the section, crossing each root at 90 degrees with neutral throttle.
4. Gradually increase speed on subsequent passes, maintaining the 90-degree rule.
5. Test what happens at speed when a root is crossed at 45 degrees (carefully, at low speed) — feel the difference in grip.
**Success Criteria:** Rider consistently crosses wet roots at or near 90 degrees at moderate speed, with no slides or wheel deflections.
**Duration/Reps:** Repeated over multiple wet sessions.
**Progression:** Increase speed. Add roots with conflicting optimal crossing angles (where you cannot cross both at 90 degrees) — learn to prioritise.

### Drill 3: Brake Recovery After Water Crossing
**Purpose:** Build the automatic habit of drying brakes after water crossings and develop a feel for reduced wet brake performance.
**Setup:** A shallow water crossing that can be repeated safely.
**Execution:**
1. Cross the water feature.
2. Immediately (within 10m of crossing) apply the front brake lightly — once, twice, three times. Note the feel difference between the first application (reduced grip) and the third (drying).
3. Note at which application the brake returns to normal feel.
4. Apply the same process to the rear brake.
5. In subsequent crossings, perform the same protocol automatically.
**Success Criteria:** Rider habitually dries brakes after every water crossing without being prompted. Can articulate the expected recovery time.
**Duration/Reps:** Performed at every water crossing until automatic.
**Progression:** Test with larger water features where the rotors are more thoroughly wet. Time the recovery more precisely.

### Drill 4: Dry-After-Wet Recognition Training
**Purpose:** Develop visual recognition of the dry-after-wet grip illusion surface condition before entering it at pace.
**Setup:** A trail after a rain event that is now "drying out."
**Execution:**
1. Stop before each corner and visually assess the surface: look for the dull, chalky appearance of the dry-after-wet transition (vs the bright, powdery appearance of fully dry loose surfaces).
2. Enter the corner at conservative speed. Assess grip.
3. After the corner: was your visual assessment correct? Did the surface grip as dry or as the dry-after-wet illusion?
4. Build a personal vocabulary for how the two conditions look in your local terrain types.
**Success Criteria:** Rider can visually distinguish dry-after-wet from fully dry with 70%+ accuracy before entering the surface.
**Duration/Reps:** Post-rain sessions, repeated over one season.
**Progression:** Apply the assessment at higher confidence speeds as visual discrimination improves.

## 8. TERRAIN & CONTEXT VARIATIONS

### Mountain Enduro / Alpine Terrain
Mountain weather changes rapidly and unpredictably. A session that starts dry may see rain within an hour. The altitude effect (terrain at 2,000m dries more slowly than at 500m because humidity is higher and temperature is lower) means wet conditions persist longer. Shaded north-facing sections may stay wet all day even in sunny conditions. Mountain-specific weather monitoring is essential.

### Desert / Dry Climate
Rain events in desert environments produce a unique terrain change: the soil, which has little experience of water, initially repels rain on a hardened surface crust, then absorbs rapidly as the crust breaks down. Flash flooding risk. After rain, desert terrain often becomes incredibly slippery on the still-hardened but surface-wet sections. Allow 24-48 hours before riding desert terrain after rain if possible.

### Forest / Loamy Terrain
Loamy forest terrain absorbs moisture well and dries moderately quickly in the upper layer. However, beneath the dry top layer, moisture may persist for days — especially under the leaf litter and root layer. Forest terrain may appear and feel dry but have a damp, low-grip base exposed during aggressive inputs. The dry-after-wet phenomenon is particularly pronounced in loamy forest terrain.

### Motocross Track in Rain
MX tracks, designed to be raced on and groomed regularly, handle rain differently from natural terrain. Groomed tracks typically become faster in light rain (moisture compacts the loose top layer). In heavy rain, the track becomes a mud race. The rapid transition from dry MX to wet MX is well-studied — many riders maintain aggressive pace in light rain on MX tracks, but the surface can suddenly deteriorate in heavy rain.

## 9. EXPERT INSIGHTS & SOURCES

### Key Insights
- **Taddy Blazusiak** (5x Hard Enduro World Champion): has spoken in multiple interviews about the grip "completely disappearing" on wet rock in extreme enduro — noting that the only adjustment is anticipating the zero-grip situation in advance, not trying to find grip in it. "If it's wet rock, you don't have grip. You plan around that fact."
- **Weather forecasting for enduro events** (Enduro21 event preparation guides): mountain weather forecasts diverge significantly from valley forecasts and should be consulted separately using mountain-specific resources. Enduro21 consistently advises riders to check altitude-specific forecasts for events above 500m elevation.
- **Wet root physics** (documented in trail building and motorcycle coaching literature): wet bark-on roots have a coefficient of friction approaching 0.05 — comparable to wet ice. No tyre compound or pressure adjustment meaningfully changes this. The only effective technique is minimising contact time and load on the root.
- **Dry-after-wet rider crashes** (enduro accident analysis): a significant proportion of enduro crashes occur in the first 1-2 laps of events held shortly after rain, specifically in corners that appear dry. This is the grip illusion condition — documented in FIM enduro safety studies.
- **Brake fade in wet conditions** (brake manufacturer testing): water on a disc-pad interface temporarily reduces friction coefficient by 30-50% from dry. Recovery is confirmed to take 3-5 moderate applications. This is the technical basis for the post-water-crossing brake-drying protocol.
