# Altitude Effects on Performance

## 1. OVERVIEW

Altitude is a performance variable that affects both the machine and the rider simultaneously and in compounding ways. At elevation, air density decreases — approximately 3% per 300m of altitude gain. Lower air density means less oxygen available for both combustion (reducing engine power) and respiration (reducing the rider's aerobic capacity). At 2,000m above sea level, an engine produces approximately 85-88% of its sea-level power output, and a rider's VO2 max is reduced by 8-12% compared to sea level. These reductions interact: a less powerful engine and a less capable rider must navigate terrain that is often more demanding precisely because of the altitude (steeper, more technical mountain terrain). Understanding altitude effects allows riders to prepare appropriately, set realistic performance expectations, and make informed setup decisions — particularly jetting on carburettor-equipped bikes.

## 2. CORE PRINCIPLES

### Principle 1: Air Density Decreases Predictably With Altitude
Air density follows a known relationship with altitude. At sea level: approximately 1.225 kg/m³. At 1,000m: approximately 1.112 kg/m³ (9% less). At 2,000m: approximately 1.007 kg/m³ (18% less). At 3,000m: approximately 0.909 kg/m³ (26% less). The practical rule of thumb: approximately 3% power loss per 300m (1,000 feet) of altitude gain. This is a linear approximation valid up to approximately 4,000m — at higher altitudes the relationship becomes non-linear.

### Principle 2: Engine Power Loss — Different Mechanisms for Carburettor vs Fuel Injection
**Carburettor-equipped bikes:** The carburettor meters fuel based on airflow — it is calibrated for a specific air density. At altitude, the same volume of air flowing through the carburettor contains less oxygen, so the fuel-air mixture becomes richer (relatively too much fuel). Rich running produces: reduced power, flat throttle response, increased fuel consumption, black smoke from the exhaust, and potential fouling of spark plugs on two-strokes. The solution is to install smaller main jets (leaner jetting) for sustained riding at altitude. The required change: approximately one main jet step (3-5 sizes) per 1,000m of altitude gain.

**Fuel-injected bikes:** The ECU uses inputs from the MAP (Manifold Absolute Pressure) or barometric pressure sensor to automatically adjust fuelling for altitude. Modern FI bikes (KTM, Husqvarna, GASGAS, TM, Beta) compensate automatically and require no rider intervention for jetting. However, even FI bikes lose power at altitude — the ECU can optimise the fuel-air ratio but cannot increase the oxygen content of the air.

### Principle 3: Rider Performance at Altitude — VO2 Max Reduction
The rider's aerobic performance follows altitude in a predictable way. VO2 max (the maximum rate of oxygen consumption during exercise) decreases approximately 1% per 100m above 1,500m. Below 1,500m, the effect is minimal for most riders. Above 2,000m, the effect is noticeable. At 3,000m, VO2 max is approximately 15-20% below sea-level values for unacclimatised riders. Practical implications: the rider fatigues faster at altitude, requires more rest between demanding sections, and should expect their normal pace to feel harder — or accept a slower pace to maintain the same perceived exertion.

### Principle 4: Acclimatisation Restores a Significant Proportion of Performance
The body adapts to altitude over 2-10 days of exposure. The primary adaptation is an increase in red blood cell count and haemoglobin concentration, improving oxygen transport capacity. After 3-5 days at altitude, most riders recover 50-70% of their altitude-induced VO2 max loss. After 2-3 weeks, near-complete acclimatisation is achieved. For multi-day enduro events at altitude: riders who arrive 2-3 days early, sleep at or near the event altitude, and avoid hard training on day 1 will perform measurably better on race days than riders who arrive the day before and push hard immediately.

### Principle 5: Altitude Sickness — Recognise and Respond
Altitude sickness (Acute Mountain Sickness, AMS) can affect riders above 2,500m, particularly those who ascend rapidly. Symptoms: headache (most common), nausea, fatigue, dizziness, difficulty sleeping. AMS is a medical condition — it requires rest, descent if symptoms worsen, and hydration. Continue riding is not the appropriate response to AMS. Riding with AMS symptoms significantly degrades decision-making and reaction time — the combination with technical terrain is dangerous. Key rule: if a riding partner shows AMS symptoms, stop the session and descend to a lower altitude. If headache persists for more than 4 hours at rest, seek medical attention.

### Principle 6: Brake Fluid Boiling Point at Altitude
Water boils at a lower temperature as altitude increases: at sea level 100°C; at 3,000m approximately 90°C. Brake fluid is formulated to resist boiling, but the boiling point threshold is relative to ambient conditions. At altitude, the temperature threshold at which brake fluid vapour-locks is effectively lower — the fluid reaches its boiling point more easily in sustained hard braking. This is most relevant for long alpine descents at high altitude. Use fresh brake fluid and sintered pads in high-altitude events with significant descent.

## 3. TECHNIQUE BREAKDOWN BY LEVEL

### 3a. Beginner Level
**Goal:** Understand that altitude reduces both engine power and personal performance. Set realistic expectations for altitude riding. Know the AMS warning signs.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Understanding that feeling more tired than expected at altitude is normal and not a sign of fitness failure
- Recognising AMS symptoms and knowing the correct response (rest and descend)
- Not trying to match sea-level performance on the first day at altitude
- Staying hydrated — altitude accelerates dehydration through increased respiration

**What Good Looks Like:**
On their first day at a mountain enduro event at 2,500m, the beginner rider notices the sections feel harder and they are getting out of breath more quickly than at home. Rather than pushing harder to compensate, they reduce pace slightly, take more frequent rest breaks, and stay well-hydrated. They do not develop AMS symptoms.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Attributing altitude performance reduction to technique failure:** "I can't ride this section — I must be worse than I thought." → Altitude performance reduction is physiological, not skill-related. → Adjust pace expectations, not self-assessment.
- **Ignoring headache as "just a headache":** AMS headache is a symptom requiring attention. → Treat headache at altitude as AMS until proven otherwise. Rest and monitor.

### 3b. Intermediate Level
**Goal:** Plan for altitude events with appropriate preparation (acclimatisation, jetting if applicable). Understand the altitude-specific setup requirements.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Planning arrival at altitude 2-3 days before events
- Checking whether the bike is carburettor or fuel-injected and knowing what adjustment (if any) is needed for altitude
- Main jet change calculation for carburettor bikes at altitude
- Brake fluid freshness for high-altitude descents
- Hydration strategy at altitude: increased fluid requirement

**What Good Looks Like:**
Two days before a mountain enduro at 2,000m, the intermediate rider arrives, rides easy sessions the first day, and rests well. Their 300F has FI and needs no jetting adjustment. They have replaced the brake fluid with fresh DOT 4 before the event. By race day, they have partially acclimatised and ride at 90% of their normal performance level — well aware it won't be 100% and having set expectations accordingly.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Not accounting for acclimatisation time:** Arriving the day before a race at 2,500m and expecting full performance. → Allow 2-3 days minimum. → Plan travel accordingly.
- **Forgetting that carburetted bikes need jetting changes:** Running a carburetted bike at altitude without lean jet adjustment produces rich running, reduced power, and potential fouled plugs. → Know your bike's setup requirements before travelling to altitude.

### 3c. Advanced Level
**Goal:** Optimise setup and strategy specifically for high-altitude events. Manage altitude acclimatisation as a deliberate performance strategy.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Jet selection for specific altitudes (carrying jet kits to events)
- The interaction between altitude power loss and gearing: shorter gearing partially compensates for altitude power loss (see SETUP-03)
- Acclimatisation protocols for competition: sleep high, train low approach
- Altitude-specific pacing: sustainable pace at altitude is lower than sea level — managing this across a multi-day event without accumulated fatigue
- Managing the AMS vs overexertion distinction in teammates/riding partners

**What Good Looks Like:**
An advanced rider preparing for the Erzbergrodeo (event altitude approximately 1,500m). They arrive 3 days early, complete two easy reconnaissance sessions at altitude. Their two-stroke 300 has a carburettor — they install a main jet 4 sizes leaner than their sea-level setup. They add one tooth to the front sprocket (taller gearing) to compensate for the reduced top-end power at altitude. On race day, they are partially acclimatised and the bike is correctly jetted. They pace the event 5% below their normal sea-level effort.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Over-leaning carburettors at altitude:** Going too many jet sizes lean can produce lean seizure on two-strokes. → Change 2-3 jet sizes at a time and test. Do not go straight to maximum lean setting.
- **Ignoring altitude effects on two-stroke power delivery:** Two-strokes lose their powerband characteristics at altitude — the peak-power RPM changes with air density. The powerband becomes broader and lower. Riders expecting the same hit will be surprised.

## 4. DIAGNOSTIC CUES (AI COACHING ENGINE REFERENCE)

### Visual Cues — Correct Technique
- Rider maintaining consistent (if reduced) performance across altitude-exposed sections
- Appropriate rest breaks at altitude without embarrassment — normal altitude response
- Carburettor bike running cleanly (no black smoke, no fouled plug) indicating correct jetting
- Rider hydrating consistently throughout an altitude session

### Visual Cues — Error Indicators
- Carburettor bike producing black smoke from exhaust: rich running from altitude un-compensated jetting
- Rider showing disorientation, headache complaints, nausea at altitude: AMS symptoms — should stop
- Rider pushing dramatically harder than sustainable to maintain sea-level pace: not accounting for altitude limitation
- Excessive fatigue early in session relative to terrain difficulty: altitude fatigue onset, not fitness failure

### Audio Cues
- Four-stroke engine "hunting" or stumbling on throttle at altitude without jetting adjustment: rich mixture from altitude un-compensated carburation
- Two-stroke with reduced powerband or "flat" top-end: altitude power loss, normal — may need leaner jetting to partially restore the powerband
- Engine stalling on light throttle: over-rich condition from altitude

### Sensor Cues
- GPS altitude data above 1,500m should flag expected performance reductions for calibration purposes
- Heart rate at altitude elevated for equivalent effort compared to sea-level baseline: normal altitude response
- Engine RPM data at altitude showing same RPM as sea level but less vehicle speed: power loss at same engine output — speed reduction normal

## 5. COACHING CUES & LANGUAGE

### Positive Reinforcement
- "You managed your altitude session well. You paced appropriately, hydrated, took rest breaks. Your performance will improve day 2 and day 3 as you acclimatise."
- "The bike is running cleanly at altitude — your jetting change was correct. That clean power delivery will give you better results than a too-rich setup."
- "Good self-awareness — you recognised the altitude fatigue and backed off the pace before it became a safety issue. That is experienced riding."

### Corrective Feedback
- "Your bike is running rich at altitude — black smoke and flat response. We need to drop 3-4 main jet sizes before the next section. Riding a rich-running carburetted bike at altitude costs you significant power."
- "You're pushing much harder than you should to maintain your normal pace. Altitude reduces your capacity and this is real. Back off 10-15% and you'll be sustainable for the whole event — push this hard and you'll blow up by midday."
- "Your riding partner has a headache and is looking pale — those are AMS symptoms. We stop here, rest for 30 minutes, and if the headache doesn't improve we descend. There is no riding through AMS safely."

### Progression Prompts
- "Before your next altitude event, calculate the expected power loss: take the event altitude in metres, divide by 300, multiply by 3%. That is your percentage power loss. If you're going to 2,400m on a 250cc, expect roughly 24% power reduction. Plan accordingly."
- "If you're riding a carburetted bike, get a jet kit before any event above 1,500m. Being able to change jetting at the trailhead is worth far more than the £30 the kit costs."
- "Plan your next altitude event with a 3-day acclimatisation window. Compare your performance on day 1 vs day 3. That data will tell you whether your body acclimatises quickly or slowly — that is personalised information you can use for future planning."

## 6. DRILLS & PRACTICE EXERCISES

### Drill 1: Altitude Performance Baseline Assessment
**Purpose:** Establish personal altitude performance data to enable accurate expectation-setting and pacing for future altitude events.
**Setup:** A known test section ridden both at sea level/low altitude and at a target altitude event.
**Execution:**
1. At a familiar trail at low altitude, ride a consistent test section and note: time, perceived exertion (1-10 scale), heart rate if available.
2. At an altitude event or training session, ride a section of equivalent difficulty. Note the same metrics.
3. Calculate the performance difference: how much slower? How much higher perceived exertion?
4. After 2-3 days at altitude, re-test. Note any improvement.
**Success Criteria:** Rider has personal data on their altitude performance impact and acclimatisation rate.
**Duration/Reps:** Two measurement sessions: day 1 and day 3 at altitude.
**Progression:** Repeat at multiple altitude levels (1,500m, 2,000m, 2,500m) to build a personal altitude-performance curve.

### Drill 2: Carburettor Jetting Altitude Adjustment
**Purpose:** Learn to correctly re-jet a carburettor for altitude riding and verify the result.
**Setup:** A jet kit appropriate for the bike (main jets typically in increments of 2-5 units), appropriate tools.
**Execution:**
1. Identify the current main jet size (stamped on the jet, or from the owner's manual).
2. Calculate the target altitude jet size: approximately 2-3 jet sizes smaller per 1,000m of altitude gain.
3. Fit the new jet. Start the engine and observe: does it idle cleanly? Is there black smoke? Does it respond cleanly to throttle?
4. Ride a moderate section and assess: any flat spots? Fouling? Black smoke?
5. If still running rich (symptoms present): go one more jet size lean. Verify again.
6. If running lean symptoms appear (popping on overrun, reduced pulling power, overheating): go one jet size richer.
**Success Criteria:** Rider can correctly identify and install the appropriate main jet for altitude, verify the result, and make fine adjustments.
**Duration/Reps:** One practice session at home before an altitude event. At the event: final adjustment after initial test ride.
**Progression:** Also adjust needle position (clip) for finer control if needed. More advanced jetting involves pilot jet and air screw adjustments.

### Drill 3: Altitude Pacing Session
**Purpose:** Develop the felt sense of appropriate altitude pace and practise maintaining it rather than defaulting to sea-level effort levels.
**Setup:** An altitude session above 1,500m. Heart rate monitor (helpful but not required).
**Execution:**
1. Set a target perceived exertion of 6/10 — noticeably working but not at maximum effort.
2. Ride for 30 minutes at this perceived exertion.
3. Note the actual speed and terrain coverage compared to a sea-level session at the same perceived exertion.
4. Resist the urge to push harder to match sea-level speed — maintain the 6/10 effort level.
5. At the end of the 30 minutes, note: is the effort still 6/10 or has it crept up to 8/10?
**Success Criteria:** Rider maintains consistent effort level across the session without pushing above altitude-appropriate levels.
**Duration/Reps:** Applied to every altitude session on day 1 and day 2.
**Progression:** Extend the sustainable effort level and duration as acclimatisation progresses across the trip.

### Drill 4: AMS Symptom Recognition Training
**Purpose:** Ensure all riding group members can identify AMS symptoms and know the correct response.
**Setup:** Pre-ride briefing before any altitude session above 2,000m.
**Execution:**
1. Brief the group on the three primary AMS symptoms: headache (most important), nausea, and unusual fatigue.
2. Establish the group protocol: any rider reporting a headache at altitude reports it to the group immediately.
3. Agree the response: rest for 20-30 minutes. If headache does not improve with rest, descend immediately.
4. Agree that no social pressure to continue riding overrides the protocol.
5. At the end of the session, debrief: did anyone experience any symptoms? Was the protocol followed?
**Success Criteria:** All group members can name the three AMS symptoms and the correct response.
**Duration/Reps:** Once before every altitude session above 2,000m.
**Progression:** Add the HACE (High Altitude Cerebral Edema) and HAPE (High Altitude Pulmonary Edema) warning signs for rides above 3,500m.

## 8. TERRAIN & CONTEXT VARIATIONS

### European Mountain Enduro (Alps, Pyrenees)
Events commonly reach 2,000-2,500m. Day stages may start at lower altitude and climb significantly — the rider experiences the power loss as they ascend, not from the start. Pacing across altitude change within a day requires continuous adjustment.

### South American Events (Patagonia, Andes)
Some enduro events in South America operate above 3,000m. At these altitudes, AMS risk is real and should be taken seriously. Acclimatisation is non-negotiable at these heights. Engine power loss is significant (25%+ for a carburetted bike) — leaner jetting and shorter gearing are both relevant.

### Erzbergrodeo (Austria, ~1,500m)
The Erzberg iron ore mine operates at approximately 1,500m elevation. Power loss at this altitude is modest (approximately 12-15%), but the combination with the extreme technical demands of the event makes the residual reduction significant. Most carburetted bikes benefit from slight jet correction.

### Scotland / UK Highland Events
UK mountain events rarely exceed 1,200m — altitude effects are minimal for most riders. The primary concern in UK highland riding is weather (cold, wet) rather than altitude. AMS risk is not significant below 2,000m in acclimatised European riders.

### Desert High Altitude (Morocco, Atlas Mountains)
Desert altitude events combine heat at lower elevations with significant altitude gain to cooler conditions. The temperature-altitude interaction creates variable conditions within a single day's riding. Hydration management is critical — both altitude and desert heat drive dehydration.

## 9. EXPERT INSIGHTS & SOURCES

### Key Insights
- **KTM Factory Racing altitude preparation**: documented approach of arriving 3 days early at any event above 1,500m, with specific nutrition and hydration protocols for acclimatisation. Factory riders treat altitude as a performance variable to be managed, not an unavoidable inconvenience.
- **International Society for Mountain Medicine (ISMM)**: Lake Louise criteria for altitude sickness include headache as the primary diagnostic symptom. The ISMM recommends descent as the primary treatment for any AMS that does not improve with rest.
- **Carburettor jetting at altitude** (Sudco international technical guide): provides specific jetting recommendations by altitude for a range of carburation systems. The general rule of one jet step per 300m above sea level is a close approximation of the more precise recommendations in this document.
- **Enduro21 coverage of high-altitude events**: consistently reports that teams from sea-level countries (UK, Netherlands) perform below expectation on day 1 of mountain events, with performance improving on days 2-3 as acclimatisation occurs. This is observational evidence of the acclimatisation window.
- **Fuel injection altitude compensation** (Bosch Motorsport documentation on MAP sensor-based fuel management): confirms that production FI systems on enduro bikes (Keihin, Mikuni FI units) automatically adjust fuelling for altitude via the MAP sensor. The adjustment is not perfect but is significantly better than no adjustment. Power loss from the physical air density reduction remains even with perfect fuelling compensation.
