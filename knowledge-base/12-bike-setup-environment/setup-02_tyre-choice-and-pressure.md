# Tyre Choice and Pressure (Including Mousse)

## 1. OVERVIEW

Tyre selection and pressure are the single most impactful setup decisions a rider can make per unit of effort. The difference between a correctly pressured, terrain-matched tyre and an incorrect one is the difference between a bike that finds grip and one that slides. Many riders run tyres significantly over-pressured — either from habit, mechanical sympathy, or fear of rim damage — and as a result sacrifice substantial grip and performance on technical terrain. Tyre choice involves three variables that must all match the intended terrain: compound hardness, knob pattern, and the decision between tubed/tubeless/mousse. Each variable affects traction, durability, weight, and maintenance requirements differently. This topic covers pressure selection across terrain types, mousse technology and its trade-offs, compound selection, and pattern matching to terrain.

## 2. CORE PRINCIPLES

### Principle 1: Lower Pressure Increases Grip — Within Limits
Air pressure determines how much of the tyre's contact patch is in contact with the ground. Lower pressure allows the tyre to deform around obstacles — rocks, roots, ruts — increasing the effective contact area and therefore friction. A tyre at 14 PSI on rocky terrain will have a significantly larger contact area than the same tyre at 18 PSI. The gains in grip from lowering pressure are substantial. The lower limit: below approximately 9 PSI for most tyres, the risk of the tyre rolling off the rim during aggressive cornering becomes significant, and rim damage from rim-to-ground contact increases dramatically.

### Principle 2: Pressure Ranges by Terrain and Discipline
Reference ranges for 18-inch rear and 21-inch front standard enduro tyres:
- **Hard enduro / extreme technical terrain:** Rear 10-12 PSI, Front 12-14 PSI
- **Cross-country enduro / mixed terrain:** Rear 14-16 PSI, Front 15-17 PSI
- **Fast trail / enduro sprint:** Rear 16-18 PSI, Front 16-18 PSI
- **Motocross / hard pack:** Rear 12-14 PSI, Front 14-16 PSI
These are starting ranges — the optimal pressure within each range depends on tyre carcass stiffness (stiffer casings can run slightly higher), terrain temperature, and rider weight and style.

### Principle 3: Mousse — No Pressure, No Punctures, Different Feel
A mousse (foam insert) replaces the air tube entirely. The foam fills the tyre and provides a roughly constant resistance equivalent to approximately 12-14 PSI. Key mousse characteristics: no puncture risk (major advantage in rocky extreme enduro), no pressure management required, weight increase of approximately 400-600g per wheel over a standard tube, and temperature sensitivity (the foam softens as it heats, effectively becoming softer during a ride — most pronounced in hot conditions and long sessions). Mousse is not rebuildable after significant damage and has a limited life (typically 2,000-4,000 km depending on brand and terrain). Mousse is the standard choice for extreme hard enduro and is increasingly used in regular enduro.

### Principle 4: Tyre Compound — Grip vs Durability Trade-Off
Tyre rubber compound ranges from soft to hard:
- **Soft compound:** Higher grip coefficient, more conforming to surface micro-texture, faster wear. Appropriate for rocky, technical terrain where maximum grip is required and durability is secondary.
- **Medium compound:** Balanced grip and durability. Suitable for most enduro applications.
- **Hard compound:** Lower grip, longer life, harder feel. Best for hard-pack motocross, sandy tracks, high-mileage trail riding.
The compound is usually designated by manufacturer codes (e.g., Michelin 42 = soft, 60 = medium; Dunlop AT81 vs AT81EX; Maxxis "S" suffix for soft). When in doubt for enduro technical terrain, lean toward softer.

### Principle 5: Knob Pattern Matches Terrain Type
Tyre knobs function by digging into terrain surface and shearing against it. Pattern selection:
- **Aggressive knobbies (deep, widely spaced knobs):** Mud, soft terrain, loam. The spaces between knobs allow mud to clear rather than packing the tyre. Examples: Dunlop AT81, Michelin Enduro Medium.
- **Intermediate pattern (medium depth, moderate spacing):** Mixed terrain, cross-country enduro. Best all-round choice. Examples: Maxxis MaxEnduro, Pirelli Scorpion Enduro.
- **Hard-pack / low-profile pattern:** Hard-pack, rocky terrain where knobs would deflect off the surface rather than digging in. Lower, more closely-spaced knobs maximise contact patch on hard surfaces.
- **Paddle/sand tyre:** Deep, scoop-shaped knobs for deep sand propulsion — directional only.

### Principle 6: Front vs Rear Tyre Requirements Differ
Front and rear tyres perform different functions and should be selected accordingly. The front tyre is primarily responsible for steering precision and front-end grip under braking — typically runs slightly higher pressure than the rear to maintain feel and precision. The rear tyre handles drive traction, braking traction, and the majority of power transmission — can run slightly lower pressure to maximise the contact patch. It is common and correct to run a different tyre on front and rear — a knobby rear for drive traction and a harder-compound front for durability and precision.

## 3. TECHNIQUE BREAKDOWN BY LEVEL

### 3a. Beginner Level
**Goal:** Understand the pressure ranges relevant to the terrain being ridden. Stop running the first-time fill pressure (often 20+ PSI) that comes standard on a new bike. Learn to check and set pressure before each session.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Buying a reliable, accurate tyre pressure gauge (analogue or digital, 0-30 PSI range — higher range gauges are less accurate in the critical 10-20 PSI range)
- Checking and setting pressure before each session, not just when the tyre looks flat
- Matching pressure to the primary terrain type of the day
- Knowing the lower limit: do not go below 10 PSI without rim protection or mousse

**What Good Looks Like:**
Before each session, the beginner rider checks both tyres with a gauge, adjusts to the appropriate pressure for the terrain, and notes the setting. After the session, they check again and note any pressure change (tube leak, slow puncture, mousse heating effect).

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Running pressure by feel rather than gauge:** Tyre pressure cannot be assessed accurately by pressing with a thumb in the enduro range. → Always use a gauge.
- **Not knowing their tyres' lower safe limit:** Different tyre/rim combinations have different minimum pressures. → Check with the rim manufacturer and tyre manufacturer if uncertain.

### 3b. Intermediate Level
**Goal:** Make informed tyre choice decisions for different terrain types. Understand the mousse option and its appropriate application. Develop the ability to change a tube in the field.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Selecting different tyre options for different event types (rocky enduro vs sandy cross-country)
- Understanding mousse: when it is worth the weight and cost premium (extreme enduro, events where puncture risk is very high)
- Developing field puncture repair ability: CO2 inflators, tyre levers, spare tube
- Reading tyre wear to understand personal riding habits (rear tyre centre wear = over-acceleration; edge wear = aggressive cornering)

**What Good Looks Like:**
For a rocky enduro event, the intermediate rider selects a soft-compound, intermediate-knob rear tyre at 10-11 PSI with a reinforced tube, and a medium-compound front at 12-13 PSI. For a sandy event the following week, they fit an aggressive-knob rear and run 14-15 PSI, adjusting for the soft terrain.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Running aggressive mud tyres on hard-pack:** The knobs deflect off hard surfaces rather than gripping — the bike slides laterally. → Knob pattern must match surface type.
- **Under-estimating mousse weight for cross-country events:** The 400-600g weight penalty is significant in a 3-hour enduro where puncture risk is low. → Mousse for extreme/puncture-risk events; tube for longer cross-country events on cleaner terrain.

### 3c. Advanced Level
**Goal:** Optimise tyre choice and pressure for specific event profiles. Understand how tyre temperature affects performance during a session. Make cost-effective decisions about tyre replacement frequency.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Tyre warming strategy: cold tyres (below approximately 40°C) are harder compounds — allow for a warm-up lap at moderate pace before pushing grip limits
- Reading rubber transfer on the tyre surface to understand which zones are working hardest
- Mousse temperature management: in long, slow hard enduro, mousse heats and softens — handling changes across a session
- Tubeless conversion evaluation: tubeless offers lower running weight, self-sealing for small punctures, ability to run 2-3 PSI lower than tubed equivalents

**What Good Looks Like:**
At an extreme enduro event, the advanced rider runs mousse front and rear. They know from experience that the mousse is at its stiffest for the first 20 minutes and softens to its operational feel after that. They ride the first section conservatively as both rider and tyres warm up. Their tyre choices were made 2 weeks before the event based on the event organisers' terrain report.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Not replacing tyres frequently enough:** A worn tyre with 40% knob depth remaining has significantly less grip than a new tyre. → Replace before the grip degradation becomes dangerous, not after.
- **Mixing tyre generations:** Running a new rear with a half-worn front creates asymmetric grip that can produce unexpected handling. → Replace front and rear together, or at least understand the grip differential.

## 4. DIAGNOSTIC CUES (AI COACHING ENGINE REFERENCE)

### Visual Cues — Correct Technique
- Tyre contact patch visibly flattening on rock surface: appropriate pressure allowing conformity
- Rear tyre leaving a full-width track in mud/soft terrain: pressure low enough for maximum contact
- No visible rim contact with ground during aggressive cornering (no low-pressure rim-strike)
- Tyre tread showing even wear across the contact patch width

### Visual Cues — Error Indicators
- Rear tyre contact patch appearing narrow and rounded on rocky terrain: over-pressure
- Rim impact visible in soft terrain (rim imprint in mud alongside tyre track): severely under-pressured
- Tyre visibly deflecting sideways in corners rather than gripping: over-pressure allowing tyre to roll on rim
- Uneven tread wear: centre-only wear (over-pressure) or edge-only wear (sustained cornering with over-pressure)
- Tyre rolling visibly off the rim bead in a corner: critically under-pressured

### Audio Cues
- Rim-strike metallic "clunk" on rock hits: under-pressure — rim contacting rock
- Tyre squeal or scrubbing in corners: over-pressure, contact patch too small, compound slipping
- "Thwack" sound from aggressive knobbies at high speed on hard-pack: incorrect tyre pattern for terrain

### Sensor Cues
- Accelerometer showing consistent high-impact events at rock hits that soft tyre should absorb: over-pressure allowing impacts to transmit through rigid tyre carcass
- Lateral slip measurements in corners above expected threshold for current speed: over-pressure reducing contact patch and grip
- Tyre temperature sensor (if fitted): below 40°C = compound not at operating temperature; above 80°C = compound degrading

## 5. COACHING CUES & LANGUAGE

### Positive Reinforcement
- "Good pressure choice for this terrain — I can see the tyre wrapping the rocks rather than bouncing off them. That is the grip difference you're looking for."
- "Your front tyre is doing its job in those corners — the contact patch is working and the steering is responsive. That compound is right for this surface."
- "You've matched the tyre to the terrain today. The setup is working for you, not against you."

### Corrective Feedback
- "Your rear tyre is skipping off rocks because you're over-pressured. On this terrain I'd want 10-11 PSI in the rear. Let some out now and try the section again — you'll feel the difference immediately."
- "That knob pattern is for mud — on this hard-pack it's deflecting instead of gripping. It's like running snow tyres on dry tarmac. The right tyre for this terrain is a harder-compound intermediate."
- "The rim is hitting the ground in those tight rocky sections — you're under 9 PSI. That is rim damage territory. Add 2 PSI and make sure you have a spare tube before we continue."

### Progression Prompts
- "Get a quality gauge. The £5 gauge from the petrol station reads 3 PSI high in the enduro range — that's the difference between riding well and fighting for grip all day."
- "Next event, write down your tyre pressures and the terrain type. After the event, rate your grip out of 10. Do this for 5 events and you'll have your personal pressure map."
- "Consider mousse for this type of event. One rocky event without a puncture stop is worth more time than the weight penalty costs you on the uphill."

## 6. DRILLS & PRACTICE EXERCISES

### Drill 1: The Pressure Comparison Test
**Purpose:** Develop direct sensory experience of the grip difference between correct and incorrect tyre pressure.
**Setup:** A rocky or rooty section where grip is clearly relevant. A tyre pressure gauge.
**Execution:**
1. Set rear tyre to 18 PSI (deliberately over-pressured). Ride the section and note grip feel, slide frequency, and confidence.
2. Let the tyre down to 11-12 PSI. Ride the same section. Note the differences.
3. Let down further to 9 PSI. Ride the section again. Note both the grip improvement and any negative handling changes.
4. Set to your preferred pressure based on the test. This is now a felt, personal reference, not an abstract number.
**Success Criteria:** Rider can articulate clearly what 11 PSI feels like different from 15 PSI and from 9 PSI on this specific section.
**Duration/Reps:** One session dedicated to this comparison.
**Progression:** Repeat on different terrain types (mud, sand, hard-pack) to build a pressure-feel reference library.

### Drill 2: Mousse Break-In Session
**Purpose:** Learn the specific handling characteristics of mousse before relying on it in competition.
**Setup:** Freshly fitted mousse tyres. A varied terrain loop.
**Execution:**
1. First 20 minutes: ride conservatively — mousse is at its stiffest when cold.
2. After 20 minutes: note the handling change as mousse has warmed. It becomes noticeably more compliant.
3. Ride a full 2-hour session and note any further changes in the final 30 minutes (hot mousse becomes softer).
4. Note cornering feel compared to tubes: mousse offers slightly different feedback because the pressure cannot spike on impacts.
**Success Criteria:** Rider understands the cold-to-hot mousse feel progression and adjusts riding style accordingly.
**Duration/Reps:** First 2-3 sessions on new mousse.
**Progression:** Use mousse in progressively more technical terrain to build confidence in it before competition.

### Drill 3: Field Puncture Repair Speed Test
**Purpose:** Build the mechanical skill to change a tube in the field within a competition-relevant time window (target: under 15 minutes).
**Setup:** Bike flat on its side or on a stand. Tyre levers, spare tube, CO2 inflators or mini pump.
**Execution:**
1. Remove the wheel (practice removing both wheels, as this varies by bike).
2. Remove the old tube using tyre levers — avoid pinching the new tube.
3. Check inside the tyre for the puncture cause before fitting the new tube.
4. Fit the new tube starting at the valve.
5. Re-seat the tyre bead carefully (do not use metal tyre levers near the bead on the final pass — use plastic or a tyre bead jack).
6. Inflate to correct pressure.
7. Time the whole process. Target under 15 minutes.
**Success Criteria:** Rider completes the full process within 15 minutes without damaging the new tube during fitting.
**Duration/Reps:** Practise 3 times before competition season. Repeat annually.
**Progression:** Work down toward 10 minutes. Practise in simulated field conditions (kneeling on rough ground, dim light).

### Drill 4: Tyre Wear Reading Session
**Purpose:** Use tyre wear patterns as a diagnostic tool to understand personal riding habits and technique.
**Setup:** Post-session, while cleaning the bike.
**Execution:**
1. After a session, examine the rear tyre: where is wear concentrated? Centre (straight-line braking/acceleration), edges (cornering with insufficient lean), or uniform?
2. Examine the front tyre: centre wear (hard braking reliance, insufficient corner entry), or edge wear (aggressive cornering).
3. Compare wear patterns to expected patterns for the terrain ridden.
4. Draw conclusions: "My centre rear wear suggests I'm spinning rather than driving forward — grip control issue" or "My even wear suggests good technique distribution."
**Success Criteria:** Rider can consistently read 2-3 meaningful insights from tyre wear patterns after each session.
**Duration/Reps:** 10 minutes post-session.
**Progression:** Track wear patterns across a full season to identify recurring patterns vs session-specific anomalies.

## 8. TERRAIN & CONTEXT VARIATIONS

### Rocky Technical Terrain
The highest-priority application for correct tyre pressure. Maximum grip on rocks requires soft compound and low pressure (10-12 PSI rear, 12-14 PSI front). Sidewall protection is critical on extremely rocky terrain — reinforced tubes or mousse prevent the sidewall punctures (snakebite punctures) that are the most common failure mode in rocky enduro.

### Deep Mud
Run the most aggressive knob pattern available. Pressure: 12-14 PSI (slightly higher than rocky enduro) because the main failure mode in mud is the tyre deforming sideways and rolling off the bead, not insufficient contact area — the mud itself provides conformity. Mud tyre choice is more important than pressure in mud.

### Sand Dunes / Deep Sand
Higher pressure than other off-road terrain: 15-18 PSI rear, 16-18 PSI front. Counter-intuitively, higher pressure in sand prevents the tyre from digging in and creating drag. Sand tyres with paddle knobs or long, widely-spaced knobs provide drive. Front tyre on sand: medium compound, moderately spaced knob — the front is steering, not driving.

### Hard-Pack / Dry Rocky
Intermediate pressure: 12-14 PSI rear, 13-15 PSI front. Hard-pack allows a somewhat higher pressure because the terrain is more consistent and forgiving of tyre rigidity. Hard-compound tyre is appropriate for durability on surfaces that are abrasive to soft compounds.

### Wet / Slippery Conditions
Lower pressure by 1-2 PSI from the dry equivalent to maximise contact area on slippery surfaces. Wet grip is fundamentally about tyre compound — hard compounds lose grip much faster on wet surfaces than soft. In wet conditions, soft compound is significantly more important than in dry.

## 9. EXPERT INSIGHTS & SOURCES

### Key Insights
- **Manuel Lettenbichler** (Extreme Enduro World Champion): consistently uses mousse in extreme enduro events where a single puncture would end a race. Described the switch to mousse as "removing one worry from a race that has enough of them."
- **Enduro Engineering**: documents that the most common rider error in tyre setup is running over-pressure — specifically running the "road bicycle" mentality of "harder is safer" in an off-road context where the opposite is true.
- **Dunlop** and **Michelin** factory enduro tyre recommendations: both manufacturers publish lower recommended pressure ranges for off-road applications than most riders actually use. Dunlop AT81 recommended enduro pressure: 11-13 PSI rear (vs the 18+ PSI many recreational riders use).
- **Mousse temperature effect** (Mefo/Michelin technical data): mousse equivalent pressure increases by approximately 2 PSI per 10°C temperature increase from baseline. A mousse set at the factory equivalent of 12 PSI in 20°C air will feel closer to 14-16 PSI equivalent in hot conditions (40°C+). Relevant for hot summer extreme enduro.
- **Tubeless conversion** (increasingly common in enduro): allows running 1-3 PSI lower than equivalent tubed setup because there is no tube pinch-flat risk. The primary limitation is that not all enduro wheels are tubeless-compatible without conversion kits.
