# FEATURE-08 — Berm / Banked Turn: Feature Profile

---

## 1. Feature Geometry & Physics *(MANDATORY)*

A berm is a corner surface that banks inward relative to the rider's direction of travel — the outer edge raised above the inner edge. The banking geometry contributes a component of the normal force directed toward the inside of the turn, supplementing centripetal force that would otherwise require lateral tyre friction. This makes the berm the only Feature KB entry where the terrain actively assists the rider rather than presenting a challenge to overcome.

**Key Mechanic: Terrain Does the Cornering Work.** Banking angle determines how much centripetal force the terrain provides. At optimal speed for the banking angle and radius, the terrain provides all required centripetal force; the tyre's lateral friction demand approaches zero. Over-speed shifts that demand back onto the tyres and ultimately beyond available traction.

**Primary Observable Signature:** Corner where the outer edge is visibly raised above the inner edge; rider traverses the arc at reduced lean angle relative to a flat corner at the same speed.

**Dominant Risk Axis:** Entry speed exceeding the berm's banking support capacity — driving the rider up the outer wall or over the lip; and deteriorated berm geometry removing expected banking support at exit.

### Physical Zones

- **Entry zone:** Last opportunity to set speed, body position, and line before committing to the banked arc. Speed selected here determines the required line: too fast forces a high line near the outer wall; too slow forces a base line into ruts and debris. On natural berms the banking is progressive; on built berms the entry transition is defined.
- **Arc zone:** The feature's operative extent — banking is active and providing centripetal support. Banking is not uniform: angle varies, surface hazards may interrupt, outer wall may be inconsistent. The rider's task is to use the terrain's geometry, not fight it.
- **Exit zone:** Banking reduces and disappears. Exit speed is typically higher than entry speed for riders who have used the berm effectively — this higher speed must be managed on terrain with no banking support. Natural berms frequently fail at exit: the outer wall crumbles while the main arc remains intact, a trap for riders whose speed was calibrated to the berm.

### Commitment & Reversibility Profile

**Point of no return:** Entry at speed. Once committed to the banked arc, significant speed reduction is not available — the banking constrains trail width and limits what braking can achieve on the tilted surface.

**Recovery window:** Full arc zone, narrowing toward exit. Early recognition of a speed or line error yields more options than late recognition. Mid-arc corrections must be smooth and progressive; abrupt steering inputs on a banked surface change effective lean angle rapidly.

**Bailout options:** Approach zone only at moderate and above. On minor berms, a foot-down stop is feasible at low speed. On moderate-to-significant berms at trail speed, the only practical bailout is stopping before entry.

## 2. Feature Forms & Variants *(MANDATORY)*

> **Classification hard rule:** A feature classifies as `berm` when the trail surface banks inward relative to the rider's direction of travel through the turn — the outer edge of the trail is raised above the inner edge, and the surface normal force component acts toward the inside of the turn, supplementing centripetal force. A feature classifies as `off_camber` (FEATURE-02) when the surface banks outward — the outer edge is lower than the inner edge, and the surface normal force opposes the required centripetal force. The discriminator is not the presence of a raised outer wall but the direction of the surface slope relative to the rider's turn direction: does the surface assist the turn (berm) or oppose it (off-camber)?

> **Classification boundary note — berm vs off-camber:** Trace the rider's direction of travel and identify the turn direction. If the trail surface falls toward the inside of the turn (outer edge lower, inner edge higher), it is off-camber regardless of any raised outer wall. If the surface rises toward the outside of the turn (outer edge higher, inner edge lower), it is a berm. Speed changes consequence but not classification. A berm taken at excess speed remains a berm; the resulting failure (running wide, launching over the lip) is documented within this entry. A deteriorated berm that has inverted at exit (outer edge now lower) transitions from berm to off-camber at the inversion point — classify by the dominant geometry for the observed failure event and note the transition.

> **Classification boundary note — berm vs jump (FEATURE-01):** When excess speed causes the rider to exit vertically over a berm's outer lip and become airborne, the airborne phase classifies as a jump event (FEATURE-01). The berm itself remains the approach geometry — the berm classification does not change because of the failure mode. In pipeline terms: if footage shows a berm followed by an airborne phase, classify `feature_type: berm` for the banked arc and note the subsequent airborne event. FEATURE-08 is relevant for diagnosing the decision and speed errors that led to the launch; FEATURE-01 is relevant for diagnosing the airborne and landing dynamics.

### Built / Constructed Berm

A berm formed intentionally on motocross tracks, purpose-built enduro courses, trail centres, and bike parks. Banking angle is close to uniform, the outer wall is defined, and geometry reflects deliberate design for a specific speed and radius.

**Severity range:** Minor to significant. Built berms rarely reach major in their designed state. Severity elevates when approach speed significantly exceeds design speed or when surface condition changes (dry clay to wet).

**Distinct failure pattern — casing the outer lip:** At excess speed on a built berm with a defined outer lip, the tyre contacts the lip edge, deflects upward briefly, and either drops back onto the trail or continues over. This lip-catch mechanism is specific to built berms with a distinct upper edge profile — it does not occur on natural berms with gradual crests.

### Natural Berm

A berm formed by erosion or repeated rider-deposited material at the outside of a corner. Geometry is irregular — banking angle varies through the arc, the outer wall may be partially overgrown, and the surface includes roots and rocks.

**Severity range:** Minor to major. Natural berm severity is highly variable — a well-developed natural berm on a frequently ridden trail can equal a built berm's consistency; a newly forming berm may have steep banking at one section and flat or off-camber at another.

**Distinct failure pattern — embedded obstacle mid-arc:** A root or rock on the berm face is not visible from approach because the surface is tilted away from the rider's sightline. At speed mid-arc, front tyre contact deflects the wheel either higher (toward the outer wall) or lower (into the rutted base). Recovery requires immediate steering correction while already in the corner arc — a higher-difficulty correction context than the equivalent on flat terrain.

### Deteriorated Berm

A natural or built berm whose geometry has degraded through erosion, repeated high-speed contact, weather, or lack of maintenance. Particularly hazardous because the entry arc may appear normal while the outer wall integrity and exit banking have changed invisibly from approach.

**Severity range:** Moderate to major. Deterioration elevates any berm's base severity because the support geometry may not be present at the critical moment.

**Distinct failure pattern — banking reversal at exit:** The main arc is intact and the rider uses the banking to carry speed through the corner. At the exit transition, the outer wall height reduces sharply — banking angle decreases or inverts to slightly outward-sloping. The rider arrives at the exit at berm arc speed with lateral support removed simultaneously as off-camber demand is imposed. This simultaneous removal and imposition is the defining trap of deteriorated berm exits.

### Berm on Descending Gradient

A berm located where the trail descends through or approaching the corner. The gradient adds approach speed that the rider may not fully account for — the bike accelerates between the last braking point and berm entry.

**Severity range:** Gradient multiplier elevates base severity by at least one tier.

**Distinct failure pattern — unplanned entry speed:** The rider brakes correctly but the gradient continues to accelerate the bike toward entry. Speed at berm engagement is above the intended level. The rider applies correct technique to a miscalibrated speed input — the error is in speed assessment, not execution.

## 3. Bike Behaviour *(MANDATORY)*

This section describes the mechanical response of the motorcycle to berm terrain at the physics level. No coaching language, rider intent, or corrective advice — that content belongs in Section 4.

### Normal Force and Centripetal Contribution

On a banked surface, the normal force is tilted toward the inside of the turn. Its lateral component toward the turn centre is the centripetal contribution — not friction, so it does not consume the traction budget. As banking angle increases, the tyre's lateral friction demand decreases for the same speed and radius. At the optimal speed for a given banking angle, the banking provides exactly the required centripetal force and the tyre needs zero lateral friction for the corner. Over-speed beyond this point shifts centripetal demand back onto the tyres.

### Lean Angle Reduction

On a banked surface, the required lean angle reduces by approximately the banking angle. At optimal speed, the bike traverses the corner near-vertical. The tyre contact patch operates closer to its vertical design axis, engaging the crown — the highest-traction zone — more fully than a flat corner at the same speed. At excess speed, the bike must lean more than the banking compensates; lateral friction demand increases and the bike tracks wide.

### Rear Wheel Traction Under Drive

The banking's normal force loads the rear tyre into the berm surface — the terrain actively presses the tyre into the ground rather than allowing gravity to pull it sideways. This is the mechanical opposite of off-camber (FEATURE-02), where throttle threatens lateral spin. Excessive throttle on a berm produces longitudinal wheelspin (in the direction of travel) rather than lateral slip — less dangerous because trajectory is not redirected.

### Suspension Pre-Compression on the Berm Face

The centripetal normal force from the banking pre-compresses the fork and shock on the berm face. This reduces available suspension travel for mid-arc terrain inputs. A bump on the berm face meets a suspension system already partially compressed — particularly relevant on high-speed berms where embedded roots or rocks are present.

### Steering Geometry on the Berm Face

Because the bike is tilted on the berm, the fork's steering axis is also tilted relative to the berm surface. Steering inputs produce different responses than the same inputs on flat terrain. Mid-arc corrections must be smoother and more progressive than the equivalent flat-ground correction to avoid abrupt lean angle changes.

### What Happens at Excess Entry Speed

The bike cannot follow the corner radius — its trajectory is wider than the berm's curve and tracks up the berm face toward the outer wall. On a berm with a defined outer lip, the tyre contacts the lip edge; the outcome (deflection back onto trail vs launch) depends on approach angle and speed. On a natural berm without a defined outer wall, the bike exits onto whatever terrain exists beyond the berm structure.

## 4. Technique by Severity *(MANDATORY)*

Severity is defined primarily by observable berm geometry (banking angle, consistency, outer wall integrity, surface condition) and approach speed relative to the berm's support capacity. Rider skill requirement is secondary commentary.

### Minor (Clear inward bank 15–25°, consistent geometry, dry firm surface)

**Entry technique:** Approach at or near normal trail speed. Identify the mid-height line before entry — not at the base (ruts and debris) and not at the top (less centripetal support). Standing, neutral weight through both pegs. No braking within the berm.

**Sustained technique:** Allow the banking to work. Maintain steady throttle through the arc. Look through the exit. Weight through both pegs equally — the banking provides lateral support, so peg weighting can be neutral. Allow the bike to run more upright than a flat corner at the same speed.

**Exit technique:** Allow the arc to open naturally at exit. Confirm the exit surface before accelerating. Gradual throttle application as banking reduces.

**Common errors:** Braking within the berm (removes the momentum that makes banking useful; can produce front traction loss). Riding through the base (rutted, loose — optimal line is mid-height). Adding unnecessary lean (reduces available traction budget without benefit).

**Coaching gate:** Standing technique (BIOMECH-01) confirmed. Smooth throttle discipline (CONTROL-01). No berm-specific prerequisites at minor severity.

### Moderate (Natural or partially deteriorated, bank 25–40°, mixed surface or damp conditions)

**Entry technique:** Scan the arc before committing — outer wall consistency, exit condition, visible surface hazards, approach gradient. Set entry speed deliberately — the berm's support is not uniform and excess speed removes mid-arc response margin. Enter mid-height, standing, neutral weight.

**Sustained technique:** Maintain steady drive but moderate if the surface changes. Scan ahead through the arc, not at the immediate surface. If banking varies through the arc, adjust line accordingly. Do not attempt significant mid-arc line changes — corrections must be progressive and smooth.

**Exit technique:** Begin assessing the exit while still in the arc. Reduce throttle smoothly approaching the exit on natural berms. If the exit transitions to flat or off-camber, speed reduction must begin before the transition, not after.

**Common errors:** Entering too low then steering up the berm face mid-arc (banked surface makes upward correction difficult; front may wash as rider pushes outward). Throttle on/off mid-arc (alters rear tyre loading against the berm face; can produce rear step-out on loose or damp surfaces). Scanning only the immediate surface (misses exit deterioration until too late).

**Coaching gate:** Standing technique (BIOMECH-01) confirmed. Smooth throttle discipline (CONTROL-01). Basic corner line reading — demonstrated ability to identify entry point, mid-arc line, and exit before committing.

### Significant (Deteriorating geometry or high speed, bank 40°+, embedded hazards, off-camber exit possible)

**Entry technique:** Full deliberate assessment before entry. Identify: outer wall intact through visible arc? Exit condition — banking continues or reduces/inverts? Visible surface hazards mid-arc? Approach gradient contribution? Set entry speed conservatively — berm assistance is lower value when geometry is uncertain. Enter mid-height.

**Sustained technique:** Maximum attentiveness through the arc. Each rock or root contact requires immediate response. Do not attempt to accelerate through deteriorating sections. Know from approach assessment which outer wall sections are intact — a missing section changes the consequence of excess speed from wall contact to exit onto unknown terrain. Knees slightly flexed throughout, ready to absorb mid-arc impact.

**Exit technique:** Begin deliberately reducing speed in the final section before exit geometry changes. If the exit transitions to off-camber, speed reduction must begin early enough to manage the transition where tyre friction must supply the full centripetal demand without banking assistance.

**Common errors:** Entering too fast without accounting for deteriorated outer wall sections. Carrying berm arc speed into a deteriorated exit (the most common crash scenario at significant severity). Treating a significant natural berm as a built berm without assessing outer wall integrity.

**Coaching gate — do not prescribe significant berm technique without confirmed prerequisites:** Rider must demonstrate reliable navigation of moderate berms without consistent line errors, mid-berm foot-down events, or post-berm traction loss. Exit speed management on natural berms must be confirmed in footage before coaching up to significant severity.

### Major (Severely deteriorated, partially inverted geometry, or launch-risk lip)

Major berm severity is a hazard and decision tier. A severely deteriorated berm — outer wall collapsed, banking partially inverted, exit geometry unknown — presents higher consequence than standard technique can reliably manage. A berm whose lip geometry at approach speed constitutes a launch ramp requires assessment of whether the airborne event can be managed (FEATURE-01 applies to the airborne phase) or whether the feature should be avoided or walked.

**Entry technique (if committed):** Minimum speed that allows the berm to be ridden. Line on the lower half of the face to maximise distance from any missing or inverted outer wall sections.

**Sustained technique:** Navigate each arc section as it presents — use the berm where it provides support, manage independently where it does not. Throttle discipline critical throughout varying geometry.

**Major severity assessment (before any technique prescription):** Stage 9 (Decision Engine) must assess before any technique recommendation: Does the outer wall provide consistent support through the full arc? Is exit geometry known and manageable at approach speed? Can approach speed be calibrated to avoid relying on the outer wall as a constraint? If the outer wall is absent or inverted at a critical section, or exit geometry is unknown: "This berm appears to have deteriorated to a point where the outer wall support cannot be relied on. Assessing on foot before riding, or choosing an alternative line, is the appropriate response."

## 5. Interaction Patterns & Failure Triggers *(MANDATORY)*

**Expected Outcome Patterns:**
- clean arc traverse (consistent mid-height line, speed matched to banking, smooth exit)
- imperfect but controlled (line slightly high or low, minor momentum adjustment mid-arc, section completed)
- wide exit (speed slightly above optimal, rider exits slightly past intended exit point but remains on trail)
- front wash at entry (front lateral traction loss on berm face, lowside)
- high exit over outer wall (trajectory carries bike over the lip, highside or tip-over)
- berm-to-launch (excess speed generates airborne exit, OTB or ejection)
- bog/stall at berm base (wrong line into rutted base, momentum arrest, tip-over or stall-drop)
- off-camber exit crash (banking support disappears at exit, lateral traction failure)
- bail / controlled stop on approach before entry

### Failure Chain: Front Wash on Entry — Excess Speed or Low Line → Lowside (technique / decision)

**Trigger:** Rider enters a moderate or significant berm at speed above the banking's support capacity, or on a line too low on the berm face where banking angle is least steep.

**Mechanism:** The berm's banking provides centripetal force proportional to banking angle. At entry speed and berm radius, the required centripetal force exceeds this contribution plus the front tyre's available lateral friction. The front tyre slides outward. On a line through the berm base, banking angle is typically shallower than mid-berm — the base receives less support at the same speed. Front wash at the base frequently occurs when a rider brakes late then releases: speed increases as the bike enters the section of least banking support precisely when the berm's assistance is smallest.

**Outcome:** Lowside at the berm entry zone or base, fall toward the inside of the turn. At trail speed on a minor berm, recoverable; on a moderate or significant berm at speed, significant injury risk.

**Stage 6 classification:** `technique` (primary — entry speed not calibrated to the berm's support capacity at the chosen line), `decision` (contributing — approach speed choice not adjusted to observed berm geometry)

**Stage 7 classification:** `lowside`

### Failure Chain: High Exit Over the Outer Wall → Highside or Tip-Over (line_choice / technique)

**Trigger:** Rider navigates a moderate or significant berm on a line too high on the berm face — from entry miscalibration, mid-arc steering error, or surface deflection from an embedded obstacle.

**Mechanism:** On the upper portion of the berm face, geometry typically provides less centripetal support than the mid-face. The rider's trajectory approaches the outer wall or lip at berm arc speed. At moderate speed, the outer wall absorbs contact and deflects the bike back. At higher speed, the bike contacts the outer wall at an angle that produces sudden lateral load on the front tyre — the front hooks momentarily against the wall and releases, the precondition for a highside. Alternatively, the front rides slowly over the outer wall and the fall is a tip-over onto outside terrain.

**Outcome:** Highside (if front tyre hooks and releases abruptly at outer wall contact) or tip-over (if bike rides slowly over the outer wall). Outside terrain is typically not prepared — may be rough, vegetated, or sloped away.

**Stage 6 classification:** `line_choice` (primary — line taken too high, approaching or contacting the outer wall), `technique` (contributing — mid-arc line correction not made when the high line was recognised)

**Stage 7 classification:** `highside` (if snap-back from outer wall contact), `tip_over` (if controlled-speed exit over outer wall)

### Failure Chain: Berm-to-Launch → OTB or Ejection (decision / momentum)

**Trigger:** Rider enters a berm at significantly excess speed — above the level where banking and available traction can redirect the trajectory into the corner arc.

**Mechanism:** Entry speed generates centripetal force requirement exceeding the sum of banking contribution plus maximum available lateral friction. The bike tracks progressively higher up the berm face and approaches the outer lip with upward velocity. On a built berm with a defined upper edge, the lip acts as an ascending ramp. Launch angle is determined by lip geometry and speed. Once airborne, FEATURE-01 dynamics apply: landing geometry and distance are critical variables, and available control inputs are very limited.

**Outcome:** Airborne flight with landing on terrain beyond the berm's outer wall. OTB if the rider's body momentum carries them forward over the bars during the airborne phase or on landing. Ejection if the landing geometry separates rider from bike.

**Stage 6 classification:** `decision` (primary — approach speed was not calibrated to the berm's capacity; commitment to that speed was the decisive error), `momentum` (contributing — excess forward momentum carried the bike beyond the berm's redirecting geometry)

**Stage 7 classification:** `otb` (if rider pitches forward at launch or on landing), `ejection` (if rider and bike separate in flight or on landing)

**Pipeline note:** When footage shows a berm followed by an airborne phase, classify `feature_type: berm` for the banked arc and note the subsequent airborne event for Stage 7. Co-retrieve FEATURE-01 for the airborne and landing diagnostic alongside FEATURE-08 for the pre-launch speed and decision diagnostic.

### Failure Chain: Stall / Bog in Rutted Berm Base → Tip-Over or Stall-Drop (line_choice / momentum)

**Trigger:** Rider takes a line too low through the berm — at or through the base of the banking rather than the mid-height optimal line.

**Mechanism:** The berm base accumulates braking debris, ruts, and displaced material. Even a berm whose main arc is firm may have a rutted, loose, or muddy base zone. The front wheel drops into a base rut — sudden forward deceleration. If momentum is insufficient to carry through (common after late braking), the bike stalls mid-berm with the front wheel in a rut and the banking pressing the bike laterally. The rider must hold the bike upright against the banking's lateral force while attempting to restart on uneven terrain.

**Outcome:** Stall-drop (bike falls slowly under the banking's lateral force) or tip-over (rider cannot balance the stationary bike on the banked surface). Fall is typically toward the inside of the berm — low-energy, near zero speed.

**Stage 6 classification:** `line_choice` (primary — the line through the berm base was the committed error), `momentum` (contributing — insufficient entry speed to carry through base material)

**Stage 7 classification:** `tip_over` (if bike falls laterally while stationary on the banking), `stall_drop` (if controlled slow-speed fall under the banking's lateral force)

### Failure Chain: Off-Camber Exit Surprise → Lowside or Tip-Over (decision / traction)

**Trigger:** Rider uses the main berm arc to carry speed through the corner. The berm's outer wall deteriorates or reduces at exit, transitioning to flat or slightly off-camber terrain. The rider arrives at berm arc speed with body position calibrated for banked cornering.

**Mechanism:** Throughout the berm arc, banking reduces traction demand below a flat corner at the same speed. At the exit transition, banking angle reduces toward zero or inverts. Centripetal contribution drops to zero at the same moment. If the exit surface is slightly off-camber, the terrain now contributes a centrifugal force (outward) rather than a centripetal one (inward) — combined lateral demand at berm exit speed, on a surface that may have different traction than the berm face, frequently exceeds the available traction budget.

**Outcome:** Lowside at the berm exit (front or rear lateral traction failure as centripetal support disappears and demand increases). Tip-over if the rider stabilises after a low-speed traction event at the exit.

**Stage 6 classification:** `decision` (primary — rider did not prepare for the exit geometry change; continued berm arc speed into a section requiring reduction), `traction` (contributing — lateral traction demand at exit speed on the off-camber transition exceeded the available budget)

**Stage 7 classification:** `lowside`, `tip_over`

### Pipeline Identification Notes

**Visual indicators for Stage 4 `feature_type: berm` detection:**

*Approach indicators:*
- Outer edge of a corner is visibly raised above the inner edge — the trail surface banks inward
- Previous rider tyre marks visible on a banked face at the corner's outside edge
- The corner geometry shows a defined raised outer wall on the outside of the turn direction
- Rider reduces speed modestly but maintains commitment and standing position before the corner — not a foot-down or full-stop approach

*Execution indicators (in-berm):*
- Bike runs with reduced lean angle for the observed speed and corner radius — notably more upright than a flat corner at the same speed would require
- Rider's upper body is more upright relative to the bike than a flat corner would require
- Bike tracks the berm face at consistent mid-face height rather than at trail surface level
- Exit speed appears higher than the turn geometry alone would suggest — the berm has supported higher arc speed

*Post-event indicators:*
- Bike and rider visible on the far side of the corner at exit = successful traverse
- Bike and rider on the outside of the berm, beyond the outer wall = exit over the lip or too-high line
- Bike stationary inside the berm at the base with rider still aboard = stall in rutted base
- Bike and rider on the inside of the corner (inside edge) = lowside at entry
- Rider airborne beyond the outer wall = berm-to-launch; co-retrieve FEATURE-01

**Audio markers:**
- Reduced or no engine braking sound through the arc — a berm allows maintained speed without hard braking
- Tyre scrubbing sound on the berm face — lateral tyre contact with the banked surface at speed
- Distinct impact sound at the outer wall — indicates contact with outer wall (too-high line or excess speed)
- No heavy braking sound before the corner — a well-ridden berm allows a shorter or absent braking zone compared to a flat corner at the same speed

**Severity indicators — distinguishing minor from significant:**
- Banking angle: gentle visible slope = minor; clearly steep face = moderate to significant; near-vertical outer wall = significant to major
- Surface consistency: uniform compacted surface = minor to moderate; visible roots or rocks embedded = moderate to significant
- Exit geometry: visible clean exit to normal trail = minor to moderate; exit geometry uncertain or outer wall reducing = significant
- Approach gradient: flat or gentle = base severity; steep descent into corner = elevate by one tier
- Surface condition: dry = base severity; damp = elevate by one tier; wet = elevate by one to two tiers

**Edge cases where Stage 4 classification confidence should be flagged low:**
- POV footage: inward banking is extremely difficult to confirm from a helmet-cam because the camera tilts with the rider. Classification confidence for berm should be flagged low for POV footage unless the outer wall is clearly visible above the rider's sightline.
- Distant third-person footage: gentle banking (15–20°) may not be distinguishable from a flat corner at distance.
- Natural berm in dense vegetation: the outer wall may be obscured. Reduced lean angle at a corner can suggest a berm but confidence is reduced.
- Berm vs off-camber at the same corner: some natural corners have berm-like geometry on one line and off-camber on a different line — classify based on the rider's observed line and turn direction.
- Deteriorated berm partially inverted at exit: classify the intact arc section as berm; note the inverted section as off-camber. If the clip covers only the exit, the feature may misclassify as off-camber — look for the main berm arc in approach footage.

### Observability Notes

**1. Reliably confirmable from footage (directly observable):**
- Presence of a visibly raised outer edge at a corner (berm classification)
- Whether the rider completed the corner, exited over the outer wall, or fell inside the berm
- Whether a front wash occurred at entry — visible as sudden lateral bike movement toward the inside of the corner
- Whether the bike contacted the outer wall — visible impact event or outer wall disturbance
- Approximate outer wall consistency — whether the outer wall appears uniform or shows visible deterioration through the visible arc
- Surface moisture on the berm face — wet or dry visible from sheen and colour

**2. Inferable with caveats (can be estimated but not confirmed):**
- Banking angle: estimated from the rider's lean angle and observed speed, but lean angle at speed is difficult to measure accurately from footage, and apparent banking depends heavily on camera angle and distance
- Entry speed relative to the berm's optimal: inferred from the arc the rider takes (high line = likely too fast; base line = likely too slow), but requires accurate assessment of corner radius and banking angle — both uncertain from footage
- Whether exit geometry transitions to off-camber: visible if the outer wall clearly reduces at the exit; difficult to confirm if the transition is gradual or occurs off-camera
- Whether the berm base is rutted: visible from close or clear footage; not confirmable at distance or in shadow
- Whether approach gradient contributed excess entry speed: visible if the descent is clearly observable in approach footage

**3. Cannot be determined from footage (pipeline must flag as unknown):**
- Exact banking angle in degrees
- Whether embedded roots or rocks exist in the berm face at angles or positions not visible in the footage angle
- Whether the outer wall has structural integrity (compacted earth vs loose and crumbling) — appearance can be misleading
- Tyre type, pressure, and compound — critical for traction calculation but not observable
- Exact entry speed — can be estimated relative to normal trail speed but precise speed is not observable
- Whether the rider intentionally chose a high line or was deflected to it by an embedded obstacle — rider intent is not observable

## 6. Entry Discipline *(adapted from Approach & Setup Requirements)*

> *For this continuous feature, this section covers what the rider must establish before the berm's arc begins.*

### Speed Selection

Speed selection for a berm is the inverse of most features. A berm rewards calibrating entry speed upward — the correct speed is the highest at which the mid-height berm line can be maintained with a controlled exit, not the lowest safe speed. Riders who default to braking before every corner under-use berms systematically.

For natural berms with uncertain exit geometry, calibration should lean conservative — exit surprise is the most frequent cause of significant berm crashes precisely because the main arc encouraged higher speed than the exit can support.

Gradient adjustment: if approaching a berm on a descent, entry speed is not the speed at the braking point — it is the speed after gradient acceleration through the approach zone. Braking intensity must account for this.

### Line Selection at Entry

Optimal entry line is lower-to-mid on the berm face — above the base (ruts, debris, minimum banking) and below the upper portion (less centripetal support, higher consequence of excess speed). On a well-formed berm, this is typically a line where the upper portion of the outer wall is visible above the rider while riding. A late entry — arriving already past the arc apex — creates a situation where the rider must steer sharply into the banking at full approach speed.

### Body Position Setup

Standing position established before the berm entry boundary. Neutral weight through both pegs — unlike most features where rearward bias is protective, extreme rearward bias on a berm entry over-loads the rear tyre and can produce rear wheel lateral movement on the berm face. Grip firm but not tense — the berm's lateral forces produce steering impulses and these should be absorbed through a relaxed grip.

### Gear and Throttle Preparation

A berm is an arc, not a single event — throttle is continuous. Select the gear that provides smooth drive through the arc without demanding abrupt power delivery: one gear above minimum viable is typical. Throttle should be steady or progressively increasing through the arc. Throttle fluctuation mid-arc changes rear tyre loading against the berm face and can produce instability on deteriorated or loose surfaces.

## 7. Terrain & Condition Interaction

**This section flags which Terrain KB entries should be co-retrieved and documents the feature-level interaction. It does not duplicate Terrain KB content.**

### Loam and Organic Soil Berms (retrieve TERRAIN-04)

Loam berms provide good traction when dry but the surface layer slides over packed substrate when damp — the outer wall of a loam berm is particularly susceptible to surface-layer slip under tyre contact at speed. Co-retrieve TERRAIN-04 when loam berm surfaces are identified or suspected.

### Clay and Hardpack Berms (retrieve TERRAIN-02, TERRAIN-05)

Dry clay berm surfaces provide excellent traction. Wet clay can lubricate the tyre contact patch abruptly rather than progressively, with limited warning — severity elevation from dry to wet clay berm is significant. Co-retrieve TERRAIN-02 when clay berm surfaces are present. Co-retrieve TERRAIN-05 when the berm base substrate is compacted hardpack.

### Mud Accumulation at Berm Base (retrieve TERRAIN-01)

The berm base is a natural collection zone for water and displaced soil — even a dry-surface berm may have a mud zone at the base. A rider taking a base line during or after wet periods encounters a sudden traction switch from berm surface (moderate to high) to base mud (low) at the moment of maximum lateral force demand. Co-retrieve TERRAIN-01 when wet conditions are present or berm base mud is visible.

### Condition Modifiers

**Dry conditions:** Most predictable traction and reliable banking support. Base severity classification applies.

**Damp / wet conditions:** Reduces available traction for the front tyre's lateral component and the rear tyre's drive component. A speed that flows cleanly through a berm dry will exceed available traction wet at the same banking angle. Elevate base severity by one tier when damp; two tiers when wet.

**Frozen conditions:** Near-zero traction on smooth portions. The banking geometry exists but the tyre cannot grip reliably enough to exploit it. Classify at major severity regardless of geometry — surface condition overrides geometric advantage.

**Post-rain erosion:** After heavy rain, the outer wall of natural berms may have experienced erosion. Assume one tier higher than dry assessment until the berm is confirmed intact by direct inspection.

## 8. Section Exit *(adapted from Exit, Landing & Recovery)*

> *Renamed from "Exit, Landing & Recovery" to "Section Exit" per schema adaptation rules for continuous features.*

### Exit Zone Management

Riders who have exploited the berm's geometry arrive at the exit faster than they entered. This higher speed must be managed on terrain with no banking support.

**Speed management into the exit:** Begin managing exit speed in the final section of the arc — not at the exit transition itself. Gentle throttle reduction or engine braking in the last one to two bike lengths prepares the bike for the terrain change. Braking on a berm face late in the arc compresses the suspension, changes the traction budget, and shifts weight forward at the moment banking support is already reducing.

**Body position transition at exit:** Berm technique (neutral or near-upright posture, weight through both pegs) must transition to post-berm terrain requirements before the exit boundary — not at it. If the exit is off-camber, transition to FEATURE-02 technique.

**Unknown exit geometry:** If exit geometry was not assessed before entry — common on natural berms where the exit is not visible — carry conservative speed into the exit zone. Exit onto off-camber at berm arc speed is one of the most dangerous berm scenarios and is entirely avoidable with exit geometry assessment before entry.

### Recovery from Within-Arc Errors

**Early in the arc:** Progressive steering correction is possible. Move the line lower by steering fractionally toward the berm base while maintaining throttle. Corrections must be smooth — abrupt steering input on a berm face changes effective lean angle rapidly and can produce front traction loss.

**Mid-arc:** Recovery is constrained. If the line is too high, choose between maintaining speed (accepting a high exit) or reducing speed (which changes rear tyre loading and may produce lateral movement). If the line is too low, maintaining throttle and allowing the banking to redirect the bike may work if the base rut is not deep.

**Late in the arc:** Commit to completing the exit with the current speed and line. Any significant line change in the final section is more dangerous than accepting the exit at the current trajectory.

### Consequence of Exit at Excess Speed

Options: engine braking (progressively before the exit transition), brake application (risky if traction is low or off-camber is present at exit), or accepting the wide line (if trail width allows). On narrow trails, a wide exit line may not be available. The worst outcome is late brake application on a partially off-camber exit at berm arc speed — this combines maximum traction demand with braking's weight-forward transfer, matching the off-camber exit failure chain in Section 5.

## 9. False Reads & Misidentification

### Berm That Reads as Off-Camber (and Vice Versa)

The most critical misidentification risk. The discrimination is turn direction relative to slope direction. If the slope's high edge is on the outside of the turn (banking inward), it is a berm. If the slope's high edge is on the inside of the turn (banking outward), it is off-camber. This requires: clearly identifying the turn direction, then identifying which edge of the trail is higher. A deteriorated natural berm that has partially inverted at exit may be both — classify by the dominant geometry for the observed failure event and note the transition.

### Berm Misread as Jump Approach

A steep natural berm outer wall on a corner may be misread as a jump ramp if approach footage shows the trail banking steeply upward. The discrimination is turn geometry: a jump has a ramp leading to an airborne exit; a berm redirects the rider through the turn. If the trajectory at the feature's terminus continues the corner arc, it is a berm exit. If trajectory has a significant upward component at a defined lip, FEATURE-01 applies to the airborne phase. When footage shows a berm followed by an airborne event, classify both features and co-retrieve both entries.

### Gentle Drainage Slope Misread as Berm

A berm requires both inward banking and a corner (a turn in the rider's direction of travel). A slight drainage slope on a straight section is flat trail with minor off-camber. A flat corner with a raised outer edge that provides less than 10° banking is a flat corner with a wall, not a berm.

### Severity Underestimation of Natural Berms on Descent

Camera footage of a natural berm on a descending approach compresses the apparent entry speed because all reference terrain is moving at the same rate. Severity assessment for natural berms on descending approaches should assume approach speed is higher than visually apparent — elevate severity by at least one tier when the berm is on or at the bottom of a visible descent.

### Built Berm Reads as Minor Regardless of Speed

A well-formed built berm reads as minor regardless of approach speed. Severity on built berms is determined by the rider's speed relative to the berm's design speed and geometry — not by the berm's visual tidiness. When assessing severity on built berms, the primary indicator is approach speed relative to corner geometry, not the berm's visual condition.

## 10. Coaching Gates & Prerequisites

### Minor Severity Prerequisites

- Standing technique (BIOMECH-01): confirmed — rider must be in standing position to use the berm's banking through the pegs
- Smooth throttle discipline (CONTROL-01): rider must maintain steady throttle rather than braking through the arc

Minor berms are accessible to beginners with confirmed standing fundamentals. The berm's banking assistance actually reduces the technique difficulty of the corner compared to a flat corner at the same speed — coaching should communicate this explicitly to riders who instinctively brake before every corner.

### Moderate Severity Prerequisites

- Standing technique (BIOMECH-01): confirmed and reliable on varied terrain
- Throttle consistency through corners (CONTROL-01): demonstrated ability to maintain smooth drive through a corner arc
- Basic line reading: ability to identify entry point, mid-arc line, and exit before committing to a corner

**Coaching gate:** If footage shows the rider braking within the berm arc, riding through the base consistently, or failing to carry any speed advantage compared to a flat corner at the same severity, coach BIOMECH-01 and basic line reading before addressing moderate berm technique.

Future skill tag: `berm_line_selection`, `corner_arc_throttle`

### Significant Severity Prerequisites

- Confirmed reliable moderate berm navigation — rider consistently completes moderate berms without base-line errors or mid-arc speed adjustments visible in footage
- Exit geometry assessment — rider demonstrates evidence of exit preparation before entry (line choice in final arc reflects visible post-corner terrain, not just the main arc)
- Throttle management through varied surface — sustained smooth throttle on surfaces with embedded hazards in a corner context

**Coaching gate — do not prescribe significant berm technique without confirmed prerequisites:** Rider must demonstrate reliable exit speed management on moderate natural berms before receiving coaching on significant severity berm technique. Consistent wide exits on moderate berms or post-berm traction loss indicates the exit management skill is not confirmed.

Future skill tag: `berm_exit_management`, `deteriorated_berm_assessment`

### Significant to Major Threshold — Decision-Level Gate

Before prescribing any technique for a major severity berm (severely deteriorated outer wall, partially inverted geometry, or confirmed launch-risk lip), Stage 9 (Decision Engine) should assess:
1. Whether the rider's footage demonstrates consistent significant berm navigation without outer-wall contact or exit traction failures
2. Whether the specific major berm has intact outer wall geometry through the full arc
3. Whether the exit geometry is known and manageable at approach speed

If any assessment is negative: "This berm appears to have deteriorated to a point where the outer wall geometry cannot be relied on for cornering support. Assessing on foot before riding, or choosing an alternative line, is appropriate."

### Progression Model

Minor (trail-speed berm, use the banking, maintain throttle, clean exit) → Moderate (deliberate line selection, mid-height entry, exit geometry assessment before commitment) → Significant (embedded hazard management, exit condition preparation, deteriorated geometry identification) → Major (decision-level assessment before entry; hazard classification tier, not a technique progression target)

The key coaching progression for berms is the inversion of instinct: the berm is an asset, not a threat. Coaching should systematically move the rider from "slow down for corners" to "use berm geometry to carry corner speed," while building the assessment skills to know when a specific berm's geometry and conditions support that exploitation and when they do not.

## 11. Feature Demands / Constraints

### Minimum Technique for Safe Navigation

- Standing position (BIOMECH-01): mandatory at all severity levels — seated position prevents independent weight management through the pegs and reduces the rider's ability to use the banking effectively
- Smooth throttle maintenance (CONTROL-01): sustained steady or progressively increasing throttle through the arc
- Line selection at entry: mid-height berm face, not through the base or along the outer wall
- Exit geometry assessment: mandatory at moderate and above severity before committing to entry

### Equipment Considerations

**Tyres:** Aggressive block tread provides better mechanical interlock on soft natural berm surfaces (loam, clay). Intermediate tread performs better on hard compacted built berms. In wet conditions, rubber compound becomes more important as mechanical interlock reduces. Not observable from footage.

**Tyre pressure:** Lower pressures increase contact patch compliance on irregular natural berm surfaces but increase pinch flat risk. Not observable from footage.

**Suspension:** Rebound damping speed affects how quickly suspension recovers between the approach zone and the berm face. Too-slow rebound on a berm entry transition can leave the fork compressed from the approach at berm engagement, reducing absorption capacity for mid-arc impacts. Not directly observable from footage as a coaching lever.
