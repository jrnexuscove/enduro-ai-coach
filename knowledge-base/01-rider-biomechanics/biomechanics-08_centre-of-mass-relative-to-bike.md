# Centre of Mass Relative to Bike

## 1. OVERVIEW

The centre of mass (CoM) is the single point that represents where all of a rider's body weight acts relative to the bike. Its position — forward, back, high, low, and lateral — determines how the bike handles, how traction is distributed between the front and rear wheels, and how stable the rider-bike system is in any given situation. Understanding CoM is essential for off-road riding because the terrain constantly changes the bike's pitch and roll, and the rider must consciously move their CoM to compensate. In off-road riding — particularly trials and extreme enduro — deliberate, precise CoM manipulation is one of the highest skills, allowing a rider to climb terrain that physics would suggest is impossible, hold the bike on steep cambers, and recover from near-falls.

## 2. CORE PRINCIPLES

### Principle 1: CoM vs Weight Distribution — The Distinction
CoM and weight distribution are related but not identical. Weight distribution refers to the proportion of the combined rider-bike system weight carried by each wheel. CoM refers to the spatial position of the rider's body mass relative to the bike's pivot points. A rider can shift weight distribution without dramatically moving their CoM, or can move their CoM dramatically. Understanding the distinction helps riders think more precisely about what adjustment they need.

### Principle 2: The Pitch Axis and Wheel Loading
The bike has a natural pitch axis roughly through the wheel contact patches. When a rider moves their CoM forward on a steep climb, they reduce rear wheel traction. When they move it rearward, they lighten the front. The relevant question is always: where is my CoM relative to the demands of this terrain? On a steep climb, CoM should be kept far enough forward to maintain front wheel steering contact but far enough back to load the rear wheel for traction. This balance point changes with gradient, surface, and speed.

### Principle 3: High CoM vs Low CoM
The height of the CoM affects lateral stability. A lower CoM (crouching, bending the knees, keeping the trunk down) is more stable laterally — useful in off-camber situations, loose corners, and moments of instability. A higher CoM (standing tall, upright trunk) is less laterally stable but gives better terrain vision and allows more dynamic movement. Expert riders lower their CoM deliberately in high-instability situations and raise it for vision and mobility on sustained technical terrain.

### Principle 4: Forward CoM for Front Traction, Rearward for Rear Traction
Moving the CoM forward loads the front wheel — useful for front wheel traction in corners, preventing front wheel washout on loose descents, and braking. Moving it backward loads the rear — useful for wheelspin control on climbs, preventing stoppies under braking on descents, and maintaining rear traction in soft terrain.

### Principle 5: Lateral CoM for Cornering
In corners, the CoM must move inside the corner (body lean, hip out) to create the lean angle needed for the bike to turn. Off-road cornering often involves the body leaning more than the bike — keeping the bike more upright to maintain tyre contact with the ground while the body compensates by leaning further. This is the opposite of road racing's hang-off technique. Understanding that the bike and body can have independent angles is fundamental to advanced off-road cornering.

### Principle 6: Trials Mastery as CoM Extremity
Trials riding represents the most extreme deliberate CoM manipulation in motorcycling. Trials riders balance stationary on one wheel, hop, rotate, and climb objects that appear physically impossible — all through precise, rapid CoM shifts coordinated with throttle and clutch. While enduro riders do not need trials-level mastery, the principles taught in trials translate directly to extreme enduro and technical terrain management. A brief course in trials riding delivers outsized improvement in CoM awareness.

## 3. TECHNIQUE BREAKDOWN BY LEVEL

### 3a. Beginner Level
**Goal:** Understand that body position affects how the bike handles, and develop the habit of moving the hips and trunk deliberately on climbs and descents.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Move hips forward (toward tank) on steep downhill sections — reduces stoppie risk, maintains rear contact
- Move hips backward (behind seat) on uphill sections — loads rear wheel for traction
- Understand why "sitting back" on a steep climb helps traction

**What Good Looks Like:**
On a moderate slope, the rider visibly adjusts their position as the gradient changes — forward on descent approach, rearward on climb approach. The adjustments are deliberate and in the right direction. The rider understands the cause-and-effect relationship between where they sit and how the bike responds.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **No CoM adjustment on gradient change:** Rider stays in one position regardless of terrain. → Teach the physics first: ask the rider to feel what happens when they sit forward vs back on a gentle slope — the front/rear loading change is immediately perceptible.
- **Moving too late:** Rider adjusts position after the gradient has already changed. → Pre-position: move CoM before reaching the slope, not once on it.
- **Confusing upper body lean with hip movement:** Leaning the shoulders forward is not the same as moving the hips forward. → Cue: "move your belly button forward/back," not "lean forward."

### 3b. Intermediate Level
**Goal:** Dynamic and anticipatory CoM adjustment — moving before terrain demands it, with precision appropriate to the terrain type and gradient.

**Progression Markers from Beginner:** Rider consistently adjusts for climbs and descents. No longer loses front traction on descents or spins the rear unnecessarily on climbs.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Pre-position CoM before entering technical sections — not reactive
- Begin using lateral CoM shift in corners (body lean independent of bike lean)
- Understand how surface type affects the optimal CoM position for a given terrain

**What Good Looks Like:**
The rider is moving their body position continuously and fluidly as terrain changes. In corners, body is visibly inside the bike's lean angle. On climbs, hips are behind the seat. On descents, body is forward of neutral. Transitions between positions are smooth and pre-emptive.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Body and bike lean at same angle in off-road corners:** The bike needs to be more upright than the body for traction on loose surfaces. → Drill: deliberately keep the bike vertical while leaning the body — exaggerate the separation to feel it.
- **Not lowering CoM in high-instability moments:** Riders stay tall when the bike slides or steps out. → Cue: when in doubt, lower yourself and widen your base (elbows out, knees bent).
- **Sitting too far back on moderate climbs:** Over-correcting from beginner habit, causing front wheel to lift and steer poorly. → Find the gradient-appropriate balance point.

### 3c. Advanced Level
**Goal:** Precise, rapid, and continuous CoM manipulation as a primary control input — used to manage traction, line, and bike attitude in real time.

**Progression Markers from Intermediate:** CoM adjustment is pre-emptive and terrain-specific. Body-bike angle separation in corners is habitual. Position changes are fast and precise.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Rapid CoM shift for extreme terrain: ledge climbs, drop-offs, step-ups
- Use CoM shift to actively steer the bike on technical terrain
- Combine CoM with throttle and clutch for maximum traction control on climbs
- Low CoM recovery position: when the bike is about to topple, lowering CoM extends the recovery window

**What Good Looks Like:**
The rider's body is constantly moving — there is no static position. Weight shifts fluidly from front to back, side to side, with each terrain feature receiving the appropriate adjustment. On steep climbs, the body hangs far behind the seat at extreme gradient. On ledges, the rider throws weight forward sharply over the step. On off-camber sections, the body leans dramatically inside while the bike stays near vertical.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Slow CoM transition for high-speed terrain features:** At pace, the transition from rear to forward CoM (cresting a hill into a descent) must be fast. Late transition = airtime with rearward CoM = potential endo on landing. → Specific high-speed transitions drill.
- **Over-relying on throttle to solve what CoM should address:** Riders who haven't mastered CoM use throttle to manage traction on climbs rather than weight position. → Remove throttle as a variable (slow climbs at minimum power) and solve with position only.

## 4. DIAGNOSTIC CUES (AI COACHING ENGINE REFERENCE)

### Visual Cues — Correct Technique
- Rider body visible behind seat on steep climbs, hips low and rearward — from side camera
- Rider body forward over front section of bike on steep descents — from side camera
- Body lean angle greater than bike lean angle in off-road corners — rider inside relative to bike — from front or behind camera
- Continuous, fluid body position changes as terrain changes — no static held position over varied terrain
- Weight shifts timed to terrain arrival (pre-emptive) rather than coinciding with or following the terrain change

### Visual Cues — Error Indicators
- Rider sitting centrally or forward on steep climbs — hips on or forward of the seat — from side camera
- Rear wheel spinning excessively despite open throttle — CoM too far forward removing rear traction load
- Front wheel losing contact with ground on climbs — CoM too far back, under-loading the front
- Body and bike at the same lean angle in corners — no lateral CoM separation — from front camera
- No body movement across varied terrain — rider maintaining one fixed position throughout

### Audio Cues
- Excessive rear wheel spin on gradients that should be manageable — indicates CoM too far forward
- Engine lugging or stalling on steep climbs despite sufficient throttle — rear wheel losing traction from forward CoM
- Abrupt engine stall on steep climbs — CoM shifted too far back, bike pitching rearward

### Sensor Cues
- Pitch angle data showing insufficient forward lean compensation on descents — consistent with neutral or rearward CoM
- Rear wheel speed significantly higher than front wheel speed on climbs — wheelspin from insufficient rear traction load
- Lean angle data showing bike and body lean at equivalent angles in corners — no lateral CoM separation

## 5. COACHING CUES & LANGUAGE

### Positive Reinforcement
- "Your weight was back before that climb started. That's why you had rear traction all the way up."
- "Good — body inside the bike in that corner. You kept the bike upright and leaned yourself. That's what keeps the tyre on the ground."
- "You threw your weight forward over that ledge perfectly. Front wheel loaded up and pulled you over."

### Corrective Feedback
- "You're sitting in the same position on flat, climb, and descent. The terrain is changing and your body needs to change with it."
- "Your rear wheel is spinning because your weight is too far forward. Move your hips completely behind the seat — get off the back of it — and feel the difference."
- "You're leaning the bike and your body at the same angle in that corner. Lean your body more, keep the bike more upright. The tyre needs to be as flat on the ground as possible."
- "You're adjusting your position after you're already on the steep section. Move before you get there — as you approach the bottom of the climb, start moving back."

### Progression Prompts
- "You've got the basic front-back position. Now start moving laterally in corners — keep the bike more upright by leaning your body inside."
- "Your CoM adjustments are correct but slow. Work on making them faster — the terrain won't wait."
- "Start thinking about CoM as your primary tool on this climb, not the throttle. Position first, throttle second."

## 6. DRILLS & PRACTICE EXERCISES

### Drill 1: The Slope Walk-Through
**Purpose:** Teaches the basic effect of CoM on front/rear loading in a low-risk context.
**Setup:** A gentle, safe slope (10-20 degrees). Bike running, first gear, clutch feathering.
**Execution:**
1. At the bottom of the slope, sit in a neutral position and note how the bike feels.
2. Slowly ride up the slope sitting forward — feel how the rear wheel responds.
3. Ride with hips well behind the seat — feel rear traction increase, front go light.
4. Find the balance point where front has contact and rear has traction.
5. Repeat on descent — forward position loads front; find the balance point.
**Success Criteria:** Rider can articulate what changed as position changed, and can identify the "balance point" for the given gradient.
**Duration/Reps:** 10-15 gradient passes.
**Progression:** Steepen the gradient progressively. Add surface variation.

### Drill 2: Body-Inside-Bike Corner Drill
**Purpose:** Develops lateral CoM separation from bike lean angle.
**Setup:** A gentle, open corner on a loose or soft surface where the bike will be more stable at steeper body lean.
**Execution:**
1. Ride the corner with body and bike at matched lean angles. Note the feel.
2. On the next pass, keep the bike significantly more upright while leaning the body inside to compensate.
3. Observe that the corner can still be navigated, with more tyre in contact with the ground.
4. Progressively increase speed, maintaining body-inside-bike separation.
**Success Criteria:** Rider can clearly demonstrate body lean greater than bike lean, and corner speed is maintained or increased.
**Duration/Reps:** 15-20 corner passes. Both directions.
**Progression:** Move to a corner with a looser surface where the benefit of a more upright bike is more dramatic.

### Drill 3: Extreme Rearward Position Climb
**Purpose:** Forces maximum rearward CoM position to feel the rear traction benefit and front wheel control challenge.
**Setup:** A moderately steep climb (20-30 degrees) on a surface with moderate grip.
**Execution:**
1. First pass: climb in neutral/centre position. Note where traction is lost.
2. Second pass: move hips completely off the back of the seat — standing pegs or hanging off the rear of the seat.
3. Maintain this extreme rearward position throughout the climb.
4. Notice when the front wheel becomes hard to steer — find the rearmost position that still maintains front contact.
**Success Criteria:** Rider can complete the climb with zero wheelspin in the extreme rearward position, and can identify the rearward limit where front traction is compromised.
**Duration/Reps:** 8-10 climb passes.
**Progression:** Steepen gradient. Add surface changes. Combine with clutch slip drill.

### Drill 4: Instability Recovery — Lower the CoM
**Purpose:** Teaches the instinctive response to sudden instability — lower CoM to extend recovery window.
**Setup:** A safe, slightly off-camber gentle section where controlled destabilisation is possible.
**Execution:**
1. Ride the section normally.
2. Deliberately allow the bike to drift toward the fall side briefly.
3. Recovery: immediately lower CoM by bending knees deeply, lowering trunk, and weighting the uphill peg.
4. Feel how lowering CoM changes the balance recovery window.
5. Repeat from the other direction.
**Success Criteria:** Rider can demonstrate lowering CoM in response to lateral instability and successfully preventing a topple.
**Duration/Reps:** 10-15 passes.
**Progression:** Increase off-camber angle. Increase speed. Apply on natural off-camber trail features.

## 8. TERRAIN & CONTEXT VARIATIONS

### Sand
In sand, CoM should be held rearward on sandy climbs and maintained rearward on flat sand to prevent the front wheel from burying. However, in sandy corners, a slightly more central CoM prevents the rear from stepping out. The optimal CoM in sand is typically further back than on firm terrain for equivalent gradients.

### Mud
Mud requires CoM to be managed carefully because both wheels can lose traction simultaneously. In deep mud, moving the CoM rearward is often necessary to keep the front wheel on top rather than pushing into it. Avoid sudden CoM shifts in mud — smooth, gradual transitions prevent triggering slides.

### Rocky Terrain
In rocky terrain, CoM must shift rapidly with each major feature. A ledge step-up requires a rapid forward CoM shift as the front wheel rises, then rearward as the rear wheel follows. A rock garden requires continuous fine CoM adjustment. This level of automaticity requires extensive practice.

### Steep Incline
On very steep slopes (35+ degrees), the rider may effectively be standing on the rear pegs with the body over the very rear of the bike — which looks wrong to beginners but is physically correct. Crest transitions demand a rapid forward CoM shift as the bike pitches from climb to flat.

### Steep Descent
On steep descents, forward CoM is essential: it loads the front wheel for braking traction and prevents the rear from rising. The temptation to lean back (which feels safer) unloads the front tyre and removes steering authority.

### Tight Trees / Narrow Sections
In tight trees, lateral CoM adjustment must be precise and small. The emphasis shifts to front-back CoM for speed management and low CoM for stability in narrow passages.

## 9. EXPERT INSIGHTS & SOURCES

### Key Insights
- Trials riding is universally cited by off-road coaches as the best training ground for CoM mastery. Toni Bou has described managing CoM as "the only tool that matters when the throttle won't help" — in extreme slow-speed situations where traction is zero, CoM positioning is the primary variable.
- The physics of motorcycle stability (inverted pendulum dynamics) confirm that a rider on a two-wheeled vehicle is inherently unstable and stability requires continuous CoM adjustment — this is not just technique, it is physics. Riding IS CoM management.
- Research in motocross biomechanics shows that elite riders make significantly more CoM shifts per unit time than novice riders, and their shifts are timed more accurately to terrain features.
- "Every time the terrain changes, your body should change first." — attributed to multiple enduro coaches in instructional literature. Anticipatory CoM adjustment is consistently identified as the key differentiator between intermediate and expert riders.
