# Peg Weighting and Force Application

## 1. OVERVIEW

The footpegs are the most important contact points between rider and motorcycle when standing — they are where all weight, balance, and lateral force is applied. Peg weighting is the deliberate act of applying force through specific pegs (inside, outside, front, rear) to control traction, cornering force, and bike attitude. Unlike the seat — which passively supports the rider — the pegs are an active control interface. When a rider "weights the outside peg" in a corner, they are not just balancing: they are applying a lateral force through the bike's chassis that alters how the tyre loads the ground. Trials riding has elevated peg control to an art form — and enduro riders who understand peg technique from trials translate directly to better traction management, smoother cornering, and more precise obstacle negotiation.

## 2. CORE PRINCIPLES

### Principle 1: The Footpeg Is a Lever, Not Just a Platform
Force applied to a footpeg transmits through the bike's frame to the wheel contact patches. Pressing down on the outside peg in a corner loads the outside wheel, increasing tyre contact patch pressure and improving lateral grip. This is analogous to a skier edging — applying force through the boot to the ski creates carving force. The lever arm is significant: the rider's leg, engaged as a lever from hip to peg, generates substantial downforce.

### Principle 2: Heel Down Maximises Peg Control
With the heel level with or slightly below the peg (weight on the arch/ball of foot), the whole leg from hip to ankle forms an integrated force-transmission chain. With the heel raised (tiptoe), the ankle joint becomes a weak link and the transmission of force from leg to peg is dramatically reduced. Heels down is not a stylistic choice — it is biomechanically necessary for effective peg force application.

### Principle 3: Outside Peg Weighting Is the Foundation of Off-Road Cornering
In virtually all off-road cornering techniques, the outside peg bears significantly more weight than the inside. This keeps the outside tyre loaded, prevents the bike from falling too far into the corner (increasing crash risk on loose terrain), and drives the chassis through the turn. This is different from road riding, where equal or inside-weighted body position is common.

### Principle 4: Peg Forces Work Independently on Each Side
The left and right pegs can be weighted independently and to different degrees. Advanced riders exploit this constantly: weighting the rear portion of the outside peg to push the rear of the bike through a turn, weighting the front portion of the inside peg to help pivot the front wheel. This independent control requires strong hip and leg control.

### Principle 5: Trials Riding Is the Master Class in Peg Control
Trials riders at elite level control their bikes almost entirely through peg forces and body mass shifts — they make minimal handlebar inputs relative to the extreme manoeuvers they execute. A rider who has studied or practised trials, even briefly, typically has far superior peg sensitivity than a pure enduro rider. The precision required at near-zero speed in trials directly transfers to higher-speed enduro.

### Principle 6: Peg Weighting Connects to the Whole-Body Chain
The force through the peg begins at the hip, travels through the knee and ankle to the foot. A rider whose core is not engaged (BIOMECH-07) cannot effectively drive force through the peg — the chain breaks. Similarly, a rider whose knee angle is wrong (BIOMECH-11) cannot generate efficient peg force. Peg weighting cannot be isolated from the rest of the body position system.

## 3. TECHNIQUE BREAKDOWN BY LEVEL

### 3a. Beginner Level
**Goal:** Understand that weight goes through the pegs, establish heel-down habit, and learn outside peg weighting in corners.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Heels level with or below the pegs — always
- Conscious effort to stand with weight on the balls of feet, arches of feet (not tiptoe)
- In corners: push the outside foot down into the peg
- Awareness that the seat is NOT the primary contact point when standing

**What Good Looks Like:**
Rider stands with heels level or lower. When entering a corner, the outside foot is visibly pressing down — heel firmly on the peg, weight driving through the leg. There may be slight exaggeration (almost a stomping motion on the outside peg) as the rider consciously applies the technique. Body position shifts slightly to the outside of the turn.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Tiptoe stance:** Heels are raised, rider balanced on balls of feet. Why it happens: instinctive, tiptoeing feels like "being ready to react." → Consciously push heels down. Imagine trying to push the footpegs out through the bottom of the engine.
- **Ignoring pegs in corners:** Rider corners with equal weight on both pegs. Why it happens: no awareness of peg weighting as a technique. → Assign one task per corner: "Outside peg down, every corner."
- **Gripping with the seat:** Rider squeezes the bike with knees and seat, using the upper body to hang on instead of loading the pegs. → Weight must go DOWN through the pegs, not in through the knees.

### 3b. Intermediate Level
**Goal:** Make peg weighting automatic in corners, begin using inside peg independently, and start applying rear peg bias for traction management.

**Progression Markers from Beginner:** Outside peg weighting in corners is consistent without thinking. Heels-down is habitual. Rider can describe what peg weighting feels like on a corner.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Differentiate front/rear of peg weighting (ball of foot forward on peg vs arch/heel position changes leverage point)
- Weight the inside peg lightly to help steer front wheel
- Apply rear peg bias (weight slightly toward the rear of the pegs) on loose climbs to drive rear traction
- Begin reading terrain to anticipate which peg to weight and when

**What Good Looks Like:**
In a rut corner, rider is clearly weighting the outside peg — body slightly outside the bike, inside knee tracking the turn direction. On a loose climb, rider's weight appears to be pushing downward through the pegs (not collapsing onto the seat). When a sidehill or adverse camber section appears, the uphill peg instantly receives more weight without a visible decision-making pause.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Over-weighting one peg exclusively:** Outside peg gets maximum weight but inside is forgotten — rider's upper body overcompensates. Why it happens: beginner habit taken too far. → Both legs are in contact; the outside just has priority. Think 70% outside / 30% inside as a starting ratio.
- **Peg weighting without hip engagement:** The foot presses but the hip is not engaged, so force transmission breaks. Why it happens: treating peg weighting as a foot action only. → Drive from the hip downward through the knee to the foot — the whole leg is the lever.
- **Inconsistent application:** Remembers peg weighting on flat bermed corners but forgets it on off-camber or loose turns. Why it happens: only learned the technique in ideal conditions. → Practise specifically on adverse-camber corners where the technique matters most.

### 3c. Advanced Level
**Goal:** Use peg forces as a primary steering and stability input — applying precise, independent forces through each peg in real time to manage traction, line, and bike attitude.

**Progression Markers from Intermediate:** Peg weighting is automatic and terrain-specific. Rider uses peg forces proactively to prevent traction loss, not reactively. Can demonstrate inside/outside peg independence.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Using peg forces to steer without significant handlebar input (particularly useful in ruts)
- Applying upward pressure on inside peg to help lift and pivot front wheel over obstacles
- Micro-adjusting peg weight distribution continuously through a corner (not a single set-and-forget)
- Combining peg weighting with body mass shifts and throttle for complete chassis control
- Applying deliberate fore/aft peg force shifts to modulate suspension loading through rough sections

**What Good Looks Like:**
In a deep rut, the rider's outside leg is visibly driving downward — the bike is being "steered" by peg force with minimal bar input. On a loose sidehill, the uphill peg carries maximum force and the rider's hip is angled into the hill, keeping the bike tracking without breaking traction. On a rock step-up, a brief upward pull on the inside peg (combined with throttle) helps unload and lift the front wheel cleanly. The peg work is seamless, rapid, and continuous — not separate events.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Forgetting peg weighting when under pressure:** In high-consequence sections, riders often abandon technique and revert to instinct. Why it happens: cognitive overload. → Peg weighting must be drilled to automaticity — it should require zero conscious thought.
- **Using bars to compensate for peg technique:** When the bike goes wrong, the response is handlebar input rather than peg adjustment. Why it happens: bar input is more obvious. → In a controlled environment, practise recovering bike attitude using peg force only — build the reflex.

## 4. DIAGNOSTIC CUES (AI COACHING ENGINE REFERENCE)

### Visual Cues — Correct Technique
- From rear/chase camera: rider's outside leg appears slightly straightened/loaded in corners — visible drive through the outside foot; inside leg has slight bend
- Heels are level with or slightly below the footpegs — visible from side camera at any speed
- In corners, rider's outside hip is lower and more pushed back than the inside — consistent with peg drive
- From side camera: rider's ankle is in a neutral/dorsiflexed position, not plantarflexed (tiptoe)
- On rough sections, rider's feet remain stable on the pegs — not bouncing or lifting off

### Visual Cues — Error Indicators
- From side camera: heels are visibly raised (tiptoe stance) — often constant throughout riding
- In corners, rider's body leans with the bike rather than independently weighting the outside peg — both rider and bike at same angle (road riding style)
- Rider's inside foot lifts off the inside peg in corners — weight entirely on outside but inside abandoned
- Feet shift position on pegs frequently — not solidly planted
- From rear camera: both pegs appear equally loaded through all terrain types — no differentiation

### Audio Cues
- Rear wheel spinning excessively on corners or climbs when traction should be available: suggests insufficient rear/outside peg loading
- Front wheel wash sounds (tyre scrubbing) on corners: may indicate insufficient outside peg weighting allowing too much lean
- Bike bouncing off line in ruts with repeated tyre-scrub sounds: suggests peg forces not being used to steer within the rut

### Sensor Cues
- Left/right peg force sensors (future integration): direct measurement of peg force differential through corners
- Lean angle sensor: excessive lean angle for a given corner speed suggests insufficient outside peg loading
- Rear wheel slip sensor: excessive slip on climbs suggests insufficient rear-peg-driven weight distribution

## 5. COACHING CUES & LANGUAGE

### Positive Reinforcement
- "That's it — I can see your outside leg working. That's how you drive the bike through the corner, not fight it."
- "Look at that — you weighted the uphill peg on that sidehill without thinking. That's instinct now. Great work."
- "Your heels are down the whole section. That means your pegs are working for you. Perfect."

### Corrective Feedback
- "You're on your tiptoes — I can see it from here. Push your heels down. Feel the peg under your whole foot, not just your toes."
- "In that corner, both pegs were the same. You need to stamp on that outside peg — not just rest on it, drive it. Like you're trying to push the bike into the ground through that outside foot."
- "You're using your arms to fight the bike through that rut. Stop. Less hands, more outside peg. Drive your outside leg down and let the bike track."
- "Your hips are in the wrong place — you're not getting leverage through the peg. Drop your outside hip and drive your whole leg downward, not just your foot."

### Progression Prompts
- "Next lap, I want you to think of nothing except outside peg. Every corner: stomp the outside foot. Build the reflex."
- "Try this sidehill section and focus on the uphill peg. Push down like you're trying to put a dent in it. Feel how the bike stops sliding."
- "Now let's try steering with just peg force — no bar input. Exaggerate the outside peg on this flat corner and see if the bike turns by itself."

## 6. DRILLS & PRACTICE EXERCISES

### Drill 1: Heel Press Awareness
**Purpose:** Build the heel-down habit and peg-contact awareness before adding complexity.
**Setup:** Flat area, parking lot or open trail.
**Execution:**
1. Stand on the bike, stationary. Place feet on pegs. Check: are heels level or below pegs? Weight on arch/ball, not toes?
2. Ride at slow walking pace (5 km/h). Maintain heel-down position.
3. Increase to 15 km/h. Maintain heel-down.
4. Make gentle left and right turns. Maintain heel-down throughout.
5. Stop and check feet position after each section.
**Success Criteria:** Heels consistently level or below pegs throughout all speeds and turns. No tiptoe regression under pressure.
**Duration/Reps:** 10 minutes. Repeat at the start of every session until it's automatic.
**Progression:** Add obstacle weaving. Add speed. Add rough terrain.

### Drill 2: Corner Peg Stomp
**Purpose:** Build the outside-peg-weighting reflex in corners.
**Setup:** A clear dirt corner with moderate radius — berm or flat turn.
**Execution:**
1. Ride the corner 5 times normally. Note how the bike behaves.
2. Next 10 passes: at corner entry, deliberately and firmly press the outside foot into the peg — not gently, but with clear intention. Think "stomp."
3. Notice how bike behaviour changes (should feel more stable, more grip, better tracking).
4. Compare the "stomped" passes to the "normal" passes.
**Success Criteria:** Rider feels a clear difference in corner stability with deliberate outside peg weighting. The reflex begins to form after 20+ repetitions.
**Duration/Reps:** 30 minutes minimum. Both left and right corners equally.
**Progression:** Move to loose corners. Move to off-camber corners where peg weighting is critical for safety.

### Drill 3: The Trials Balance Drill
**Purpose:** Develop sensitivity to peg forces at very low speed — inspired by trials riding fundamentals.
**Setup:** Flat area with a small log, raised plank, or marked line.
**Execution:**
1. Roll up to the obstacle at walking pace. Stop with both wheels on it (or balance at the line).
2. Balance stationary using peg forces only — no foot down, no dabs.
3. Feel how left/right peg weighting affects balance. Front/rear peg shifts affect pitch.
4. Try to hold for 5, 10, 15 seconds using peg control.
**Success Criteria:** Rider can hold static balance for 5+ seconds using peg forces, demonstrating real-time sensitivity to peg control.
**Duration/Reps:** 15 minutes of balance attempts.
**Progression:** Reduce peg size (use a narrower flat plank). Add a slight incline. Add a tilt to simulate off-camber.

### Drill 4: Peg-Steer Corner
**Purpose:** Train peg-based steering — reducing handlebar input and increasing peg force steering authority.
**Setup:** Open flat area with a gentle curve or cone-marked corner.
**Execution:**
1. Ride the corner at 15-20 km/h.
2. Enter the corner with hands resting lightly on the bars — minimal grip. Weight the outside peg heavily.
3. Notice how much the bike turns from peg force alone.
4. Gradually reduce bar input until the corner is completed primarily through peg weight.
**Success Criteria:** Rider can navigate a gentle corner using outside peg weighting with noticeably reduced bar input. Builds proprioceptive understanding of peg steering authority.
**Duration/Reps:** 20 minutes. This drill is revelatory — take time with it.
**Progression:** Tighter corner radius. Add loose surface. Try rut sections where bar input is destructive.

## 8. TERRAIN & CONTEXT VARIATIONS

### Sand
In soft sand corners, weight the rear of the outside peg (arch/heel) to keep the rear wheel driving forward rather than washing. Too much front-of-peg loading can cause front wheel to dig in. Keep the outside peg weighted but the pressure focused slightly rearward.

### Mud
In mud, outside peg weighting is even more critical — the tyre needs maximum lateral loading to find grip. The risk of the outside foot sliding off the peg increases, so consider using gear boots with good peg grip. Weight the outside peg firmly and keep heels down.

### Rocky Terrain
On rocks, peg weighting becomes dynamic and rapid — each rock that deflects the front or rear wheel requires an immediate peg response to correct. Feet must stay solidly on pegs (heels down) even through impacts. The outside peg on corners over rocks is weighted but the rider must also be ready to absorb vertical impacts through bent legs.

### Steep Incline
On climbs, both pegs share load but the weight drives downward through both evenly — no lateral bias needed on straight climbs. The focus shifts to keeping heels down and driving downward to maintain rear wheel traction. On climbing corners, outside peg weighting becomes critical again.

### Steep Descent
On descents, the rear peg receives more weight — the body moves rearward and the force through the rear peg helps keep the rear wheel from lifting. Heels remain down. Do not stand on tiptoe on descents — a slip of the foot at this point is dangerous.

### Tight Trees / Narrow Sections
In tight trees at slow speed, peg forces do most of the steering work. The handlebar clearance may be limited; peg-based balance and micro-steering using body mass over the pegs becomes the primary navigation tool. Trials-style peg sensitivity is directly applicable here.
