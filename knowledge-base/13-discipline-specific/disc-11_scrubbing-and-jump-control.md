# Scrubbing and Jump Control

## 1. OVERVIEW

Scrubbing is one of the most advanced and visually distinctive techniques in motocross — the act of intentionally laying the bike sideways on the lip of a jump to reduce the apex height and minimise airtime, saving 0.2–0.5 seconds per jump in competition. The technique originates with James "Bubba" Stewart, who brought it to unprecedented levels of commitment, though the principle of reducing airtime for speed existed in rider practice before it was codified. The unique demand of scrubbing is that it requires complete mastery of normal jump technique as a prerequisite — a rider who cannot consistently clear a jump safely, land on both wheels, and manage throttle in the air has no business attempting a scrub. Beyond scrubbing, this topic covers the broader domain of jump control: throttle management on the lip, body position in the air, recovery from off-balance landings, and the two-wheel landing imperative. Enduro riders increasingly encounter jump-like terrain features on modern EnduroGP tracks and extreme enduro venues, making these techniques increasingly relevant beyond pure motocross.

## 2. CORE PRINCIPLES

### Principle 1: Jump Control Fundamentals Before Scrubbing
A rider must achieve consistent, controlled jump technique before attempting scrubbing. The fundamentals: throttle maintained (not chopped) through the lip, body weight centred to neutral on takeoff, vision to the landing zone throughout airtime, and both wheels landing simultaneously or rear wheel slightly first. These fundamentals must be automatic — not conscious — before the added complexity of scrubbing is introduced.

### Principle 2: The Scrub Reduces Airtime By Reducing Apex Height
By laying the bike sideways on the lip of the jump and having the tyre contact the face of the jump slightly longer than a normal takeoff, the scrubbing rider effectively "uses up" some of the jump's energy horizontally (allowing the bike to slide across the face) rather than vertically (launching into the air). The result: the bike leaves the lip at a lower angle with less upward momentum, achieving a lower apex and therefore less airtime. The time saved is real and measurable — 0.2–0.5 seconds per jump across a full motocross race compounds significantly.

### Principle 3: Throttle Management Is the Most Critical Safety Variable
In the air, the throttle controls the pitch of the bike (nose up or nose down) through rear wheel gyroscopic force and engine braking. Chopping the throttle in the air typically produces nose-down pitch (endover risk). Maintaining throttle level or adding slight throttle prevents this. A rider who panics and chops throttle mid-air frequently faces a front-first landing — the most dangerous jump outcome. Throttle management in the air is the highest-priority safety skill in jumping.

### Principle 4: The Landing — Rear First, Then Front; Never Front First
A correct jump landing contacts the rear wheel slightly before (or simultaneously with) the front wheel. The rear wheel and shock absorb the primary landing impact; the front fork and wheel then absorb the secondary load. A front-first landing sends the entire landing force through the forks, compresses them fully, and typically pitches the rider over the bars (endover). No other landing type produces this outcome as consistently. Rear-first landing is the target — in all conditions, at all speeds.

### Principle 5: The Bubba Scrub — The Extreme Version
The "Bubba scrub" named for James Stewart involves laying the bike so aggressively sideways that the wheel and the side of the bike contact the jump face. This extreme scrub requires full commitment to a lateral lean before the lip, timed precisely so the bike is upright again before leaving the lip surface. Poorly timed, this technique results in the bike launching sideways — a dangerous outcome. The Bubba scrub is an elite professional technique; the standard scrub (bike laid at 15–30 degrees rather than 45+) is the appropriate target for competent amateur riders.

### Principle 6: Safety Considerations — Know the Jump Before Attempting the Scrub
Before any scrubbing technique, the rider must know the jump intimately: the lip geometry, the landing zone distance, and the consequences of a mistimed or misjudged attempt. Scrubbing an unknown jump is not acceptable risk management. The sequence is: walk the jump, observe the landing, ride it in normal technique several times, then — if normal jumps are consistent and controlled — introduce the scrub progressively.

## 3. TECHNIQUE BREAKDOWN BY LEVEL

### 3a. Beginner Level
**Goal:** Learn safe jump control fundamentals — not scrubbing. Develop consistent, controlled air technique.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Approach the lip in a neutral standing position — centred fore/aft
- Maintain throttle through the lip and into the air — do not chop
- Look at the landing zone from the moment of takeoff
- Allow natural forward body position in the air — do not fight the airtime
- Land rear wheel first or simultaneously with front
- Absorb the landing with bent knees — do not stiffen on impact

**What Good Looks Like:**
A beginner rider approaches a small (30–50cm) table top or roller at appropriate speed, maintains throttle through the lip, stays neutral in the air, and lands cleanly on both wheels. The landing is absorbed by bent knees. No panic, no throttle chop in the air.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Chopping throttle at the lip:** Rider closes throttle just before launch. Why it happens: instinctive hesitation. → This is the most dangerous mistake — it pitches the front down in the air. Commit to maintaining throttle through the lip.
- **Looking down at the landing zone from above rather than ahead to it:** Rider looks straight down at the ground rather than toward the landing. → Look at the end of the landing zone — where you want to land, not what is directly below.
- **Stiffening on landing:** Arms and legs locked at impact. Why it happens: fear of the landing force. → Bent knees and elbows on landing. The same principle as riding rough terrain — be the suspension.

### 3b. Intermediate Level
**Goal:** Develop consistent jump technique across multiple jump types, including table tops, doubles, and rollers, and begin understanding the scrub concept.

**Progression Markers from Beginner:** Rider can jump tables and rollers consistently with correct technique. No panic in the air.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Distance management: matching approach speed to jump distance — too fast overshoots the landing, too slow under-jumps
- Throttle in the air for pitch correction: brief throttle application lifts the nose; engine braking drops it
- Body adjustment in the air: small weight shifts fore and aft can correct minor pitch deviations
- Introduction to scrub concept: understand what a scrub is and why it works before attempting
- Body lean at the lip: practise a very mild lateral lean (5–10 degrees) at the lip of a known jump to feel the beginning of the scrub movement

**What Good Looks Like:**
The rider approaches a small double, correctly judges the distance with approach speed, maintains throttle through the lip, adjusts pitch slightly in the air with a brief throttle touch, and lands rear-first cleanly on the downslope of the landing.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Over-jumping on doubles (flying past the landing):** Too much speed — lands on the upslope or past the landing entirely. Why it happens: misjudging the jump distance. → Walk the jump first. Estimate the landing zone. First pass at conservative speed, add speed incrementally.
- **Under-jumping (landing short):** Too little speed — front wheel hits the face of the second mound on a double. This is a crash. → On unknown jumps, always err on the side of too fast — overshooting a landing is generally safer than under-jumping.

### 3c. Advanced Level
**Goal:** Execute a consistent scrub technique on known jumps. Manage all jump types confidently, including large doubles and rhythmic jump sequences.

**Progression Markers from Intermediate:** Rider is fully consistent on doubles and jump sequences. Normal jumping technique is fully automatic.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Standard scrub (15–30 degree lean): approaching the lip with a lateral lean, tyre contacting the upper face of the jump, bike returning to upright before leaving the lip
- Entry speed for scrub: same as normal jump speed — the lean reduces apex, not entry speed
- Timing the lean: the lean begins approximately 2–3 bike lengths before the lip, reaches maximum just before the tyre leaves the face
- Body position during scrub: outside arm slightly lower, inside arm raised, hips turning with the bike
- The Bubba scrub (advanced): extreme version, for riders with full commitment and professional ambition — begins with perfecting the standard scrub first
- Recovery from off-scrub landings: if the scrub does not straighten fully, the bike lands slightly sideways — absorb and correct on landing

**What Good Looks Like:**
Advanced rider approaches a known jump with the scrub lean established before the lip. The bike's wheel contacts the upper face of the jump with the bike at approximately 20 degrees of lateral angle. As the bike leaves the face, it returns to near-upright. The jump apex is visibly lower than a non-scrubbing rider's apex. Landing is clean. Exit speed is higher than a normal jump.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Scrubbing without sufficient jump technique foundation:** Rider attempting scrubs with inconsistent normal jumping. → Do not begin scrubbing until 50+ normal jumps on the same jump have been executed consistently. The scrub adds complexity to a foundation that must already be solid.
- **Returning to upright too late:** Bike still laterally angled when leaving the face — launches sideways. → The straightening must be complete before the tyre leaves the jump face. This is a timing drill, not a force drill.

## 4. DIAGNOSTIC CUES (AI COACHING ENGINE REFERENCE)

### Visual Cues — Correct Technique
- From side camera: bike apex is visibly lower than normal jump line — scrub reducing height
- Bike clearly returning to upright before or as it leaves the jump face
- Rear wheel landing before or simultaneously with front wheel — clean rear-first landing
- Throttle hand position consistent through the lip — no visible wrist close at launch
- Body forward and neutral in the air — not leaning back
- Knees bent at landing — absorbing the impact, not stiffened

### Visual Cues — Error Indicators
- Throttle hand closing visibly at lip approach — pre-launch chop
- Bike launching with nose low — throttle was chopped (front will arrive first)
- Front wheel landing first — endover risk, technique failure
- Bike leaving the face of the jump in a lateral attitude — scrub not straightened before departure
- Rider leaning back in the air — tail-first crash risk
- Arms stiff and straight on landing — no absorption

### Audio Cues
- Consistent engine note through the lip and into the air: correct throttle management
- Engine note dropping at the lip: throttle chop — most dangerous audio indicator
- Brief engine note increase in the air: throttle for pitch correction — correct technique
- Heavy front-wheel landing sound: front arrived first — technique failure
- Clean dual-wheel landing (rear then front): correct — softer landing sound pattern

### Sensor Cues
- GPS altitude data: peak altitude in a scrub jump lower than peak altitude for same jump without scrub — scrub working
- Accelerometer: rear-first landing produces characteristic dual-peak pattern (rear impact then front impact) — correct
- Front-first landing: single high-peak accelerometer spike — incorrect, dangerous
- Throttle position sensor: maintained or slight increase through the lip — correct; sharp close — error
- Air time duration: measurably shorter in scrub jump vs normal jump at the same entry speed — time saving confirmed

## 5. COACHING CUES & LANGUAGE

### Positive Reinforcement
- "I could see the difference — your apex was at least half a metre lower than the previous jump. That's the scrub working. You saved time."
- "Rear wheel landed first, clean two-step. That's the correct landing sequence. You're absorbing rather than stiffening. Good."
- "Throttle maintained all the way through the lip. That's the hardest habit to build and you've got it. The bike stayed flat because of that."

### Corrective Feedback
- "You closed the throttle just before the lip. I can hear it on the video. That's why the nose came down in the air. Maintain the throttle through the lip — the same setting you had on the approach."
- "You're attempting scrubs but your normal jumping still has inconsistencies. The scrub adds a layer of complexity to a foundation that needs to be solid first. Back to 30 clean normal jumps on this jump before we introduce the lean."
- "The bike left the face of the jump still leaning. You need to complete the straightening before you leave the face — not after. Work on the timing: lean, reach maximum angle, then begin straightening before the final contact point."
- "Your front wheel arrived first. Again. That's the most dangerous landing type. We need to understand why — are you leaning back in the air, or did you chop the throttle? Either one causes this."

### Progression Prompts
- "This session: 20 normal jumps on this jump. I want every one of them to have the same throttle position through the lip. When 20 are consistent — then we talk about adding the lean."
- "Try a 5-degree lean on the next pass — barely anything, just the beginning of the movement. Feel where the bike wants to go. Don't try to produce a scrub yet, just feel the entry to it."
- "Walk the jump before every session on it. Check the face, check the landing. Know where the landing zone is before you try to hit it."

## 6. DRILLS & PRACTICE EXERCISES

### Drill 1: The Table Top Fundamental Drill
**Purpose:** Build consistent basic jump technique on the safest possible jump type (table top — missing the landing does not result in a gap crash).
**Setup:** A table top jump of appropriate size (30–80cm height, table at least 3 metres long).
**Execution:**
1. First pass at very slow speed — just enough to clear the lip.
2. Increase speed by 10% each pass.
3. On each pass, focus exclusively on: throttle maintained through the lip, look at the landing.
4. Aim for 20 consecutive clean two-wheel landings before increasing complexity.
**Success Criteria:** 20 consecutive clean landings — rear first, both wheels, no bounce.
**Duration/Reps:** Full session on the table top before attempting doubles.
**Progression:** Move to a small double (1–2 metre gap). Then larger doubles. Only when doubles are consistent, introduce the scrub lean.

### Drill 2: The Throttle Commitment Drill
**Purpose:** Build the habit of maintaining throttle through the lip as an automatic response.
**Setup:** A roller or small jump feature. Cones marking the lip position.
**Execution:**
1. Approach the lip. The only focus: do not change throttle position from 10 metres before the lip until 10 metres after it.
2. Ride through the lip at the same throttle setting regardless of the takeoff sensation.
3. Repeat 10 times.
**Success Criteria:** Rider completes 10 passes without throttle change through the lip. Engine note confirms consistency.
**Duration/Reps:** 10 passes per session, 3 sessions before moving to larger jumps.
**Progression:** Increase the jump size progressively while maintaining the throttle commitment habit.

### Drill 3: The Lean Introduction Drill
**Purpose:** Introduce the scrub lean in a controlled, progressive way on a known jump.
**Setup:** A familiar table top or natural jump where normal technique is fully consistent.
**Execution:**
1. Normal jump — no lean. Note the apex height.
2. Add 5 degrees of lateral lean at the lip. Note whether apex changes.
3. Add 10 degrees on the next pass. Note the change.
4. Add 15 degrees. Note when the apex begins to meaningfully reduce.
5. Find the lean angle that produces a lower apex without losing control of the bike's attitude.
**Success Criteria:** Rider can produce a measurably lower apex with a controlled lean and return to clean landing.
**Duration/Reps:** 3–5 passes at each lean angle, progressively increasing.
**Progression:** Increase lean angle incrementally over multiple sessions. Never increase lean angle until the previous angle is fully consistent.

### Drill 4: The Off-Balance Landing Recovery Drill
**Purpose:** Build confidence and technique for recovering from imperfect landings.
**Setup:** A gentle roller where a slight sideways landing is possible at low speed without danger.
**Execution:**
1. Deliberately introduce a slight lateral imbalance at takeoff (small lean).
2. Land slightly off-balance and practice the body correction: weight shift, steering correction, knee absorption.
3. Build confidence that a slightly sideways landing is recoverable with correct technique.
**Success Criteria:** Rider can recover consistently from 10–15 degree off-axis landings without crashing.
**Duration/Reps:** 10 passes at low speed.
**Progression:** Gradually increase the landing imbalance. The goal is building a recovery response that is automatic under pressure.

## 8. TERRAIN & CONTEXT VARIATIONS

### Table Top Jumps
Safest jump type — a long flat landing surface means missing the optimal landing does not result in a gap crash. Ideal for building all jump fundamentals and practising scrub introduction. No penalty for going short or long within the table's length.

### Double Jumps
Gap between takeoff mound and landing mound — landing short results in hitting the back side of the takeoff or the face of the landing mound. Speed must be correctly calibrated before attempting doubles. First pass should always be slightly over-speed (overshooting a double is generally safer than under-jumping it).

### Step-Up / Step-Down Jumps
Step-up: landing higher than takeoff — requires additional speed to clear. Step-down: landing lower than takeoff — natural tendency to overshoot, which is typically manageable. Both require specific speed calibration.

### Natural Terrain Jumps (Enduro Context)
Formed by natural features — crests, drop-offs, root faces. Less predictable than purpose-built jumps. Landing zones may be rocky, root-covered, or narrow. Normal jump control applies but with more conservative speed management and mandatory pre-observation (walk the feature or observe from the side before jumping it).

## 9. EXPERT INSIGHTS & SOURCES

### Key Insights
- **James "Bubba" Stewart**: the originator of the modern extreme scrub — his development of the technique during his AMA career (2000s–2010s) produced a measurable competitive advantage that competitors adopted within years. The technique has been analysed in frame-by-frame video and biomechanical studies.
- **Ricky Carmichael** coaching on jump control: extensively documented position where he identified throttle management through the lip as the single most important safety and performance variable in jumping. Riders who can manage throttle in the air can correct almost any other jump error.
- **Jump control physics**: the rear wheel's gyroscopic force and engine braking create pitch torque when the throttle is open or closed. This is not minor — at motocross speeds, a brief throttle chop in the air can rotate the bike's nose 15–20 degrees downward in 0.5 seconds. Understanding this physics changes how riders relate to throttle in the air.
- **Enduro application**: as extreme enduro venues (Red Bull Romaniacs, Erzbergrodeo) incorporate larger man-made features, jump control has become a necessary skill for serious enduro competitors — previously the domain of MX-specific training only.
- **Table top learning tool**: explicitly recommended by virtually every professional jump technique coach — the table top provides the full jump experience (takeoff, airtime, landing) with the safety of a long, flat landing surface that forgives minor technique errors.
