# Wheel Lifts (Front and Rear)

## 1. OVERVIEW

Deliberate wheel lifting — raising either the front or rear wheel independently to clear or manage obstacles — is a trials-derived technique that has become increasingly important in enduro as terrain difficulty escalates. The unique demand of wheel lifts in enduro versus other contexts is that they must be executed in real riding conditions: at low speed with limited run-up, on loose or irregular surfaces, while maintaining balance and forward direction. Unlike the motocross context where wheelies are for distance and show, enduro wheel lifts are precision tools: the front wheel is lifted to clear a log, step, or rock face; the rear wheel is lifted to clear a log, slot a rear wheel into a specific landing point, or allow a front-wheel pivot. The height of the lift is matched precisely to the obstacle — no higher than necessary. Understanding which wheel to lift, when to lift it, and how to return to both-wheel control is the practical skill set. Safety is central: knowing the limits of each technique and the consequences of exceeding them prevents the most common wheel-lift injuries.

## 2. CORE PRINCIPLES

### Principle 1: Front Wheel Lift vs Power Wheelie — Different Tools
The enduro front wheel lift is primarily a clutch-assisted technique: releasing the clutch quickly while at a specific throttle setting creates a rapid power pulse that lifts the front wheel. This is not the same as a power wheelie (sustained front-wheel lift through continued throttle and balance), though they share the initial technique. The clutch-up lift is brief, precise, and height-controlled — the rider controls exactly how high the wheel goes by the timing and degree of the clutch release. The power wheelie requires balance-point management and is a separate advanced skill.

### Principle 2: Rear Wheel Lift — Front Brake Dive
The rear wheel lift is performed by applying the front brake firmly and smoothly while the body weight moves forward — the deceleration force and forward CoM shift cause the rear wheel to rise off the ground. The height achieved depends on the aggressiveness of the brake application and the speed of the weight transfer. In enduro, the rear wheel lift is used to clear a rear-wheel obstacle (log crossing, step-up from behind), reposition the rear wheel on a slope, or prepare for a front-wheel pivot (DISC-16). The safety consideration: if the front brake is applied too aggressively at too high a speed, the endover (front flip) becomes a real risk.

### Principle 3: Height Matching — Only as High as Needed
The goal is never to lift the wheel higher than the obstacle requires. A 30cm log requires the wheel to clear 30cm plus a small margin — lifting 80cm wastes energy, reduces control, and extends the time the bike is in an unbalanced single-wheel state. Precision height matching is a specific skill that develops through practice: learning to produce a 20cm lift, a 40cm lift, and an 80cm lift on demand rather than just "as high as possible."

### Principle 4: The Manual — Balance Point Wheelie
A manual is a sustained front-wheel lift at near-zero throttle, maintained through rear brake modulation to prevent the bike from going over backward. This is not a power-dependent technique — it is a balance skill. The rider finds the balance point (CoM directly over the rear axle) and uses tiny rear brake applications to stay there. This is the full expression of front wheel lift for trials-style obstacles and is distinct from the clutch-up (which is brief) and the power wheelie (which is sustained through throttle).

### Principle 5: Rear Wheel Hop — Repositioning Tool
A rear wheel hop — a brief, controlled rear lift followed by a sideways movement — allows the rider to reposition the rear wheel without significant forward movement. This is used when the rear wheel is in an unfavourable position (off a ledge, on the wrong side of a rock) and needs to be moved to a better position. The hop combines the front brake dive (for initial rear wheel lift) with a body lean to shift the wheel laterally. This is a trials-derived precision tool rarely used in motocross.

### Principle 6: Safety — The Consequence of Overcommitment
Front wheel lift risk: going over the balance point (past vertical) without rear brake or throttle correction → rear of bike falls, front flip, crash. Prevention: always know where the rear brake is and be ready to use it. Rear wheel lift risk: applying front brake too aggressively at speed → endover. Prevention: start rear wheel lifts at very slow speed. Height risk: lifting either wheel higher than intended → extended time in unbalanced state, higher consequence of error. Prevention: practise height control from slow, small lifts upward.

## 3. TECHNIQUE BREAKDOWN BY LEVEL

### 3a. Beginner Level
**Goal:** Perform a basic clutch-up front wheel lift (5–15cm) and a basic rear wheel lift (5–15cm) in a controlled setting.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Clutch-up front lift: at walking pace, sharp clutch release — feel the front wheel unload momentarily
- The lift is not a wheelie — the wheel rises, clears an imaginary obstacle, and returns immediately
- Rear lift: gentle, progressive front brake application while weight shifts forward — feel the rear wheel lighten
- Both lifts at very slow speed, small height initially
- Head and eyes stay level throughout — no watching the wheel

**What Good Looks Like:**
A beginner produces a front wheel lift of 10cm over a small marked obstacle (tape on ground) while at walking pace. The front wheel clears the mark and lands cleanly. The rear wheel lift produces a noticeable lightening of the rear with heel slight rise — not a dramatic lift but a controlled beginning.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Full power clutch dump for front lift — launching forward:** Too aggressive clutch release combined with too much throttle. Why it happens: rider wants to lift higher. → Light throttle, sharp (not explosive) clutch release. Height is determined by throttle, not clutch speed.
- **Front brake fully applied immediately for rear lift — near-endover:** Full front brake at normal speed. Why it happens: rider applies normal braking force to a lift technique. → For learning, reduce speed to near-zero first. Apply front brake progressively, not sharply.
- **Looking at the wheel being lifted:** Head drops to watch the wheel. Why it happens: natural instinct to watch what is doing the work. → Eyes forward and level. Feel the lift, don't watch it.

### 3b. Intermediate Level
**Goal:** Execute both wheel lifts to specific heights (15–40cm) on demand and over actual obstacles.

**Progression Markers from Beginner:** Rider can produce both wheel lifts in a controlled setting. Now practising precision height and obstacle application.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Height control: vary the front lift height from 15cm to 40cm by adjusting throttle and clutch release speed
- Obstacle application: front wheel lift over an actual log or step (not just tape on ground)
- Rear wheel lift over log: approach log at very slow speed, front wheel clears (rolled over), front brake applied as rear wheel approaches — rear wheel rises to clear the log
- Sequence lift (log crossing): front lift → front lands → rear lift → both wheels clear
- Manual introduction: finding the balance point at very slow speed and holding for 1–2 seconds

**What Good Looks Like:**
The rider approaches a 25cm log at walking pace. The front wheel is lifted with a clutch-up over the log and lands cleanly on the other side. As the rear wheel approaches, the front brake is applied to lift the rear wheel over the log. Both wheels clear without foot contact. The sequence is smooth and deliberate.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Front lift too early — front wheel lands on log instead of clearing it:** Poor timing of the lift initiation. → The lift begins when the front wheel is approximately one wheel-length before the obstacle — not 3 metres before.
- **Rear lift timing wrong — rear wheel contacts log before brake applied:** Braking too late for the rear lift. → The front brake for the rear lift begins as the front wheel lands from the log — not after the rear wheel is already at the log.

### 3c. Advanced Level
**Goal:** Execute precision wheel lifts to navigate extreme obstacles — steps, ledges, and off-camber features — with height matching and directional control throughout.

**Progression Markers from Intermediate:** Rider can execute both wheel lifts over real obstacles consistently. Now developing precision and applying in complex obstacle sequences.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Large front lifts (50–80cm) for step-up and ledge climbing
- Manual (balance point front wheel lift) sustained for 3–5 seconds
- Rear wheel hop for repositioning — moving the rear wheel laterally without forward movement
- Combined sequence: front lift → balance → rear lift → forward — trials-style obstacle negotiation
- One-handed readiness: during a front lift, the rear brake must be available instantly — body position must allow immediate rear brake access

**What Good Looks Like:**
Advanced rider approaches a ledge with a 60cm vertical face. They lift the front wheel to clear the top of the ledge face, front wheel lands on top, bike pauses momentarily balanced on the ledge edge, rear wheel lifts to clear the face. Both wheels on top — the ledge is climbed. Foot contact: zero.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Attempting large lifts without small lift mastery:** Rider attempts a 60cm lift having only practised 20cm lifts. → Add 5–10cm to maximum lift height per session. Mastery requires progressive overload.
- **Rear brake not accessible during manual:** Rider reaches the balance point and cannot reach the rear brake — front flip risk. → Check body position during the lift: can you reach the rear brake? If not, adjust your foot position on the peg.

## 4. DIAGNOSTIC CUES (AI COACHING ENGINE REFERENCE)

### Visual Cues — Correct Technique
- From side camera: front wheel lifts to exactly the height of the obstacle — not significantly higher
- Lift initiates at the correct distance before the obstacle — not too early, not too late
- Head remains level throughout the lift — not watching the wheel
- Rear wheel lift shows clean lightening of the rear followed by obstacle clearance
- Both-wheel landing after sequence: rear touches first or both simultaneously

### Visual Cues — Error Indicators
- Front wheel lifted far higher than obstacle requires — imprecise height control
- Front lift initiated too far from obstacle — wheel coming back down before reaching obstacle
- Head drops to watch the lifting wheel
- Rear wheel dragging or catching on obstacle — lift timing wrong
- Front brake application causing front wheel to skid (locked) — too much brake for current speed

### Audio Cues
- Sharp engine RPM spike followed by quick return: clutch-up technique — correct
- Prolonged high RPM: power wheelie, not enduro precision lift
- Front brake application sound (squeal or friction): rear lift in progress
- Obstacle impact sound: wheel lift unsuccessful — obstacle contacted

### Sensor Cues
- Front wheel accelerometer: brief vertical spike (wheel off ground) then landing: front lift confirmed
- Rear wheel sensor: brief unloading (rear wheel rises): rear lift confirmed
- Throttle position: brief spike aligned with clutch release: correct clutch-up technique
- Front brake pressure: progressive increase during rear lift approach — correct technique

## 5. COACHING CUES & LANGUAGE

### Positive Reinforcement
- "The front wheel cleared that log by exactly 5cm. That's precision — you didn't waste height, you just cleared it. That's what we're after."
- "Front lift, landing, rear lift — clean sequence, no foot contact. That's trials technique applied to enduro. That's the skill."
- "You matched the height perfectly. The wheel went exactly as high as the obstacle and not a centimetre more. That comes from technique, not luck."

### Corrective Feedback
- "You lifted the front wheel a metre for a 25cm log. You're not trying to clear the moon — match the height to the obstacle. Half the throttle, same clutch timing."
- "The clutch dump is too explosive — you're launching forward rather than lifting upward. The clutch release should be quick, but not instantaneous. There's a difference between snap and quick."
- "You're looking at the front wheel when it lifts. Eyes forward, always. Feel the lift — you can feel it through the bars. You don't need to see it."
- "The rear wheel caught the log because you applied the front brake too late. The moment the front wheel lands from the log, that's when the rear brake begins. Practice the timing — it's a sequence, not an impulse."

### Progression Prompts
- "This session: front lift over a piece of tape on the ground at 10cm. Next session: 20cm. Add height only when the previous height is precise every time."
- "Practise the rear lift from near-zero speed this week — just making the rear wheel light. When you can do that 10 times consecutively, add actual log clearance."
- "Try to adjust the height of your front lift consciously: small-medium-large on three consecutive passes. The height should change noticeably between each."

## 6. DRILLS & PRACTICE EXERCISES

### Drill 1: The Tape Height Progression
**Purpose:** Develop precise front wheel lift height control through measurable progression.
**Setup:** Tape on the ground at progressively marked heights (10cm, 20cm, 30cm, 40cm marked with sticks or cones).
**Execution:**
1. Front wheel lift at 10cm height — clear the 10cm marker by a small margin only.
2. Increase to 20cm. Adjust throttle and clutch to achieve the correct height.
3. Progress through heights as precision develops.
**Success Criteria:** Rider can reliably produce front lifts within 5cm of the target height on demand.
**Duration/Reps:** 5 passes at each height. 20 minutes per session.
**Progression:** Add a landing target — specific landing zone that the wheel must land in rather than anywhere after the obstacle.

### Drill 2: The Log Sequence Drill
**Purpose:** Build the complete front-then-rear wheel lift sequence for log crossing.
**Setup:** A log of approximately 20–30cm height.
**Execution:**
1. Approach at walking pace.
2. Front lift: clutch-up, front wheel clears the log, lands on the other side.
3. Immediate transition: as front wheel lands, prepare front brake for rear lift.
4. Rear lift: front brake applied, rear wheel rises and clears the log.
5. Both wheels on far side — complete.
**Success Criteria:** 10 consecutive log crossings without foot contact.
**Duration/Reps:** 15 minutes per session on the same log.
**Progression:** Increase log height to 40cm. Then add a second log in sequence.

### Drill 3: The Manual Introduction
**Purpose:** Find the balance point of the front wheel lift for sustained manual technique.
**Setup:** Flat surface. Slight uphill optional (aids balance point finding).
**Execution:**
1. At near-zero speed: clutch-up to lift the front wheel.
2. As the wheel rises, find the moment where the bike is balanced on the rear wheel — neither falling forward nor backward.
3. If falling backward: apply rear brake (not throttle). This saves from going over.
4. Hold the balance point for as long as possible.
**Success Criteria:** 2-second sustained front wheel off ground with balance maintained.
**Duration/Reps:** 20 attempts per session.
**Progression:** Extend duration to 5 seconds. Then practise a slight forward roll-out from the balance point — returning to both wheels deliberately.

### Drill 4: The Rear Wheel Hop
**Purpose:** Develop lateral rear wheel repositioning as a precision tool.
**Setup:** Two parallel lines on the ground. The rear wheel starts between the lines and must move to outside one line.
**Execution:**
1. At very slow speed, apply front brake to lift rear wheel.
2. As rear wheel rises, shift body weight toward the target direction.
3. Rear wheel descends shifted laterally by 20–30cm.
4. Assess: did the wheel land where intended?
**Success Criteria:** Rear wheel lands within 15cm of intended position consistently.
**Duration/Reps:** 20 hops per session.
**Progression:** Increase the required lateral movement. Apply over an obstacle (rare rock on one side — hop rear wheel over it).

## 8. TERRAIN & CONTEXT VARIATIONS

### Hardpack Flat Surface
Easiest learning environment — predictable traction for both the lifting technique and the landing. Ideal for height calibration and sequence timing work.

### Rocky Technical Terrain
Front lifts must be timed to the specific rock face geometry — the lift initiates where the rock begins, not before. Rear lifts require awareness of where the rear wheel will land after the rock — if the landing is unstable, additional stabilisation is needed immediately.

### Muddy Terrain
Reduced traction affects both the lift (clutch-up may cause rear wheel spin instead of lift) and the landing (less grip for bike stabilisation). Reduce throttle and be more deliberate with clutch release. Rear wheel lifts: reduced front brake pressure needed due to lower traction — less stopping force produces less rear lift.

### Uphill Obstacle
A front lift over an uphill step requires more height than the same obstacle on flat ground — the wheel must clear both the obstacle height and the additional distance created by the slope. Throttle requirement is higher. The rear wheel lift on uphill: the gradient adds natural rear-wheel lightness — less brake force needed.

### Tight Off-Camber (Side Slope)
Wheel lifts on off-camber terrain require simultaneous lateral body compensation — the bike is naturally leaning toward the low side, and the lift changes the balance dynamics. Only for advanced riders with consistent technique on flat surfaces.

## 9. EXPERT INSIGHTS & SOURCES

### Key Insights
- **Dougie Lampkin and Toni Bou**: trials world championship technique at its highest level — the precision with which they execute wheel lifts (to centimetre accuracy, at specific heights on demand) demonstrates the full potential of this skill developed to mastery.
- **Graham Jarvis** (extreme enduro): Jarvis' ability to climb near-vertical rock steps using front and rear wheel lifts in combination is the most visible example of these skills applied in competition enduro context — his technique is extensively analysed in coaching communities.
- **Physics of the clutch-up front lift**: the front wheel lift is created by a combination of the bike's angular momentum (rotating mass), the sudden torque spike from clutch engagement, and the rider's weight shift. Understanding the physics helps coaches explain why small changes to throttle or clutch timing produce large changes in lift height.
- **Trials derivation**: trials riding built all wheel lift techniques as foundational skills — every obstacle in trials requires one or both wheels to be lifted. The transfer to enduro is direct and the technique is unchanged; only the context differs.
- **The "rear brake as the safety device" for manuals**: consistently cited in trials coaching as the non-negotiable safety knowledge — before any manual practice, the rider must be able to reach the rear brake instantly and reflexively. Coaches test this before allowing manual progression.
