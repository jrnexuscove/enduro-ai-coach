# Whoops

## 1. OVERVIEW

Whoops are a dense sequence of rounded mounds — typically 30–120cm high with 1–3 metres between features — that appear in both purpose-built motocross tracks and natural terrain. They are one of the most technically demanding rhythm section types because they exploit a very specific physical threshold: below the correct speed, the bike enters the valley between each whoop and impacts harshly; at or above the correct speed, the bike skims across the tops of the whoops with minimal suspension compression and dramatically reduced impact force. This speed threshold creates a binary experience — whoops either feel controlled and fast, or brutal and exhausting. The unique demand of whoops versus other rhythm sections is the density and height of the features: the spacing is tight enough that normal body movement patterns from standard rhythm sections do not work, and the height is significant enough that being wrong carries real consequences. Whoops are a motocross-specific skill that has increasing relevance in enduro as EnduroGP and extreme enduro venues incorporate more MX-style terrain. On natural desert terrain, sand whoops develop organically and can reach extreme sizes.

## 2. CORE PRINCIPLES

### Principle 1: The Speed Threshold Is Critical — Above It Is Better Than Below It
In whoops, below the critical speed is more dangerous than above it. A rider who enters whoops too slowly gets their suspension fully compressed on each whoop face, the bike bucks unpredictably, and the rider's body receives the full impact. A rider above the critical speed skims across the tops — the wheels barely dip between whoops, suspension travel is minimal, and the ride becomes smooth. This is counterintuitive: going faster makes whoops easier. The challenge is that the "too slow" range can feel fast — riders pull speed just as they are approaching the threshold.

### Principle 2: Standing in a Wide, Low Body Position Is Mandatory
Whoops require a specific body position: standing with knees bent to approximately 45 degrees (significantly more bent than the standard attack position), feet firmly on the pegs, body low and forward. The deep knee bend is the suspension the rider provides — the bike's suspension cannot fully cycle at the speed and frequency of whoops, so the rider's legs must do additional work. A rider with straight or nearly straight knees in whoops will be violently bucked.

### Principle 3: Throttle Is Consistent — Not Reactive
The temptation in whoops is to close the throttle as the bike rises on each face and open it in the valley. This creates speed variation that disrupts the momentum needed to skim the tops. Expert whoops technique maintains a steady, consistent throttle throughout the section. The throttle is set at entry and held — the bike's momentum does the work.

### Principle 4: Let the Bike Move — Do Not Resist It
The bike will move significantly through its chassis under the whoops. The suspension, swingarm, and chassis will flex and twist as each whoop is navigated. A rider who tries to hold the bike "straight" — resisting the natural movement — fights the physics and accelerates fatigue. The correct approach is to let the bike move underneath while the body absorbs with bent knees and relaxed (not gripping) arms. Upper and lower body decoupling (BIOMECH-09) is essential here.

### Principle 5: Vision Covers the Exit of the Entire Section
Whoops sections often have a defined end — where the smooth track resumes. The expert rider enters the whoops and immediately targets the exit point with their vision, not any individual whoop within the section. This long vision creates mental calm (the rider sees where the section ends) and prevents fixation on individual features within the section.

### Principle 6: Enduro Whoops vs MX Whoops — Scale Differences
Motocross whoops are purpose-built to specific heights and spacings designed for MX bikes and competition. Enduro whoops — whether on an EnduroGP track or natural terrain — tend to be less perfectly shaped but can be larger and more variable. Sand whoops in desert terrain can reach 1.5–2 metres in height. The principles are the same but the speed thresholds differ significantly — natural whoops require more adaptive reading and sometimes do not have a clean rhythm speed due to irregular spacing.

## 3. TECHNIQUE BREAKDOWN BY LEVEL

### 3a. Beginner Level
**Goal:** Navigate whoops safely at below-threshold speed by standing deeply and absorbing each feature individually rather than skimming.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Stand in a very low, deep-kneed position — almost a squat over the pegs
- Reduce speed significantly before entering — individual absorption at slow speed is safer than partial rhythm
- Keep throttle steady (or slightly closed to reduce speed) through the section
- Do not sit — ever — in a whoops section

**What Good Looks Like:**
A beginner approaches whoops, slows appropriately, adopts a deep standing position, and rides through each whoop at a controlled pace. The body absorbs each feature through deep knee compression. The ride is bumpy but controlled. They exit safely.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Sitting through whoops:** Rider defaults to sitting when the terrain becomes intimidating. Why it happens: sitting feels stable. → Sitting in whoops means every feature impact goes directly into the spine. Stand. Deep knees. Non-negotiable.
- **Going in at half the wrong speed:** Fast enough to be destabilising, slow enough not to skim. This is the worst zone. → Either slow way down and absorb individually, or find the threshold speed and skim. The middle ground is dangerous.
- **Gripping the handlebars tightly:** Arms locked rigid in whoops. Why it happens: fear. → Light grip. Let the bars move. Stiff arms transmit every impact directly and cause arm pump catastrophically fast.

### 3b. Intermediate Level
**Goal:** Find the whoops threshold speed and begin skimming across the tops rather than entering the valleys.

**Progression Markers from Beginner:** Rider can navigate whoops safely at slow speed. Now working toward the threshold.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Speed progression: increase speed in controlled increments toward the threshold
- Feel the change: at the threshold, the suspension stop bottoming and the ride becomes dramatically smoother
- Throttle lock: practise holding a fixed throttle position through the section
- Leg-absorption endurance: build the quad and calf strength to sustain the deeply bent position through a long whoops section

**What Good Looks Like:**
The rider makes 6–8 passes through a whoops section, increasing speed by 5% on each pass. On one pass, the quality changes dramatically — the section feels smooth. The rider identifies this pass and returns to that speed. They can sustain it for the length of the whoops section.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Pulling speed at the threshold:** Getting to the right speed but then backing off — the threshold feels fast and triggers a speed-reduction instinct. → Commit through the threshold. The feeling of smoothness will immediately confirm the speed is correct.
- **Losing the throttle position mid-section:** Throttle creeps open or closed as the section becomes challenging. → Use a visual or physical cue for the target wrist position and try to maintain it exactly.

### 3c. Advanced Level
**Goal:** Ride whoops at threshold speed consistently, manage large natural whoops, and build the physical endurance to sustain the position through long whoops sections.

**Progression Markers from Intermediate:** Rider finds the threshold on first or second pass and can sustain whoops technique for 30+ seconds.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Large natural whoops: applying threshold principles to irregular, oversized features
- Whoops entry and exit: the transition from normal terrain into whoops and back out requires speed adjustment
- Physical endurance for whoops: leg strength and endurance specifically for the sustained deep-bent position
- Adaptation when rhythm breaks: knowing how to absorb defensively when a feature disrupts the rhythm
- Double-tapping through very dense whoops: allowing both wheels to track across tops alternately rather than simultaneously

**What Good Looks Like:**
Advanced rider enters a long whoops section at threshold speed, immediately adopts the correct position, and maintains it to the exit. The body absorbs any minor disruptions with deep knee flex. The engine note is consistent throughout. On exit, the rider's technique shows no meaningful degradation from what it was at entry.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Technique degrading as the section lengthens:** Good first 5 seconds, then knees straighten, grip tightens, and the rider starts taking impacts. → Physical conditioning issue as much as technique. Specifically train quad endurance for whoops position.
- **Not adapting when natural whoops become irregular:** Rider maintains threshold speed even when spacing changes and the section becomes dangerous. → Natural whoops demand constant adaptation — hold the readiness to absorb defensively if the pattern changes.

## 4. DIAGNOSTIC CUES (AI COACHING ENGINE REFERENCE)

### Visual Cues — Correct Technique
- From chase camera: bike appears to skim across the tops of the whoops — wheels barely visible between features
- Rider in deep knee-bent position, legs clearly working as suspension
- Throttle hand position stable — no visible wrist movement during the section
- Head up and forward throughout — not watching individual whoops
- Minimal chassis pitch (nose up/down) — bike tracking level across the tops

### Visual Cues — Error Indicators
- Front wheel diving into each valley between whoops — below threshold speed
- Rider's body bouncing violently on each impact — knees not absorbing, position too upright
- Visible throttle pulsing — wrist rotating between whoops
- Rider sitting in whoops — immediate red flag
- Arms fully extended and rigid — white-knuckling the bars

### Audio Cues
- Smooth consistent engine note through the section: correct throttle, at or above threshold speed
- Rhythmic impact sounds with smooth engine note: approaching threshold from below — impacts reducing
- Heavy individual impact sounds with irregular engine: well below threshold, absorbing individually
- Worsening impact sounds as section progresses: technique degrading, legs straightening

### Sensor Cues
- Accelerometer: low amplitude consistent vertical oscillation = threshold speed found; high amplitude = below threshold
- Throttle position sensor: flat line throughout section = correct; jagged = pulsing
- Suspension compression: shallow and consistent = skimming; deep individual compressions = entering valleys
- Speed data: consistent speed throughout section = correct; decelerating through section = speed loss from impacts

## 5. COACHING CUES & LANGUAGE

### Positive Reinforcement
- "That's the threshold — I can hear it. The engine note smoothed out and you stopped fighting. That's the correct speed."
- "Deep knees. Your legs are eating those impacts and your torso isn't moving. That's the correct body position."
- "You didn't move the throttle the whole way through. That's the discipline that keeps the whoops smooth."

### Corrective Feedback
- "You're entering these at the worst possible speed — fast enough to be dangerous, slow enough not to skim. Go slower and absorb them individually, then work up to the threshold. Don't live in the middle."
- "Your knees are straightening through the section. That means your legs are giving up and the bike is taking over. Build the leg strength — whoops position is a physical fitness demand, not just technique."
- "You're pulling the throttle at the third whoop every time. Hold it. Set it at the entry and do not touch it again until you're out. Your hand is destroying the momentum you built."
- "You sat down. In whoops. That's why your back is sore. Stand up, bend your knees to almost a squat, and never touch that seat in a whoops section."

### Progression Prompts
- "Five passes, adding 5% speed each time. Tell me which pass felt the most different — that's your threshold. Stay at that speed for the next 5 passes."
- "Practise the whoops position in the car park — stand on the pegs in a deep squat and hold it for 60 seconds. If your legs burn, that's what's failing you in the section. Build that endurance."
- "Listen to your engine. If the note changes during the section, your throttle changed. Try again until the note is flat the whole way through."

## 6. DRILLS & PRACTICE EXERCISES

### Drill 1: The Threshold Hunt
**Purpose:** Find the specific threshold speed for a known whoops section through systematic progression.
**Setup:** A whoops section with 6+ features. Start from the entry.
**Execution:**
1. Pass 1 at very slow speed — walk speed almost. Feel each feature individually.
2. Each subsequent pass: add approximately 5 km/h.
3. Note the pass where the character of the section changes from "impacting individually" to "skimming."
4. Make 5 more passes at that threshold speed to embed it.
**Success Criteria:** Rider can identify the threshold speed and reproduce it reliably.
**Duration/Reps:** As many passes as needed to find threshold, then 5 consolidation passes.
**Progression:** Repeat monthly. As skill develops, the threshold speed becomes easier to find on the first or second pass.

### Drill 2: The Fixed Throttle Drill
**Purpose:** Train throttle discipline through whoops by making wrist movement the sole focus.
**Setup:** Whoops section, entry marked with a cone.
**Execution:**
1. At the entry cone, set the throttle to a position that provides moderate speed.
2. Do not move the throttle wrist at all until exiting the section.
3. Note: does speed increase, decrease, or stay consistent? Adjust entry throttle position accordingly.
**Success Criteria:** Rider exits the section at the same speed as entered. Engine note consistent throughout.
**Duration/Reps:** 8 passes per session.
**Progression:** Raise the entry throttle position progressively until threshold speed is maintained with a single fixed setting.

### Drill 3: The Whoops Squat Endurance Drill
**Purpose:** Build the leg muscle endurance required to sustain the whoops position through a long section.
**Setup:** Off-bike. Gym or home.
**Execution:**
1. Stand with feet shoulder-width apart. Drop into a squat to approximately 45-degree knee angle.
2. Hold the position. Target: 60 seconds without straightening.
3. Rest 30 seconds. Repeat 5 times.
4. Progress to wall sits with the same 45-degree angle.
**Success Criteria:** Rider can hold the whoops position for 90 seconds without leg failure.
**Duration/Reps:** 3 sessions per week during active training.
**Progression:** Add weight (hold dumbbells). Then add pulse squats — micro up-down movements in the position, mimicking the leg absorption action.

### Drill 4: The Exit Targeting Drill
**Purpose:** Develop long vision through whoops by looking at the section exit rather than individual features.
**Setup:** A whoops section with a clearly visible exit point.
**Execution:**
1. At entry, locate the exit visually. Fix eyes on it.
2. Ride the section looking only at the exit — do not look at any individual whoop.
3. Notice: does the section feel different when looking at the exit vs looking at individual features?
**Success Criteria:** Rider can complete the section while looking at the exit the entire time. Section feels more controlled with long vision.
**Duration/Reps:** 5 passes with exit-focus vision, 5 passes with normal vision. Compare.
**Progression:** Practise on increasingly long whoops sections where the exit is further away.

## 8. TERRAIN & CONTEXT VARIATIONS

### MX-Track Whoops (Purpose-Built)
Precisely shaped, consistent height and spacing. The most learnable form — consistent features allow consistent technique. Heights typically 30–60cm. Speed threshold is relatively predictable once calibrated. Double-tapping (front and rear wheel contacting different whoops alternately) is the expert technique at MX speed.

### Sand Whoops (Desert Terrain)
Can grow to 1–2 metres in height through erosion patterns. Threshold speed is much higher due to feature size. Traction is reduced (sand). Whoops can change shape and spacing within a single section — adaptive reading is mandatory. Sand whoops are one of the most physically demanding single-terrain features in off-road riding.

### Natural Dirt Whoops (Enduro)
Often found at trail intersections or in areas of repeated erosion. Smaller than MX (typically 15–35cm) but irregular. A rhythm threshold may or may not exist depending on spacing consistency. Conservative speed management and defensive body position are appropriate until the section is well-known.

### Wet Whoops
Reduced traction dramatically lowers the safe threshold speed. Whoops in wet or muddy conditions should be approached as individual absorption problems rather than rhythm sections — the grip for skimming the tops is not reliably available. Deep standing position and very cautious speed management.

## 9. EXPERT INSIGHTS & SOURCES

### Key Insights
- **Ryan Villopoto** (four-time AMA Supercross Champion): widely cited as the definitive modern whoops technician — his ability to find and hold the threshold speed on the first lap of any new track was a primary competitive advantage across his career.
- **Ricky Carmichael** coaching methodology: taught whoops to developing riders by making them feel the difference between below-threshold and at-threshold — the physical sensation change is the teaching tool, not verbal description.
- **Biomechanical research on whoops riding**: studies of professional motocross riders in whoops sections show that expert riders exhibit approximately 40% less peak vertical acceleration at handlebar level than novice riders at the same speed — the leg absorption technique and threshold speed management account for the majority of this difference.
- **Sand whoops at Dakar**: Dakar Rally stages routinely include giant natural sand whoops. Stage winning speeds in heavy sand whoops sections require finding the rhythm threshold — teams use reconnaissance footage to pre-calibrate threshold speed estimates before the stage.
- **Enduro relevance increasing**: as EnduroGP and extreme enduro venue design increasingly incorporates manufactured rhythm sections and whoops, enduro riders who only trained in natural terrain are at a growing disadvantage to riders with MX-track experience.
