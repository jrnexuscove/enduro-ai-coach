# Single-Track Optimisation

## 1. OVERVIEW

Single-track is the defining terrain of enduro riding — a trail typically 60–120cm wide, winding through forest, rock, or hillside, where line choices are tightly constrained by the track boundary and natural obstacles. The unique demand of single-track is that the rider cannot choose a dramatically different line the way they can on open terrain; they must maximise efficiency within a corridor that allows only marginal variation. Speed on single-track comes not from raw pace but from linking corners smoothly, reading the track 3–4 seconds ahead, and developing a rhythm that syncs with the trail's natural flow. The fastest single-track riders are not necessarily the most technically skilled in isolation — they are the ones who "read" a trail fastest and enter a state of reactive efficiency that reduces the cognitive gap between seeing and doing. This topic is the essence of enduro performance: not maximum speed in isolation, but maximum speed within the constraints of a fixed, narrow channel.

## 2. CORE PRINCIPLES

### Principle 1: Vision Range Determines Maximum Speed
On single-track, the rider can only go as fast as they can process what is ahead. Rider vision — how far ahead the rider looks and how well they read that information — directly caps maximum single-track speed. A rider who looks at the front wheel sees 0.5 seconds ahead. A rider who looks at the exit of the next corner sees 3–4 seconds ahead. The difference in safe maximum speed between these two riders is enormous. Vision extension is the single most impactful single-track skill improvement available.

### Principle 2: The Exit of Each Corner Sets Up the Entry to the Next
Single-track corners are not independent events — they are a chain. The exit line of Corner 1 determines where the bike is physically positioned for Corner 2. A wide exit from a left-hander that pushes the bike to the right edge of the trail is the correct position for the entry to a right-hander that follows. A tight exit that keeps the bike on the left does not set up the subsequent corner. Reading the track as a sequence — not as individual corners — is the foundation of single-track efficiency.

### Principle 3: Rhythm Is Speed on Single-Track
Single-track has a natural rhythm — a pattern of obstacles, corners, and straights that repeats with variations. A rider who finds the trail's rhythm and matches their body movements and throttle timing to it is "in flow." This is not a mystical concept: it is the result of vision far enough ahead to anticipate the next input, body movements pre-timed to terrain, and throttle applied at the optimal moment for each section. Rhythm cannot be forced — it emerges when the other fundamentals (vision, line, smooth inputs) are working correctly.

### Principle 4: Familiarity Breeds Speed — The Learning Loop
Single-track speed builds with repetition of the same trail. The first pass of an unfamiliar trail requires maximum cognitive effort to read obstacles, corners, and surface type. The third pass is significantly faster — the brain has pre-loaded the trail map and can focus cognitive resources on execution rather than discovery. This is why repeated laps of a practice loop build speed faster than constant new terrain exploration. Deliberate repetition of a known trail section is the most effective single-track development tool.

### Principle 5: Trail Width Management — Where in the Trail, Not Just Which Line
On single-track, a "line" is not a wide-line or tight-line choice as on a motocross track — the trail is too narrow for that level of variation. Instead, the rider manages which edge of the trail they use: inside edge (tight to obstacles), centre, or outside edge. These small variations — sometimes as little as 20–30cm — affect corner geometry, traction surface quality, and momentum. Expert single-track riders make these micro-adjustments continuously and automatically.

### Principle 6: The Passing Rider and Trail Etiquette
On shared single-track (not in competition), faster riders from behind require a specific protocol. Awareness of following riders — sound, peripheral vision at corners — allows the slower rider to make a safe gap for passing rather than being surprised. Competitive enduro passing on single-track follows different rules (DISC-10), but social trail riding requires cooperation and communication to pass safely on terrain where stopping quickly is not always possible.

## 3. TECHNIQUE BREAKDOWN BY LEVEL

### 3a. Beginner Level
**Goal:** Develop consistent vision range and begin reading single-track 2–3 seconds ahead rather than at the front wheel.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Active vision: consciously look at the next corner exit, not the immediate obstacle
- Riding within capability on unfamiliar single-track — speed is earned by familiarity, not forced
- Smooth throttle through tight sections — single-track does not reward sudden acceleration
- Identifying the rideable line rather than searching for alternatives that do not exist

**What Good Looks Like:**
A beginner on single-track is looking consistently to the first bend ahead rather than at the ground immediately in front. Their pace is conservative but smooth. They do not brake suddenly or panic at obstacles they saw late. They stay on the trail without repeatedly going wide.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Looking at the front wheel or immediate ground:** Classic beginner tunnel vision. Why it happens: focus narrows under stress to the closest threat. → Pick a point 3 seconds ahead and look at it. If you are looking at the current obstacle, you are already too late.
- **Panic braking at first sight of a corner:** Heavy late braking every time a corner appears. Why it happens: trail seems to rush toward the rider. → If you can see the corner, you have enough time to make smooth inputs. Brake earlier and lighter.
- **Attempting to go fast on the first lap of unfamiliar trail:** Rider pushes pace before the brain has mapped the trail. Why it happens: eagerness, overconfidence. → First lap is a reconnaissance lap. Speed comes on the second and third lap.

### 3b. Intermediate Level
**Goal:** Link corners smoothly and begin developing a consistent rhythm on known single-track sections.

**Progression Markers from Beginner:** Rider can complete a technical single-track section without panic braking and maintains vision 2+ seconds ahead.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Corner linking: consciously position the exit of each corner for the entry to the next
- Rhythm identification: recognise sections of trail that have a natural rhythm and begin matching body movement to it
- Throttle timing on single-track: open throttle at corner apex, not before, not significantly after
- Repeated laps of a practice section to build speed through familiarity

**What Good Looks Like:**
The rider completes a familiar single-track loop 3 times in a session. Each lap is measurably faster. On the third lap, they can identify 2–3 sections where rhythm "clicked" — where the trail and the body were synchronised and the riding felt effortless.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Treating each corner as isolated:** Braking hard to the apex of each corner, powering out independently, braking for the next. → Ask: "where does this corner send me for the next corner?" Start planning Corner 2 while entering Corner 1.
- **Looking for the "perfect line" that does not exist:** Constantly scanning for different lines on narrow single-track where the variation is 20cm. → On single-track, the line is mostly given. Focus on execution of the available line rather than searching for an alternative.

### 3c. Advanced Level
**Goal:** Maintain near-maximum pace on familiar single-track, execute smooth lines under competition conditions, and develop the adaptive reading required for unfamiliar race sections.

**Progression Markers from Intermediate:** Rider's single-track speed on familiar trails approaches their physical maximum. They ride in flow state for significant sections.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Active adaptation: reading subtle surface changes (wet roots, fresh mud, tyre marks indicating soft spots) while maintaining pace
- Competition single-track: maintaining pace on test sections seen only once (first-lap race pace)
- Handling traffic: being behind a slower rider in a test and managing time pressure without over-riding
- Vision under physical fatigue: maintaining vision range in the second half of a long test when fatigue tends to shorten it
- The micro-line: consistent 20–30cm line choices that over a full single-track test section compound into meaningful time

**What Good Looks Like:**
Advanced rider completes an unfamiliar competition test section at 85% of their familiar-trail pace on the first pass. Their vision range is consistently 3–4 seconds. They make no visible panic inputs. They read the trail as it comes and adapt smoothly to unexpected sections.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Speed reducing on unfamiliar terrain too dramatically:** Rider slows to 50% on first pass of an unfamiliar test. → A well-developed trail reading skill allows 80%+ pace on first pass. Trust the reading skill, not just memory.
- **Vision shortening under fatigue:** In the second half of a long test, the rider's vision drops back to looking 1 second ahead. → Active check: "where am I looking?" Re-extend deliberately when vision shortens.

## 4. DIAGNOSTIC CUES (AI COACHING ENGINE REFERENCE)

### Visual Cues — Correct Technique
- Head position consistently directed toward the next corner exit, not the current obstacle (from helmet camera or chase camera: sight line targets 3+ seconds ahead)
- Smooth, flowing cornering without visible panic braking or abrupt throttle changes
- Bike tracks a consistent efficient line through corners — no wide exits or wide entries that are not geometrically optimal
- Body position pre-adjusted before corners rather than reacting during — weight already shifted at corner entry
- Speed increasing progressively on repeated laps of the same section

### Visual Cues — Error Indicators
- Head looking downward at front wheel or immediate ground — tunnel vision
- Visible panic braking (rear wheel hop or skid) on single-track corners seen at the last moment
- Wide, inefficient exits from corners that sacrifice the entry position for the following corner
- Static body position throughout single-track — rider not pre-loading corners
- Same speed lap after lap of a practice section — not learning the trail, just surviving it

### Audio Cues
- Smooth, consistent engine note through corners: correct throttle timing — apex open, smooth roll-on
- Frequent brake squeal or skid sounds on single-track: late braking, vision too short
- Irregular throttle changes on smooth single-track: rider reacting rather than anticipating
- Engine note stabilising and smoothing on repeated laps: rhythm developing — learning signal

### Sensor Cues
- GPS lap times: progressively faster across repeated laps of same section — learning curve active
- Speed data: smooth curves through corners (no sharp V-shaped speed drops) — momentum maintained
- Accelerometer: high lateral G through corners maintained consistently — full commitment to the line
- GPS track overlay on known trail: consistent line within tight corridor — controlled micro-line management

## 5. COACHING CUES & LANGUAGE

### Positive Reinforcement
- "I can see you looking through the corner — your head is already at the exit when the front wheel is at the entry. That's fast-rider vision."
- "Third lap and you're two seconds faster. You didn't get fitter — you learned the trail. That's how single-track speed works."
- "That section had a rhythm and you found it. When it clicks like that, stop thinking and let it happen."

### Corrective Feedback
- "You're looking at the ground in front of your wheel. That's why you're braking late. Look further. If you can already see the obstacle, you should already be planning your response."
- "Every corner you're treating like it's isolated. You're not setting up Corner 2 with your Corner 1 exit. Look at where Corner 1 sends you and aim your exit there deliberately."
- "You're going the same speed every lap and calling yourself practiced. That's not practice — that's repetition. I need you to push 5% faster each lap until something breaks down, then fix that thing."
- "First lap on this trail should be slow — it's a map-reading lap. You'll go faster on lap 3 with a slower lap 1 than you will by pushing a dangerous pace on lap 1."

### Progression Prompts
- "Pick one section of this trail — just 30 seconds long. Do 10 passes in a row and try to improve each time. Tell me what changed between pass 1 and pass 10."
- "On the next lap, watch where your exit from the left-hander sends you. Is it setting you up for the right-hander? If not, adjust your exit line and see what happens."
- "This lap: look only at the very next corner exit, not the corner you're in. Do nothing else differently. Report whether your speed changed."

## 6. DRILLS & PRACTICE EXERCISES

### Drill 1: The Vision Lap
**Purpose:** Extend vision range on a known trail by making looking-ahead the sole focus.
**Setup:** A familiar single-track loop.
**Execution:**
1. Ride the loop at 70% pace. The only instruction: look at the exit of every corner before entering it.
2. Do not focus on speed, smoothness, or technique — just vision.
3. On completion: identify three places where extending vision revealed something unexpected.
**Success Criteria:** Rider reports seeing corners before they arrive at them. Panic braking reduced from the baseline.
**Duration/Reps:** One dedicated vision lap per session for 3 sessions.
**Progression:** Progressive laps: lap 1 is vision-only at 70%, lap 2 is vision + smooth throttle at 80%, lap 3 is all elements at 90%.

### Drill 2: The Repeated Section Drill
**Purpose:** Build speed through familiarity on a short single-track section using deliberate repetition.
**Setup:** A 30–60 second single-track section with 3–4 corners. Time it.
**Execution:**
1. Pass 1: ride at comfortable pace. Time it.
2. Pass 2–4: ride the same section, trying to improve time on each pass.
3. Pass 5: ride at maximum confidence speed. Time it.
4. Compare Pass 1 to Pass 5 times. The improvement is the "familiarity dividend."
**Success Criteria:** Pass 5 is at least 10% faster than Pass 1. Rider can explain what changed on each pass.
**Duration/Reps:** 5 passes minimum per section. Use 2–3 different short sections per session.
**Progression:** Add an unfamiliar section and compare the rate of improvement to the familiar section — demonstrates the learning curve in action.

### Drill 3: The Corner-Linking Exercise
**Purpose:** Train the habit of using each corner's exit to set up the next corner.
**Setup:** A section of single-track with a clear double-corner: a left-hander followed closely by a right-hander.
**Execution:**
1. Ride the double-corner focusing only on Corner 1. Exit it "safely."
2. Note where the exit positions the bike for Corner 2.
3. Ride again, but on Corner 1, specifically target the exit that sets up the Corner 2 entry optimally.
4. Compare how Corner 2 feels when set up from Corner 1 vs when not.
**Success Criteria:** Rider can execute a smooth, uninterrupted linked double-corner where Corner 2 requires less braking because Corner 1 positioned it correctly.
**Duration/Reps:** 10 passes of the double-corner. Then apply the concept to the full trail loop.
**Progression:** Find triple-corner sequences. Can all three be linked without a brake application?

### Drill 4: The Rhythm Section Ride
**Purpose:** Develop trail rhythm by matching body movement to terrain pattern rather than reacting to each obstacle.
**Setup:** A section of trail with a regular repeating pattern — roots, whoops, small rollers.
**Execution:**
1. First pass: ride reactively — respond to each obstacle as it arrives.
2. Second pass: look ahead, identify the pattern, and pre-position the body for the next obstacle before it arrives.
3. Third pass: match throttle timing to the pattern — slight throttle between obstacles, slight release as the bike goes over.
4. Count how many passes before the section feels "rhythmic" rather than "reactive."
**Success Criteria:** Rider reports a subjective feeling of rhythm on the section — the trail is "with" them rather than "against" them.
**Duration/Reps:** 5–8 passes of the same section.
**Progression:** Increase speed on each pass until rhythm breaks. Identify what speed causes rhythm loss and work on the specific obstacle that disrupts it.

## 8. TERRAIN & CONTEXT VARIATIONS

### Forest Single-Track (Roots)
Rhythm is especially important — root sections have natural spacing that rewards matching body movement to the root pattern. Braking before roots (not on them) and accelerating after is the fundamental technique. Low light under tree canopy reduces vision — look for gaps between trees rather than focusing on the roots.

### Rocky Single-Track
Line precision matters most here — the difference between a rideable line and an unrideable line may be 20cm. Momentum preservation is critical; speed is lost to rocks and must be rebuilt. Higher gear than intuition suggests for rocky single-track — low gear creates too much throttle sensitivity.

### Hillside Single-Track (Camber)
The trail leans across a slope — one tyre always higher than the other. Body position must compensate for the camber: lean into the hillside, outside peg weighted more. Line management is about staying on the most solid part of the trail surface — the outside edge of a hillside trail is often softer or more exposed. Grip the line and commit to it.

### Wet/Muddy Single-Track
Traction reduces dramatically. Vision becomes even more critical — identifying wet root and rock patches 3+ seconds ahead allows early speed management. Smooth inputs matter more here: any abrupt throttle or brake on wet single-track causes immediate traction loss. Commit fully to the line — hesitation on wet single-track is more dangerous than commitment.

## 9. EXPERT INSIGHTS & SOURCES

### Key Insights
- **Taddy Blazusiak** (multiple Indoor Extreme Enduro Champion, King of the Enduro): cited as one of the most efficient single-track riders in the world — coaches consistently note his vision range and the smoothness of his inputs as the primary differentiators from other riders with equal physical capability.
- **Flow state research** (Csikszentmihalyi): the flow state experienced by expert single-track riders is a well-documented psychological phenomenon — it occurs when challenge level and skill level are exactly matched, producing effortless high performance. Single-track familiarity is the primary route to this state in off-road riding.
- **Vision leading action**: neuroscience of action-perception coupling — the brain begins preparing motor responses to visual input before the visual signal is consciously processed. This is why "looking ahead" genuinely makes riders faster, not because they have more time to think but because the pre-motor response begins earlier.
- **Pace note principle** (from rally driving): rally co-pilots call pace notes ahead of corners so the driver can respond before seeing the corner. Single-track riders develop an internal equivalent — a pre-loaded trail map on known sections that functions the same way. Familiarity = your internal co-pilot.
- **Trail "memory" across skill levels**: studies of expert vs novice trail riders show experts make more look-aheads per unit distance, spend less time looking at immediate terrain, and have faster trail memory acquisition. These are trainable skills, not innate.
