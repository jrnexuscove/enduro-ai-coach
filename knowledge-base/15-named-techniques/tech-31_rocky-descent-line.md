# Rocky Descent Line

## 1. OVERVIEW

A rocky descent line is a planned path through a downhill section of large rocks, boulders, and irregular terrain — requiring pre-ride visual analysis, committed body position, speed management between rocks, and constant suspension absorption. Unlike a smooth descent where the line is obvious, rocky descents present dozens of potential line choices, each with different traction, obstacle, and speed characteristics. Selecting and committing to a line, then executing it with correct body position and brake management, is the combined challenge. Rocky descents are a defining skill in hard enduro — riders who can descend rocky terrain quickly and smoothly while maintaining control are the ones who clean difficult stages. Riders who let the rocks "happen to them" reactively will be slow, fatigued, and will crash more often.

## 2. CORE PRINCIPLES

### Principle 1: Vision Must Lead — Look 5–10 Metres Ahead
The most important principle in rocky descent riding is where the rider looks. Eyes fixed on the rock directly in front of the front wheel means the rider is always reacting — the rock is already there by the time it's seen. Eyes 5–10 metres ahead allow the rider to plan, select the next rock contact point, and position the body in advance. The rock directly in front of the wheel is already decided — the next one is what matters. Building this visual range is the primary training lever for improving rocky descent speed.

### Principle 2: Body Rearward Balances the Downhill Gradient
On a steep rocky descent, the gradient wants to pitch the rider's mass forward over the bars. The rider must actively counterbalance this by maintaining rearward body position — hips back, arms extended toward the bars. This position is more extreme than on flat terrain and must be held continuously throughout the descent, not just on individual rocks. Fatigue causes riders to drift forward — which is when descent crashes most often occur.

### Principle 3: Braking Between Rocks — Not Over Them
Braking on a rock's surface reduces traction significantly — the tyre is already managing lateral deflection forces, and adding braking force can cause the tyre to slip sideways. The correct approach is to brake in the gaps between rocks (where the tyre is on relatively flat terrain) and release the brakes as the tyre approaches each rock. This gives the tyre maximum traction for deflection management on the rock.

### Principle 4: Speed Must Be Calibrated to Visual Reading Ability
The fundamental limit on rocky descent speed is not the bike's capability — it is the rider's ability to read the line. Riding faster than the rider can read the terrain produces reactive, uncontrolled descent. The rider who can read 10 metres ahead can ride faster than the rider who reads 3 metres ahead. Building visual speed (reading distance and speed) is the training leverage point — speed follows vision, not the other way around.

### Principle 5: The Line Is Committed — Small Corrections Only
Once a line is selected through a section of rocks, small corrections are made with braking and body position. The bike should not be steered drastically between rocks — each large steering input requires traction that the rocky surface may not provide. The line is selected, and the bike is guided through it with minimal dramatic intervention.

## 3. TECHNIQUE BREAKDOWN BY LEVEL

### 3a. Beginner Level
**Goal:** Descend a moderate rocky section at slow speed — pre-reading the line, maintaining rearward body position.

**Step-by-Step Execution:**
1. Walk the section before riding — identify the main line, the key rocks to contact, the gaps for braking.
2. At the top of the section, establish rearward body position before the first rock. Hips back, arms extended.
3. Eyes 3–4 metres ahead — not at the immediate ground.
4. As the bike approaches each rock: brakes applied in the gap before it, released as the tyre approaches contact.
5. Allow the front tyre to deflect off the rock — do not fight it. Absorb through bent knees.
6. In the gap after the rock: brakes applied again if speed is too high.
7. Continue this pattern through the section — brake, release, rock, absorb, brake.

**What the Rider Should Feel:**
- Pre-section: Deliberate body rearward position. Weight on the rear pegs. Arms extended but not locked.
- Gap braking: A brief, controlled deceleration. The tyre grips the dirt between rocks.
- Rock contact: A sudden directional change felt through the bars. Arms absorb. Knees absorb. Body barely moves.
- Pattern developing: The rhythm of brake-release-absorb building into a consistent sequence.

**What Good Looks Like:**
At slow speed through a rocky descent, the rider is clearly rearward, arms extended. Braking happens in the gaps between rocks — the tyre can be heard to stop skidding as it reaches each rock. The line pre-read from the walk is being followed. No major crashes.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Braking on rocks:** Tyre slides sideways on rock surface. → Brake in gaps only. Release before tyre contact with each rock.
- **Sitting on rocky descent:** Weight forward and lower — reduces reaction range and loads the front. → Stand, rearward, the entire descent.
- **No pre-ride:** Riding blind into an unknown rocky descent. → Always walk it first. The 60-second walk saves many crashes.

### 3b. Intermediate Level
**Goal:** Descend rocky sections at moderate speed — using pre-read combined with on-the-fly line selection for familiar terrain.

**Progression Markers from Beginner:** Rider can descend moderate rocky terrain. Body position is consistently rearward. Braking pattern is improving.

**Step-by-Step Execution:**
1. Pre-read the section — either walk it or study it carefully from the top.
2. Enter at moderate speed. Eyes immediately at 5–7 metres ahead.
3. Gap-brake-release rhythm: brake applications are brief and in gaps. Release before each rock.
4. On each rock contact: absorb through legs. Allow the bike to deflect. Correct the direction subtly between rocks.
5. Vision constantly scanning forward — not at current rock, but at the next cluster.
6. Adjust speed throughout: more braking in gaps before difficult rock clusters, less in easier sections.
7. Maintain rearward position throughout — especially as fatigue builds.

**What the Rider Should Feel at Each Stage:**
- Entry: Speed set. Eyes already picking up the next rock cluster 6 metres away. Body rearward.
- Gap braking: A brief front and rear brake application in the smooth section between two rocks. The deceleration is felt and the speed drops slightly.
- Brake release: As the next rock approaches, brake released. The tyre is free to deflect without traction loss from braking.
- Rock contact: The front tyre deflects off or rolls over the rock. Felt as a sharp directional change or pitch — the legs absorb. Body barely moves.
- Post-rock: Speed has been momentarily altered by the deflection. Eyes already on the next feature.
- Continuous rhythm: This pattern repeats — gap brake, release, rock deflect, absorb, gap brake.

**What Good Looks Like:**
Through a rocky descent at 15–20 km/h, the rider's eyes are clearly ahead. Brief brake applications are visible in the gaps between rocks. The bike deflects off individual rocks — the rider absorbs through bent legs, the body barely moves. The line pre-selected is being followed with minor corrections.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Braking on rocks still happening:** Ingrained habit. → Conscious verbal cue — "brake, release, rock" repeated during descent. On-slope coaching repetition is needed.
- **Eyes too close:** Looking at immediate rocks rather than ahead. → Deliberately pick a rock 6 metres away and keep looking at features at that distance. Vision leads the bike.
- **Sitting through rocky descent:** Too slow and sitting is attempted. → Standing for all rocky descents above walking pace.

### 3c. Advanced Level
**Goal:** Descend challenging rocky terrain at pace — reading lines at full approach speed, braking pattern automatic, body position adaptive.

**Progression Markers from Intermediate:** Rider descends moderate rocky terrain with controlled body position. Braking pattern is instinctive. Visual look-ahead is 5+ metres.

**Step-by-Step Execution — Competition Pace:**
1. Pre-read from the top — identify the 2–3 key line decisions that will determine the full descent.
2. Enter at pace. Eyes immediately at 8–10 metres ahead.
3. Gap-brake pattern is now largely instinctive — conscious attention only on the unusual features.
4. Multi-rock planning: reading 3–4 contacts ahead simultaneously. The brain is processing the next sequence while the body handles the current one.
5. Large deflection events: when a rock deflects the front significantly, recover with subtle steering and continue. No panic braking.
6. Speed escalation: pace increases as vision speed increases — not by deciding to go faster.

**What Good Looks Like:**
At full trail speed, the rider reads 8–10 metres ahead. Brake applications happen in gaps automatically. Rock contacts are absorbed efficiently. The rider chooses line variations on-the-fly based on what the eye reads. Speed is limited by vision, not fear.

**Failure Modes and Causes:**
- **Too fast for line reading:** Speed exceeds the rider's visual processing — each rock is a surprise. → Reduce speed to match reading ability. Speed builds as vision speed builds.
- **Over-braking between rocks:** Too much speed reduction in gaps — braking too hard or for too long. Braking is for speed management, not stopping. → Light, quick brake applications. The gap is short.
- **Sitting when should stand:** Fatigue causes the rider to sit on a long rocky descent. Weight shifts forward, front loads, front wash risk increases. → Stand throughout or take a rest stop. Sitting on rocky descents at speed is not a fatigue management strategy.

## 4. DIAGNOSTIC CUES (AI COACHING ENGINE REFERENCE)

### Visual Cues — Correct Technique
- Rider head and eyes clearly forward — not looking at immediate ground
- Weight rearward — hips back, arms extended, visible gap between seat and rider
- Brief brake applications in gaps between rocks — no continuous brake drag
- Bike deflecting off rocks while rider's body remains relatively stable — absorption occurring

### Visual Cues — Error Indicators
- Rider sitting on rocky descent — seat visible in contact
- Braking on rocks visible — tyre skid marks on rock surfaces
- Rider's body slamming forward on rock deflections — not absorbing, no leg compliance
- Eyes at the immediate ground — reactive riding, not planned

### Audio Cues
- Correct: Brief brake squeal or drag sound in gaps between rocks
- Correct: Consistent engine note or slight engine braking — speed managed
- Error: Continuous brake application — braking on everything including rocks
- Error: Hard rock impacts with body impact sound — not absorbing, sitting

### Sensor Cues
- Correct: Speed reduction events (braking) correlated with gaps between rocks — not on rock contacts
- Correct: Moderate vertical acceleration variations — absorption occurring
- Error: Braking events coinciding with lateral acceleration spikes — braking on rocks, traction loss

## 5. COACHING CUES & LANGUAGE

### Positive Reinforcement
- "Eyes were well ahead — I could see you picking up rocks 6 metres out. That's the vision that enables pace."
- "Brake in the gaps, release on the rocks. Perfect pattern. The tyre could manage each rock without fighting the brake."
- "Body barely moved through that deflection. Your legs took it. That's correct absorption."

### Corrective Feedback
- "Eyes are too close — you're looking at the rock right in front of the wheel. Pick up a rock 6 metres away and watch from there. The immediate rock is already decided."
- "Braking on the rocks — I can see skid marks on the rock surfaces. Brake in the gaps. Release before tyre contact."
- "You sat down mid-descent. Stand. Weight stays back, standing, the whole descent."

### Progression Prompts
- "You're reading 4 metres ahead. Push to 6. Look further. Your speed can increase as your visual range increases."
- "Try this descent with your eyes deliberately further ahead than feels comfortable. Commit to looking further. Trust the bike to handle the immediate rocks."
- "On your next pass, don't decide your line at the top. Read it as you go — practice on-the-fly line selection."

## 6. DRILLS & PRACTICE EXERCISES

### Drill 1: Walk-and-Ride Comparison
**Purpose:** Build the habit of pre-riding section analysis and following a planned line.
**Setup:** A rocky descent section.
**Execution:**
1. Walk the section completely. Identify the line — every key rock, every braking gap.
2. Ride the section following the walked line.
3. Compare: how closely did the ridden line match the walked line? Where did it diverge?
**Success Criteria:** Rider can articulate the line walked and ride it as planned. Divergences identified and corrected.
**Duration/Reps:** 5 walk-and-ride cycles.

### Drill 2: Gap-Brake Pattern
**Purpose:** Build the braking-in-gaps habit through deliberate, repetitive practice.
**Setup:** A rocky section with clear gaps between rocks.
**Execution:**
1. Ride the section with a verbal or mental "brake — release — rock" rhythm.
2. After the run, check rock surfaces for skid marks — any skid mark on a rock means a brake was applied on it.
**Success Criteria:** Braking events only in gaps — no skid marks on rock surfaces.
**Duration/Reps:** 15 passes.

### Drill 3: Visual Look-Ahead Extension
**Purpose:** Extend the visual look-ahead distance deliberately.
**Setup:** A rocky section with identifiable rocks at different distances.
**Execution:**
1. Before each pass, identify a specific rock 6 metres ahead as the "visual target."
2. While riding, keep looking at features at that 6-metre distance — not closer.
3. After each pass, note: could you see the features further ahead? Did your brain process them?
**Success Criteria:** Rider reports seeing rocks earlier than before. Speed can increase without loss of control.
**Duration/Reps:** 10 passes.

### Drill 4: Speed Escalation
**Purpose:** Build descent pace as line-reading ability improves — speed as the outcome, not the target.
**Setup:** A known rocky descent section.
**Execution:**
1. Each pass, increase speed by 5%. Not a target — an assessment.
2. After each pass: did line reading keep up? Or was the speed over the visual limit?
3. If the line reading kept up: increase again. If it didn't: hold at that speed until vision catches up.
**Success Criteria:** Speed increases while technique quality is maintained. Rider identifies their current visual speed limit.
**Duration/Reps:** 5 escalation passes.

## 8. TERRAIN & CONTEXT VARIATIONS

### Dry Rocks
Best traction. Standard technique. Vision is the primary limit — not traction.

### Wet Rocks
Significantly lower traction. Braking on rocks causes immediate slide. Even more important to brake only in gaps. Speed must be reduced — the visual reading must be more cautious and approach speeds lowered.

### Mixed Rocky and Dirt
Some gaps have loose dirt — braking on loose dirt between rocks produces less predictable deceleration than braking on firm surfaces. Adjust brake pressure for each gap surface.

### Night Descent
Visual range dramatically reduced. Skim technique replaces long-range reading. Speed must be very conservative — the visual range determines everything, and headlights only reach 3–5 metres at speed.

## 9. EXPERT INSIGHTS & SOURCES

### Key Insights
- **The vision principle:** Graham Jarvis is described by coaches as having exceptional visual look-ahead — he reads terrain so far ahead that individual rocks don't require conscious decisions. This is learned vision speed, not innate ability. Any rider can develop it through deliberate practice.
- **Brake pattern as the key habit:** The braking-in-gaps, releasing-on-rocks pattern is the single most trainable aspect of rocky descent technique — and the one with the highest impact on crash rate and speed improvement. Coaches note that riders who master this pattern improve their rocky descent times more than those who focus on any other single skill.
- **Speed follows vision:** A common coaching framing for this technique: "don't ride faster — read further." The pace improvement is a consequence of vision improvement, not effort or courage.
- **Rearward weight on long descents:** Coaches note that the rearward body position required for rocky descent is physically demanding to maintain over a long descent. Riders who cannot hold it for the full section will drift forward as fatigue sets in — this is when descent crashes most commonly occur. Specific conditioning for the rearward position (practice holds, descending with a deliberate rearward bias) builds the muscular endurance that prevents fatigue-induced crashes.
- **Walk the section once — ride it many times:** For any new or unknown rocky descent section, a single walk-through before riding provides return on investment that compounds over many attempts. The walk builds the mental map that makes every subsequent pass faster and safer. Riders who skip the walk spend several slow passes learning what the walk would have shown immediately.
- **The three limits of rocky descent speed:** Coaches identify three separate limiting factors on rocky descent pace: (1) visual speed — how far ahead the rider can read; (2) absorption capacity — how effectively the rider can absorb deflections through body position; (3) line commitment — how well the rider holds a chosen line through rocks. Identifying which of these three is the weakest for a given rider focuses the training.
- **Rock size relative to wheel diameter matters more than rock density:** A rocky descent that appears severe from above can be faster than a moderate rock section if the individual rocks are smaller than wheel diameter — the tyre rolls over rather than deflecting off. Coaches teach riders to assess rocks relative to wheel size, not relative to their appearance from a standing position. A field of fist-sized rocks is manageable; a line with a single rock the size of the wheel rim at a difficult approach angle is a significant obstacle requiring line adjustment.
- **Brake fade on long technical descents:** On extended rocky descents at events, rear brake fade is a real risk that changes technique requirements partway down. Coaches note that riders who rely heavily on rear brake for the entire descent may find the brake progressively less responsive in the lower sections — a problem that compounds exactly when the rider is tired. Training for long descents includes awareness of brake temperature and deliberately varying brake use to allow cooling: brief coasting, alternating rear and engine braking, and using momentum management to reduce the total braking required.
- **The rhythm of rocky terrain:** Experienced coaches describe successful rocky descent as finding a rhythm within the chaos — the rider is not fighting each rock individually but settling into a cadence that uses the terrain's natural flow. This rhythm comes only with repetition on similar terrain, and coaches recommend deliberately riding the same rocky descent multiple times in a session to find and internalise the rhythm, rather than moving to new terrain before the existing section is absorbed.
