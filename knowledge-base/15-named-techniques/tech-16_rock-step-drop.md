# Rock Step Drop

## 1. OVERVIEW

A rock step drop is the technique of descending a sharp, abrupt drop in the terrain — typically a vertical or near-vertical rock step of 20–80 cm — where the front wheel drops off the edge before the rear wheel reaches it. Unlike a smooth descent where both wheels maintain contact progressively, a rock step drop requires the rider to manage the front-drop moment, the airborne phase as the bike pitches forward, and the landing and recovery on the lower surface. This is a fundamental hard enduro and technical trail skill — rock steps appear constantly in mountain terrain and the ability to descend them confidently and cleanly separates fluid descenders from riders who must dismount or walk sections.

## 2. CORE PRINCIPLES

### Principle 1: Rearward Weight Is Non-Negotiable
When the front wheel drops off a rock step, the bike immediately pitches nose-down. The steeper and higher the step, the more violent this pitch. The rider's body weight must be significantly rearward before the front drops — not at the moment of the drop, and certainly not after. A rider who is centred or forward-weighted when the front drops will be thrown over the bars. The rearward weight must be pre-positioned in the approach.

### Principle 2: Speed Must Be Managed Before the Edge
Excessive speed at the edge of a rock step produces a long front-drop arc — the front drops far before the rear follows, and the landing angle is steep and unstable. Too little speed means no momentum to carry through the landing absorption. The correct speed allows the front to drop and land while the rear is still near the ledge edge — producing a controlled, predictable arc.

### Principle 3: The Front Landing Absorption Is the Critical Moment
When the front wheel lands at the base of the step, the front forks absorb the impact — but the rider's arms and upper body must absorb the remainder. A rider who is stiff-armed at the front landing will be thrown over the bars. A rider with bent arms and loose shoulders can absorb the front landing and allow the front to compress without transmitting the full impact forward.

### Principle 4: Throttle on Landing Re-Establishes Drive and Prevents Nose-Over
As the front wheel contacts the landing surface, applying throttle drives the rear wheel and loads the rear of the bike — counteracting the forward pitch of the front landing. A rider who is off throttle at the front landing allows the front to compress fully while the rear has no drive — the bike can nose-over. A brief throttle application at the moment of front landing re-establishes rear-wheel load and keeps the bike balanced.

### Principle 5: Eyes on the Landing Zone — Not the Edge
A rider who watches the edge of the rock step as they approach will instinctively brake at the last second — removing the momentum needed for a controlled crossing. Eyes should be on the landing zone at the base of the step — planning where the front wheel will land — not on the lip of the step. This forward vision prevents the instinctive edge-braking mistake.

## 3. TECHNIQUE BREAKDOWN BY LEVEL

### 3a. Beginner Level
**Goal:** Descend small rock steps (20–30 cm) at very low speed with conscious weight positioning and controlled landing.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Rearward weight position set before approaching the edge
- Very low approach speed — walking pace or just above
- Both arms bent at front landing
- Throttle maintained or gently applied after landing

**What Good Looks Like:**
At walking pace, the rider approaches the step with weight rearward. The front drops off the edge. The rider's weight is back, preventing a nose-over. The front lands. The rider absorbs the landing with bent arms. The rear drops. Forward drive resumes. No panic.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Braking at the edge:** Rider sees the drop and brakes suddenly — removes momentum, bike tips nose-down at the edge. Fix: eyes on the landing, not the edge. Pre-determine the speed before the approach.
- **Weight forward at the drop moment:** Rider gets scared and leans into the drop — dramatically increases nose-down pitch. Fix: rearward weight must be pre-positioned.
- **Stiff arms on front landing:** Impact goes straight to the torso and the rider is thrown forward. Fix: elbows bent and loose throughout the drop.

### 3b. Intermediate Level
**Goal:** Descend rock steps of 30–60 cm at moderate speed — managing the front drop arc and landing cleanly with drive.

**Progression Markers from Beginner:** Rider can descend small steps without panic. Weight positioning is deliberate. Landing absorption is present.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Speed calibration: faster than beginner pace but controlled — not momentum-wild
- Body position sequence: weight rearward on approach, arms bent throughout, slight forward shift as rear drops
- Throttle timing: apply as front lands — not before (too early) and not after (too late)
- Reading the step before approaching: assessing height, landing surface, and exit

**What Good Looks Like:**
Approaching a 45 cm rock step at 10–15 km/h, rider's hips are rearward and low. Arms are bent. The front wheel drops off the edge, front forks extend fully before landing. The landing is absorbed through bent arms and leg flex. Throttle applied as front contacts the landing — rear wheel begins to drive. The rear drops off the step and the bike settles into forward drive.

**What the Rider Should Feel at Each Stage:**
- Approach (2–3 metres before edge): Weight shifts rearward — felt as the pressure moving from the front tyre toward the rear tyre. Arms remain bent but not braced. Eyes on the landing zone.
- Front drop: The front wheel goes over the edge and drops — felt as sudden weightlessness at the front of the bike, the bars becoming lighter. The bike is now pitching nose-down. The rearward weight is what prevents this pitch from becoming uncontrollable.
- Airborne phase: The bike is pitched down, front wheel in the air below the edge level. Felt as the body weight entirely on the rear pegs — the front is no longer a reference point. Arms extended but not rigid.
- Front landing: The front wheel makes contact with the lower surface. A sharp compression felt through the forks and up through the arms. Bent arms absorb — the elbows flex more deeply. Throttle applied at this exact moment.
- Rear drop: The rear wheel reaches the edge and drops. Felt as the rear pegs suddenly dropping — the bike levels out as both wheels are now on the lower surface.
- Exit: Both wheels on the lower surface. The bike is now driving forward at the lower level. Throttle maintains momentum.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Front slams:** Front landing is too hard — rider was slightly forward at impact. Fix: more rearward weight. If the front is slamming, weight is not far enough back.
- **Rear loops forward over front:** Too much speed — rear momentum carries it over the front on landing. Fix: slow down. For every drop height increment, the maximum safe speed is lower than it feels.
- **Loss of traction on landing:** Front or rear slides on the landing surface. Fix: assess landing surface traction before the drop. Wet rock on the landing is a serious risk. Reduce speed accordingly.

### 3c. Advanced Level
**Goal:** Descend large rock steps (60–80+ cm) at tempo — reading them from a distance and executing as part of a flowing descent.

**Progression Markers from Intermediate:** Rider can descend 50 cm steps cleanly at moderate speed. Landing is absorbed. Throttle timing is automatic.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Reading large rock steps and calibrating approach speed from 10+ metres
- Managing the extended airborne phase on larger drops — maintaining body position through longer front-down flight
- Landing on irregular surfaces — rocks, roots, angled terrain
- Combining rock step drop with immediate uphill or corner on the exit

**What Good Looks Like:**
On a steep technical descent with a series of rock steps, the rider drops each step at pace — weight rearward, front landing absorbed, throttle on, rear drops and drives — without breaking the rhythm of the descent. The steps are just features in the descent rather than events requiring special preparation.

**Failure Modes and Causes:**
- **Front slams and nose-over:** The most severe failure. Causes: weight forward, stiff arms, too much speed. Fix: all three must be corrected. This failure can cause serious crashes.
- **Rear loops forward:** Speed too high, rear momentum exceeds front landing resistance. Fix: slower approach. Assess the maximum speed for the specific drop height.
- **Loss of traction on landing surface:** Rock or wet surface under the front wheel on landing — front washes sideways. Fix: identify the landing surface before dropping. Wet rock requires much lower speed.

## 4. DIAGNOSTIC CUES (AI COACHING ENGINE REFERENCE)

### Visual Cues
- Correct: Rider weight clearly rearward on approach — hips back, arms extended toward bars
- Correct: Arms visibly bending on front landing — absorption visible
- Correct: Rear wheel drives off the edge under maintained or applied throttle
- Error: Rider body forward at the edge — leaning into the drop, nose-over risk
- Error: Arms stiff on landing — rider thrown forward noticeably
- Error: Brake applied at the edge — bike nose-dips immediately

### Audio Cues
- Correct: Engine note maintained or increasing through the landing — throttle applied
- Correct: Front suspension impact sound — brief, sharp, absorbed
- Error: Engine note cutting before landing — rider off throttle at wrong time
- Error: Heavy suspension bottoming sound — speed too high, landing too hard for absorption

### Sensor Cues
- Correct: Pitch angle increases (nose down) during the drop phase, then stabilises on landing
- Correct: Throttle position sensor shows application at or just before front wheel ground contact
- Error: Pitch angle continues increasing after landing — nose-over in progress
- Error: Abrupt deceleration at the edge — braking occurred at the worst moment

## 5. COACHING CUES & LANGUAGE

### Positive Reinforcement
- "Weight was back before the front went over — I could see your hips rearward on approach. That's what kept the nose from going too far."
- "Arms absorbed the landing — your elbows bent right on impact. Good landing technique."
- "Throttle was right on the landing — I heard the engine pick up as the front hit. That's what keeps the bike balanced."

### Corrective Feedback
- "You braked at the edge. That's the most common mistake on rock steps. Eyes on the landing, not the edge — your speed was already set. Trust it."
- "Your arms were straight on the front landing — all that impact went into your shoulders. Bent arms. Think of it like a push-up catch — you catch the fall with bent elbows."
- "Rear looped over on that one — too much speed for that height. Slow down 20% for the next attempt."
- "You were forward when the front dropped. It looked like you were going to go over. Rearward weight before the edge — not at it."

### Progression Prompts
- "You've got the 30 cm step clean. Try the 45 cm one — the principle is identical but your arms need to bend more on landing and your weight needs to be further back."
- "Next, read the landing surface before you commit. Walk to the base first. Know exactly where your front wheel will land and what the traction is like."
- "Try linking the drop into a corner on the exit. The landing is the approach to the corner — don't stop after landing, keep moving immediately."

## 6. DRILLS & PRACTICE EXERCISES

### Drill 1: Small Drop Familiarisation
**Purpose:** Build drop confidence and develop the rearward weight habit.
**Setup:** A 20–25 cm constructed drop (wooden ramp or natural step) with a clear flat landing.
**Execution:**
1. Approach at walking pace.
2. Rearward weight set before the edge.
3. Drop and absorb.
4. Throttle on landing.
**Success Criteria:** 10 consecutive drops with no front slam and no panic braking.
**Duration/Reps:** 20 drops.
**Progression:** Increase drop height to 35 cm.

### Drill 2: Speed Calibration
**Purpose:** Find the correct approach speed for a given drop height.
**Setup:** A 35 cm rock step or constructed drop.
**Execution:**
1. First pass: walking pace (too slow is safe).
2. Increase speed 3 km/h each pass.
3. Find the speed that produces the cleanest landing.
4. Note: too slow = awkward, too fast = slammy. The sweet spot is identifiable.
**Success Criteria:** Rider identifies and can consistently reproduce the optimal speed.
**Duration/Reps:** 10 passes at different speeds.
**Progression:** Apply to a different drop height.

### Drill 3: Arm Absorption Drill
**Purpose:** Develop the front landing arm absorption independently.
**Setup:** A small ramp producing a 5 cm drop — just enough to feel the front impact.
**Execution:**
1. Hit the ramp at 20 km/h.
2. As front lands, deliberately deepen elbow bend — simulate an exaggerated absorption.
3. Check: were the elbows softening or hardening on impact?
**Success Criteria:** Rider can identify the elbow softening sensation and reproduce it deliberately.
**Duration/Reps:** 20 passes.
**Progression:** Increase drop to 15 cm.

### Drill 4: Technical Rock Step Sequence
**Purpose:** Apply the technique to real rock steps in sequence.
**Setup:** A trail section with 3–4 rock steps of varying heights.
**Execution:**
1. Walk the section first — assess each drop height and landing surface.
2. Ride the section at moderate pace.
3. After each drop, assess: clean landing? Weight correct? Throttle timed?
**Success Criteria:** All drops in the section navigated cleanly at moderate pace.
**Duration/Reps:** 5 full section passes.
**Progression:** Increase approach speed.

## 8. TERRAIN & CONTEXT VARIATIONS

### Clean Rock Landing
Best case — predictable, grippy surface. Standard technique applies.

### Muddy or Wet Landing
Front traction on landing significantly reduced. Approach at much lower speed. Front wash risk on landing is high on wet rock.

### Angled Landing Surface
If the landing surface is at an angle relative to the bike's direction, the front wheel can deflect sideways on impact. Approach angle and landing surface angle must be compatible.

### Drop Into a Corner
The exit of the drop leads into a corner. Landing must be smooth enough to initiate cornering immediately. Speed management is critical — too fast through the drop = too fast into the corner.

### Night or Low Visibility
Landing zone assessment is harder. Light must illuminate the landing zone completely before committing. If landing zone is not visible, slow dramatically or walk the section.

## 9. EXPERT INSIGHTS & SOURCES

### Key Insights
- **The "commit early" principle:** Every experienced enduro coach emphasises that the rock step drop is committed to from the moment the approach speed is set — not at the edge. Mental commitment must precede physical commitment by 2–3 metres.
- **Graham Jarvis on drops:** Jarvis describes his approach to large drops as "look at where you want to land, then trust the bike to get you there." The mental target is the landing zone, not the drop itself.
- **The rearward weight is a habit, not a reaction:** Coaches who film riders descending rock steps report that rearward weight positioned as a conscious habit before the drop produces consistently better outcomes than rearward weight applied reactively during the drop.
- **Arm absorption training:** Some coaches use stationary handlebar drop exercises (controlled drops of a front wheel from a platform) to train the arm absorption muscle memory before applying it at speed.
