# Sand Whoops

## 1. OVERVIEW

Sand whoops are rhythmically spaced mounds of sand — typically 30–60 cm high and 1–3 metres apart — created by wind or the passage of many vehicles. They appear in desert enduro, dune environments, and sandy trail sections, and require a specific technique that differs significantly from rocky terrain or standard whoops. Sand whoops are softer and more compressible than hard-pack whoops, which means the suspension behaves differently and traction is low. The correct technique involves either absorbing each whoop individually (at low speed) or allowing the suspension to skip across the tops of the whoops at higher speed — the "skim" technique — maintaining momentum rather than bottoming out in each trough. Sand whoops are deeply fatiguing if ridden incorrectly and surprisingly efficient if the rhythm and speed are found.

## 2. CORE PRINCIPLES

### Principle 1: Speed Determines the Approach — Absorb vs Skim
At low speed, the bike drops into each trough — the rider must absorb each whoop individually. This is tiring and slow. At sufficient speed, the suspension cannot fully compress for each whoop — the bike begins to skip across the tops of the mounds rather than dropping into the troughs. This "skimming" is dramatically faster and much less tiring. Finding the minimum speed for skimming the specific whoop size is the first task.

### Principle 2: Throttle Consistency Maintains the Rhythm
In any whoop section, throttle variation disrupts the rhythm. Acceleration causes the bike to dig into one trough deeper; deceleration causes it to drop into a different trough. The most stable and consistent sand whoop riding uses a steady throttle that maintains the correct skimming speed. Throttle adjustments should be very gradual — not sharp.

### Principle 3: Body Decoupled from Bike in Standing Position
At skimming speed, the bike pitches rapidly up and down as the suspension contacts each whoop top. The rider's legs absorb this pitching — the body remains relatively still while the bike moves beneath. This is the same upper/lower body decoupling principle as high-speed log crossings (TECH-15) and hard-pack whoops (TECH-23), but in sand the pitching is slightly slower and the suspension compresses more deeply.

### Principle 4: Sitting Can Be Effective in Sand Whoops at Lower Speeds
Unlike hard-pack whoops where standing is almost always superior, sand whoops have a specific low-to-moderate speed range where sitting is effective — the softer surface absorbs impacts well enough that the seated position can manage the bouncing, and the sitting position allows the rider to use the seat as additional suspension and the lower CoM helps prevent the over-pitching that causes problems in standing at low speeds. Knowing when to sit vs stand is a sand-specific adaptation.

### Principle 5: Line Straightness Maximises Efficiency
Sand whoops are designed by nature or traffic in a relatively straight or consistent pattern. Riding straight through them — aligned with the whoop direction — allows the suspension to work rhythmically. Any deviation from straight (cornering in whoops) disrupts the rhythm, changes the effective whoop spacing, and can cause the bike to cross-load (hit each whoop at an angle), which multiplies the pitching forces dramatically.

## 3. TECHNIQUE BREAKDOWN BY LEVEL

### 3a. Beginner Level
**Goal:** Absorb sand whoops individually at low speed — maintaining balance and not getting thrown.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Standing position with bent knees
- Arms bent and loose — not rigid
- Maintained throttle — no chopping
- Eyes forward — not looking at each whoop

**What Good Looks Like:**
Through sand whoops at 20 km/h, the rider absorbs each impact through bent knees and loose arms. The bike bucks and pitches but the rider stays on. No falls. Speed is maintained.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Sitting through sand whoops:** Gets bounced progressively worse. Fix: stand. Legs absorb what the seat cannot.
- **Throttle chop in whoops:** Speed variation disrupts rhythm and increases bouncing. Fix: steady throttle.
- **Tense arms:** Each whoop impact is transmitted directly to the torso. Fix: bent elbows, loose shoulders.

### 3b. Intermediate Level
**Goal:** Find the skimming speed for the specific whoop size and ride efficiently at that speed.

**Progression Markers from Beginner:** Rider can absorb whoops at low speed without falling. Standing position established.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Finding the skimming threshold speed by gradually increasing speed through a whoop section
- Recognising the transition from absorbing to skimming — the bike feels smoother at higher speed
- Consistent throttle at skimming speed
- Body decoupling — torso still, legs absorbing rapid movements

**What Good Looks Like:**
At 40 km/h through sand whoops, the bike skips from top to top with only partial trough contact. The rider's legs move rhythmically with the suspension — knees rising and falling with each whoop top. The torso is level. Speed is maintained. The section is significantly less tiring than at 20 km/h.

**What the Rider Should Feel at Each Stage:**
- Entry to whoop section: Speed builds — the rider commits to the approach speed rather than entering too slowly.
- Low-speed whoops (absorbing): Each whoop drops into a trough — felt as a significant up-down pitch. The legs work hard. This is the range to exit by increasing speed.
- Transition (finding skim speed): As speed increases, the pitching smooths out. The troughs feel shallower. The suspension is topping out between hits. This is the skim threshold. Felt as a sudden smoothing — "it clicked."
- Skimming: The bike pitches more rapidly but less deeply. Each whoop is a brief contact on the top — felt as a compression spike, then rebound, then the next compression before the previous rebound is complete. Legs move quickly, small range.
- Exit: Speed maintained through the last whoop. Clear terrain on the other side. Speed and position reset.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Under-speed (never finding skim):** Rider is too cautious to reach skim speed — absorbing at medium speed is the worst outcome (not slow enough to be manageable, not fast enough to skim). Fix: increase speed decisively. Commitment is required to find the skim threshold.
- **Over-throttle ejection:** At very high speed in sand whoops, the bike can launch off a whoop top and become uncontrolled. Fix: the skim speed is not maximum throttle — it is the minimum speed for skimming. Do not exceed it significantly in sand.
- **Out of rhythm:** The rider bounces out of sync with the whoops — each whoop hits at the wrong point in the suspension cycle. Fix: steady throttle returns the rhythm. Throttle variation disrupts it.

### 3c. Advanced Level
**Goal:** Navigate sand whoops at maximum efficient speed — choosing sit vs stand, adapting to varying whoop shapes, cornering out of the whoop section.

**Progression Markers from Intermediate:** Rider can skim sand whoops consistently. Rhythm is maintained. Not tiring quickly.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Sit vs stand decision based on speed and whoop size
- Adapting to irregular whoops (some bigger, some smaller)
- Cornering at the exit of a whoop section — managing direction change while whoops are still active
- Sand whoop fatigue management: when to sit briefly to recover arms and legs

**What Good Looks Like:**
On a desert enduro stage with 200 metres of sand whoops, the rider selects skimming speed at entry, maintains steady throttle throughout, sits briefly in a larger trough to recover position, then resumes standing for the final 50 metres. The exit is a 90-degree corner — the rider begins to modulate speed at the last 5 whoops before the corner. Total time through the section is 40% faster than an absorb-speed approach.

**Failure Modes and Causes:**
- **Getting out of rhythm:** A single throttle variation or unexpected large whoop disrupts the suspension timing — the bike starts bottoming in troughs. Fix: steady throttle or accept the slower absorb technique until rhythm is re-established.
- **Front diving into whoop:** At higher speed if the front wheel dips into a trough rather than topping the next crest — the front dives and the bars wash. Fix: slightly more rearward weight. The front must be light enough to skim.
- **Over-jumping a whoop:** At very high speed, a whoop can launch the bike airborne. Landing back into a trough at full speed can be very hard. Fix: speed management — skim speed, not launch speed.

## 4. DIAGNOSTIC CUES (AI COACHING ENGINE REFERENCE)

### Visual Cues
- Correct: Bike skipping across whoop tops — barely contacting troughs
- Correct: Rider legs absorbing rapidly — knees rising and falling with whoop rhythm
- Correct: Rider torso relatively still — upper body decoupled from bike movement
- Error: Bike bottoming into each trough — speed too low for skimming
- Error: Rider being bounced significantly — body not decoupled
- Error: Speed oscillation visible — throttle variations disrupting rhythm

### Audio Cues
- Correct: Consistent engine note — steady throttle maintained
- Correct: Rapid but consistent suspension contact sounds — regular whoop rhythm
- Error: Engine note varying — throttle changes in the section
- Error: Hard suspension bottoming sounds — bike dropping into troughs

### Sensor Cues
- Correct: Rapid, consistent vertical acceleration pulses — whoop rhythm maintained
- Correct: Forward speed consistent — minimal variation through whoops
- Error: Deep vertical acceleration spikes — bike bottoming in troughs
- Error: Speed oscillation — throttle management poor

## 5. COACHING CUES & LANGUAGE

### Positive Reinforcement
- "You found the skim speed — I could see the bike topping the mounds instead of dropping into the troughs. Much smoother, much faster."
- "Legs were moving with the whoops but your torso stayed level. That's decoupled riding. That's the technique."
- "Consistent throttle the whole way through. No bumps, no chops. The rhythm held because you held the throttle."

### Corrective Feedback
- "Too slow — you're absorbing each whoop individually. Increase speed by 10 km/h and see if the bike smooths out. You need to find the skim threshold."
- "Throttle variation is destroying your rhythm. Pick a throttle position and hold it through the entire whoop section. No adjustments inside."
- "You're sitting too early — at this speed the bouncing is going to get worse seated. Stand up and let your legs do the work."
- "Arms are straight and stiff — each whoop is going straight to your shoulders. Bent elbows. Think of your arms as additional suspension."

### Progression Prompts
- "You've got the skim. Now work on the entry transition — can you build to skim speed faster? The first few whoops should not be absorb-speed whoops."
- "Try the varied section — the whoops are different sizes. You'll need to adapt the throttle slightly for bigger whoops without losing overall rhythm."
- "Can you corner immediately after the last whoop? The challenge is managing direction change while still in whoop mode mentally."

## 6. DRILLS & PRACTICE EXERCISES

### Drill 1: Speed Threshold Finder
**Purpose:** Identify the specific skim threshold for a known whoop section.
**Setup:** A consistent sand whoop section.
**Execution:**
1. Pass at 20 km/h — absorbing.
2. Pass at 30 km/h.
3. Pass at 40 km/h.
4. Pass at 50 km/h.
5. Identify at what speed the transition from absorb to skim occurred.
**Success Criteria:** Rider can identify and articulate the skim threshold speed.
**Duration/Reps:** 4 speed-escalation passes.
**Progression:** Find threshold on different whoop sizes.

### Drill 2: Throttle Hold
**Purpose:** Build the consistent throttle habit for whoop sections.
**Setup:** A sand whoop section.
**Execution:**
1. Set throttle at a fixed position before entering.
2. Do not adjust throttle until completely through the section.
3. Note: did rhythm hold? Was the exit speed appropriate?
**Success Criteria:** Fixed throttle produces consistent rhythm through the section.
**Duration/Reps:** 10 passes.
**Progression:** Vary the initial throttle set point.

### Drill 3: Stand vs Sit Comparison
**Purpose:** Experience the difference between standing and sitting in sand whoops at the same speed.
**Setup:** A moderate sand whoop section.
**Execution:**
1. First 5 passes: standing. Note comfort, fatigue, control.
2. Next 5 passes: sitting. Same speed. Note differences.
3. Identify which technique is better for this speed and whoop size.
**Success Criteria:** Rider can articulate clearly which technique is superior for the given conditions and why.
**Duration/Reps:** 10 passes.
**Progression:** Vary speed and repeat the comparison.

### Drill 4: Extended Whoop Endurance
**Purpose:** Build the physical and mental endurance for sustained whoop riding.
**Setup:** A sand whoop section of 100+ metres.
**Execution:**
1. Enter at skim speed.
2. Maintain for the full section length.
3. Note: where did fatigue first appear? Arms? Legs?
**Success Criteria:** Full section completed at skim speed without stopping or significant technique breakdown.
**Duration/Reps:** 5 full passes.
**Progression:** Longer section. Higher skim speed.

## 8. TERRAIN & CONTEXT VARIATIONS

### Deep Loose Sand
Maximum absorption required — the whoops are soft and the troughs may be very deep. Skim speed may be higher than expected because the soft surface reduces the impact of each contact.

### Wet Sand
Firmer surface — the whoops behave more like hard-pack. Skim threshold is lower. Rhythm is easier to find but transitions to hard-pack-like firmness if the sand is very wet.

### Variable Size Whoops
Real desert terrain has inconsistent whoop sizes. The rider must adapt — throttle very slightly for larger whoops, maintain for smaller. The principle is the same but execution requires more sensitivity.

### Sand Whoops Into a Corner
The most demanding application — exiting a whoop section into a corner requires speed reduction while the suspension is still in whoop mode. The braking must be very gradual — abrupt braking in whoops can cause the bike to bottom out violently.

### Night Riding
Sand whoops are particularly challenging at night — depth and rhythm are harder to read without good lighting. Reduce speed and accept absorb technique rather than attempting skim in poor visibility.

## 9. EXPERT INSIGHTS & SOURCES

### Key Insights
- **Dakar and desert enduro:** Sand whoops are a defining challenge in desert racing. The fastest riders consistently use skim technique — the time savings over absorb technique in a 200-metre whoop section are significant.
- **The "click" moment:** Riders who master sand whoops almost universally describe a specific moment when skim speed was first found and everything "clicked" — the ride suddenly became smoother at higher speed. Coaching for this moment involves building confidence to increase speed past the uncomfortable medium range.
- **Fatigue management:** Extended sand whoop riding is disproportionately tiring even with correct technique. Elite desert enduro riders train specifically for whoop endurance. Sit/stand alternation in whoops (where the whoops allow it) is a recovery strategy.
- **Suspension setup:** Sand whoops expose suspension setup flaws more clearly than almost any other terrain. Too stiff suspension causes the bike to bounce excessively. Too soft causes excessive bottoming. Riders who spend time in sand whoops should discuss suspension setup with a tuner.
