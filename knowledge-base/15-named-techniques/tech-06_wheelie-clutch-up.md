# Wheelie (Clutch-Up)

## 1. OVERVIEW

The clutch-up wheelie is the technique of using a sharp clutch release combined with engine torque to lift the front wheel — producing a controlled, purpose-driven front wheel lift for crossing obstacles, clearing steep entry lips, or maintaining momentum on technical terrain. Unlike the manual front wheel lift (which uses body mechanics only), the clutch-up uses drivetrain torque to generate the lift — making it more powerful, more applicable at lower approach speeds, and capable of lifting the front wheel higher with less athletic effort. In enduro riding the clutch-up is not a showmanship move — it is a functional tool used constantly in technical terrain to lift the front over rocks, logs, ledges, and rutted entries. The difference between a tidy wheelie and a dangerous one is gear selection, throttle depth, balance point awareness, and rear brake control.

## 2. CORE PRINCIPLES

### Principle 1: Torque Spike Creates the Lift
The clutch-up works because the drivetrain torque spike (see TECH-02: Clutch Pop) loads the rear wheel and pitches the bike's chassis rearward around the rear axle. When the torque exceeds the weight transfer toward the front, the front wheel lifts. The lift height is determined by the magnitude of the torque spike — which is controlled by gear selection, throttle pre-load level, and clutch release speed.

### Principle 2: Second Gear Is the Standard Starting Point
First gear multiplies torque so aggressively that the front wheel can easily loop to vertical and beyond — too much for controlled use in most technical situations. Second gear provides sufficient torque for a useful front wheel lift while keeping the rate of lift predictable and manageable. Third gear may be used at higher speeds where the engine is already spinning faster. Always assess terrain speed and gear before attempting.

### Principle 3: Balance Point Is the Apex of the Wheelie
As the front wheel rises, there is a point — called the balance point — where the bike is perfectly balanced on the rear wheel with the front wheel at approximately 30–40 degrees from horizontal. At this point, no throttle input is needed to maintain height; the bike is stable. Below the balance point, the front wants to come back down. Above it, the bike wants to continue rotating toward loop-out. Training for the balance point awareness is the central skill of wheelie control.

### Principle 4: Rear Brake Is the Safety Mechanism
If the front wheel rises past the balance point, the rear brake — applied with one finger — immediately transfers weight forward by creating drag on the rear wheel, lowering the front. The rear brake is not the same as the throttle — it is more precise and more immediate for height correction at the balance point. Every wheelie attempted without a ready rear brake finger is a riskier wheelie.

### Principle 5: Throttle Holds the Wheelie; Rear Brake Lowers It
Once the front is in the air, throttle maintains height and speed. Increasing throttle increases height. The rear brake lowers height and can bring the wheel down at the appropriate moment. In an obstacle-crossing wheelie, the throttle is held as the front clears the obstacle, and then both the front is allowed to come down (throttle hold or slight reduction) and the rear is driven over (throttle maintained). This two-phase approach — lift and drive — is the fundamental obstacle wheelie sequence.

## 3. TECHNIQUE BREAKDOWN BY LEVEL

### 3a. Beginner Level
**Goal:** Understand the clutch-up mechanism and produce small, controlled front lifts without approaching the balance point.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- 2nd gear selection before any attempt
- Moderate throttle pre-load (not full throttle)
- Quick but not violent clutch release
- Rear brake finger always in position
- Target: 10–15 cm front wheel height maximum

**What Good Looks Like:**
On flat ground, rider in 2nd gear executes a clutch-up that lifts the front wheel 10–15 cm and holds it for 1 second before settling. No panic. Rear brake finger available. The bike does not loop — it simply lifts and comes back down.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **First gear selection:** Lift is violent, immediate loop risk. → Always 2nd gear until technique is established.
- **Too much throttle pre-load:** Front comes up aggressively — hard to control. → Start with moderate throttle (not full open). Build pre-load gradually across sessions.
- **No rear brake readiness:** If the front comes up unexpectedly high, rider has no safety response. → One finger on rear brake before every attempt.
- **Releasing throttle abruptly when front lifts:** Front crashes down hard. → Smooth transition — reduce throttle gradually, not suddenly.

### 3b. Intermediate Level
**Goal:** Use the clutch-up wheelie to cross real obstacles — calibrating lift height and timing to specific terrain features.

**Progression Markers from Beginner:** Rider can produce consistent front wheel lifts on flat ground. Does not loop. Rear brake awareness is instinctive. Comfortable holding the front up for 1–2 seconds.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Timing the clutch pop to place the front wheel at the correct height at the correct moment relative to the obstacle
- Two-phase obstacle crossing: lift phase and drive phase
- Reading obstacle height and calibrating pre-load accordingly
- Front wheel placement accuracy on the obstacle

**What Good Looks Like:**
Approaching a 30 cm log at 20 km/h in 2nd gear, the rider initiates the clutch-up 1 metre from the log — the front wheel rises to 35 cm (just clearing the log) and is placed on the far side. The rear wheel then drives over the log under maintained throttle. The crossing is smooth and forward speed is maintained.

**What the Rider Should Feel at Each Stage:**
- Clutch slip initiation: RPM rises freely as engine load drops off. Felt as the bars becoming slightly lighter.
- Throttle pre-load hold: Engine revving at 4,000–5,000 RPM against the slip. Slight forward pressure from the rear tyre as it receives partial drive.
- Clutch pop: Fingers snap off the lever — immediate rear traction spike. Felt as a sharp forward push through the hips and a simultaneous lightening of the front wheel.
- Front wheel rise: The bike pitches rearward at the front. Bars become weightless. The rider feels the weight distribution shifting entirely to the rear pegs.
- Balance point approach: The front wheel is at 30–40 degrees. The rearward pitch slows. The balance point feels like a moment of stillness — the bike wants to sit here.
- Height correction with rear brake: A light tap on the rear brake felt as a slight deceleration and immediate forward weight shift — front begins to lower.
- Front landing: Front wheel drops onto the obstacle or far side. Felt as a controlled forward weight return.
- Drive-through: Throttle maintained, rear wheel approaches the obstacle. Rear contacts it — slight rearward pitch — then drives over.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Looping out:** Front goes past balance point and bike flips rearward. Cause: too much throttle pre-load, too fast clutch release, or both. Fix: reduce pre-load. Use rear brake immediately when front passes balance point.
- **Insufficient lift height:** Front hits the obstacle and bounces back. Cause: under-powered pop, wrong timing, or approach speed too low. Fix: increase pre-load slightly or time the pop earlier.
- **Front crashes back down hard:** Rider reduces throttle too quickly after pop — front drops fast. Fix: hold throttle steady after the pop — do not reduce until the front is at the correct height.

### 3c. Advanced Level
**Goal:** Execute clutch-up wheelies as part of a fluid technical rhythm — linking multiple lifts, adapting to terrain variation, and riding at balance point for ledge steps and longer crossings.

**Progression Markers from Intermediate:** Rider can cross logs and obstacles consistently. Lift height is calibrated. Rear brake use is instinctive. No longer thinks about the sequence consciously.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Riding the balance point for a sustained period — maintaining it for 2–3 metres to clear longer obstacles
- Clutch-up on uneven terrain — initiating on a slight slope or loose surface
- Combining clutch-up with body flick for increased lift height
- Using the wheelie to navigate steep ledge entries (TECH-17)

**What Good Looks Like:**
Approaching a steep rock ledge entry, the rider executes a precisely timed clutch-up that places the front wheel exactly on the ledge surface — neither too short (front hits the ledge face) nor too long (front overshoots the landing zone). The rear drives up immediately. The ledge is crossed without the front wheel contacting the vertical face.

**Failure Modes and Causes:**
- **Looping out on ledge entry:** Gradient adds to the pitch force — the wheelie is more aggressive on uphill entries. Fix: reduce pre-load on uphill attempts. The slope amplifies the torque spike effect.
- **Front crashes back down into ledge face:** Lift height calibration off — front rises but not high enough. Fix: more pre-load or earlier timing.
- **Rear wheels lose traction on drive-through:** Not enough throttle during the drive phase. Fix: maintain or increase throttle as the rear approaches the obstacle.

## 4. DIAGNOSTIC CUES (AI COACHING ENGINE REFERENCE)

### Visual Cues
- Correct: Front wheel rises to obstacle height or slightly above — appropriate calibration
- Correct: Rear brake light briefly illuminates (if visible) during height correction — technique safety component active
- Correct: Front wheel placed accurately on the obstacle landing zone — not random
- Error: Front wheel rises dramatically past 45 degrees and bike pitches violently rearward — loop-out event
- Error: Front wheel lifts then immediately drops back — throttle reduced too soon, pop too weak
- Error: Clutch lever being pulled during the "ride" phase — rider still slipping when they should be driving

### Audio Cues
- Correct: Clear RPM rise (clutch slip), then RPM drop (engagement), then steady engine note — the clean three-phase sound of a clutch-up
- Error: Extended RPM flare after clutch release — rider held too much throttle, over-revved on engagement
- Error: Engine bog after pop — pre-load too low, engine loaded down immediately on engagement

### Sensor Cues
- Correct: Front wheel speed drops to zero at lift, returns on landing — clean lift confirmed
- Error: Rear wheel deceleration spike on engagement — over-pop with engine braking
- Error: Front wheel speed never reaches zero — lift did not fully unweight front, more pre-load needed

## 5. COACHING CUES & LANGUAGE

### Positive Reinforcement
- "That's the height — front was right at obstacle level and you placed it perfectly. That's what we're building to."
- "I can see your rear brake finger was ready the whole time. That's safe technique. Keep that habit."
- "Clean three-phase sound — slip, pop, hold. Your engine told me that was right."

### Corrective Feedback
- "You're in first gear — I can hear it. That's why the lift is so violent. Go up to second, same pre-load, and it'll be half as aggressive."
- "Your front went straight back down — you pulled the throttle off as soon as it lifted. Hold the throttle after the pop. The lift needs sustained power."
- "The front went too high — pre-load was too deep. Roll the throttle back slightly before you pop. The lift should feel deliberate, not alarming."
- "Where's your rear brake finger? If you're not touching the rear brake lever, you have no safety net. One finger on it before every attempt."

### Progression Prompts
- "You've got the flat-ground wheelie sorted. Now try it approaching that log. Same pop, same timing — the only difference is there's an obstacle to clear."
- "Next session, try holding the front wheel up for two metres after the pop. Find the balance point. Just hold steady throttle and feel where the bike wants to sit."
- "Try combining the wheelie with a hip flick. As you pop the clutch, push your hips back simultaneously. You'll get more height with less throttle pre-load."

## 6. DRILLS & PRACTICE EXERCISES

### Drill 1: Flat Ground Balance Point Finder
**Purpose:** Find and hold the balance point safely before applying the technique to obstacles.
**Setup:** Flat open area, 2nd gear. Rear brake finger positioned.
**Execution:**
1. Ride at 20 km/h.
2. Execute a clutch-up with moderate pre-load.
3. As front rises, reduce throttle slightly to hold at 30 degrees.
4. Try to hold that angle for 1 second using throttle and rear brake.
5. Let front come down gently.
**Success Criteria:** Front held at 20–40 degrees for 1+ second on 5 out of 10 attempts without loop.
**Duration/Reps:** 15 attempts, 20 minutes.
**Progression:** Hold for 2 seconds. Then try walking pace wheelies (very slow balance point work).

### Drill 2: Height Calibration Gates
**Purpose:** Train precise throttle pre-load for specific obstacle heights.
**Setup:** Three marked targets on flat ground — a cone at 20 cm, 30 cm, and 40 cm height.
**Execution:**
1. Execute a clutch-up aimed at the 20 cm target — front wheel should not rise higher.
2. Evaluate: too high, too low, or correct?
3. Adjust pre-load and repeat for each target height.
**Success Criteria:** Rider can produce front wheel lifts consistently within 5 cm of target height for each target.
**Duration/Reps:** 10 attempts per height target, 30 minutes.
**Progression:** Add a horizontal gate — front wheel must pass within 5 cm of the target height AND within a 1 m window.

### Drill 3: Log Obstacle Wheelie
**Purpose:** Apply the clutch-up to a real obstacle crossing.
**Setup:** 15–25 cm diameter log on flat terrain.
**Execution:**
1. Approach at 15–20 km/h in 2nd gear.
2. Initiate pop 1 metre from log.
3. Front wheel clears and lands on far side.
4. Drive rear over with maintained throttle.
**Success Criteria:** Clean front wheel clearance and smooth rear drive-through on 7 out of 10 passes.
**Duration/Reps:** 20 passes per session.
**Progression:** Increase log height to 35 cm. Then try at higher approach speed.

### Drill 4: Ledge Placement Wheelie
**Purpose:** Develop precision for ledge-type obstacles where front placement on the ledge surface is critical.
**Setup:** A wooden platform or natural ledge 25–35 cm high, 40 cm deep.
**Execution:**
1. Approach at 15 km/h.
2. Execute clutch-up timed to place front wheel ON the ledge surface — not over it.
3. Front wheel must land on the ledge flat, not on the edge.
4. Drive rear up immediately.
**Success Criteria:** Front lands on the ledge surface (not the vertical face) on 6 out of 10 attempts.
**Duration/Reps:** 15 attempts per session.
**Progression:** Increase ledge height. Then try with a slight uphill approach angle.

## 8. TERRAIN & CONTEXT VARIATIONS

### Flat Hard-Pack
Standard learning environment — predictable, consistent traction. Most pre-load and timing calibration should happen here.

### Uphill Approach to Ledge
The slope amplifies the torque spike and adds to the front-lift angle. Reduce throttle pre-load on uphill approaches — less is needed for the same lift height. Loop risk is higher on uphill clutch-ups.

### Downhill Entry
The downhill gradient reduces the effective pitch force — more pre-load may be needed. The front also wants to dive on downhill approach, so timing must be earlier.

### Mud and Loose Terrain
Rear wheel traction is lower — the clutch pop may spin the rear rather than lifting the front. Use in a higher gear or reduce pre-load. The front may not lift as cleanly if the rear spins on engagement.

### Rocks
Rear wheel contact with a rock at the pop moment changes the torque dynamics — if the rear hits a rock as the clutch releases, the lift can be more aggressive than intended. Factor in terrain texture under the rear wheel when planning the timing.

## 9. EXPERT INSIGHTS & SOURCES

### Key Insights
- **Jonny Walker obstacle technique:** In competition footage, Walker uses clutch-up wheelies for virtually all log and ledge crossings — the technique is a continuous part of his technical riding rhythm, not a special move deployed occasionally.
- **Balance point as a skill stage:** Elite coaches note that most riders who attempt clutch-up wheelies never find the balance point — they either loop or drop the front immediately. Finding the balance point is the milestone that unlocks the full potential of the technique.
- **Two-stroke vs four-stroke:** Two-stroke clutch-ups are more violent due to the abrupt power delivery curve. Four-stroke clutch-ups are more progressive. The same pre-load on a 2T will produce a higher lift than on a 4T — riders switching must recalibrate.
- **The rear brake habit:** Jimmy Lewis consistently coaches that the rear brake readiness for wheelie control is not a skill — it is a mandatory habit. Attempting wheelies without the finger positioned is considered poor form regardless of skill level.
