# Brake Slide

## 1. OVERVIEW

The brake slide is a controlled rear-wheel lock initiated with the rear brake to slide the rear of the bike into or through a corner — using the locked-wheel slide to rotate the bike and scrub speed simultaneously, rather than braking and then cornering as separate actions. Unlike rear wheel steer (which uses throttle to break traction), the brake slide uses rear brake pressure to lock or near-lock the rear wheel, causing it to slide sideways as the bike continues forward. This technique is used for tight corners on loose or hard surfaces where a conventional braking-and-cornering approach would either run wide or require such low entry speed that momentum is killed. It is also used as a stopping slide (TECH-40) and as a setup move for pivot turns. The brake slide is one of the most recognisable skills in enduro — it is also one of the most misunderstood, because it looks like aggression but requires precise calibration.

## 2. CORE PRINCIPLES

### Principle 1: A Locked Rear Wheel Cannot Steer — But It Can Rotate
When the rear wheel locks under braking, it loses lateral traction because a sliding surface develops less friction than a rolling one. This means the rear can slide sideways — but it cannot provide steering force. The bike rotates around the front wheel contact point. This rotation is what makes the brake slide a direction-change tool: the rear goes wide, the nose tracks toward the exit.

### Principle 2: The Front Wheel Provides the Directional Authority
During a brake slide, the front wheel remains rolling (or near-rolling) and continues to grip. It is the front wheel's steering angle that determines the exit direction. If the front wheel is not pointed toward the exit when the rear slides, the bike will spin uncontrollably rather than rotate toward the intended line. Front wheel direction is the steering input — the brake slide just allows the rear to follow a different arc.

### Principle 3: Slide Arc Length Is Controlled by Brake Pressure Duration
The longer the rear brake is held, the further the rear slides. Releasing the brake earlier produces a shorter slide arc. Holding it longer produces a longer arc — potentially rotating the bike through 90+ degrees. Advanced riders control slide arc length precisely by modulating rear brake pressure rather than a single lock-and-release event.

### Principle 4: Body Position Inside the Corner During the Slide
During a brake slide, the rider moves body weight to the inside of the corner — the inside foot forward, knee tracking the corner, body weight over the inside peg. This lowered inside body position keeps the bike more upright during the slide, maintaining traction on the front tyre and preventing the bike from falling over at the inside. A rider who sits bolt upright during a brake slide is much more likely to lose the front.

### Principle 5: Traction Reapplication Must Be Progressive
When the rear brake is released, the rear tyre regains traction. If the bike is still sliding sideways at the moment of traction return and the throttle is applied abruptly, the sudden traction can cause a high-side (the rear grips and the bike snaps upright violently). Traction reapplication must be progressive — rear brake released gradually, bike aligned with the intended direction, then throttle applied smoothly.

## 3. TECHNIQUE BREAKDOWN BY LEVEL

### 3a. Beginner Level
**Goal:** Understand controlled rear wheel lock on a safe surface and recognise the difference between a slide and a skid-out.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Experiencing rear wheel lock in a straight line — just locking and releasing
- Keeping the front wheel rolling during rear lock
- Maintaining body position and not panicking during a slide
- Recognising when traction returns and releasing brake smoothly

**What Good Looks Like:**
On a loose gravel surface, rider locks the rear wheel in a straight line, holds it for 2 metres, and releases cleanly. The rear tracks straight (not sideways) because the bike is still going straight. Rider does not panic. Traction returns smoothly.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Simultaneously locking front and rear:** Both wheels locked = no steering, no control. → Rear only. Front brake off during the slide.
- **Releasing the rear brake abruptly on traction return:** High-side snap. → Gradual release, always.
- **Attempting corner slides before straight-line slides:** Cornering adds complexity. Build straight-line comfort first.

### 3b. Intermediate Level
**Goal:** Execute a controlled brake slide into a corner on loose terrain — sliding the rear wide and rotating toward the exit.

**Progression Markers from Beginner:** Rider can lock the rear in a straight line and release cleanly. No panic during a slide. Front wheel maintained as rolling.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Initiating the rear lock at the right moment — corner entry, not mid-corner
- Body position moving inside simultaneously with brake application
- Front wheel steered toward the exit during the slide
- Slide arc matched to the corner radius — not too much, not too little
- Release timing and progressive traction reapplication

**What Good Looks Like:**
Approaching a left-hand bend on a loose dirt trail at 30 km/h, rider applies rear brake firmly at corner entry. Rear slides out to the right. Body weight moves inside (left). Front wheel points toward the left exit. As the rotation is complete, rear brake is released progressively. Rear regains traction. Throttle applied for exit drive. The corner was navigated with higher entry speed and tighter exit line than conventional technique.

**What the Rider Should Feel at Each Stage:**
- Rear brake application: Rear tyre traction breaks — felt as the rear starting to push sideways, the back of the bike wants to overtake the front. Pegs shift laterally.
- Body weight inside shift: Inside peg loads — felt as weight transferring from central to the inside footpeg. Knee drops toward the inside.
- Slide arc: The rear is moving sideways and the bike is rotating. The rotation is felt as the nose tracking around toward the exit. The front wheel feels like a pivot point — weight through the bars as it holds the line.
- Slide hold: Maintained rear brake. The slide holds at a consistent arc. If brake pressure increases, arc deepens. If it decreases, the rear begins to regain traction.
- Release: Rear brake released progressively — felt as the rear wheel regaining grip, the lateral movement stopping. The bike re-aligns with the direction of travel.
- Throttle reapplication: As the rear aligns, throttle opens. Felt as the familiar drive push from the rear, now in the exit direction.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **High-side on traction return:** Rear brake released too quickly while rear is still sideways — rear snaps back and bike pitches violently. Fix: release gradually, ensure bike is roughly aligned before releasing.
- **Over-rotation:** Rear slides too far — bike points backward from intended direction. Fix: shorter brake application, or wider corner radius.
- **Front wash during slide:** Front tyre loses grip while rear is sliding — front collapses, low-side fall. Fix: keep front rolling, front brake off, body inside to maintain front tyre load.

### 3c. Advanced Level
**Goal:** Execute brake slides as a standard cornering tool — calibrated for arc length, exit line, and immediate exit acceleration.

**Progression Markers from Intermediate:** Rider can execute brake slides into corners consistently. Rotation is controlled. Exit line is predictable.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Executing brake slides on hard-pack (shorter, snappier slides with higher traction return risk)
- Brake slides on bermed corners — using the slide to avoid climbing the berm
- Combining brake slide with immediate rear wheel steer on exit
- Managing slides in sequence on multiple-corner descents

**What Good Looks Like:**
On a fast descent with two consecutive tight corners, rider executes a brake slide into corner 1, exits with throttle, immediately transitions into a slide entry for corner 2. The pair of corners is navigated at near-full speed with controlled slides providing all the direction changes. No conventional braking is visible.

**Failure Modes and Causes:**
- **High-side from traction recovery mid-slide:** Throttle applied while rear is still significantly sideways — rear grips and the energy ejects the rider. Fix: never throttle while the rear is still sliding significantly sideways.
- **Over-sliding past stop point:** In TECH-40 (brake-slide to stop), sliding too far past the intended stop. Fix: read the terrain and the arc more precisely.
- **Uncontrolled yaw:** The rear slides further than the intended rotation — bike keeps rotating past the exit direction. Fix: less brake pressure, shorter application.

## 4. DIAGNOSTIC CUES (AI COACHING ENGINE REFERENCE)

### Visual Cues
- Correct: Rear tyre leaves a clear skid mark showing controlled arc — not a straight line and not an uncontrolled spiral
- Correct: Body inside the corner — rider visibly leaning toward the inside of the turn
- Correct: Front wheel rolling and aimed toward the exit direction during slide
- Error: Both front and rear skid marks visible — front brake applied during slide, high crash risk
- Error: Rider body upright or leaning outside during slide — poor weight positioning
- Error: Rear slide extending past the exit line and continuing — over-committed slide

### Audio Cues
- Correct: Rear tyre scrubbing sound — consistent, not erratic — controlled slide
- Correct: Engine note dropping (rear brake applied, not throttle) — correct technique confirmed
- Error: Front tyre scrubbing sound during rear slide — front brake engaged, dangerous combination
- Error: Sudden tyre squeal and abrupt engine note increase — traction return snap, high-side risk

### Sensor Cues
- Correct: Rear wheel speed drops to near-zero during slide — confirmed lock
- Correct: Yaw rate data shows controlled rotation matching corner radius
- Error: Abrupt yaw rate spike at traction return — rear snapped back, high-side event beginning
- Error: Front wheel speed also drops during slide — front brake engaged, loss of steering authority

## 5. COACHING CUES & LANGUAGE

### Positive Reinforcement
- "I can see the skid mark arc — that was a controlled slide, not a chaotic one. The arc matched the corner. That's technique, not luck."
- "Your body was inside the whole time. That's what kept the front in the corner. Exactly right."
- "Clean traction return — you released progressively and the rear settled beautifully. No snap."

### Corrective Feedback
- "You went high-side risk on that one — you released the rear brake too sharply while it was still sideways. Release it the same way you applied it. Gradually."
- "Your body was upright during the slide — that's loading the wrong wheel. Inside. Move your weight to the inside of the corner before the brake even goes on."
- "The rear went too far — you held the brake too long. The arc should match the corner. Release the brake when the rotation is complete, not after."
- "Front and rear lock together — I can see both skid marks. Front brake off during the slide. Front is your steering. Protect it."

### Progression Prompts
- "You've got it on loose dirt. Now try the same corner on the firmer surface on the other side of the trail. The slide will be shorter and sharper — you need to release sooner."
- "Next step is going into the slide with more entry speed. The higher speed gives you more rotation for less arc. Try it."
- "Try linking two brake slides — corner 1 into corner 2 without stopping between. The exit of the first becomes the entry of the second."

## 6. DRILLS & PRACTICE EXERCISES

### Drill 1: Straight-Line Rear Lock
**Purpose:** Build rear brake lock comfort before adding cornering complexity.
**Setup:** Loose gravel or dirt, straight section.
**Execution:**
1. Ride at 30 km/h.
2. Apply rear brake firmly — lock the rear wheel.
3. Hold locked for 3 metres.
4. Release progressively.
5. Repeat, increasing lock intensity each time.
**Success Criteria:** Rear locks cleanly and releases cleanly on 8 out of 10 attempts. No front brake applied.
**Duration/Reps:** 20 attempts.
**Progression:** Apply at increasing speeds. Then apply on a slight curve.

### Drill 2: Controlled Arc Slide
**Purpose:** Execute a rear brake slide around a marked arc — matching the arc to the marker.
**Setup:** A marked circle on loose ground, approximately 10 metres diameter.
**Execution:**
1. Ride the circle entry in 2nd gear.
2. Apply rear brake at the circle entry.
3. Allow rear to slide — aim for the rear to track outside the circle while the front tracks on it.
4. Release brake when front is pointed at the exit direction.
**Success Criteria:** Consistent arc — rear slides controlled amount, front tracks intended line.
**Duration/Reps:** 20 laps per session.
**Progression:** Tighten the circle. Increase entry speed.

### Drill 3: Corner Entry Slide
**Purpose:** Apply brake slide technique to a real corner.
**Setup:** A natural loose-surface corner on trail.
**Execution:**
1. Approach the corner at moderate speed.
2. Rear brake at corner entry.
3. Body inside.
4. Front steering toward exit.
5. Release and drive.
**Success Criteria:** Corner navigated with higher entry speed than without the slide. Exit line is tight and correct.
**Duration/Reps:** 15 corner passes per session.
**Progression:** Increase entry speed. Add a second consecutive corner.

### Drill 4: Recovery Drill (Safe Traction Return)
**Purpose:** Train progressive traction reapplication specifically — the most dangerous moment.
**Setup:** Straight loose section.
**Execution:**
1. Lock rear wheel.
2. Allow slight sideways drift (5–10 degrees).
3. Gradually release rear brake — very slowly.
4. Feel the traction return and the bike re-aligning.
5. Repeat, trying to make the re-alignment as smooth as possible.
**Success Criteria:** Rear aligns without any snap or jolt. Smooth re-tracking on 8 out of 10 attempts.
**Duration/Reps:** 20 attempts.
**Progression:** Release from a slightly deeper slide angle. Then add throttle on release.

## 8. TERRAIN & CONTEXT VARIATIONS

### Loose Dirt
Primary brake slide terrain — predictable slide initiation and arc control. Standard learning environment.

### Hard-Pack
Higher traction threshold — more brake pressure needed. Slides are shorter and snap back harder on traction return. More aggressive release timing required. Higher high-side risk on hard-pack.

### Gravel
Deep gravel absorbs the slide slightly — less lateral movement for the same brake pressure. The rear tends to dig rather than slide cleanly. Technique adaptation: less brake pressure, more weight inside.

### Mud
Very low traction — rear slides extremely easily. Brake slides in mud require minimal pressure. Risk of front wash is lower (front also in low traction), but recovery requires very gradual reapplication.

### Steep Downhill
Gravity adds to the rotation force — brake slides on steep downhills rotate more aggressively. Shorter brake application needed. The rear may try to come around very quickly. More demanding technically.

## 9. EXPERT INSIGHTS & SOURCES

### Key Insights
- **Enduro vs MX brake slides:** In motocross, brake slides are performed at higher speeds on more consistent terrain. In enduro, slides must adapt to terrain variability — the technique must be more conservative and more precise, not more aggressive.
- **The "front wheel is the key" principle:** Every experienced enduro coach teaching brake slide technique emphasises that the slide is only safe when the front wheel is actively steering. A drifting front wheel combined with a sliding rear is an uncontrolled fall, not a technique.
- **Jarvis and Walker technique observation:** Both riders use brake slides extensively on tight technical terrain — but their slides are often barely visible because the arc is short and precisely controlled. The dramatic long slides seen in amateur riding are actually less controlled, not more skilled.
- **High-side education:** Teaching riders about high-side risk during brake slide recovery is considered mandatory by professional coaches — it is not an advanced topic, it is a safety-critical prerequisite.
