# Drop Off Technique

## 1. OVERVIEW

A drop-off is a deliberate descent off a ledge, cliff edge, or abrupt terrain change where the bike becomes airborne and must be controlled through the air before landing. Unlike a rock step drop (TECH-16) where the front wheel drops while the rear is still on the ledge, a drop-off is a full airborne event where both wheels leave the ground simultaneously or the front launches off the edge first at sufficient speed. Drop-offs are common on trail riding, enduro stages, and built-terrain features ranging from 0.5 to 3+ metres. The technique is fundamentally about commitment — riders who hesitate at the edge or brake at the worst moment face the most dangerous outcome. Correct technique requires reading the drop before approaching, setting approach speed, committing through the edge, and absorbing the landing.

## 2. CORE PRINCIPLES

### Principle 1: Commit — Do Not Brake at the Edge
The most dangerous moment in a drop-off is the moment the rider sees the drop clearly and instinctively brakes. Hard front braking at the edge of a drop transfers weight to the front wheel — which then drops off the edge with all the weight loaded forward. The bike pitches sharply nose-down and the rider is thrown over the bars. The approach speed must be decided before the edge and maintained through it. Braking occurs before the edge, not at it. This principle is non-negotiable.

### Principle 2: Weight Rearward Is the Foundation of All Drop Technique
Regardless of drop height, weight must be rearward before the front wheel reaches the edge. This counterbalances the nose-down pitch that occurs when the front drops. The higher the drop, the further rearward the weight must be. On large drops, the rider's hips may be near the rear fender. This feels extreme but is correct — it is the weight position that prevents the nose-over.

### Principle 3: Speed Determines the Landing Zone
The approach speed determines how far from the base of the drop the bike will land. Faster approach = landing further from the base. Slower approach = landing closer to the base. Knowing the landing zone from the approach speed allows the rider to plan where the bike will land. This is critical for drop-offs where the landing zone has obstacles or terrain changes beyond the base.

### Principle 4: Throttle Maintenance Controls Rear Pitch
In the air, the throttle controls pitch. Holding steady throttle keeps the rear wheel spinning at a rate consistent with the current pitch. Cutting throttle allows the nose to pitch down. In long drops, most riders instinctively reduce throttle on the way down — this must be managed. A completely cut throttle on a large drop produces a severe nose-down pitch.

### Principle 5: Landing Absorption Is the Completion of the Technique
A drop-off is not complete when the bike becomes airborne — it is complete when both wheels are on the ground and control is re-established. The landing absorption must be prepared in the air: legs bent, arms slightly bent, weight appropriately rearward for slightly rear-wheel-first contact. A poor landing means the technique was incomplete, regardless of how good the approach was.

## 3. TECHNIQUE BREAKDOWN BY LEVEL

### 3a. Beginner Level
**Goal:** Execute a small drop-off (0.5–0.8 m) with correct pre-approach speed and rearward weight, landing with bent-knee absorption.

**Step-by-Step Execution:**
1. Walk the drop before riding — assess the height, the base, and the landing zone.
2. Approach at the decided speed. Set it 5 metres before the edge — do not accelerate or decelerate into the edge.
3. Begin shifting weight rearward 3 metres before the edge. Hips move back. Arms extend slightly.
4. At the edge: do not brake. Front wheel goes over — let it drop.
5. Both wheels are now airborne. Rear weight helps prevent nose-dive.
6. Assess pitch. Hold throttle steady or very slightly reduce if nose is pitching down.
7. Bend knees deeply in the air. Arms slightly bent. Weight remains rearward.
8. Land: rear wheel first or flat. Legs absorb deeply — the suspension alone cannot absorb a full drop landing.
9. Re-establish forward drive. Continue.

**What the Rider Should Feel:**
- Approach: Weight shifting rearward — a deliberate, calm movement. The edge is coming. Speed is set.
- Edge: Sudden weightlessness at the front. The front drops. The rear is still loaded.
- Air: Weight entirely on rear pegs. The drop is happening. Quiet moment — nose check.
- Landing: A firm impact through the rear pegs first, then the front. Knees drive upward to absorb.

**What Good Looks Like:**
Approaching a 0.6 m drop at 15 km/h, the rider is already rearward before the edge. No braking at the edge. The bike drops. Rearward weight prevents nose-over. The landing is absorbed through bent knees. Forward drive resumes.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Braking at the edge:** Weight forward, nose-down in air, thrown over bars. → All speed management before the edge. Never brake at it.
- **Weight forward at the edge:** Same result as braking — nose pitches over. → Rearward weight begins 3 metres before the edge, not at it.
- **Rigid landing:** Arms and legs locked at impact — the drop force goes straight into the body. → Bend knees in the air. Prepare the absorption before the landing arrives.

### 3b. Intermediate Level
**Goal:** Execute drops of 0.8–1.5 m with precise speed calibration and controlled airborne phase.

**Progression Markers from Beginner:** Rider can drop small features without front-nose crash. Rearward weight is established. Landing absorption is present.

**Step-by-Step Execution:**
1. Walk the drop — note height, base character, and landing zone quality.
2. Calculate approach speed: faster = lands further from base; slower = closer. Choose the landing zone.
3. Weight rearward from 3 metres before the edge. More extreme rearward position than on a small drop.
4. No braking from 3 metres to the edge. Speed is committed.
5. Front goes over the edge — the drop is larger, so the nose-down pitch will be more pronounced.
6. In the air: throttle held steady. If nose is pitching down, a small throttle input helps.
7. Legs bent deeply. Arms ready. Weight rearward.
8. Rear contacts first — a significant impact through the rear suspension and legs. Then front contacts.
9. Absorb both contacts. Re-establish drive.

**What the Rider Should Feel at Each Stage:**
- Approach: Speed constant. Weight shifting rearward as the edge approaches.
- Edge: No braking. The front wheel goes over the edge — felt as sudden weightlessness at the front.
- Air: The bike is dropping. Weight entirely on rear pegs. The nose angle is assessed.
- Pitch monitoring: Is the nose level? The air seems quiet during this assessment.
- Landing preparation: Legs bent deeply. Arms ready. Weight slightly rearward for rear-wheel-first contact.
- Landing: The rear contacts first — a hard compression felt through the rear suspension and the rider's knees. Then the front contacts. A sequential absorption rather than simultaneous.
- Recovery: Both wheels on ground. Throttle maintained. Forward drive re-established.

**What Good Looks Like:**
Approaching a 1.2 m drop at 20 km/h, weight already rearward. The bike launches off the edge. In the air, the rider monitors pitch — it is slightly nose-down, throttle maintained to avoid worsening. Landing absorption through deep knee flex and bent arms. Clean exit.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Braking at the edge:** The single biggest mistake. Results in severe nose-down pitch. → Speed committed before the edge. Nothing changes at the edge except the ground disappearing.
- **Over-speed with long range:** Too much speed — the bike lands beyond the planned zone into unknown terrain. → Conservative approach for new drops. Build up.
- **Front-heavy landing:** Body too far forward at landing. → Rearward weight must be maintained into and through the landing.

### 3c. Advanced Level
**Goal:** Execute large drops (1.5–3+ m) with airborne body position control and precise landing zone targeting.

**Progression Markers from Intermediate:** Rider can execute 1.5 m drops cleanly. Airborne pitch is managed. Landing is absorbed, not crashed.

**Step-by-Step Execution — Large Drops:**
1. Walk the drop — large drops require understanding every variable: height, trajectory, landing zone surface quality, and exit terrain.
2. Set approach speed based on desired landing zone. On large drops, even small speed differences change the landing zone significantly.
3. Weight very rearward — on a 2+ m drop, hips may be over the rear fender. Extreme but correct.
4. No braking from well before the edge. Committed approach.
5. Extended airborne phase — more time to monitor pitch and correct.
6. Throttle management in the air: hold steady for most of the drop. Small adjustments only.
7. Legs deeply bent before landing. Arms bent. The suspension alone will not absorb a large drop.
8. Rear lands — absorb. Front lands — absorb. Both must be caught by the rider.
9. Do not immediately apply throttle — recover stability first, then proceed.

**Failure Modes and Causes:**
- **Front flip from edge braking:** Rider brakes hard at the edge — the most catastrophic drop failure. → Never brake at the edge. This is not an intermediate correction — it is a fundamental safety principle that cannot be compromised.
- **Over-speed long-range landing in rough terrain:** Too fast — the bike lands far from the drop base in unplanned terrain. → Assess landing zone range from approach speed before committing.
- **Front-heavy landing causing crash:** Weight too far forward at landing — front dives and washes or pitches rider forward. → Rearward weight must be active throughout the air phase, not just at the edge.

## 4. DIAGNOSTIC CUES (AI COACHING ENGINE REFERENCE)

### Visual Cues — Correct Technique
- Weight clearly rearward before the edge — hips back, arms extended toward the bars
- No braking visible at the edge — consistent approach speed maintained to the edge
- Knee flex visible on landing — deep absorption, not rigid impact
- Body returns to a balanced position after landing — weight transitions forward after the rear contact

### Visual Cues — Error Indicators
- Braking at edge visible — bike pitches nose-down violently in the air
- Body forward at landing — rider thrown forward on impact
- Rigid leg landing — bike absorbs everything, body slams down
- Rider releasing the bars mid-air from panic — hands coming off the bars

### Audio Cues
- Correct: Engine note maintained to edge — no throttle cut before the drop
- Error: Engine note dropping at edge — braking occurring, weight transferring forward

### Sensor Cues
- Correct: Pitch angle neutral to slightly rear-high in air — correct weight position
- Error: Pitch angle increasing nose-down in air — front-heavy or throttle cut

## 5. COACHING CUES & LANGUAGE

### Positive Reinforcement
- "Weight was back before the edge. The nose held level through the air. That's drop technique."
- "No braking at the edge — you committed. That commitment is what made it safe."
- "Deep knee absorption on landing. The suspension had backup. That's correct."
- "You read the landing zone before you approached. That planning is what gives you control."

### Corrective Feedback
- "You braked at the edge. That is the single most dangerous thing you can do on a drop. Speed management before the edge. Never at it."
- "Weight was still centred when you went over the edge — nose went too far down. Rearward weight before the approach. Not at the edge."
- "Rigid landing — legs were straight when you hit. Bend the knees in the air. The suspension cannot absorb a drop on its own."

### Progression Prompts
- "You can handle 0.8 m. Try 1.2 m. The principle is identical — rearward weight is just more extreme."
- "Read the landing zone on your next approach. Where will the bike land at your approach speed? Plan to land there."
- "Walk this drop before you ride it. Tell me: where will you land at 15 km/h? At 20 km/h? Know the difference before you commit."

## 6. DRILLS & PRACTICE EXERCISES

### Drill 1: Small Drop Commitment
**Purpose:** Build commitment habit — no braking at the edge.
**Setup:** A 0.5 m drop with clear landing zone and visible base.
**Execution:**
1. Set speed 5 metres before the approach.
2. No braking from 3 metres before edge through the drop.
3. Measure whether speed was constant at the edge.
**Success Criteria:** Consistent speed approach on 8 out of 10 drops. No brake grab at the edge.
**Duration/Reps:** 15 drops. **Progression:** Increase drop height to 0.8 m.

### Drill 2: Rearward Weight Graduation
**Purpose:** Build the rearward weight habit progressively with increasing drop height.
**Setup:** Drops of 0.5, 0.8, and 1.2 m available for progression.
**Execution:**
1. At each height, establish rearward weight before the edge.
2. Compare how much further rearward the 1.2 m drop requires versus 0.5 m.
3. Film from the side to confirm weight position at the edge.
**Success Criteria:** Clean landing at each height with no front crash.
**Duration/Reps:** 5 drops at each height. **Progression:** Add 1.5 m.

### Drill 3: Landing Zone Targeting
**Purpose:** Calibrate approach speed to a specific landing zone.
**Setup:** A 1 m drop with a marked landing zone (a tyre, a log, or tape).
**Execution:**
1. Walk the drop — estimate where 15 km/h lands vs 20 km/h.
2. Ride at the slower speed — confirm landing zone estimate.
3. Ride at faster speed — confirm the landing moves further from base.
**Success Criteria:** Landing within zone on 6 out of 10 drops. Rider can predict which zone each speed produces.
**Duration/Reps:** 20 drops. **Progression:** Narrow the zone. Choose a specific landing point.

### Drill 4: Landing Absorption Focus
**Purpose:** Develop deep-knee landing absorption as a trained habit.
**Setup:** Any small drop (0.5–0.8 m).
**Execution:**
1. Focus entirely on bending the knees in the air before landing.
2. Exaggerate the absorption — knees driving up toward the chest on impact.
3. After 10 drops, film one and review whether knees were visibly bending on impact.
**Success Criteria:** Deep flex visible on every landing. No rigid-leg impacts.
**Duration/Reps:** 15 drops. **Progression:** Assess on video. Look for torso height staying constant through the landing.

## 8. TERRAIN & CONTEXT VARIATIONS

### Clear Landing Zone
Standard — plan approach speed to land in zone. Walk first if the landing zone has not been seen before.

### Wet or Muddy Landing
Landing traction reduced — front wash risk on landing is higher. Lower approach speed. Ensure absorption is maximum to avoid the front sliding on impact.

### Rocky Landing
Hard landing surface — absorption must be excellent. No room for error in leg or arm flex. Maximum knee bend in the air.

### Drop Into Corner
Must manage speed immediately after landing to navigate the corner. Landing speed must be appropriate for the corner that follows. The exit after the drop is part of the technique — plan it before dropping.

### Drop With Obstacles at Base
If obstacles exist at the base of the drop, landing zone must be planned precisely. Under-speed risks landing on the obstacle. Conservative approach with walk-and-assess is mandatory.

## 9. EXPERT INSIGHTS & SOURCES

### Key Insights
- **"Commit and absorb":** The universal coaching message for drops. These two principles are the technique in their entirety. Commit at the approach, absorb at the landing. Everything else follows from these.
- **No braking at edge:** This is the most repeated coaching correction across all drop technique instruction — it is never fully unlearnable until specifically trained against. Riders who have not specifically practised no-braking at a drop edge will brake under pressure. This must be drilled explicitly.
- **Video feedback:** Showing a rider their own drop technique on video is the fastest coaching tool — riders can immediately see if their weight was forward or backward, if they braked, and if they absorbed the landing. Self-analysis from video is the most efficient way to correct weight position habits.
- **Big drops are not harder, they are longer:** Coaches who teach large drops consistently note that the technique is identical to small drops — the approach speed changes, the rearward weight is more extreme, and the airborne time is longer. But the principles are the same. Riders who have excellent small drop technique will manage large drops more quickly than riders who skip stages.

- **Fear management is a technique:** Many riders who understand drop technique intellectually freeze at the approach because the height triggers a fear response that overrides the technique. Coaches note that this is managed by progressive exposure — walking the drop, then standing at the edge, then doing a very small similar drop, then the target drop. The fear response diminishes with graduated exposure, not with talking about it.
- **The rearward weight feels wrong:** Nearly every rider who first correctly positions rearward weight on a large drop reports that it feels extreme and unsafe. Coaches use this as a calibration cue: "if it feels too far back, it is probably correct." The correct position for a 1.5 m drop feels nearly as extreme as falling off the back.
