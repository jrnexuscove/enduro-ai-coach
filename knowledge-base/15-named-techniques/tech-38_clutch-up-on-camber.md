# Clutch-Up on Camber

## 1. OVERVIEW

A clutch-up on camber is the application of the clutch-up wheelie technique adapted specifically for off-camber terrain — slopes that tilt laterally, threatening to push the bike downhill while maintaining forward progress. On flat terrain, a clutch-up lifts the front wheel to clear an obstacle or gain momentum, and the bike's weight and inertia are managed symmetrically. On camber, the same technique requires the additional management of lateral body weight — the rider must simultaneously lift the front wheel and counteract the downhill lean force created by the slope. Applied correctly, a clutch-up on camber can redirect the front wheel away from the fall line, establish a new uphill line, or clear an obstacle that crosses a camber section. Applied incorrectly, the lifted front wheel follows the slope's fall line and the bike slides downhill during the lift.

## 2. CORE PRINCIPLES

### Principle 1: The Front Wheel Goes Where Weight and Momentum Direct It When Airborne
When the front wheel is lifted in a clutch-up, it is momentarily free to follow any direction determined by the rider's weight distribution and steering input. On flat terrain, straight momentum keeps it straight. On camber, the slope creates a lateral force pulling the front wheel downhill. If the rider does not counter this, the lifted front wheel will drift downhill during the lift — and land pointed at the fall line.

### Principle 2: Body Weight Must Be Uphill of the Bike's Centreline During the Lift
To counteract the downhill lateral force during a clutch-up on camber, the rider's body weight must be biased uphill — leaning into the slope slightly more than on flat terrain. This counterbalances the slope's pull on the front wheel and allows the front wheel to lift and return to the desired line rather than following the fall line.

### Principle 3: The Clutch-Up Must Be Timed to the Uphill Wheel's Traction
On camber, the downhill wheel bears more weight (loaded by gravity). The uphill wheel is lightly loaded — traction is lower. The clutch-up is driven by the rear wheel, which must have traction for the power delivery to work. On steep camber, the rear may be predominantly the downhill wheel — which has traction. On extreme camber, the rear may be losing traction uphill. Know which wheel is driving.

### Principle 4: Front Wheel Direction on Landing Must Be Controlled
The most critical moment in a clutch-up on camber is the front wheel landing. If the wheel lands pointed downhill (slope has pulled it during the lift), the bike immediately begins heading downhill. The front wheel direction on landing must be controlled — maintained parallel to the desired line, not the fall line.

### Principle 5: Forward Momentum Assists — Lateral Momentum Must Be Managed
The forward momentum from the clutch-up helps carry the bike through the technique. But on camber, any forward momentum that is not precisely aligned with the desired line becomes a component of downhill slide. The line must be precisely chosen before the clutch-up is initiated — there is no adjustment mid-technique.

## 3. TECHNIQUE BREAKDOWN BY LEVEL

### 3a. Beginner Level
**Goal:** Understand the clutch-up modification required on off-camber terrain — practise on gentle camber only.

**Step-by-Step Execution — Gentle Camber:**
1. Identify the camber angle before approaching — is the slope gentle enough (under 15 degrees)?
2. Locate the obstacle to be cleared and the desired line through it.
3. Pre-load uphill body weight before approaching — lean slightly into the slope.
4. This lean should feel slightly exaggerated compared to flat terrain.
5. Approach the obstacle. Uphill lean maintained.
6. Execute clutch-up: clutch pull, throttle blip, clutch release — front lifts.
7. During the brief airborne moment, maintain uphill lean.
8. Front wheel returns to desired line — not the fall line.
9. Post-landing: progressive throttle on the cambered surface.

**What the Rider Should Feel:**
- Uphill lean: Slightly more pressure on the uphill peg than normal. Body leaning into the slope.
- Clutch pop: Front rises — the lift feels slightly asymmetric on camber.
- Airborne: The slope is still pulling the front toward the fall line. The lean counteracts this.
- Landing: Front wheel on desired line — success feels like a normal clutch-up landing.

**What Good Looks Like:**
On a 10-degree camber section, the rider approaches an obstacle with slightly more uphill body lean than normal. The clutch-up is initiated — front wheel lifts. The rider maintains uphill lean during the brief lift. Front wheel returns to the desired line. Obstacle cleared.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Flat-terrain body position on camber:** No uphill lean bias — front wheel follows the fall line during the lift. → Lean uphill before and during the lift.
- **Too much camber angle for first attempts:** Steep camber amplifies all errors. → Start on gentle camber only.

### 3b. Intermediate Level
**Goal:** Execute a clutch-up on moderate camber — managing body weight, timing the lift, and controlling front wheel direction on landing.

**Progression Markers from Beginner:** Rider can execute clutch-ups on flat terrain consistently. Camber riding on moderate slopes is established.

**Step-by-Step Execution — Moderate Camber (15–25 degrees):**
1. Assess the camber angle — 15–25 degrees requires a clear uphill lean bias.
2. Identify the obstacle and the desired line.
3. Pre-load uphill body weight 2 metres before the obstacle.
4. Felt as deliberate pressure on the uphill peg — not dramatic, but clear.
5. Approach the obstacle with the uphill lean established.
6. Clutch preparation: clutch pulled in, throttle ready for blip.
7. At the obstacle, commit to the pop — more decisive than on flat because the uphill lean adds effective weight over the rear.
8. Front lifts. Uphill lean maintained throughout.
9. Front wheel descends to the desired line.
10. Landing: front wheel on line. Immediate progressive throttle — not aggressive.

**What the Rider Should Feel at Each Stage:**
- Camber assessment: The slope is felt as a constant lateral lean in the body — gravity pulling toward the downhill side. The uphill peg is lighter, the downhill peg is heavier.
- Approach and body shift: Weight shifts slightly uphill — felt as more pressure on the uphill peg and a slight lean of the upper body toward the slope. This counteracts the fall line tendency.
- Clutch preparation: Clutch pulled in, throttle blipped, ready for the pop. The bike is on a consistent line approaching the obstacle.
- Clutch pop: The clutch is released quickly — felt as the rear wheel driving and the front end rising. The lift force must be decisive — a weak pop on camber may not clear the obstacle because uphill body lean requires more drive.
- Airborne moment: Front wheel is up. The slope's lateral force is pulling the front wheel downhill. The uphill body lean counteracts this — the front wheel stays aimed at the desired line.
- Landing: Front wheel returns to the surface on the desired line, not the fall line. Felt as the front tyre contacting the slope surface at the correct angle.
- Post-landing: Progressive throttle applied. The rear wheel is on a sloped surface — aggressive throttle will cause it to spin and the bike to slide downhill.

**What Good Looks Like:**
On a 20-degree camber with a root crossing the slope, the rider assesses the root 3 metres ahead. Body weight shifts slightly uphill. Clutch engagement is prepared. At 1 metre, the clutch is popped — front wheel lifts. The uphill lean counteracts the fall line pull. Front wheel clears the root. Landing is on the desired line. Throttle is progressive post-landing.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Insufficient uphill lean:** Front wheel follows fall line during lift. Landing pointed downhill. → More uphill body bias before and during the lift.
- **Weak clutch pop:** Front wheel barely lifts — obstacle contact. On camber, more decisive pop is needed. → More committed pop.
- **Aggressive post-landing throttle:** Rear wheel spins on camber surface — bike slides downhill. → Progressive throttle immediately post-landing.

### 3c. Advanced Level
**Goal:** Execute clutch-ups on steep camber at pace — reading the obstacle and camber angle simultaneously and executing with precision.

**Progression Markers from Intermediate:** Rider can execute clutch-ups on moderate camber. Uphill lean bias is calibrated. Landing direction is controlled.

**Step-by-Step Execution — Steep Camber:**
1. Steep camber (25+ degrees): the lateral force is significant. The uphill lean must be very deliberate.
2. Read the entire camber section from a safe position — identify all obstacles and the best line.
3. Pre-load uphill weight well before the first obstacle — not last-second.
4. Pop must be more decisive: the uphill lean increases effective rear weight, requiring more drive.
5. Front wheel airborne: the slope is pulling hard toward the fall line. Only a decisive uphill lean prevents the front from following.
6. Landing with front on line: immediate but progressive throttle.
7. If multiple obstacles: each requires its own pre-loaded lean position. Chain them.

**Failure Modes and Causes:**
- **Bike sliding downhill post-landing:** Landing with front pointed downhill, or post-landing throttle too aggressive. → Uphill lean during lift, progressive throttle after landing.
- **Rear wheel losing traction on pop:** On steep camber, the rear tyre is on a sloped surface — aggressive clutch pop spins the rear, no lift achieved. → More committed pop, or choose a slightly firmer spot for the rear contact point.
- **Front wheel catches obstacle on camber:** Lift insufficient — front wheel hits rather than clears. → Approach angle adjustment — the obstacle height on camber is effectively different on the uphill and downhill sides.

## 4. DIAGNOSTIC CUES (AI COACHING ENGINE REFERENCE)

### Visual Cues — Correct Technique
- Rider body clearly biased uphill before and during the lift on camber
- Front wheel landing parallel to the desired line, not the fall line
- Progressive throttle post-landing — no rear wheel spin

### Visual Cues — Error Indicators
- Front wheel turning downhill during the lift — insufficient uphill lean
- Bike sliding downhill immediately post-landing — front wheel landed on fall line
- Rear wheel spin on landing — too aggressive throttle on cambered surface

### Audio Cues
- Correct: Decisive engine note rise on clutch pop — committed pop, front end rises
- Correct: Progressive throttle sound post-landing — controlled rear drive
- Error: Rear wheel spin sound post-landing — too aggressive throttle on cambered surface
- Error: Weak engine note on pop — insufficient commitment, front barely lifted

### Sensor Cues
- Correct: Lateral acceleration stable during lift — uphill lean counteracting slope
- Error: Lateral acceleration spike downhill during lift — bike following fall line
- Error: Rear wheel speed spike post-landing — rear wheel spin on cambered surface

## 5. COACHING CUES & LANGUAGE

### Positive Reinforcement
- "Body was uphill before the pop — front wheel stayed on line through the lift. That's the bias that makes camber clutch-ups work."
- "Progressive throttle post-landing. Rear didn't spin on the slope. You gave it traction control through your right hand."
- "Decisive pop — front cleared the root cleanly. The uphill lean gave you direction, the pop gave you height."

### Corrective Feedback
- "No uphill lean before the pop — front wheel followed the fall line when it lifted. You need to be leaning into the slope before you start the technique. Pre-load the uphill side."
- "Throttle too aggressive after landing on the slope — rear spun and the bike slid. Progressive on a slope. The surface can't take the same throttle as flat ground."
- "Weak pop — barely lifted. On camber you're fighting the slope too, so the pop must be more committed. More decisive clutch release."

### Progression Prompts
- "You've got gentle camber clutch-ups. Try a steeper section — the lateral force is noticeably higher. You'll need more uphill bias than felt right on the gentle slope."
- "On the next pass, tell me which peg you were weighting most before the pop. The uphill peg should feel loaded. If the downhill peg is heavier, you haven't shifted your weight."

## 6. DRILLS & PRACTICE EXERCISES

### Drill 1: Flat-to-Camber Clutch-Up Comparison
**Purpose:** Feel the difference between a flat and cambered clutch-up — understand why body position changes.
**Setup:** A known obstacle on flat terrain, then the same obstacle type on a gentle camber.
**Execution:**
1. Execute the clutch-up on flat terrain — feel how the front wheel stays straight.
2. Execute the same technique on the gentle camber with no body position change — feel the front wheel follow the fall line.
3. Add uphill body lean and repeat — feel the front wheel stay on the desired line.
**Success Criteria:** Rider can articulate the difference felt in front wheel direction between the three attempts.
**Duration/Reps:** 10 flat, 10 camber without lean, 10 camber with lean.

### Drill 2: Uphill Lean Calibration
**Purpose:** Find the correct uphill lean for the specific camber angle.
**Setup:** A marked camber section with a small obstacle.
**Execution:**
1. Attempt the clutch-up with minimal uphill lean — note front wheel direction on landing.
2. Add more uphill lean — repeat.
3. Identify the lean angle that keeps the front wheel on the desired line.
**Success Criteria:** Rider can find and reproduce the correct lean for the given camber.
**Duration/Reps:** 15 attempts.

### Drill 3: Post-Landing Throttle Control on Camber
**Purpose:** Develop progressive throttle habit after clutch-up landing on a slope.
**Setup:** Any cambered surface with a landing zone.
**Execution:**
1. Execute clutch-up and land.
2. Immediately apply throttle progressively — very light to start.
3. Monitor rear wheel — any spin is too much throttle. Reduce.
4. Find the progressive throttle level that gives drive without spin.
**Success Criteria:** Post-landing drive achieved without rear spin on 8 out of 10 landings.
**Duration/Reps:** 20 landings.

### Drill 4: Camber Section Linking
**Purpose:** Chain multiple clutch-ups on a camber section.
**Setup:** A 20-metre camber section with 2–3 roots or obstacles crossing it.
**Execution:**
1. Pre-ride the section — identify each obstacle and the uphill lean required for each.
2. Ride the section linking all clutch-ups.
3. Assess: front wheel direction at each landing, rear traction post-landing.
**Success Criteria:** All obstacles cleared with front wheel on desired line on 5 out of 10 passes.
**Duration/Reps:** 10 passes.

## 8. TERRAIN & CONTEXT VARIATIONS

### Gentle Camber (Under 15 Degrees)
Manageable lateral force. Minor uphill lean bias required. Good for initial practice.

### Moderate Camber (15–25 Degrees)
Significant lateral force. Clear uphill lean required. Front wheel direction becomes critical.

### Steep Camber (25+ Degrees)
High lateral force. Very decisive uphill lean required. Rear traction management critical — rear may be on loose, sloped surface.

### Wet Cambered Surface
Reduced traction on both tyres. Clutch pop must be calibrated to available traction. Post-landing throttle even more progressive than on dry.

## 9. EXPERT INSIGHTS & SOURCES

### Key Insights
- **Camber as a force, not just an angle:** Coaches who teach this technique consistently describe camber as a constant force acting on every input — every clutch-up, every throttle, every landing has a lateral component that must be accounted for. Riders who learn this conceptual frame manage camber intuitively.
- **Uphill bias is pre-loaded, not reactive:** The body position adjustment for camber must happen before the technique begins, not during. A rider who tries to adjust during the airborne phase is too late. The pre-load is the technique.
- **Weak pop on camber:** A surprising number of riders who can execute flat clutch-ups produce weak pops on camber — the unfamiliar terrain causes hesitation. Coaches note that the pop on camber needs to be equally or more decisive than on flat, despite the unfamiliar body position. Hesitation produces a partial lift that catches the obstacle.
- **Scanning the camber section before riding:** Coaches recommend walking or observing any challenging camber section before the first ride attempt — identifying the obstacles, assessing the camber angle, and planning the line and body position before mounting. Camber sections have a disorienting quality at low speed that is reduced by prior visual analysis.
- **The line must be set before the technique begins:** On camber, unlike flat terrain, a small error in the initial approach line is amplified by the slope's fall line tendency. A front wheel that is 10 degrees off the desired line at the start of a camber section will drift further from the desired line as the technique progresses. Setting the correct approach line before the clutch-up is initiated is as important as the technique itself.
