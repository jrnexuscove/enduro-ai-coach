# Feet-Up Creep

## 1. OVERVIEW

Feet-up creeping is the technique of navigating extremely technical terrain at walking pace or slower — with both feet on the pegs and no footing — using precise clutch and brake balance to maintain control through areas where a single foot touch could result in an ankle injury, a fall, or a loss of momentum. In extreme enduro, hard sections are often covered in rocks, roots, and technical features where footing is genuinely dangerous — a foot placed on a sharp rock edge can break an ankle, and a foot placed on the wrong surface can trip the rider. Feet-up creeping is the alternative: maintaining balance and forward progress using only the bike's controls. The skill builds directly on the track stand (TECH-11) and clutch feathering (TECH-03), combining them into forward motion through the most technical terrain.

## 2. CORE PRINCIPLES

### Principle 1: Clutch and Rear Brake Are the Speed Controllers
At creep pace, throttle alone gives too much power — the engine produces too much torque even at idle in first gear for the available traction. Clutch feathering (holding the clutch at partial slip) reduces drive to a trickle. The rear brake provides additional drag when even the feathered drive is too much. The combination of feathered clutch and slight rear brake creates a precise power delivery window — moving the bike forward at whatever exact speed the terrain demands.

### Principle 2: Centre of Mass Must Stay Within the Base of Support
At near-zero speed, the base of support is the area between the two tyre contact patches. The rider's centre of mass must stay over or near this area at all times. This requires constant awareness of the bike's lean angle and small body corrections — tilting with the bike, not against it, while keeping overall CoM above the support base.

### Principle 3: Eyes Lead the Creep — Look 2–3 Metres Ahead
At walking pace, terrain can be read much more precisely than at speed. The eyes should scan ahead — not looking at the wheel, not looking at the immediate ground, but reading the terrain 2–3 metres ahead. This gives the rider time to position the front wheel precisely on the desired surface and prepare for the next obstacle.

### Principle 4: Obstacle Impact Is Managed With Throttle Pulse
When the front wheel hits a rock or root at creep pace, the bike slows and tips. A brief throttle pulse — with clutch slightly released — drives the bike over the obstacle and restores forward momentum. This micro-throttle management is the engine of creep technique: constant tiny throttle adjustments responding to each terrain feature.

### Principle 5: Standing Position Enables Maximum Balance Authority
The creep technique requires standing on the pegs — not sitting. Standing raises the centre of mass, which paradoxically improves balance at low speed by allowing faster and larger body weight corrections. A seated rider at near-zero speed has very limited body movement available. A standing rider can shift weight left, right, forward, and back to maintain the critical CoM position above the support base.

## 3. TECHNIQUE BREAKDOWN BY LEVEL

### 3a. Beginner Level
**Goal:** Creep through a simple technical section at walking pace with no more than 2–3 foot touches.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Clutch at bite point with slight throttle — not clutch fully in
- Standing position throughout
- Accepting foot touches as safety rather than failure — but reducing them progressively
- Eyes 2 metres ahead, not at the ground in front of the wheel

**What Good Looks Like:**
Through a rocky flat section, the rider moves at walking pace standing on the pegs. Two foot touches occur when balance is momentarily lost. The bike negotiates rocks and roots cleanly. No crashes. Progress is maintained throughout.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Clutch fully in, relying on momentum:** Bike decelerates into each obstacle and tips. → Clutch at bite point. Slight drive throughout.
- **Looking at the immediate ground in front of wheel:** Rider reacts to obstacles rather than anticipating them. → Eyes 2 metres ahead.
- **Sitting down for slow-speed sections:** Reduces balance authority significantly. → Stand. Always stand through technical terrain.

### 3b. Intermediate Level
**Goal:** Creep through genuine technical terrain (rocks, roots, ruts) at walking pace with zero or one foot touch.

**Progression Markers from Beginner:** Rider can creep through moderate terrain with 2–3 foot touches. Standing position established. Clutch bite point is instinctive.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Precise throttle pulsing over individual obstacles
- Continuous rear brake use to prevent over-speed
- Body weight shifts anticipating each obstacle — not reacting to them
- Front wheel placement accuracy — choosing exactly which surface the tyre contacts

**What Good Looks Like:**
On a section of large boulders and exposed roots, the rider creeps through at a pace slightly slower than walking. Both feet are on the pegs. The front wheel is placed precisely on flat rock surfaces where possible, avoiding the unstable edges. No foot touches. The rider looks calm and deliberate.

**What the Rider Should Feel at Each Stage:**
- Clutch control: The bite point is held continuously. Subtle clutch adjustments feel like squeezing a very small range — 2–3 mm either way. The throttle is slightly elevated but steady.
- Rear brake drag: A slight rear brake engagement can be felt as mild resistance — slowing the overall pace without disconnecting drive. The combination of drive and drag feels balanced.
- Obstacle approach: Front wheel about to contact a rock — the rider feels the impact coming through the bars as the tyre makes contact. A clutch micro-release adds drive at this moment — felt as a slight surge.
- Tip correction: Bike leans left — rider shifts hip right, adds left peg pressure. Felt as a rebalancing — the bike comes back upright. The correction is small and natural.
- Front wheel placement: Looking 2–3 metres ahead, identifying the next good surface. The front wheel placement is deliberate — the rider is choosing the exact square centimetre.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Jerky throttle pulsing:** Too much throttle on each rock causes the front to hop — the bike lunges rather than creeping. Fix: smaller throttle pulses, more frequent, gentler.
- **Rear brake causing stall:** Too much rear brake combined with clutch at bite point kills the engine. Fix: rear brake is a supplement, not a primary control. Main control is clutch.
- **Wrong foot placement on foot touch (ankle risk):** When a foot does touch, it lands on the edge of a rock — ankle roll risk. Fix: scan for safe foot placement before deciding to use a foot touch. If no safe foothold is visible, do not touch.

### 3c. Advanced Level
**Goal:** Creep through extreme technical terrain — large rocks, tight ruts, steep technical — with consistent zero foot touches.

**Progression Markers from Intermediate:** Rider can creep through moderate terrain with zero foot touches consistently. Throttle pulsing is automatic. Front wheel placement is precise.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Sustained zero foot-touch creep through genuinely extreme terrain
- Combining creep with brief track stands when route-reading is required
- Managing clutch heat on extended technical creep sections
- Creep through tight tree sections where handlebar clearance is millimetres

**What Good Looks Like:**
In a section of extreme rocky terrain, the rider creeps through a 50-metre section that takes 3 minutes. Zero foot touches. The bike picks its way through boulders and root networks with the front wheel always on a carefully selected surface. Brief pauses (track stands) occur when the route ahead is unclear. When clarity arrives, the creep continues.

**Failure Modes and Causes:**
- **Stall on technical feature:** Clutch slips fully in when front wheel hits a large obstacle — engine loses load and stalls. Fix: pre-empt large obstacles with a throttle pulse before contact, not during.
- **Jerky motion from over-correcting:** Too many large throttle inputs create a hopping, lunging motion. Fix: smaller inputs. At creep pace, control is achieved through frequency of adjustment, not amplitude.
- **Foot-down on dangerous terrain:** A foot touch on a technical rock section when no safe foothold exists — serious ankle injury risk. Fix: commit to zero foot touches through dangerous sections. If balance is marginal, a controlled stall is safer than a dangerous foot touch.

## 4. DIAGNOSTIC CUES (AI COACHING ENGINE REFERENCE)

### Visual Cues
- Correct: Both feet on pegs through the technical section — no foot touches visible
- Correct: Standing position maintained — clear gap between seat and rider
- Correct: Front wheel visibly placed on specific surface choices — not random contact
- Correct: Body makes small lateral and fore-aft corrections — active balance management
- Error: Repeated foot touches — balance not maintained, technique insufficient for terrain
- Error: Sitting through the section — reduced balance authority
- Error: Front wheel randomly bouncing through terrain — no placement precision

### Audio Cues
- Correct: Steady low engine RPM with micro-pulses — clutch feathering audible as slight RPM variations
- Correct: Rear brake drag audible as slight pad contact sound at low speed
- Error: Engine stall — clutch management failed on obstacle
- Error: Jerky throttle blips — large uncontrolled throttle pulses

### Sensor Cues
- Correct: Near-zero forward speed maintained through the section — under 5 km/h
- Correct: Smooth speed trace — no large speed spikes or drops
- Error: Speed drops to zero then suddenly rises — stall and restart sequence
- Error: Repeated lateral acceleration micro-spikes — foot touches occurring

## 5. COACHING CUES & LANGUAGE

### Positive Reinforcement
- "Whole section, both feet on the pegs. That's the standard we're building to. You proved it's possible."
- "I can see you placing the front wheel deliberately. You're choosing the surface, not accepting it. That's technical precision."
- "Your body corrections were small and early. You didn't wait until the bike was falling — you fixed it at a degree. That's elite balance."

### Corrective Feedback
- "Clutch is going fully in on each big rock — then the bike dies. Keep the clutch at the bite point and add a throttle pulse as the wheel hits. Don't disengage."
- "You're sitting down. Stand up. At this speed, you need your legs for balance. The seat gives you no correction range."
- "Stop looking at the ground in front of your wheel. Eyes up — look 2 metres ahead. By the time you see the rock at your wheel it's too late to place the front wheel precisely."
- "Your foot touched on that loose rock face. That's exactly where you cannot put a foot. In dangerous terrain, a stall is better than an ankle injury."

### Progression Prompts
- "You got through with 3 foot touches. Next run aim for 2. Identify which moments caused the touches — were they avoidable?"
- "You can creep at this speed. Now try slower. The slower you go, the more precision is required but the less speed-related error there is."
- "Try combining a track stand in the middle — pause, read, then continue. The pause should be zero-foot too. That's the advanced version."

## 6. DRILLS & PRACTICE EXERCISES

### Drill 1: Flat Rock Garden Creep
**Purpose:** Build basic creep technique on a predictable but technical surface.
**Setup:** A constructed or natural rock garden — flat rocks of varying sizes on flat ground.
**Execution:**
1. Enter at walking pace, standing.
2. Clutch at bite point, slight throttle.
3. Navigate the rock garden with the goal of zero foot touches.
4. Focus on front wheel placement precision.
**Success Criteria:** Zero foot touches through a 10-metre rock garden on 3 consecutive passes.
**Duration/Reps:** 15 passes per session.
**Progression:** Increase rock size. Add tight gaps requiring handlebar navigation.

### Drill 2: Root Network Creep
**Purpose:** Apply creep technique to exposed roots — an unpredictable, angular surface.
**Setup:** A section of trail with exposed root networks.
**Execution:**
1. Creep through the root section at below-walking pace.
2. Place front wheel on flat root surfaces where possible.
3. Zero foot touches target.
**Success Criteria:** Zero foot touches through a 15-metre root section.
**Duration/Reps:** 10 passes.
**Progression:** Wet roots. Then add gradient.

### Drill 3: Tight Tree Slalom Creep
**Purpose:** Navigate handlebar-width gaps between trees at creep pace.
**Setup:** A natural or marked section where trees or posts are placed 1 metre apart (approximately handlebar width plus 30 cm clearance).
**Execution:**
1. Enter the slalom at creep pace.
2. Navigate each gap with the handlebars clearing the tree trunks.
3. Zero foot touches.
**Success Criteria:** All gaps cleared without handlebar contact on 5 consecutive passes.
**Duration/Reps:** 15 passes.
**Progression:** Reduce gap width to handlebar width plus 15 cm.

### Drill 4: Extended Technical Creep
**Purpose:** Sustain the creep technique over an extended technical section to build endurance and consistency.
**Setup:** A 50–100 metre technical section of trail — mixed rocks, roots, and gradient.
**Execution:**
1. Complete the full section at creep pace, both feet on pegs.
2. Time the section.
3. Count foot touches.
4. On each run, attempt to reduce foot touches.
**Success Criteria:** Full section completed with zero foot touches on 2 consecutive runs.
**Duration/Reps:** 5 full section passes per session.
**Progression:** Use more extreme terrain. Add a brief track stand in the middle of the section.

## 8. TERRAIN & CONTEXT VARIATIONS

### Rocky Technical Terrain
Primary application. Large rocks require precise front wheel placement. Obstacle contacts require throttle pulse. The technique is used for its purpose here.

### Steep Technical Sections
Gradient adds to the difficulty — the bike wants to either roll forward (downhill) or stall (uphill). Rear brake use increases on downhill sections; clutch feathering becomes more critical on uphill sections.

### Mud and Bog
Creep technique in mud is about maintaining momentum — stalling in deep mud means pushing. Slightly higher speed (slightly faster creep) is often more effective than very slow creep in mud because the wheels clear mud more effectively when spinning.

### Tight Forests
Tree trunks create natural handlebar hazards. Handlebar clearance management — keeping the bars parallel to the desired line and avoiding turns that put handlebar ends into trees — becomes a precision element of the creep.

### Night Riding or Low Visibility
Creep technique is particularly valuable in low visibility where trail reading is difficult. The slow pace allows vision to catch up with terrain complexity.

## 9. EXPERT INSIGHTS & SOURCES

### Key Insights
- **Hard enduro as the proving ground:** Extreme hard enduro events (Erzbergrodeo, Romaniacs) regularly feature sections that take hours where riders are essentially foot-up creeping through boulder fields. This is where the technique is most visible in competition.
- **Trials heritage:** Feet-up creep is the enduro version of trials riding. Trials riders are scored on foot touches — each foot touch is a point against them. The enduro application adopts the same standard for the most technical sections.
- **Why it improves all riding:** Coaches consistently report that dedicated feet-up creep practice has an outsized impact on all riding — particularly clutch feel, balance, and front wheel placement precision. These are skills that transfer at any speed.
- **The ankle injury statistic:** A significant percentage of enduro ankle injuries occur when riders place feet on unstable or sharp surfaces during technical sections. Coaches who teach feet-up creep as a safety technique see reductions in these injuries in students.
