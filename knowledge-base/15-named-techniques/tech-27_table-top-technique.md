# Table Top Technique

## 1. OVERVIEW

A table-top obstacle is a flat-topped elevated feature — a rock plateau, a constructed wooden table, or a natural shelf — with a leading edge, a flat upper surface, and a back edge that drops back to the trail level. The rider must lift the front wheel over the leading edge, cross the flat top, and safely descend off the back edge. Unlike a log (which is crossed in one continuous motion), a table-top has three distinct phases: the entry (getting on), the flat-top crossing (staying on and moving), and the exit (getting off). Each phase has different technical requirements. Table-tops appear in enduro events and are a common obstacle on constructed trail courses. They range from simple low platforms to tall, wide structures that require significant approach speed and airborne management.

## 2. CORE PRINCIPLES

### Principle 1: Front Wheel Height Must Match the Table Height at Entry
The front wheel must be lifted to the table height — not above, not below. Too low and the front hits the leading edge face. Too high and the front overshoots the flat top and the bike pitches forward aggressively on the far side of the leading edge. The lift must be calibrated precisely to the table height, placing the front wheel cleanly on the flat top surface. This calibration comes from reading the table height at approach and matching the clutch pop force to it.

### Principle 2: The Flat Top Is Not Free — Maintain Momentum
Riders often make the mistake of relaxing after clearing the leading edge — the hard part seems done. But the flat top requires maintained momentum. If speed drops too much on the flat top, the rider may not have enough to lift or carry the front off the back edge — either stopping on the table or exiting too slowly for a controlled descent. Maintained or increased throttle across the flat top is always needed.

### Principle 3: The Back Edge Exit Has Two Options — Roll Off or Fly Off
The back edge exit depends on approach speed across the flat top. At low speed, the bike rolls off the back edge — a rock step drop executed at the back edge. At higher speed, the bike launches off the back edge and becomes briefly airborne — a small kicker exit. Knowing which will happen allows the rider to prepare the correct body position before arriving at the back edge.

### Principle 4: Body Position Transitions Through Three Phases
Entry body position: weight slightly rearward for the front lift. Flat top body position: weight returning to centre or slightly forward to maintain momentum. Exit body position: weight rearward for the back edge drop or launch. These are three distinct positions requiring active transition — not a single static position held throughout the crossing.

### Principle 5: Rear Wheel Must Drive Over the Leading Edge
After the front wheel is on the flat top, the rear wheel must be driven over the leading edge under power. This is identical to the rear drive phase of the log crossing — maintained throttle drives the rear up and over the leading edge. Cutting throttle here stops the rear at the leading edge and stalls the crossing. This is the most common mid-crossing failure.

## 3. TECHNIQUE BREAKDOWN BY LEVEL

### 3a. Beginner Level
**Goal:** Cross a low table-top (25–35 cm) at low speed — front lift onto the table, drive across, controlled exit.

**Step-by-Step Execution:**
1. Approach the table at 12–15 km/h, weight slightly rearward.
2. Pull the clutch in and blip the throttle to prepare a clutch pop.
3. As the front wheel reaches the leading edge, release the clutch — the front lifts to table height.
4. As the front wheel places on the flat top, shift weight forward slightly.
5. Maintain or increase throttle as the rear wheel approaches the leading edge — drive it over.
6. Both wheels now on flat top. Maintain throttle — don't relax.
7. Eyes on the back edge. Weight shifts slightly rearward as it approaches.
8. Front wheel reaches the back edge. Allow it to drop forward.
9. Rear wheel follows. Both wheels off the table. Forward drive continues.

**What the Rider Should Feel:**
- Clutch pop: A surge as the front rises — the front tyre leaving the ground and lifting.
- Rear drive-over: Resistance as the rear tyre hits the leading edge, then a clearing sensation as it drives up and over.
- Flat top: Brief stability — both wheels on a flat surface. Almost normal riding for a moment.
- Back edge: The front drops — a slight forward pitch, then the rear follows.

**What Good Looks Like:**
At 15 km/h, the rider lifts the front onto the table with a clutch pop. The rear drives over the leading edge. Throttle maintained across the flat top. At the back edge, weight rearward and the bike drops off cleanly.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Throttle cut after front clears leading edge:** Rear stops at the edge — partially on, partially off, bike stalled across the leading edge. → Maintain throttle until the rear is fully on the flat top.
- **Too slow across the flat top:** Bike decelerates to a stop on the table. → Throttle maintained or increased after the rear clears the leading edge.
- **No back edge preparation:** Rider surprises themselves at the back edge — weight forward, front dives. → Assess the back edge before approaching. Weight rearward before the back edge arrives.

### 3b. Intermediate Level
**Goal:** Cross table-tops of 35–50 cm at moderate speed — adapting entry technique to height and speed, managing exit type.

**Progression Markers from Beginner:** Rider can cross low table-tops. All three phases are managed. No stalls at the leading edge.

**Step-by-Step Execution:**
1. Approach at 18–22 km/h. Assess table height — how high does the front need to lift?
2. Weight slightly rearward 2 metres before entry.
3. Calibrated clutch pop — sized to lift the front to table height, not higher.
4. Front wheel on flat top. Immediately shift weight forward and maintain throttle.
5. Rear drives over the leading edge — committed throttle through this moment.
6. Read the back edge: how much flat top remains? Is this going to be a roll exit or a launch exit?
7. If roll exit: weight clearly rearward 1 metre before back edge. Allow front to drop off cleanly.
8. If launch exit: maintain speed. Weight slightly rearward for rear-wheel-first landing. Land and absorb.

**What the Rider Should Feel at Each Stage:**
- Approach: Speed set, entry technique chosen. Clutch slipping for the pop.
- Front lift and placement: Front wheel on the flat top. A brief landing compression felt through the bars.
- Rear drive over leading edge: Maintained throttle — rear tyre contacts the leading edge and drives up. Felt as a brief resistance then a clearing.
- Flat top crossing: Both wheels on the flat top. Speed maintained. The table may feel longer than expected — focus on the back edge ahead.
- Back edge approach: The back edge is visible. Weight shifts rearward. Speed assessment: will this be a roll or a launch?
- Exit: Roll-off — front drops, weight rearward. Or launch — front pitches off the edge, brief air, landing absorption.

**What Good Looks Like:**
At 20 km/h approaching a 40 cm table, the rider clutch-pops to table height, drives across the flat, and at the back edge (with appropriate speed) either rolls off with weight rearward or launches briefly and absorbs the landing. All three phases are deliberate and connected.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Under-speed for front lift:** Front hits the leading edge face rather than clearing it. → More approach speed or more aggressive clutch pop.
- **Too fast over flat top — rear catching back edge:** Speed too high — the rear wheel hits the back edge from above rather than rolling off. This pitches the bike forward. → Moderate speed across the flat top. Assess before approaching.
- **No weight shift for back edge:** Rider centred or forward as they reach the back edge — a roll-off pitches them forward. → Weight rearward before the back edge, not at it.

### 3c. Advanced Level
**Goal:** Cross large table-tops at pace — including airborne entry and airborne exit as part of a continuous high-speed rhythm.

**Progression Markers from Intermediate:** Rider manages all three phases of a moderate table-top. Transitions are smooth. Exit type is managed.

**Step-by-Step Execution — High Speed:**
1. Approach at 35–45 km/h. Speed carries the front wheel up onto the flat top — the kicker effect of the leading edge provides the lift.
2. Front wheel lands on flat top — absorb through the arms.
3. Rear wheel drives over the leading edge under full throttle.
4. Brief flat top crossing — at pace this is under 1 second. Eyes already on the back edge.
5. Back edge approaches fast. Weight is already rearward.
6. The bike launches off the back edge — a natural kicker. Airborne.
7. Pitch assessment in the air — correct if needed with throttle.
8. Land rear-wheel-first or flat. Deep knee absorption.

**What Good Looks Like:**
At 40 km/h, the rider launches the kicker of the leading edge, lands on the flat top with both wheels, drives across, and launches off the back edge — a small airborne phase — and absorbs the landing cleanly. The entire crossing took 2 seconds.

**Failure Modes and Causes:**
- **Under-speed — front hits leading edge face:** Approach speed too low for the entry technique chosen. → More speed, or switch to clutch pop entry.
- **Too fast over flat top — launches off back edge unexpectedly:** Not prepared for airborne exit. → Assess flat-top crossing speed. Prepare for exit type before the back edge arrives.
- **Rear catching back edge:** Table is narrow and the rear wheel hits the back edge as the front is landing — the bike is still crossing when it should be off. → More momentum across the flat. Or: a clutch pop off the leading edge to go airborne for the entire table.

## 4. DIAGNOSTIC CUES (AI COACHING ENGINE REFERENCE)

### Visual Cues — Correct Technique
- Front wheel clearly on flat top surface — not on the leading edge face
- Rider weight rearward at back edge — visible body position shift before exit
- Smooth three-phase crossing — entry, flat, exit with continuous forward motion
- Throttle maintained through all three phases — no visible engine note drop

### Visual Cues — Error Indicators
- Rear wheel stopped at leading edge — bike straddling the edge, throttle cut after front cleared
- Rider body forward at back edge — no preparation for exit, front dives
- Bike stopping on the flat top — insufficient momentum after rear clears the leading edge
- Rider looking at the leading edge face — not reading the back edge and exit

### Audio Cues
- Correct: Engine note maintained through all three phases — no throttle reduction
- Error: Engine note dropping after front clears — throttle cut, rear stopping

### Sensor Cues
- Correct: Forward speed consistent across all three phases — no significant deceleration on flat top
- Error: Speed drop on the flat top — momentum lost, risk of stopping on table

## 5. COACHING CUES & LANGUAGE

### Positive Reinforcement
- "Three phases — entry, flat, exit. All three clean. That's complete table-top technique."
- "Weight was rearward before the back edge. No surprise at the exit."
- "Throttle held through all three phases — rear drove over the leading edge perfectly."
- "You read the table width and knew what exit you were getting before you were even on it. That's preparation."

### Corrective Feedback
- "Throttle off after the front cleared — rear stopped at the edge. Hold throttle through all three phases. The rear needs to drive over the leading edge."
- "You weren't ready for the back edge. Read the table width and prepare for the exit before you're on the table."
- "Front hit the leading edge face — lift was too low. More speed or a more committed pop to get the front to table height."

### Progression Prompts
- "You've got the low table. Now try the taller one — same principle but more lift height needed at entry."
- "Try a high-speed crossing — can you maintain enough speed to launch off the back edge? Read the landing zone first."
- "Next run, tell me before you start: is this going to be a roll exit or a launch exit? Plan it, then execute it."

## 6. DRILLS & PRACTICE EXERCISES

### Drill 1: Phase Isolation — Entry Only
**Purpose:** Perfect the front lift and rear drive-over independently before adding the full crossing.
**Setup:** A table-top with a stop-line marked on the flat top — stop after clearing the leading edge.
**Execution:**
1. Approach at entry speed. Execute the clutch pop to put the front on the table.
2. Drive the rear over the leading edge with maintained throttle.
3. Stop on the flat top. Assess: was the front wheel placed cleanly? Did the rear drive over smoothly?
**Success Criteria:** Rear drives over the leading edge cleanly on 8 out of 10 attempts with no stall.
**Duration/Reps:** 15 passes. **Progression:** Remove the stop-line — add the flat top crossing.

### Drill 2: Full Crossing Low Speed
**Purpose:** Complete all three phases at low speed with focus on transitions between phases.
**Setup:** A 30 cm table-top.
**Execution:**
1. Execute entry phase as in Drill 1.
2. Cross the flat top with maintained throttle.
3. Prepare for the back edge — weight rearward before arrival.
4. Execute the roll exit.
**Success Criteria:** All three phases completed cleanly on 7 out of 10 passes. No stop, no front dive at exit.
**Duration/Reps:** 15 passes. **Progression:** Increase speed by 5 km/h on each round.

### Drill 3: Back Edge Preparation
**Purpose:** Develop the automatic habit of weight rearward before the back edge arrives.
**Setup:** A table-top with a marked warning zone 1 metre before the back edge.
**Execution:**
1. Cross the flat top normally.
2. When the front wheel crosses the warning zone marker, weight must shift rearward.
3. Exit from the rearward position.
**Success Criteria:** Weight rearward before the back edge on 9 out of 10 passes. No forward pitch on exit.
**Duration/Reps:** 20 passes. **Progression:** Remove the marking — develop the awareness without the visual cue.

### Drill 4: Speed Variation Exit Types
**Purpose:** Experience both roll-off and launch exit types intentionally, understanding which speed produces which outcome.
**Setup:** A table-top with a clear landing zone.
**Execution:**
1. 5 slow passes (12–15 km/h): roll exit. Observe what it feels like.
2. 5 fast passes (25–30 km/h): launch exit. Observe what it feels like.
3. 5 medium passes: predict the exit type from the approach speed before reaching the back edge.
**Success Criteria:** Rider correctly predicts exit type based on speed on 8 out of 10 passes.
**Duration/Reps:** 15 passes. **Progression:** Unknown speed approach — predict exit type entirely from feel while crossing.

## 8. TERRAIN & CONTEXT VARIATIONS

### Wooden Table-Top (Constructed)
Consistent surface, predictable leading and back edges. Good learning environment. May be slippery when wet — entry speed adjustment needed on wet wood.

### Natural Rock Table
Irregular surface — the flat top may not be perfectly flat. Traction varies. Assessment walk recommended before riding. The back edge may be uneven — exit line must be chosen.

### Wet Table
Both the leading edge and flat top are slippery. Rear wheel drive-over the leading edge is less reliable — wheel spin risk. Approach at lower speed. Very wet wooden tables may require manual entry or walking.

### Narrow Table-Top
Narrow flat top means little time between entry and exit. Weight transition from rearward (entry) to forward (flat) to rearward (exit) must happen very quickly. At pace, this means the flat top is essentially just a momentary contact between two kicker events.

### Tall Table (40+ cm)
More aggressive clutch pop required for entry. Higher lift means more time for the front wheel to hang before placement — body weight management is more critical. Exit launch height is also higher.

## 9. EXPERT INSIGHTS & SOURCES

### Key Insights
- **The flat top is not passive:** Many riders treat the flat top as a rest phase — a moment between the hard parts. Coaches consistently note that maintaining momentum across the flat is what separates smooth crossings from stalls or awkward exits. The throttle through the flat top is just as important as the entry technique.
- **Phase thinking:** The three-phase mental model (entry, flat, exit) is the most effective coaching framework for table-tops. It helps riders plan each transition rather than treating the obstacle as one event. Riders who think in phases manage the transitions; riders who see it as one obstacle rush the entry and forget the exit.
- **Walk before first attempt:** Any new table-top — especially natural rock tables — should be walked to assess: leading edge height, flat top surface quality, back edge drop height, and landing zone quality. This 60-second investment prevents the most common table-top crashes.

- **Exit preparation starts at the entry:** Coaches who teach table-top technique note that the most common failure — no weight rearward at the back edge — is caused by the rider's attention being entirely on the entry. The mental discipline is to begin thinking about the back edge the moment the front wheel is on the flat top. The entry is managed; the exit must be prepared.
- **Speed calibration is the key variable:** At the same table, different approach speeds produce fundamentally different techniques — a slow approach needs a clutch pop entry and a roll exit; a fast approach produces an airborne entry and a launch exit. Riders who ride tables at varying speeds without adapting their technique will be inconsistent. Speed calibration and technique matching is the advanced skill.
