# Log Crossing (High Speed)

## 1. OVERVIEW

Log crossing at high speed (above 30 km/h) is fundamentally different from low-speed crossing — the approach speed provides enough momentum to carry the wheel over the log without lifting, so the technique shifts from lifting to absorbing. At high speed, trying to lift the front wheel deliberately before the log is dangerous — the timing window is too small, and an incorrect lift can pitch the bike forward violently on the far side. Instead, the rider uses attack position with active leg absorption, a loose grip that lets the bike move freely, and maintained throttle — allowing the bike to deflect over the log as a mechanical object rather than trying to finesse it. The rider's job at high speed is to get out of the way of the log and let the bike handle it.

## 2. CORE PRINCIPLES

### Principle 1: Momentum Carries the Wheel Over — Do Not Fight It
At sufficient speed, the front wheel's rotational momentum and the bike's forward kinetic energy carry the wheel up and over the log without active input. Trying to lift the front deliberately at this speed disrupts the natural arc of the wheel's path and often results in over-pitching on the landing. The correct mental model is that the log is a bump — the suspension and the rider's legs handle bumps.

### Principle 2: The Rider Absorbs the Impact — The Bike Deflects
In attack position with bent knees, the rider's legs act as a secondary suspension above the bike's suspension. When the front wheel hits the log, the bike pitches forward — the rider's legs extend downward as the front rises, keeping the torso relatively stationary. When the rear wheel hits the log, the bike pitches again — the legs absorb this too. The rider's upper body should move very little throughout the crossing. This upper/lower body decoupling (BIOMECH-09) is the defining skill.

### Principle 3: Loose Grip Allows the Bars to Move
At high speed, the handlebar deflection when hitting a log is significant. A rider gripping tightly forces their arms to absorb this deflection — transmitted to the torso. A rider with a loose grip allows the bars to rotate slightly on the log impact, with the wrists and hands absorbing a small amount of the deflection. This reduces arm pump and prevents the rider from being thrown forward.

### Principle 4: Throttle Maintained Prevents Front-End Dive on Landing
When the front wheel clears the log and drops on the far side, maintaining throttle keeps the engine driving the rear wheel and prevents the bike from nose-diving. Cutting throttle as the front crosses the log removes the rear wheel drive — the bike becomes a free-falling object on the far side. Maintained throttle means the rear wheel continues to provide a stabilising drive force through the landing.

### Principle 5: Pre-Impact Position Determines Post-Impact Outcome
The body position at the moment of log impact determines what happens next. A rider who is forward-weighted at impact will be pitched further forward — potentially over the bars. A rider who is slightly rearward-weighted at impact will be pitched less dramatically and will recover more quickly. Pre-loading slightly rearward in the 1–2 metres before the log is the key preparation.

## 3. TECHNIQUE BREAKDOWN BY LEVEL

### 3a. Beginner Level
**Goal:** Cross small logs (15–20 cm) at 25–30 km/h without deliberate front lifting — using body position to manage the impact.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Attack position with bent knees — active legs ready
- Slight rearward weight shift 1 metre before the log
- Loose grip throughout the crossing
- Maintained throttle

**What Good Looks Like:**
Approaching the log at 25 km/h in attack position, the rider's weight shifts slightly rearward 1 metre before. The front wheel hits the log and pitches up — the rider's legs absorb the front rise. The rear wheel hits — another pitch, another absorption. The bike crosses. The rider's head barely moves.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Braking hard before the log:** Removes approach speed, converting a high-speed crossing into a slow crossing that then requires front lifting — but the speed is still too high for controlled low-speed technique. → Maintain speed for a high-speed crossing or slow fully for a low-speed technique. Do not half-commit.
- **Body slammed forward on impact:** Rider is forward-weighted at impact — the pitch amplifies. → Slight rearward pre-load before impact.
- **Gripping tightly:** Bar deflection on impact throws the rider. → Loosen the grip before impact.

### 3b. Intermediate Level
**Goal:** Cross logs of 25–35 cm at 35–45 km/h — managing the larger pitch with leg absorption and correct body position.

**Progression Markers from Beginner:** Rider can cross small logs without braking. Body absorbs the crossing. No dramatic head or torso movement.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Anticipating the crossing from further away — pre-loading the body position 2 metres before the log
- Active leg absorption: legs extending downward as the front pitches up, compressing as the front descends, extending again for the rear crossing
- Weight rearward during the front phase — allowing the front pitch to happen without amplification
- Throttle maintained or slightly increased through both crossing phases

**What Good Looks Like:**
At 40 km/h, the rider reads the log at 10 metres, pre-positions the body slightly rearward, and hits the log with loose grip and bent knees. The front wheel hits and the bike pitches — the rider's legs extend and the torso holds height. The rear hits — the legs compress again. The rider exits the crossing at nearly the same speed they entered. Head barely moved.

**What the Rider Should Feel at Each Stage:**
- Pre-log (2 metres): Slight rearward weight shift — felt as the weight moving from balls of feet toward heels on the pegs. Knees deepen their bend slightly in preparation.
- Front impact: A sharp compression through the front fork — the front rises. Felt as an upward push through the front of the bike while the rear is still level. Legs extend to compensate, keeping the torso still. Grip loosens as the bars deflect.
- Front airborne phase (over the log): A brief moment where the front wheel is at the log apex — the bike is pitched forward slightly. The rider feels this as a weight shift toward the front.
- Front landing (far side): The front drops. Felt as a compression through the bars as the forks absorb the landing. If the rider was rearward-weighted correctly, this is manageable. If forward-weighted, it feels like being thrown.
- Rear impact: The rear wheel hits the log. The back of the bike rises — the rider's legs compress (rear pegs pushing upward). Felt as a strong push up through the rear pegs. Legs flex to absorb.
- Exit: Both wheels on the far side. Speed maintained. The whole crossing took less than a second.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Body slammed forward on front landing:** Rider was too forward-weighted going in. The front landing pitch threw the rider's mass forward. Fix: pre-position weight more rearward. The rearward weight must be set before impact, not during.
- **Front washing on landing:** At high speed, the front landing can cause front-end wash if the approach was angled incorrectly or the log surface is different from the approach terrain. Fix: straight approach angle. Do not cross a log at an angle at speed.
- **Rear hop:** The rear wheel hits the log and launches the rear — the bike becomes airborne. Cause: too much approach speed for the log height, or the log is soft (hollow) and deflects. Fix: assess logs before crossing at speed. A hollow log can behave differently.

### 3c. Advanced Level
**Goal:** Cross logs of all sizes at maximum trail speed — seamlessly integrated into continuous riding with zero speed reduction.

**Progression Markers from Intermediate:** Rider can cross 30 cm logs at 40 km/h cleanly. Body absorption is automatic. No conscious thought required.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Crossing multiple logs in quick succession at pace — rhythmic absorption
- Adapting to log height variation mid-section without slowing
- Crossed logs (diagonal approach angle management)
- Combining log crossing with terrain before and after — entering from a corner, exiting into another

**What Good Looks Like:**
At full trail speed on a section with three logs in 15 metres, the rider barely appears to react to the logs. A subtle rearward weight shift before each, a brief absorption of each crossing, and continuous speed. The logs appear as minor ripples in the ride rather than distinct obstacles.

**Failure Modes and Causes:**
- **Body slammed forward on a large log:** Pre-load was calibrated for a small log but the log was larger. Fix: rearward weight shift must be proportional to anticipated log height — larger logs require more rearward pre-load.
- **Front wash on landing:** Front wheel impacts the far side at an angle or with too much weight. Fix: maintain approach angle, ensure rearward pre-load was sufficient.
- **Rear hop causing bike to become airborne:** At very high speed over a large log, the rear can launch dramatically. Fix: assess log height visually before attempting at high speed. Very large logs require speed reduction.

## 4. DIAGNOSTIC CUES (AI COACHING ENGINE REFERENCE)

### Visual Cues
- Correct: Rider's head and torso hold position through the crossing — legs absorb the movement
- Correct: No speed reduction visible approaching or through the crossing
- Correct: Slight rearward body position 1–2 metres before the log — visible weight shift
- Error: Rider body slammed forward — visible dramatic forward pitch of upper body on front landing
- Error: Speed reduction before log — rider not committed to high-speed technique
- Error: Rear end launching visibly — rear wheel significantly airborne on rear crossing

### Audio Cues
- Correct: Engine note constant through the crossing — no throttle reduction
- Correct: Two distinct impact sounds — front crossing and rear crossing — brief and sharp
- Error: Engine note drops before or during crossing — throttle reduced
- Error: Single prolonged impact — front and rear hitting simultaneously, speed too low for separation

### Sensor Cues
- Correct: Constant forward speed trace through the crossing — no deceleration event
- Correct: Two vertical acceleration spikes in sequence — front crossing and rear crossing
- Error: Speed trace drops before the crossing — rider braked before log
- Error: Large pitch angle spike post-crossing — rider was thrown forward by poor pre-position

## 5. COACHING CUES & LANGUAGE

### Positive Reinforcement
- "Head stayed level the whole crossing — that's your legs doing their job. That's elite absorption."
- "Speed didn't drop through that log. You committed and the bike handled it. That's high-speed technique."
- "I can see the rearward pre-load — slight weight back before the log, then your legs absorbed both phases. Perfect sequence."

### Corrective Feedback
- "You braked before the log — now you're too slow for high-speed technique but too fast for low-speed. Commit to one or the other. Either maintain speed and absorb, or fully slow for a clutch-pop lift."
- "Your upper body slammed forward on the front landing — you were too far forward when you hit it. Pre-load rearward before you get to the log. A metre before, not at the moment of impact."
- "Your grip is too tight. The bar deflected and threw your upper body. Loosen the grip before the impact — let the bars move independently from your arms."
- "Throttle off through the crossing — the front dove on landing because there was no rear drive. Hold throttle. Maintained throttle is what keeps the bike stable through the landing."

### Progression Prompts
- "You've got 30 km/h over this log. Try 40. The faster you go, the more the momentum handles the crossing and the less you have to do. Speed is your friend here."
- "Next step is three consecutive logs at pace. The technique doesn't change — but your timing has to be faster for each because they're coming quickly."
- "Try it with your eyes closed for the last 2 metres before the log. You should feel the impact without seeing it coming — that's what high-speed terrain reading eventually becomes."

## 6. DRILLS & PRACTICE EXERCISES

### Drill 1: Speed Threshold Finder
**Purpose:** Identify the exact speed at which a specific log transitions from lift-required to absorb-only.
**Setup:** A 20 cm log on flat ground.
**Execution:**
1. Pass at 15 km/h — note what technique is required.
2. Pass at 20 km/h — same log, does it still need a lift?
3. Pass at 25 km/h — at this speed, the momentum should carry the wheel.
4. Identify where the threshold is for this specific log.
**Success Criteria:** Rider can articulate and demonstrate the threshold speed for a given log height.
**Duration/Reps:** 5 passes at each speed.
**Progression:** Repeat with a larger log (30 cm). Threshold will be higher.

### Drill 2: Absorption Position Training
**Purpose:** Build the rearward pre-load and leg absorption pattern in isolation.
**Setup:** Flat ground with a 5 cm bump or constructed ramp.
**Execution:**
1. Hit the bump at 30 km/h.
2. Pre-load rearward 2 metres before.
3. Absorb with bent knees — head stays level.
4. Throttle maintained through.
5. Have someone video — assess head movement.
**Success Criteria:** Head height variation under 5 cm through the bump crossing.
**Duration/Reps:** 20 passes.
**Progression:** Increase bump height to 10 cm, then to a real log.

### Drill 3: Multiple Log Rhythm
**Purpose:** Cross multiple logs at pace without slowing between them.
**Setup:** Three logs (20 cm) placed 6 metres apart.
**Execution:**
1. Approach at 35 km/h.
2. Pre-load for log 1.
3. Absorb crossing.
4. Immediately pre-load for log 2 — 4 metres is not much time.
5. Absorb log 2.
6. Pre-load for log 3.
7. Assess: did speed hold through all three?
**Success Criteria:** Speed maintained within 10% through all three logs.
**Duration/Reps:** 10 runs.
**Progression:** Reduce spacing to 4 metres.

### Drill 4: Unknown Log Speed
**Purpose:** Develop the instinctive pre-load and absorption at speed without specific preparation.
**Setup:** A trail section with 2–3 logs of varying height, approaching at trail speed.
**Execution:**
1. Ride the trail section at normal pace.
2. React to each log as it appears — no preparation beyond normal trail riding.
3. Assess technique quality after each crossing.
**Success Criteria:** All logs crossed without braking and without dramatic body pitch.
**Duration/Reps:** 10 trail passes.
**Progression:** Increase trail speed.

## 8. TERRAIN & CONTEXT VARIATIONS

### Fast Trail (Single-Track at Pace)
Primary application. The technique is part of natural flow — logs are encountered and crossed without specific preparation at speed.

### Downhill
Approach speed is increased by gradient. The front landing on the far side is harder because the far side is lower. More rearward pre-load needed. Very large logs on downhills may require speed reduction.

### Uphill
Approach speed is reduced by gradient. The crossing may transition back to a low-speed technique requirement depending on the gradient. Monitor approach speed and choose technique accordingly.

### Wet/Muddy Conditions
The far side of the log may be muddy or slippery. The front landing can cause a wash if the surface grip is low. Consider this when deciding between high-speed (harder landing) and low-speed (more controlled placement) technique.

### Multiple Consecutive Logs
Rhythmic crossing technique — the absorption of one crossing is the pre-load for the next. Spacing determines whether each crossing can be individually prepared or whether a continuous absorption rhythm is required.

## 9. EXPERT INSIGHTS & SOURCES

### Key Insights
- **The "it's a bump" mindset:** The most effective coaching reframe for high-speed log crossings is to tell riders "treat it like a big bump — your suspension knows what to do." This removes the instinct to intervene and allows the bike and body to handle the crossing naturally.
- **Speed is protective:** Counterintuitively, more speed over a log is often safer than less speed, once the threshold is exceeded. At the right speed, the crossing is over before the rider can make it worse. Hesitation (braking at the wrong moment) creates the worst outcomes.
- **Video evidence:** Slow-motion footage of good and poor log crossings is one of the most effective coaching tools for this technique — seeing the head move (poor) vs stay still (good) is immediately understandable.
- **Hollow log risk:** In enduro, wood debris can include partially decayed logs that may break, flex, or collapse under the bike's weight. Riders who cross logs regularly should develop a habit of scanning log surface for signs of decay. A collapsing log at high speed can be catastrophic.
