# Rock Splitter Entry

## 1. OVERVIEW

A rock splitter is the technique of riding the bike through a narrow gap between two rocks — where the gap is close to or not much wider than the handlebars — requiring precise entry angle, speed control, grip loosening, and correct foot and body positioning to pass through cleanly. In technical enduro terrain, rock splitters are common navigational features: a gap between two large boulders that forms the only viable line through an otherwise impassable section. The technique is demanding because the margin for error is centimetres, the consequences of getting the angle wrong (handlebar catch and sudden stop) are significant, and the mental commitment required to drive into a tight gap at appropriate speed is substantial. It combines precision line reading, body positioning, speed modulation, and grip technique in a single challenging move.

## 2. CORE PRINCIPLES

### Principle 1: The Entry Angle Is Everything
The entry angle of the bike relative to the gap determines whether the handlebars will clear the rocks or catch. A handlebar is typically 75–80 cm wide. If the gap is 90 cm, the bars will only clear if the bike is aimed precisely through the centre of the gap with the handlebars perpendicular to the gap opening. Any angle — even a few degrees off-centre — reduces the effective clearance. Reading the gap and setting the entry angle is the primary technical challenge.

### Principle 2: Speed Must Match the Commitment Level Required
Too slow and the bike becomes unstable in the gap (balance lost without enough forward momentum). Too fast and there is no time to make micro-corrections if the angle is slightly off. The correct speed is the minimum needed to maintain balance and control through the gap — usually just above walking pace for very tight gaps, up to 15–20 km/h for wider gaps with more clearance.

### Principle 3: Loose Grip Allows the Bars to Deflect Safely
If the handlebar end contacts a rock, a rider with a death grip will be thrown forward or sideways — the rigid arm transmits the full impact force. A rider with a loose grip allows the bar to deflect slightly and the wrist to absorb the contact. This is why counterintuitively, grip loosening is part of the technique — not gripping tighter as the gap narrows.

### Principle 4: Body Centralisation Avoids Rock Contact on the Rider
In a very tight gap, the rider's knees, elbows, or feet could contact the rocks on either side. The rider must centralise their body — elbows in, knees in, feet on the pegs — to reduce the effective width of the rider-bike combination. Foot position is particularly important: a foot hanging outward will contact the rock wall before the handlebar does.

### Principle 5: Commitment Once Aligned — Do Not Hesitate
Once the entry angle is set and the bike is aimed at the gap correctly, the rider must commit. Hesitating mid-entry (braking, steering input, body shift) changes the angle and can cause the handlebar to catch. The entry is prepared in advance — the approach sets the line — and the gap itself is crossed with minimum intervention, maintaining the pre-set angle.

## 3. TECHNIQUE BREAKDOWN BY LEVEL

### 3a. Beginner Level
**Goal:** Understand gap assessment and ride through wider gaps (handlebar width plus 30–40 cm clearance) accurately.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Assessing the width of a gap before attempting it
- Setting the entry angle from the approach, not mid-gap
- Maintaining steady speed through the gap
- Centralising body to reduce rider width

**What Good Looks Like:**
The rider stops or slows before the gap, assesses it, aims from 3–4 metres away, and drives through at walking pace with steady throttle. The handlebars clear cleanly. No panic inside the gap.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Attempting gaps that are too narrow:** A handlebar catch at speed is a serious crash. → Assess first. If in doubt, measure the gap with your hand before riding it.
- **Steering inside the gap:** Adjusting the angle mid-gap changes the trajectory and can cause a catch. → Set the angle on the approach and hold it.
- **Accelerating inside the gap:** More speed amplifies any angle error. → Steady speed or slight deceleration through the gap.

### 3b. Intermediate Level
**Goal:** Navigate genuine rock splitter gaps — close to handlebar width — at appropriate speed with confidence.

**Progression Markers from Beginner:** Rider can accurately assess gap widths. Entry angle is set from the approach. Body is centralised through gaps.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Reading the gap geometry from 5–10 metres away — mentally planning the entry line
- Setting the approach so the bike is aimed at the centre of the gap before entering
- Grip loosening — conscious relaxation of grip as the bars enter the gap
- Foot positioning: feet on the outside edge of the pegs to maximise leg clearance
- Throttle management: holding steady speed or using clutch feather for creep pace

**What Good Looks Like:**
The rider reads the gap at 10 metres, identifies the correct entry angle, angles their approach accordingly, loosens their grip slightly as the handlebars enter the gap, and passes through cleanly at creep pace. The handlebars are centred in the gap throughout. No body parts contact the rocks.

**What the Rider Should Feel at Each Stage:**
- Gap assessment (10 metres out): Eyes scan the gap — mental image of where the centreline is, what the clearance looks like, whether there are rocks at or above handlebar height that create an additional vertical constraint.
- Approach alignment: The bike is turned to aim at the centreline of the gap. This is felt as a deliberate steering input before the gap, not during it.
- Grip relaxation: As the handlebars approach the rock faces, the grip loosens deliberately. Felt as the fingers releasing slightly — not letting go, but removing the death grip. The wrists become slightly more flexible.
- Entry: Front wheel passes into the gap. Attention on the handlebar tips — the rider has peripheral awareness of the proximity of the rocks. Speed is held steady through clutch and throttle.
- Gap passage: The bike is moving through. The rider keeps elbows in, knees close, feet on the inner edge of the pegs. The sides of the gap may be visible in peripheral vision — centimetres away.
- Exit: Front wheel clears the far side. Body begins to relax from the concentrated focus. Throttle re-established for forward drive.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Handlebar catch from wrong angle:** The approach line was not centred — one bar is closer to the rock than the other. Fix: approach alignment must centre both bars symmetrically relative to the gap.
- **Death grip causing arm catch transmission:** Bar end contacts rock slightly — rigid arms transmit the force and rider is thrown. Fix: loose grip. Practice gripping lightly through the approach.
- **Wide foot catching rock:** Outside of foot or boot contacts the rock wall inside the gap. Fix: feet on the inner edge of the pegs, toes pointed slightly inward.
- **Speed too high for gap width:** Any angle error is amplified at higher speed — the bike travels further before the rider can correct. Fix: slow is smooth for very tight gaps.

### 3c. Advanced Level
**Goal:** Navigate rock splitters at moderate pace with minimal mental overhead — as part of a fluid technical line rather than a special event.

**Progression Markers from Intermediate:** Rider can navigate genuine rock splitters cleanly and consistently. Gap assessment is accurate. No panic inside gaps.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Assessing and navigating rock splitters without stopping — reading from the approach and committing at speed
- Combining rock splitter entry with immediate obstacle management on the far side
- Managing rock splitters where the exit is not straight — requiring steering input on the exit
- Very tight gaps (handlebar width plus 5–10 cm) at creep pace

**What Good Looks Like:**
Moving at trail speed, the rider identifies a rock splitter at 15 metres, makes a micro-steering correction to align perfectly, loosens grip, and passes through without pausing. On the exit, there is a root network which the rider immediately manages. The rock splitter is navigated as one element of a continuous section, not as a distinct stopping point.

**Failure Modes and Causes:**
- **Handlebar catch and sudden stop:** The most common failure. Cause: angle off-centre, or gap width misjudged. At speed this can throw the rider over the bars. Fix: slow down for all new gaps. Speed comes with repetition and verified angle.
- **Wrong angle hang-up:** The bike enters at an angle, one bar catches, the bike pivots and becomes wedged in the gap. Fix: approach angle precision. If a gap has been entered at a bad angle, it may be necessary to lift the bar end over the rock to extract.
- **Speed too high (no time for angle correction):** The approach was not aligned correctly and the speed provided no time to correct. The bar catches. Fix: slow down before the gap. There is no speed advantage to hitting a rock splitter fast — it should always be approached slowly enough to be clean.

## 4. DIAGNOSTIC CUES (AI COACHING ENGINE REFERENCE)

### Visual Cues
- Correct: Rider's approach trajectory is aligned with the gap centreline from 5+ metres away
- Correct: Body centralised — elbows in, feet not hanging outward
- Correct: Steady, slow speed maintained through the gap
- Error: Rider steering inside the gap — steering input visible at the entry point
- Error: Handlebar catch visible — bike pivots or jerks at gap entry
- Error: Foot position wide — feet hanging outside the pegs into the gap space

### Audio Cues
- Correct: No impact sound — bars and body clear the rocks
- Error: Metallic scrape or impact sound — handlebar end contacted rock
- Correct: Engine at creep-pace RPM — consistent, steady
- Error: Throttle blip inside the gap — speed increase causing angle amplification

### Sensor Cues
- Correct: Straight forward speed trace through the gap — no lateral movement
- Error: Abrupt deceleration event inside the gap — handlebar catch
- Error: Lateral acceleration spike inside the gap — handlebar contact forcing a turn

## 5. COACHING CUES & LANGUAGE

### Positive Reinforcement
- "Clean through. Your approach angle was perfect — I could see you aligned at 10 metres. That's the preparation that made the difference."
- "Loose grip through the gap — exactly right. If that bar had touched, your wrist would have absorbed it, not your arm."
- "Body centralised the whole way — elbows in, feet on the pegs. You made yourself smaller. That's correct."

### Corrective Feedback
- "You tried to steer inside the gap. The angle needs to be set before you enter — not during. Stop 3 metres before the gap and aim yourself from there."
- "Grip is too tight. Relax the hands as you enter. If a bar touches, a loose grip saves you. A tight grip throws you."
- "Your foot was hanging out to the left. That foot was going to hit the rock before the handlebar. Feet inside — on the inner edge of the pegs."
- "That gap was too narrow for your approach speed. For gaps that close to handlebar width, walk pace is the right call. Speed first, then precision — not speed through precision sections."

### Progression Prompts
- "You're comfortable at the gaps with 30 cm clearance. Now try the one with 15 cm clearance. Same technique — the only difference is the margin of error."
- "Next run, don't stop before the gap — keep moving and align your approach from 8 metres. Practice doing the angle calculation while moving."
- "Once you can navigate the gap cleanly, work on what's on the other side. The gap leads into a root section. Connect the entry to the exit challenge."

## 6. DRILLS & PRACTICE EXERCISES

### Drill 1: Wide Gap Assessment Walk
**Purpose:** Build gap reading accuracy before any riding.
**Setup:** Set up several gaps of different widths using cones or posts: 1.2 m, 1.0 m, 0.9 m, 0.8 m (handlebar width approximately 0.78 m).
**Execution:**
1. Walk to each gap and estimate its width.
2. Check estimate against actual measurement.
3. Ride through each gap from widest to narrowest.
4. Note the approach alignment required for each.
**Success Criteria:** Width estimates accurate to within 5 cm. All gaps navigated without contact.
**Duration/Reps:** 10 passes per gap width.
**Progression:** Remove the widest gaps. Only ride the narrowest.

### Drill 2: Cone Gate Approach Alignment
**Purpose:** Develop approach angle setting from a distance.
**Setup:** Two cones placed 1 metre apart (simulating gap walls), 5 metres of clear approach.
**Execution:**
1. Stop 5 metres from the cones.
2. Aim the bike at the centre of the gap.
3. Ride through without steering inside the cones.
4. Assess: did the approach aim match the actual passage?
**Success Criteria:** Pass through the centre of the cones (within 10 cm of centre) without steering input inside the gate.
**Duration/Reps:** 20 passes.
**Progression:** Reduce cone spacing. Increase approach distance.

### Drill 3: Grip Relaxation Practice
**Purpose:** Build loose grip habit for the approach and passage.
**Setup:** Wide gap (1.5 m) for safety — focus is on grip, not gap.
**Execution:**
1. Approach the gap with normal grip.
2. 2 metres before the gap, consciously relax the grip by 50%.
3. Maintain loose grip through the entire passage.
4. Assess: did the looseness feel controllable?
**Success Criteria:** Rider can describe the specific grip change. Bike control maintained with loose grip through the gap.
**Duration/Reps:** 15 passes.
**Progression:** Reduce gap width as loose grip becomes comfortable.

### Drill 4: Real Rock Splitter Progression
**Purpose:** Apply the technique to a real terrain feature.
**Setup:** A natural rock gap on trail — ideally progressively narrower options available.
**Execution:**
1. Start with the widest available gap.
2. Walk the gap first — assess the width and angle.
3. Ride through at creep pace.
4. Repeat for narrower gaps as confidence grows.
**Success Criteria:** Cleanest available gap on trail navigated without handlebar contact.
**Duration/Reps:** 5 passes per gap width.
**Progression:** Approach at slightly higher speed. Then try without stopping first.

## 8. TERRAIN & CONTEXT VARIATIONS

### Dry Conditions
Standard application — rocks are predictable, gap widths are as assessed. Traction is reliable.

### Wet Conditions
Rocks in the gap may be wet and slippery — foot placement caution if footing is needed. The gap width does not change, but the consequences of a handlebar contact are higher because traction for recovery is lower.

### Gaps With Elevation Change
Some rock splitters slope downhill or are at the entry to a drop. The entry technique is the same but the exit requires immediate attention — managing the speed increase on the far side.

### Gaps Requiring a Turn on Exit
Some rock splitters open into a corner — the rider must navigate the gap and immediately steer for the corner exit. The gap passage must be fast enough to have steering authority on the exit.

### Night Conditions
Gap width assessment is harder in low light. LED lighting must illuminate the gap clearly before entry. If gap width cannot be assessed confidently, walk and inspect before riding.

## 9. EXPERT INSIGHTS & SOURCES

### Key Insights
- **Erzbergrodeo and extreme enduro:** Rock splitter entries are a defining feature of extreme enduro courses. Riders who can navigate them at pace gain significant time — riders who hesitate or catch a bar lose time and risk crash.
- **The loose grip counterintuition:** Experienced coaches consistently note that novice riders grip tighter as gaps narrow — exactly the opposite of correct technique. Teaching the counterintuitive loose grip is a key coaching focus.
- **Line selection as the primary skill:** Professional coaches emphasise that 80% of the rock splitter challenge is the approach alignment — the physical technique inside the gap is almost secondary. Riders who can read and set entry angles accurately clean gaps that look impossible.
- **Walk it first, always for new gaps:** Every professional rider and coach emphasises walking unknown technical sections before riding them. For rock splitters specifically, walking provides accurate gap width assessment and prevents dangerous misjudgements.
