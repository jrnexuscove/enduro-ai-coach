# Brake Slide to Stop

## 1. OVERVIEW

A brake slide to stop is the technique of using a controlled rear wheel slide — initiated by the rear brake — to decelerate and stop the motorcycle at a precisely chosen point, typically at the end of a loose or slippery section, in a corner, or before an obstacle that requires a stationary or near-stationary position. It is distinct from an emergency rear lock because it is deliberate, controlled, and directed — the slide is initiated intentionally to use the available traction and terrain to bring the bike to a stop faster and more precisely than progressive braking would allow in low-traction conditions. It is also distinct from a brake slide used for cornering (TECH-08) because the goal is a complete stop, not continued forward motion on a new line. Correctly executed, the bike arrives at the target stop point with the rear wheel having slid to a controlled stop, the front wheel having provided supplementary braking without washing out, and the rider maintaining balance through the slide and into the stationary position.

## 2. CORE PRINCIPLES

### Principle 1: The Rear Brake Initiates and Controls the Slide
The rear brake is the primary input for a brake slide to stop. Applied firmly but progressively, it causes the rear wheel to decelerate faster than the front, initiating a rear slide. The amount of slide is controlled by rear brake pressure — more pressure, more slide; less pressure, slide reduces. The slide angle (the angle the rear makes with the direction of travel) is managed throughout by rear brake modulation.

### Principle 2: The Front Brake Supplements — But Must Not Cause a Front Wash
The front brake can supplement the rear brake during a brake slide to stop — additional braking force reduces speed faster. But the front brake must not be applied aggressively during the slide, as the front tyre is already managing the directional stability of the bike while the rear is sliding. Aggressive front brake during a rear slide can cause the front to wash — a catastrophic combination of front and rear sliding simultaneously.

### Principle 3: Body Weight Rearward Controls the Slide Angle
The rider's body weight affects where the rear slides. A rearward weight bias keeps the rear tyre loaded and the front lighter — this allows the rear to slide more controllably and prevents the front from washing. As the slide progresses and the bike decelerates, the rider gradually shifts weight forward to prepare for the stationary position.

### Principle 4: The Slide Must Be Directed — Not Just Skidded
A brake slide to stop is not simply locking the rear wheel and hoping. The slide has a direction — the rear slides toward the outside of the intended stop angle, the bike adopts a slight angle to the direction of travel, and the front wheel remains pointed at the stop point. This direction is controlled by the handlebars — small steering inputs while the rear is sliding guide the front wheel toward the target stop position.

### Principle 5: The Stop Point Is the Commitment — Plan Before Initiating
A brake slide to stop is most effective when the stop point is identified before the technique begins. The rider selects the target stop position — a specific point on the terrain — and initiates the slide to arrive there. A brake slide that is started without a target will stop at an unpredictable point and possibly in an unfavourable position.

## 3. TECHNIQUE BREAKDOWN BY LEVEL

### 3a. Beginner Level
**Goal:** Execute a controlled rear brake slide to a stop on flat loose terrain — understanding that the slide is deliberate and directed.

**Step-by-Step Execution:**
1. Identify the stop point — a specific location 10–15 metres ahead.
2. Approach at 20–25 km/h.
3. At 6–8 metres from the stop point: apply rear brake firmly. Not a snap — a firm, progressive application.
4. Rear wheel decelerates faster than forward speed — the rear begins to slide.
5. Body weight rearward — hips back.
6. Light front brake supplement — very light. Just enough to add deceleration. Not the primary input.
7. Guide the front wheel toward the stop point using small steering inputs.
8. As speed approaches zero: weight shifts forward. The slide reduces as speed reduces.
9. Stop at the target point.

**What the Rider Should Feel:**
- Slide initiation: The rear end shifts sideways slightly. The bike adopts a small angle. This is correct.
- Slide control: The rear brake pressure determines the slide angle. More pressure = more angle. Less = less.
- Deceleration: The bike is slowing. The stop point is approaching.
- Stop: Speed reaches zero. Rear slide stops. Bike upright.

**What Good Looks Like:**
On loose flat terrain, approaching a marker at 20 km/h, the rider applies rear brake firmly — the rear begins to slide. Weight rearward. Light front brake supplement. The bike slides to a stop at the marker. Controlled. No front wash. No fall.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Too much front brake:** Front washes during the slide. → Rear brake is primary. Front is supplementary and light.
- **No stop point identified:** Slide goes past intended point or stops short. → Identify the stop point before initiating.

### 3b. Intermediate Level
**Goal:** Execute a brake slide to stop in a corner — arriving at the apex or exit of a corner with controlled speed or a stop.

**Progression Markers from Beginner:** Rider can brake slide to a stop on flat terrain. Rear brake is the primary input. Slide is directed.

**Step-by-Step Execution — Corner Application:**
1. Identify the corner apex or desired stop point before entering the corner.
2. Approach the corner at the speed that will require the brake slide to stop at the planned point.
3. At 4–5 metres from the apex: initiate rear brake slide.
4. Rear slides to the outside of the corner — this is the natural slide direction.
5. Weight rearward. Front wheel steered toward the apex with small inputs.
6. Light front brake supplements deceleration.
7. Slide angle managed by rear brake pressure — too much angle: release pressure slightly.
8. Speed approaching zero: weight transitions forward.
9. Arrive at apex stop point with near-zero speed. Rear wheel stopped sliding.

**What the Rider Should Feel at Each Stage:**
- Stop point identification: The corner apex or a specific point is the target. The approach speed is too high — the brake slide is the tool to arrive there correctly.
- Slide initiation: Rear brake applied firmly — felt as the rear wheel decelerating faster than forward speed, the rear beginning to slide outward. The bike adopts a slight angle.
- Weight rearward: Body shifts rearward — felt as more pressure through the rear pegs, the front lightening slightly.
- Slide control: The rear is sliding at a controlled angle. The rear brake pressure is modulated — more slide if the stop point is approaching too slowly, less if the bike is decelerating fast enough.
- Front wheel direction: Small steering inputs guide the front toward the target stop point. The front tyre is the pivot — the rear is sliding around it.
- Front brake supplement: Light front brake adds deceleration. Felt as a slight front dive and additional deceleration. Must be kept light.
- Approaching zero speed: The slide is reducing. Weight beginning to shift slightly forward. The bike is nearly stopped.
- Stop: Speed reaches zero. Rear wheel has stopped sliding. Both feet may go to ground. Bike at the target stop point.

**What Good Looks Like:**
Entering a loose corner too fast, the rider initiates a brake slide 4 metres before the apex. Rear brake applied firmly — rear slides to the outside. The front wheel is steered toward the apex. Light front brake supplements. The bike arrives at the apex with near-zero speed, rear wheel having slid in a controlled arc.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Slide angle too large:** Rear slides too far — bike ends up too sideways. → Modulate rear brake to control slide angle. More pressure = more angle.
- **Front wash during slide:** Front brake too aggressive during slide. → Front brake is supplementary and progressive — reduce front brake input.
- **Stop point missed — too early:** Brake initiated too early — slide runs out of momentum and stops before the target. → Initiate later or maintain more speed through the slide.

### 3c. Advanced Level
**Goal:** Execute brake slides to stop in competition scenarios — at pace, in technical terrain, using the slide to position the bike for the next section.

**Progression Markers from Intermediate:** Rider can brake slide to a stop in corners. Slide angle is managed. Front remains stable.

**Step-by-Step Execution — Competition Pace:**
1. Identify the stop point from further away — at competition pace, decisions happen earlier.
2. Approach at pace. Initiate the slide earlier to account for higher speed — more distance needed.
3. The slide may be more aggressive at pace — rear brake pressure calibrated to available traction.
4. Front wheel direction is managed continuously through the longer slide.
5. Stop position is precise — the stop point is the entry for the next section, so it must be correct.
6. From the stop: immediate restart if required.

**Failure Modes and Causes:**
- **Front wash combined with rear slide:** Both tyres sliding — unrecoverable fall. → Rear brake primary. Front brake must be reduced or eliminated on very loose terrain.
- **Slide angle loss of control:** Rear slides faster than front wheel can manage direction — bike spins. → Smaller rear brake input. Guide the front more actively.
- **Stop point missed — overshoot:** Too much speed, slide not long enough. → Initiate earlier. Rear brake slide requires significant approach distance on loose terrain.

## 4. DIAGNOSTIC CUES (AI COACHING ENGINE REFERENCE)

### Visual Cues — Correct Technique
- Rear tyre slide track visible — controlled arc, not a wild spin
- Front wheel pointing toward stop point throughout slide
- Body rearward during slide, transitioning to centred at stop

### Visual Cues — Error Indicators
- Front tyre slide mark — front wash occurring during rear slide
- Excessive slide angle — bike arriving very sideways at stop
- Stop point missed — bike stopping well before or after intended point

### Audio Cues
- Correct: Rear tyre sliding sound — controlled, consistent friction
- Correct: No front tyre squeal — front brake supplement kept light
- Error: Front tyre squeal during slide — front brake too aggressive
- Error: Rear tyre spinning sound without slide — wheel lock, not controlled slide

### Sensor Cues
- Correct: Rear wheel speed decelerating faster than front — controlled slide
- Correct: Gradual deceleration to zero — controlled progressive stop
- Error: Front wheel speed drop simultaneous with lateral acceleration — front washing during slide
- Error: Abrupt zero speed event — crash from loss of control

## 5. COACHING CUES & LANGUAGE

### Positive Reinforcement
- "Rear brake initiated the slide, front stayed light and steered to the apex. That's exactly the technique — two separate inputs, one doing the sliding, one doing the directing."
- "Stop point was the apex marker. That's where you arrived. The slide was precise."
- "Weight was rearward through the whole slide, then centred at the stop. That transition gave the front stability at zero speed."

### Corrective Feedback
- "Front brake too aggressive during the slide — front washed. Back off the front brake during the slide. Rear brake does the work."
- "Slide angle got too large — bike was 60 degrees sideways at the stop. Modulate the rear brake. More pressure, more angle. Reduce pressure to reduce angle."
- "You initiated too late — the slide didn't have enough distance to stop at the target. Initiate earlier. The slide needs approach distance."

### Progression Prompts
- "You can brake slide to a stop on flat terrain. Now try it on a loose corner — the stop point is the apex. The slide should do the braking while the front steers you to the point."
- "Next run: before you initiate, tell me where you're going to stop. Then execute. The planning must come before the technique, not during."

## 6. DRILLS & PRACTICE EXERCISES

### Drill 1: Flat Terrain Brake Slide to Marker
**Purpose:** Build the basic brake slide to stop with a defined target.
**Setup:** A flat loose surface with a marker placed 15 metres from the start.
**Execution:**
1. Ride toward the marker at 20–25 km/h.
2. Initiate rear brake slide 6–8 metres from the marker.
3. Control the slide to arrive at the marker.
4. Assess: arrived at marker, before it, or past it? Adjust initiation point accordingly.
**Success Criteria:** Stop within 1 metre of marker on 7 out of 10 attempts.
**Duration/Reps:** 20 attempts.

### Drill 2: Slide Angle Control
**Purpose:** Learn to modulate rear brake pressure to control slide angle.
**Setup:** A flat loose area with clear space.
**Execution:**
1. Initiate a brake slide.
2. Deliberately vary rear brake pressure — increase to increase slide angle, decrease to reduce angle.
3. Observe the slide angle changing with pressure. Feel the relationship.
**Success Criteria:** Rider can increase and decrease slide angle on demand using brake pressure.
**Duration/Reps:** 15 slides.

### Drill 3: Corner Brake Slide to Apex
**Purpose:** Apply the technique in a corner with a defined stop point.
**Setup:** A loose corner with the apex marked.
**Execution:**
1. Approach at moderate speed.
2. Initiate brake slide 4–5 metres before the apex.
3. Steer the front toward the apex while rear slides.
4. Arrive at the apex stop point.
**Success Criteria:** Stop at apex within 0.5 metres on 6 out of 10 attempts.
**Duration/Reps:** 20 corner attempts.

### Drill 4: Slide to Stop — Restart Combination
**Purpose:** Use the stopped position as the entry for the next section — linking stop to restart.
**Setup:** A corner brake slide leading directly into an uphill obstacle.
**Execution:**
1. Execute brake slide to stop at the base of the uphill.
2. Immediately restart and attack the uphill obstacle.
3. Assess: was the stop position correct for the restart? Adjust if needed.
**Success Criteria:** Clean slide to stop and immediate clean restart on 6 out of 10 attempts.
**Duration/Reps:** 15 attempts.

## 8. TERRAIN & CONTEXT VARIATIONS

### Loose Dry Dirt
Best condition for brake slide to stop. Predictable slide. Good traction gradient for modulation.

### Wet Loose Terrain
Slide initiates more easily — less brake pressure required. Risk of over-sliding is higher. Rear brake input must be lighter.

### Gravel or Scree
Slide initiates quickly. Surface is unpredictable — slide angle can change as wheel hits larger stones. Careful modulation required.

### Steep Downhill Approach
Gravity adds to the deceleration challenge. More brake pressure required. Initiate earlier than on flat terrain. Body rearward is more critical.

## 9. EXPERT INSIGHTS & SOURCES

### Key Insights
- **The slide as a tool, not an accident:** Coaches who teach this technique consistently frame the brake slide to stop as a deliberate tool — not something that happens when braking goes wrong. Riders who view it as a deliberate technique approach it with intention and control; riders who view it as a braking failure approach it with panic.
- **Separate inputs:** The most useful coaching frame is that the rear brake and the handlebars are doing two separate jobs simultaneously — the rear brake manages speed through the slide, and the handlebars manage direction. Understanding these as separable inputs allows riders to practise each independently.
- **The stop point as the primary commitment:** Coaches who teach competition-level brake slide to stop consistently note that the most important decision is where to stop — not how to stop. Riders who decide where to stop before initiating the technique arrive there reliably. Riders who initiate without a target arrive randomly.
- **Approach speed determines slide distance:** The slide distance required to stop from a given speed is significantly affected by surface type. On loose dry dirt, a 30 km/h stop may require 4 metres of slide. On wet mud, the same stop may require only 2 metres. Riders who have trained this technique only on one surface type will misjudge stop distances when the surface changes. Training on multiple surfaces is essential for competition readiness.
- **The restart position:** In competition use, the value of a brake slide to stop is not just the stop — it is the position the bike is in when it stops. A well-executed brake slide to stop positions the bike optimally for the attack that follows: front wheel pointed at the obstacle, body weight centred, throttle ready. Training the technique with an immediate restart builds the full competition sequence.
