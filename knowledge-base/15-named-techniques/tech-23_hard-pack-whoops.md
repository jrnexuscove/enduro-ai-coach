# Hard-Pack Whoops

## 1. OVERVIEW

Hard-pack whoops are rhythmically spaced mounds of firm, compacted dirt or clay — typically found on motocross tracks, enduro stages, and heavily trafficked dirt roads. Unlike sand whoops, they are rigid and unforgiving — the suspension cannot compress into a soft landing. The impact of each whoop is sharp and significant. Correct hard-pack whoop technique is almost entirely about suspension timing: the goal is to be in phase with the suspension — legs compressing on each impact and extending during each rebound — so the bike skims the whoop tops rather than crashing into each trough. Out of phase, the suspension oscillates unpredictably, and the rider is launched or deflected. Hard-pack whoops at speed are one of the most physically demanding sections in enduro because the consequences of poor technique escalate rapidly with speed.

## 2. CORE PRINCIPLES

### Principle 1: Speed Creates Stability — Below a Threshold, Whoops Are Worse
Just like sand whoops, there is a speed threshold below which hard-pack whoops are more uncomfortable and less controllable than above it. At the correct speed, the suspension transitions from bottoming in each trough (bad) to topping each crest (controlled). Finding and committing to this threshold speed is the foundational skill.

### Principle 2: Legs Must Be in Phase with the Suspension Cycle
In hard-pack whoops, the suspension compresses and rebounds rapidly. If the rider's legs are pushing down (stiff) when the suspension is also pushing up (rebound), the rider is launched — ejected upward. If the legs are compressing (accepting) when the suspension compresses, the energy is absorbed. Being "in phase" means the legs and suspension are working together — legs compressing on each whoop impact and extending slightly before the next. This phasing requires active, conscious leg movement — not passive standing.

### Principle 3: Throttle in Phase With the Suspension
In hard-pack whoops, throttle application timing matters as much as leg phasing. Applying throttle as the rear wheel contacts a whoop top (the traction moment) drives the bike forward and over the crest. Applying throttle in the trough (where the rear wheel is between whoops) can cause the rear to dig into the ascending face of the next whoop — disrupting the rhythm. Advanced riders time their throttle to the whoop crests.

### Principle 4: Attack Position Must Be Maintained Throughout
Standing attack position is mandatory in hard-pack whoops. The rider cannot sit — the compression and rebound forces transmitted through the seat at speed are brutal. The standing position allows the legs to absorb each impact independently from the torso. Bent knees are the shock absorbers. Locked knees means every whoop impact goes directly to the spine and shoulders.

### Principle 5: Visual Look-Ahead Sets the Rhythm
The rhythm of whoops is regular — the rider's eyes should scan ahead, reading the overall pattern rather than fixating on each individual mound. Looking too close means reacting to each whoop individually — which is always too late. Looking further ahead allows the rider to see the rhythm and anticipate it — adjusting body position proactively rather than reactively.

## 3. TECHNIQUE BREAKDOWN BY LEVEL

### 3a. Beginner Level
**Goal:** Ride hard-pack whoops at low speed with controlled absorption — not being thrown or bucked off line.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Standing attack position with active knees — not passive
- Arms bent and loose — not rigid
- Maintained throttle through the section
- Eyes looking 10 metres ahead — not at the immediate ground

**What Good Looks Like:**
At 25 km/h through hard-pack whoops, the rider stands with actively working knees. Each whoop impact is felt but absorbed. No dramatic upper body movement. Consistent speed maintained.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Locked knees:** Every whoop impact transmitted directly to the torso — rider becomes a projectile. Fix: active knees. Think "squats over the bike."
- **Sitting:** Brutal impacts transmitted through the seat — the rider is bounced progressively worse. Fix: stand. Always stand in whoops.
- **Throttle variation:** Each variation shifts the suspension timing — the whoops become inconsistent. Fix: set throttle, hold it.

### 3b. Intermediate Level
**Goal:** Find the skimming speed for a specific whoop section and ride in phase with the suspension cycle.

**Progression Markers from Beginner:** Rider can absorb whoops at low speed. Standing position is established. Throttle consistency improving.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Speed escalation to find the skim threshold
- Feeling the in-phase vs out-of-phase sensation — recognising when the legs are working with vs against the suspension
- Conscious throttle timing — feeling when to apply throttle relative to the whoop crest
- Body decoupling — torso level, legs moving rapidly

**What Good Looks Like:**
At 45 km/h through hard-pack whoops, the bike skims from crest to crest. The rider's legs move in rapid short strokes — compressing on each crest, extending slightly in each gap. The torso is level. Engine note is consistent.

**What the Rider Should Feel at Each Stage:**
- Entry: Speed building — the commitment to approach speed must be made before entering the whoops.
- Early whoops (absorbing): Each mound felt as a distinct impact — legs working hard. This is not yet skim speed.
- Finding skim: As speed increases, the impacts become more frequent but less deep. A "smoothing out" sensation — the suspension is not bottoming between crests. The legs change from large-range absorbing to short-rapid movements. This is the skim threshold.
- In phase: The legs compressing match the whoop contacts. It feels rhythmic — almost like running. There is a sense of control despite high speed. The bike feels planted.
- Out of phase: If the rhythm is broken (throttle variation, eyes dropped), the legs get out of sync with the suspension. The bike begins to oscillate unpredictably. The rider feels it immediately as an out-of-control pitching sensation.
- Exit: Speed maintained through the last whoop. Throttle modulation begins after the final crest.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Out of phase (bouncing off line):** Legs not in sync with suspension — the rider is being bucked rather than absorbed. Fix: return to consistent throttle. If the phase is lost, it sometimes means accepting a slower absorb speed temporarily before rebuilding the rhythm.
- **Front bucking:** The front suspension is bottoming or oscillating — the front end is deflecting upward violently. Fix: slightly rearward weight reduces front wheel loading. Speed adjustment may be needed.
- **Losing throttle rhythm:** Any throttle variation disrupts the phase — the next whoop hits at the wrong moment. Fix: set throttle, never vary inside the section.

### 3c. Advanced Level
**Goal:** Navigate hard-pack whoops at maximum sustainable speed — throttle timed to crests, legs in perfect phase, continuous rhythm through extended sections.

**Progression Markers from Intermediate:** Rider can skim consistently. Phase is maintained. No significant upper body movement at skim speed.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Throttle timing to whoop crests — micro-throttle increases as the rear wheel tops each crest
- Extended whoop sections without fatigue
- Whoops with varying rhythm — adapting mid-section
- Whoops combined with corners or obstacles on entry/exit

**What Good Looks Like:**
On an enduro stage with a 100-metre hard-pack whoop section, the rider enters at the skim threshold and maintains perfect phase for the entire section. The throttle pulses subtly with each crest. The rider exits at full trail speed, barely fatigued. The section time is near-optimal.

**Failure Modes and Causes:**
- **Bouncing off line:** Phase loss causes progressive oscillation — the bike departs the whoop line and the rider can lose control. Fix: if phase is lost, reduce speed and reset. Do not try to maintain skim speed through a phase loss.
- **Front bucking out of section:** Front wheel bouncing becomes violent and unpredictable. Fix: slightly more rearward weight. Check suspension setup — hard-pack whoops are a primary diagnostic for front suspension tuning.
- **Loss of throttle rhythm:** Advanced riders sometimes over-manage the throttle in whoops — too many micro-adjustments disrupting the phase. Fix: less is more. Set throttle, hold it. Only add micro-pulses at crests if the rhythm is solid.

## 4. DIAGNOSTIC CUES (AI COACHING ENGINE REFERENCE)

### Visual Cues
- Correct: Bike skimming whoop tops — suspension movement visible but not bottoming
- Correct: Rider torso level — head height consistent through the section
- Correct: Legs visibly moving in rapid short strokes — in-phase absorption
- Error: Bike visibly bottoming in troughs — dropping into each gap
- Error: Rider being thrown upward or forward — out of phase, launching off crests
- Error: Rider sitting — immediately recognisable by seat contact

### Audio Cues
- Correct: Consistent engine note — steady throttle
- Correct: Rapid, consistent suspension contact sounds — regular whoop rhythm
- Error: Engine note varying inside the section — throttle disruption
- Error: Hard bottoming impacts — suspension not coping with depth at current speed

### Sensor Cues
- Correct: Rapid, consistent, low-amplitude vertical acceleration — whoop tops being contacted, not troughs
- Correct: Forward speed consistent through the section
- Error: High-amplitude vertical acceleration spikes — bottoming in troughs
- Error: Speed oscillation — throttle management failing

## 5. COACHING CUES & LANGUAGE

### Positive Reinforcement
- "In phase — I can see your legs working with the whoops, not against them. That's the rhythm we're building."
- "Throttle consistent the whole way through. The rhythm held because you held the throttle. That's the most important habit in whoops."
- "Head barely moved. Your legs did all the work. That's elite absorption technique."

### Corrective Feedback
- "Your knees are locked — every whoop is going straight through to your shoulders. Bend the knees. Work them. Think of them as pistons."
- "You're sitting — get up. Whoops require standing. The suspension alone cannot manage this — your legs need to help."
- "Out of phase — you were getting launched off every crest. The throttle varied and the rhythm broke. Set a speed and hold it. The phase comes from consistent throttle."
- "Front is bucking — you're too far forward. Shift weight slightly back. The front should be light enough to skim the crests."

### Progression Prompts
- "You're absorbing cleanly at 30 km/h. Now increase to 40. The whoops will smooth out as the suspension starts topping the crests rather than troughing them."
- "Once you find the skim, try to feel when the throttle should pulse — can you time it to the crest contact? It's subtle but once you feel it, it makes the rhythm even more locked in."
- "Try the section with your eyes further ahead — look 15 metres ahead instead of 8. See if anticipating the rhythm feels different from reacting to it."

## 6. DRILLS & PRACTICE EXERCISES

### Drill 1: Speed Escalation
**Purpose:** Find the skim threshold systematically.
**Setup:** A hard-pack whoop section.
**Execution:**
1. First pass: 20 km/h.
2. Each subsequent pass: 5 km/h faster.
3. Identify the speed where the ride smooths out.
**Success Criteria:** Rider identifies the skim threshold speed and can reproduce it.
**Duration/Reps:** 8 escalation passes.
**Progression:** Identify threshold on different whoop sizes and spacings.

### Drill 2: Leg Phase Training
**Purpose:** Consciously develop in-phase leg movement.
**Setup:** A small whoop section (3–4 whoops only).
**Execution:**
1. Ride slowly through.
2. Consciously compress knees as the wheel contacts each crest.
3. Extend slightly between crests.
4. Repeat, building speed gradually.
**Success Criteria:** Rider describes feeling in-phase — the legs working with the suspension, not against it.
**Duration/Reps:** 20 passes.
**Progression:** Longer whoop sections.

### Drill 3: Throttle Hold
**Purpose:** Build the consistent throttle habit specifically for whoops.
**Setup:** A hard-pack whoop section.
**Execution:**
1. Set throttle before entry.
2. Hold without adjustment through the entire section.
3. Evaluate: did rhythm hold? Was exit speed appropriate?
**Success Criteria:** Consistent throttle across 10 complete passes through the section.
**Duration/Reps:** 10 passes.
**Progression:** Longer section.

### Drill 4: Fatigue Management
**Purpose:** Build extended whoop endurance.
**Setup:** A 50-metre whoop section.
**Execution:**
1. Complete the section at skim speed.
2. Immediately ride it again without stopping.
3. Third pass: assess if technique has degraded.
**Success Criteria:** Technique consistent across all three passes. Fatigue identified but manageable.
**Duration/Reps:** 3-pass sets, 5 minutes rest between sets.
**Progression:** Increase to 4-pass and 5-pass sets.

## 8. TERRAIN & CONTEXT VARIATIONS

### Motocross Track Whoops
The most consistent, most aggressive hard-pack whoops. Designed to be ridden at speed. Suspension setup is critical — dedicated suspension tuning for MX whoops is common.

### Enduro Stage Whoops
Variable consistency — some sections are relatively even, others are irregular from rainfall erosion or traffic. Require more adaptive throttle and vision than MX track whoops.

### Dry Clay Whoops
Very hard surface — sharp impacts. Suspension must be slightly softer to manage the abrupt hits. The skim threshold is lower because the surface doesn't compress at all.

### Rocky Whoop-Style Terrain
Not true whoops but similar rhythm terrain — rocks creating rhythmic obstacles. The same phase principle applies but the surfaces are irregular and the timing is harder to predict.

### Night Whoops
Visibility dramatically changes the experience — the shadows between crests are hard to read. Reduce speed. Accept absorb technique rather than attempting skim in poor light.

## 9. EXPERT INSIGHTS & SOURCES

### Key Insights
- **MX influence on enduro:** Hard-pack whoops are a direct import from motocross into enduro racing. MX riders who transition to enduro carry this skill advantage — it is one area where MX background strongly benefits enduro performance.
- **The phase concept:** The in-phase leg movement is the most misunderstood aspect of whoop technique. Riders instinctively stiffen their legs on each impact — exactly the opposite of what is needed. The "active knee squat" coaching cue (consciously bending knees on each impact) is the most effective way to teach in-phase technique.
- **Suspension setup impact:** More than any other terrain type, hard-pack whoops expose suspension mismatch. Riders who cannot find a skim rhythm despite correct technique may have suspension that is too stiff or too soft for the specific whoop spacing and speed. A suspension technician assessment after a hard-pack whoop session can reveal significant improvements.
- **Speed commitment:** The hardest coaching challenge in whoops is overcoming the rider's instinct to reduce speed as the impacts become more intense. Teaching riders that more speed produces a smoother experience requires demonstrating it — video evidence and side-by-side comparison are effective tools.
