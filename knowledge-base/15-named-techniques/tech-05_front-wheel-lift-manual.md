# Front Wheel Lift (Manual)

## 1. OVERVIEW

The manual front wheel lift is the technique of lifting the front wheel off the ground using body weight transfer and suspension loading rather than clutch or engine power. The rider compresses the suspension by driving weight forward, then transfers their mass rearward sharply — the suspension rebound combined with the rearward body movement unloads the front wheel and lifts it. This technique is essential for crossing obstacles where a clutch pop is not appropriate or available, for popping the front wheel over roots and rocks at moderate speed, and for setting up drops or technical entries where the front needs to be placed precisely. Unlike a clutch-up wheelie, the manual depends entirely on body mechanics — which makes it quieter, more subtle, and applicable in a wider range of situations including technical slow-speed riding.

## 2. CORE PRINCIPLES

### Principle 1: Suspension Loading Is the Energy Source
The front wheel lift begins by compressing the front suspension — pushing the forks down by driving body weight forward. When this compression is released (body moves rearward), the spring energy in the forks pushes the front wheel back up. The rider amplifies this rebound by simultaneously shifting mass rearward, unloading the front as the suspension springs it up. The suspension is not just absorbing terrain — it is being used as a launching mechanism.

### Principle 2: Body Mass Rearward Is the Trigger
The front wheel lifts because the rider's body mass has moved behind the bike's balance point. The further and faster the mass moves rearward, the higher the front wheel lifts. This is a body movement, not a throttle movement — the engine can be at a constant speed or even idle during a manual. This distinguishes the manual from the clutch-up wheelie completely.

### Principle 3: Bar Pull Amplifies But Does Not Replace Body Movement
Pulling on the handlebars at the moment of rearward body shift adds leverage to the lift — the arms extend and the bars are pulled toward the body as the hips move back. However, riders who rely on bar pull without body movement get very little height and tire their arms. The body movement must be the primary driver; bar pull is amplification.

### Principle 4: Height Is Controlled by Rearward Travel Distance
The front wheel height is proportional to how far rearward the body mass has moved. A small rearward shift produces a small lift — appropriate for crossing a root. A large rearward shift with full bar pull produces a high lift — appropriate for clearing a log or significant obstacle. The rider learns to calibrate rearward travel to the required height through practice.

### Principle 5: Throttle Holds Speed, Rear Brake Controls the Loop
During a manual front wheel lift, the throttle should be held at a steady level — not increased. Increasing throttle converts the manual into a power wheelie and changes all the dynamics. The rear brake is the safety valve — if the front comes up too high, one finger on the rear brake instantly transfers weight forward and brings the front down. This rear brake awareness is what separates safe front wheel lifts from loop-out accidents.

## 3. TECHNIQUE BREAKDOWN BY LEVEL

### 3a. Beginner Level
**Goal:** Understand the body mechanics of the manual and produce small front wheel lifts in a controlled environment.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- The compression-and-release loading pattern — feel the suspension cycle
- Rearward hip drive as the primary input
- Maintaining steady throttle (not increasing it)
- Having one finger ready on the rear brake at all times

**What Good Looks Like:**
On flat ground at 15–20 km/h, rider produces a 5–10 cm front wheel lift using body movement alone. The lift is smooth, not jerky. Front comes back down gently. Rider does not look scared — the movement feels natural.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Pulling on bars with arms only, no hip drive:** Results in very little lift and sore arms. Why it happens: instinct is to pull. → Drive the hips rearward first, then pull bars. Think "hips back, then pull."
- **Throttle increase during the lift:** Converts to a power wheelie — can loop out. Why it happens: instinct to add power during the movement. → Maintain steady throttle throughout.
- **No rear brake awareness:** If the front comes up higher than expected, rider panics and dumps throttle instead of tapping rear brake. → One finger always on rear brake before attempting any lift.

### 3b. Intermediate Level
**Goal:** Execute a manual front wheel lift over real obstacles — targeting specific height and landing placement.

**Progression Markers from Beginner:** Rider can produce consistent small front wheel lifts on flat ground. Body movement is the primary driver. Rear brake awareness established.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Approach speed calibration — faster approach allows bigger lift more easily but reduces precision
- Timing the compression and release relative to the obstacle
- Front wheel height targeting — lifting the wheel to exactly the right height for the obstacle, not higher
- Front wheel placement precision — where the front lands matters as much as how high it lifts

**What Good Looks Like:**
Approaching a 20 cm log at 20 km/h, the rider compresses slightly by pushing weight forward 1 metre before the log, then drives hips rearward sharply as the front wheel is 0.5 metres from the log. The front wheel rises 25 cm — clearing the log cleanly. The front lands on the far side of the log while the rear is still approaching. The rider drives through without losing momentum.

**What the Rider Should Feel at Each Stage:**
- Pre-compression: Weight pressed forward into the bars — forks loading down. Felt as slight increased bar pressure and front tyre grip.
- Hip drive trigger: A sharp, explosive rearward hip push — like sitting back on a chair very quickly. The pelvis moves 20–30 cm rearward in 0.2 seconds.
- Bar pull amplification: Arms pull simultaneously with the hip drive — not pulling the bars toward the body, but using arm extension from the body moving away. Felt as the bars becoming light in the hands.
- Lift peak: Front wheel is at maximum height — the bike feels balanced on the rear wheel. A brief "hover" feeling. If this continues, tap rear brake.
- Front landing: Front wheel drops — rider allows it to happen without fighting it. The suspension absorbs the drop. Felt as a controlled forward weight shift returning.
- Drive through: Weight re-centres, throttle continues, rear wheel crosses the obstacle.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Too early compression release:** Body moves back before the front is close enough to the obstacle — front rises, then falls before clearing it. → The hip drive triggers when the front wheel is 30–50 cm from the obstacle, not 1 metre before.
- **Front wheel drops front-heavy:** Front lands hard with too much weight forward — compression shock. → Allow weight to settle naturally, do not rush weight forward after the lift.
- **Insufficient height for the obstacle:** Front clears the obstacle only barely, rear hits it hard. → More aggressive hip drive, or approach with slightly more speed.

### 3c. Advanced Level
**Goal:** Execute manual front wheel lifts at speed, with precision height control, on varied terrain — including using the lift for drops and technical entries.

**Progression Markers from Intermediate:** Rider can clear logs and obstacles with manual consistently. Height is controlled rather than maximum possible. Landing placement is predictable.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- High-speed manual lifts — maintaining balance point through the lift at 40+ km/h
- Adjusting lift height dynamically — responding to terrain features that are different from expected
- Using the manual for drop entries — lifting the front to delay the drop moment and manage landing angle
- Combining manual with clutch pop for larger obstacles where body alone is insufficient

**What Good Looks Like:**
At 40 km/h approaching a rough rock step, the rider executes a precise manual lift — front comes up to ledge height, is placed accurately on the ledge surface, and the rider drives through. The lift is executed in 0.4 seconds and is barely visible as a distinct action — it looks like the bike floated over.

**Failure Modes and Causes:**
- **Looping out:** Body too far rearward, front too high, rear brake not engaged. Fix: rear brake awareness must be automatic. One finger always available. If front is at 45 degrees or higher, tap the brake.
- **Insufficient height (panic weight-forward dump):** Rider starts the lift then pushes weight forward in fear — front barely lifts and hits the obstacle. Fix: commit to the rearward movement completely. Half-commitment produces no useful lift.
- **Mistimed lift on technical terrain:** Body compresses and releases at the wrong moment relative to the obstacle. Fix: practice timed lifts on marked spots until timing is automatic.

## 4. DIAGNOSTIC CUES (AI COACHING ENGINE REFERENCE)

### Visual Cues
- Correct: Clear rearward hip movement visible before the front wheel rises — body drives the lift, not arms
- Correct: Front wheel height is appropriate for obstacle — not dramatically over-lifting
- Correct: Rider remains upright on the bike — not thrown forward or backward during the lift
- Error: Arms pulling backward visibly while body stays forward — arm-only lift, insufficient height
- Error: Throttle hand visibly opening during lift — power input converting to wheelie
- Error: Rider crouches and throws body very far back — extreme rearward movement, loop risk

### Audio Cues
- Correct: Engine note stays constant through the lift — no throttle increase
- Correct: No clutch slip sound — this is a pure manual, no engine intervention
- Error: Engine RPM rises during lift — throttle being increased, converting to power wheelie

### Sensor Cues
- Correct: Front wheel speed drops to zero (wheel off ground) while rear wheel speed continues — confirms clean front lift
- Correct: Suspension data shows compression spike followed by rebound spike — loading pattern confirmed
- Error: Throttle position sensor spike during lift — rider adding power, not executing a manual

## 5. COACHING CUES & LANGUAGE

### Positive Reinforcement
- "That's it — I saw the hips go back before the bars moved. That's the right sequence. The suspension did the work, you just triggered it."
- "Perfect height for that obstacle. Not too high, not too low — you calibrated it exactly. That's control, not luck."
- "Your rear brake finger is in the right place. I saw you ready to use it. That's safe technique."

### Corrective Feedback
- "You're using your arms to pull — I can see the hips staying forward while the bars come back. Drive the hips back first. Arms just follow the body movement."
- "Your throttle opened during the lift — that's turning it into a power wheelie. Hold the throttle steady. The lift comes from your body, not the engine."
- "You stopped the movement halfway — the front barely came up. Commit to the hip drive. Don't stop it once you start."
- "You're lifting way too high for that obstacle. You need 20 centimetres, not 50. Smaller rearward drive equals smaller lift. Calibrate."

### Progression Prompts
- "You can hit a log. Now try the same technique on a rock step — the landing surface is different so the timing changes slightly. See if you can feel that."
- "Next step is increasing your speed on the approach. The faster you go, the less aggressive the lift needs to be — the suspension does more of the work."
- "Try the lift on a slight downhill approach. The front wants to go down anyway, so you need to be quicker and more decisive. Time pressure will sharpen your technique."

## 6. DRILLS & PRACTICE EXERCISES

### Drill 1: Suspension Pump Stationary
**Purpose:** Feel the compression-rebound cycle before combining with rearward body movement.
**Setup:** Flat ground, stationary on the bike.
**Execution:**
1. Stand on the pegs in attack position.
2. Push weight down into the bars — compress the forks deliberately.
3. Release the weight quickly — let the forks rebound.
4. Feel the front of the bike rise slightly even without rearward movement.
5. Repeat 10 times, increasing the compression depth each time.
**Success Criteria:** Rider can feel the suspension rebound and the slight front rise clearly. Distinguishes between compression and release phases.
**Duration/Reps:** 5 minutes, stationary.
**Progression:** Move at walking pace and feel the same cycle while in motion.

### Drill 2: Flat Ground Mini-Manual
**Purpose:** Produce small front wheel lifts on flat ground using body mechanics only.
**Setup:** Flat grass or gravel, riding at 15–20 km/h, 2nd gear.
**Execution:**
1. Approach in attack position.
2. Push weight forward slightly (compress forks).
3. Drive hips rearward sharply while pulling bars.
4. Feel front wheel lightening — it should lift 5–10 cm.
5. Tap rear brake if it goes higher than intended.
6. Let front come down naturally.
**Success Criteria:** Consistent front wheel lift visible (photographable gap between wheel and ground) on 7 out of 10 attempts.
**Duration/Reps:** 20 attempts per session.
**Progression:** Increase target height to 20 cm. Then try over a small marked line.

### Drill 3: Log Crossing Manual
**Purpose:** Apply the lift technique over a real obstacle with height and timing requirements.
**Setup:** A 15–20 cm diameter log on flat ground.
**Execution:**
1. Approach at 20 km/h in 2nd gear.
2. Time the compression to happen 1.5 metres from the log.
3. Drive hips rearward as front wheel is 30–50 cm from the log.
4. Front wheel clears the log — aim for 5 cm of clearance.
5. Front lands on far side — continue driving through so rear crosses cleanly.
**Success Criteria:** Front clears the log cleanly (no contact) on 7 out of 10 passes. Rear drives over without jumping.
**Duration/Reps:** 20 passes per session.
**Progression:** Increase log height to 25–30 cm. Then try on a slight uphill approach.

### Drill 4: Precision Placement Manual
**Purpose:** Develop front wheel landing placement accuracy — not just height, but where the wheel lands.
**Setup:** A log crossing with two marked zones on either side — "land here" zones 20 cm wide.
**Execution:**
1. Approach and execute the manual lift.
2. Aim to land the front wheel in the marked zone on the far side.
3. Adjust timing of the lift to shift the landing point forward or back.
**Success Criteria:** Front wheel lands within the marked zone on 6 out of 10 attempts.
**Duration/Reps:** 20 attempts per session.
**Progression:** Narrow the landing zone to 10 cm. Then try at different approach speeds.

## 8. TERRAIN & CONTEXT VARIATIONS

### Flat Technical Terrain
The standard application — logs, roots, rocks. Approach speed and obstacle height determine required lift magnitude.

### Downhill Approach
The front naturally wants to dive on a downhill approach — the manual must be more aggressive and faster to overcome the slope's gravity effect. Timing the compression-release is harder because the suspension is already partially loaded.

### Uphill Approach
The uphill gradient slows the approach and helps keep weight rearward naturally. Less aggressive body movement may be needed. However, the rear wheel may not drive over the obstacle as cleanly due to gradient — throttle application matters more on uphill.

### Loose or Sandy Surface
On loose surfaces, the rear wheel may spin during the drive-through phase after the front lands. Clutch pop may supplement the manual to drive the rear over.

### Wet Conditions
Front tyre grip is reduced on wet roots and rocks. Ensure the front wheel lands on a grippy surface after the lift — not on wet rock or smooth wet root. Landing placement becomes more critical.

## 9. EXPERT INSIGHTS & SOURCES

### Key Insights
- **Trials riding foundation:** The manual front wheel lift is one of the most fundamental trials skills — trials riders lift the front wheel at near-zero speeds with extraordinary precision. The enduro adaptation adds speed to the equation but the body mechanics are identical.
- **Body mechanics vs engine use:** Professional coaches emphasise that manual front lifts are more repeatable and precise than clutch-up lifts at moderate speeds — the manual is calibratable to the centimetre, while a clutch-up at similar speeds has more variability.
- **The balance point:** A key milestone for riders is discovering the "balance point" of the manual — the angle at which the bike is perfectly balanced on the rear wheel. Riders who find this briefly realise how controlled the technique can be.
- **Looping out statistics:** Most loop-out accidents in beginners occur because they either add throttle during the lift or do not have a rear brake finger ready. Both are trainable habits — technique and safety overlap completely here.
