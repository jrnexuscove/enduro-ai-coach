# Berm Riding

## 1. OVERVIEW

A berm is a banked corner — a section of trail or track where the outside edge of the corner has been raised, creating an angled surface that provides lateral support to the bike and rider through the turn. Berms appear in motocross tracks, bike parks, and natural terrain where water or traffic has formed a banked corner. Riding a berm correctly is one of the most satisfying experiences in off-road riding — the banked surface allows higher cornering speeds than a flat turn, and a rider who loads the berm correctly generates speed through the corner rather than scrubbing it. The technique focuses on finding the right berm contact point, loading it with body weight, and timing the exit drive to generate acceleration out of the corner.

## 2. CORE PRINCIPLES

### Principle 1: The Berm Provides Lateral Support — Use It
A berm acts like the wall of a bowl — the surface is tilted to push the bike inward (toward the apex) rather than letting it wash outward. This lateral support allows the bike to corner at higher speed than a flat surface because the berm's normal force has an inward component that contributes to centripetal force. The rider must load the berm — push into the banked surface — to utilise this support. A rider who avoids the berm's main body and rides near its top or base gets the worst of both worlds.

### Principle 2: Entry Speed Determines Whether the Berm Can Support the Bike
Too slow into a berm and the bike slides down the berm face — there is not enough centrifugal force to keep the bike planted on the banked surface. The berm needs to be loaded at the right speed for its angle and radius. Too fast and the bike may climb over the top of the berm and exit wide — the centrifugal force exceeds the berm's containment. Finding the correct entry speed range is part of berm reading.

### Principle 3: Weight Into the Berm — Not Up the Berm
The rider's body weight should be directed into the berm surface — perpendicular to the banked face — not upward (standing upright on a banked surface while the berm is holding you sideways). This means the rider leans with the bike into the corner, weight loading through the outside footpeg and down into the berm face. The body is inclined with the bike — not artificially upright.

### Principle 4: Exit Drive From the Berm Bottom
The most effective berm cornering exits from the low point of the berm — riding down through the berm face rather than across it. As the bike exits the berm, the banked surface "launches" the bike forward as the lateral support transitions back to forward drive. Throttle application at the exit, timed to when the bike is aligned with the exit direction, generates significant acceleration — this is the "free speed" that berms provide to correct technique.

### Principle 5: Vision Through the Berm Determines Exit Line
Looking at the inside of the berm while riding it directs the bike toward the berm face rather than the exit. Looking through the berm to the exit track directs the bike toward the exit and allows natural steering to follow vision. This is particularly important in tight berms where the exit is not visible until late in the corner — the rider must commit to looking for the exit early.

## 3. TECHNIQUE BREAKDOWN BY LEVEL

### 3a. Beginner Level
**Goal:** Ride a berm at moderate speed with correct body lean and exit throttle.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Entering the berm at the correct entry point — not too high (top), not too low (base)
- Leaning with the bike into the berm — not sitting upright on the banked surface
- Looking through the berm to the exit
- Exit throttle applied as the bike faces the exit direction

**What Good Looks Like:**
Approaching a berm at 20 km/h, the rider enters at the correct entry height, leans with the bike into the berm, looks through to the exit, and opens throttle as the bike aligns with the exit. The bike accelerates cleanly off the berm.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Riding too high on the berm (berm top):** Less support, more risk of sliding over the top. Fix: find the midline of the berm and load it.
- **Body upright on banked surface:** The body is inclined to gravity (vertical) rather than the berm surface — the weight is not loading the berm correctly. Fix: lean with the bike.
- **No exit throttle:** Rider exits the berm coasting — no acceleration benefit from the berm. Fix: throttle as the bike faces exit direction.

### 3b. Intermediate Level
**Goal:** Use the berm to carry and generate speed — entering with more speed than a flat turn would allow and accelerating on exit.

**Progression Markers from Beginner:** Rider navigates berms at basic level. Body lean is present. Exit throttle is applied.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Entry speed selection: entering faster than a flat turn of the same radius
- Finding the berm's optimal loading zone — the part of the banked surface that provides maximum support at the chosen entry speed
- Weight through the outside footpeg — feeling the lateral support under the peg
- Throttle timing: not too early (front washes off berm) and not too late (speed not captured)

**What Good Looks Like:**
Approaching a berm at 30 km/h — 10 km/h faster than a flat corner would allow — the rider loads the berm mid-face, body leaning with the bike, outside peg weighted. Through the berm the bike tracks the banked surface without climbing or descending. At the exit, throttle opens and the bike accelerates forward. The rider exits the corner faster than they entered.

**What the Rider Should Feel at Each Stage:**
- Entry: The bike enters the berm contact zone. The banked surface begins to push the bike inward. Felt as a support under the outside of the bike — the bars don't need to be turned hard because the berm is steering.
- Loading zone: The berm surface is fully under the bike. Felt as a firm, supportive push from the outside. The outside footpeg is loaded heavily. The lean angle feels natural — like the berm is holding you.
- Mid-berm: Tracking the berm's contour. Eyes are already looking toward the exit. The front wheel is following the curve — the rider is barely steering, just directing vision.
- Exit approach: The bike is reaching the exit direction. Felt as the banked surface beginning to flatten.
- Exit drive: Throttle opens. The bike accelerates forward — felt as the familiar drive push but with additional "launch" energy as the berm transitions to flat. The bike is moving faster on the exit than the entry.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Washing off the berm top:** Entry speed too high or entry point too high — the bike reaches the top of the berm and runs out of support. Fix: lower entry point or reduce speed.
- **Under-driving the exit:** Throttle applied too late — after the bike has already exited the berm. The berm launch energy is wasted. Fix: throttle as the exit direction is reached, not after.
- **Front washing off berm:** Too much throttle too early — front wheel loses lateral grip on the berm face and slides up or over the top. Fix: throttle timing — wait for the exit alignment.

### 3c. Advanced Level
**Goal:** Maximise speed through berms — carrying maximum entry speed, loading the berm precisely, and launching the exit at full throttle.

**Progression Markers from Intermediate:** Rider carries consistently more speed through berms than flat turns. Exit throttle is timed correctly. Body lean is automatic.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Maximum sustainable entry speed for the berm's angle and condition
- Precise contact point selection — not just the mid-face but the specific point that maximises support for the speed being carried
- Throttle timing precise enough to catch the launch point exactly
- Berm reading across varied berm conditions — deteriorating berms, partial berms, wet berms

**What Good Looks Like:**
On a motocross-style berm at full pace, the rider enters at the maximum speed the berm can contain, loads the optimal contact zone with the outside peg carrying all the lateral load, looks through the exit at the first possible moment, and applies throttle at the exact exit alignment — generating a significant acceleration benefit. The rider's exit speed is noticeably higher than riders using less optimal technique.

**Failure Modes and Causes:**
- **Washing off berm top:** Berm cannot contain the entry speed. Fix: know the berm's maximum speed. Entry speed must match berm angle and traction.
- **Under-driving exit:** Speed not captured. Fix: throttle earlier. In a well-maintained berm, the exit throttle can be very early — as soon as the exit is aimed at.
- **Entering too slow for berm support:** Bike slides down the face rather than tracking. Fix: minimum berm entry speed. Going slower than the berm's support speed means the berm actively works against you.

## 4. DIAGNOSTIC CUES (AI COACHING ENGINE REFERENCE)

### Visual Cues
- Correct: Bike tracks smoothly along berm face — neither climbing to top nor sliding to base
- Correct: Body leaning with the bike — not upright on the banked surface
- Correct: Acceleration visible on exit — speed increase as bike leaves berm
- Error: Bike climbing the berm toward the top — entry speed too high or entry point too high
- Error: Bike sliding to berm base — entry speed too low, berm support failing
- Error: Body upright relative to gravity while on berm — weight not loading into berm correctly

### Audio Cues
- Correct: Engine note rises on exit — throttle applied at exit
- Correct: Smooth engine note through the berm — consistent throttle through the corner
- Error: Engine note cut inside the berm — throttle removed, speed reducing

### Sensor Cues
- Correct: Lateral acceleration data shows consistent centripetal loading — berm providing support
- Correct: Speed maintained or slightly increased through the berm — entry and exit speeds similar
- Correct: Speed spike on exit — throttle acceleration captured from berm launch
- Error: Speed dropping through the berm — underloading or braking

## 5. COACHING CUES & LANGUAGE

### Positive Reinforcement
- "You loaded the berm mid-face and the bike tracked perfectly. That's the support zone — you found it."
- "Exit throttle was timed perfectly — I could hear the engine note rise exactly as you hit the exit direction. That's where the free speed comes from."
- "You carried 10 km/h more into that corner than on the flat section. The berm gave you that. Good entry speed selection."

### Corrective Feedback
- "You're riding the top of the berm — that's where support is lowest. Come lower on the face. Load the middle of the berm, not the crest."
- "Body is upright — you're not loading the berm. Lean with the bike. Your outside peg should be taking the weight, not your seat."
- "Exit throttle too late — you were already off the berm by the time the throttle came on. Apply it as you're facing the exit direction, not after."
- "Entry speed too low — the bike slid down the berm face. Berms need to be entered at minimum speed to use their support. 5 km/h faster next time."

### Progression Prompts
- "You're comfortable at 25 km/h. Try 30 on the next pass — see if the berm holds you. If it does, that's your new entry speed."
- "Now focus on the exit throttle point — can you apply it earlier? Each metre sooner is more captured speed."
- "Try linking two consecutive berms — the exit speed from the first becomes the entry speed for the second. See how the speed builds."

## 6. DRILLS & PRACTICE EXERCISES

### Drill 1: Berm Contact Zone Finder
**Purpose:** Identify the optimal loading zone on a specific berm.
**Setup:** A well-formed berm with clear face sections.
**Execution:**
1. First pass: ride the bottom of the berm.
2. Second pass: ride the middle.
3. Third pass: ride the top.
4. Identify which position felt most supported and produced the best exit.
**Success Criteria:** Rider can identify and describe the optimal contact zone.
**Duration/Reps:** 3 passes per zone, 9 passes total.
**Progression:** Try different entry speeds at the optimal zone.

### Drill 2: Entry Speed Escalation
**Purpose:** Find the maximum sustainable berm entry speed.
**Setup:** A well-formed berm.
**Execution:**
1. Enter at 20 km/h.
2. Each pass, increase by 5 km/h.
3. Identify the speed at which the bike begins to climb the berm top.
4. The correct entry speed is just below this threshold.
**Success Criteria:** Maximum sustainable entry speed identified and reproducible.
**Duration/Reps:** Speed escalation passes.
**Progression:** Try on a less-formed berm.

### Drill 3: Exit Throttle Timing
**Purpose:** Develop precise exit throttle timing.
**Setup:** A berm with a clear exit direction marked.
**Execution:**
1. Ride the berm normally.
2. Apply throttle as soon as the front wheel is pointed at the marked exit direction.
3. Earlier throttle is the goal — but not so early that the front washes.
**Success Criteria:** Consistent throttle timing within 0.5 seconds of optimal on 7 out of 10 passes.
**Duration/Reps:** 15 passes.
**Progression:** Mark the exact throttle point and try to apply it at that point consistently.

### Drill 4: Linked Berm Chain
**Purpose:** Use exit speed from one berm as entry speed for the next.
**Setup:** Two berms in sequence — the exit of the first feeds into the second.
**Execution:**
1. Ride berm 1 with exit drive.
2. Carry the exit speed directly into berm 2 — no braking between.
3. Assess: was entry speed for berm 2 higher than normal? Did the berm handle it?
**Success Criteria:** Entry speed for berm 2 is 5+ km/h higher than when ridden individually.
**Duration/Reps:** 15 passes.
**Progression:** Add a third berm.

## 8. TERRAIN & CONTEXT VARIATIONS

### Well-Formed Hard-Pack Berm
Standard application. Maximum support, predictable behaviour. Most berm technique learning should occur here.

### Natural Loose-Dirt Berm
Formed by erosion or previous riders — less consistent than a constructed berm. The face may be loose, reducing the available speed. Entry speed must be reduced. Loading the berm collapses loose faces.

### Rocky Berm
A natural rocky outcrop forming a banked corner. Irregular surface — tyre contact patches bridge between rocks. Maximum speed is limited by the rock irregularity. Entry speed is lower.

### Deteriorating Berm
A berm that has been worn down by traffic — the face has collapsed, leaving a flat or irregular surface. Less lateral support available. Treat as a partially banked corner — use whatever support is available.

### Wet Berm
Grip is reduced on all surfaces. Maximum entry speed is lower. Throttle timing is more critical — too early with reduced grip causes front wash.

## 9. EXPERT INSIGHTS & SOURCES

### Key Insights
- **Motocross origin:** Berm riding is one of the most refined skills in motocross — entire coaching curricula are built around berm optimisation. Enduro riders who train on MX tracks specifically to develop berm technique carry a significant speed advantage.
- **The "free speed" principle:** Elite coaches describe properly executed berm exits as producing "free speed" — the cornering arc converts to straight-line acceleration with a timing advantage. The rider who applies exit throttle at the exact right moment captures this free speed; the rider who applies it late does not.
- **Berm vs flat corner time:** Timing data from motocross tracks consistently shows that riders who correctly use berms gain 0.5–1 second per berm compared to riders who treat berms like flat corners. In an enduro stage with 10–15 bermed corners, this compounds to significant time savings.
- **Berm reading:** Experienced riders assess berm quality from the approach — looking at the face angle, the wear pattern, and the exit direction to calibrate entry speed and loading zone before they arrive. This pre-assessment happens at full approach speed.
- **Rear wheel slide as a berm tool:** At advanced level, deliberately inducing a controlled rear wheel slide into a berm allows the rider to scrub approach speed while simultaneously loading the berm face — the slide positions the bike perfectly on the loading zone without requiring trail braking. This is a motocross-derived technique used by enduro competitors on tight berm entries where approach speed is too high for a clean throttle-off entry. The slide must be controlled and brief — the bike must be loaded on the berm face, not sideways across it. Coaches introduce this only after the basic berm technique is automatic.
- **Berm degradation across a race day:** A natural or semi-natural berm used by many competitors degrades significantly over a race day — each rider adds to the rut depth and erodes the face. A berm that offered firm, predictable support in the morning's first pass may be a soft, rutted surface by the afternoon. Experienced competitors who race on multiple loops adjust their entry speed and loading zone with each lap, not riding on memory of how the berm felt earlier. This adaptability is a distinguishing characteristic of high-level berm technique.
