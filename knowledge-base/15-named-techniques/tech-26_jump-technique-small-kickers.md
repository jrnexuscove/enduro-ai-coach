# Jump Technique (Small Kickers)

## 1. OVERVIEW

A kicker is a sudden, steep launch ramp in the terrain — a sharp ledge, a root cluster, or a terrain feature that abruptly pitches the front of the bike upward when hit. Small kickers (launching the bike 0.5–1.5 metres in the air) appear constantly in enduro on trail lips, rock edges, and terrain undulations. The rider faces a fundamental decision at every kicker: absorb it (compress into the feature and reduce the launch) or launch off it (attack position, accept the air, manage the landing). The correct choice depends on what lies beyond — if there is a clear safe landing zone, launching is often faster; if the terrain requires immediate control, absorbing prevents the air. Understanding both techniques and when to apply them is the core of small kicker management.

## 2. CORE PRINCIPLES

### Principle 1: The Approach Determines Whether You Absorb or Launch
The decision to absorb or launch must be made before the kicker, not at it. Absorbing requires actively pushing weight forward and compressing the suspension into the kicker. Launching requires staying in attack position and letting the kicker pitch the bike upward. Trying to decide mid-kicker produces neither outcome well — the rider ends up partially launching with no preparation for the airborne phase.

### Principle 2: In the Air, Throttle Controls Pitch
When airborne, the engine is still spinning the rear wheel — this gyroscopic effect, combined with throttle changes, influences the bike's pitch angle. Increasing throttle in the air tends to pitch the nose up (rear wheel drives down). Reducing throttle pitches the nose down. Small, deliberate throttle adjustments in the air are the primary pitch correction tool — not body movement alone. Most riders underestimate how small the correction input needs to be.

### Principle 3: Landing Absorption Is More Important Than Launch Style
A technically imperfect launch that has a good landing absorption is safer than a perfect launch with a hard, rigid landing. The landing absorption — knees and elbows absorbing the impact — determines the outcome. This must be prepared in the air: legs bent, body slightly rearward, arms ready to absorb. The rider who mentally prepares the landing before they leave the ground lands better than the rider who figures it out on the way down.

### Principle 4: Bike Pitch at Landing Must Be Neutral or Slightly Rear-Wheel-First
Landing with the front wheel first transfers all the impact force to the forks and the rider's arms — a hard front-wheel landing at speed can cause a front wash or pitch the rider over the bars. The correct landing attitude is slightly rear-wheel-first or flat (both wheels simultaneously). Rear-wheel-first allows the rear suspension to absorb before the front, which is both more comfortable and more controllable. This pitch attitude is set up in the air, not on landing.

### Principle 5: Case Landing Is the Most Dangerous Outcome — Know the Range
A case landing is when the bike's undercarriage hits the face of the landing ramp rather than the landing surface — the bike lands too short. This can severely damage the bike and the rider. Knowing the kicker's launch angle and the available landing range allows the rider to calibrate approach speed. Too slow = case landing risk; too fast = overshooting the landing zone into unknown terrain. New kickers should always be approached conservatively — walk the landing zone first.

## 3. TECHNIQUE BREAKDOWN BY LEVEL

### 3a. Beginner Level
**Goal:** Absorb small kickers cleanly — compressing the suspension into the feature and reducing launch height to near-zero.

**Step-by-Step Execution — Absorb Technique:**
1. Spot the kicker from 10–15 metres ahead. Make the absorb decision. Eyes on the landing zone beyond the kicker.
2. Three metres before the kicker, push weight forward — chest over the bars, elbows bent.
3. As the front wheel reaches the kicker lip, allow the fork to compress into the kicker rather than resisting.
4. Push down slightly through the footpegs to load the suspension into the feature.
5. The bike barely leaves the ground — a brief lightening of the front, not a full launch.
6. Continue rolling normally. The kicker is behind you.

**What the Rider Should Feel:**
- Approach: Slight weight shift forward, deliberate and calm.
- Kicker contact: A compression feeling through the legs as the front wheel rises then compresses down. Not a bang — a managed push.
- Exit: The bike feels heavier for a moment (suspension returning) then normal. No weightlessness.

**What Good Looks Like:**
Approaching a small kicker at 25 km/h, the rider pushes weight forward into the approach, compresses into the kicker, and the bike barely leaves the ground — just a brief lightening. The bike returns to normal terrain immediately. No significant air time. The body barely moves.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Attack position when trying to absorb:** The attack position is the launch position — if the rider is in attack position at a kicker they intended to absorb, they will launch. → For absorb, push weight forward and down into the kicker.
- **Looking at the kicker:** Visual focus on the kicker rather than the landing pulls the rider's attention to the wrong place. → Eyes on the landing zone beyond, not the kicker itself.
- **Stiffening on contact:** Rider braces as the kicker arrives — arms lock, legs stiffen. → Actively push into the kicker. Compression is the technique.

### 3b. Intermediate Level
**Goal:** Execute a controlled small kicker launch — managing airborne pitch and landing absorption.

**Progression Markers from Beginner:** Rider can absorb kickers. Has launched accidentally and managed it. Understanding of the absorb vs launch decision is developing.

**Step-by-Step Execution — Launch Technique:**
1. Spot the kicker from 15 metres. Make the launch decision. Assess the landing zone — confirm it is clear.
2. Set approach speed. Maintain it — do not accelerate into the kicker.
3. Enter the kicker in attack position: knees bent, weight centred, arms bent, elbows out.
4. As the kicker pitches the front up, extend the legs slightly — the kicker pushes the bike up through your feet.
5. Become airborne. Immediately assess pitch angle — is the nose high, low, or level?
6. If nose-high: reduce throttle slightly to bring nose down. If nose-low: add a small throttle input.
7. In the air, bend knees slightly and prepare for landing — weight slightly rearward, arms ready to absorb.
8. Land rear-wheel-first or flat. Legs absorb deeply — knees drive up toward the chest.
9. Arms absorb the front landing. Maintain speed out of the landing.

**What the Rider Should Feel at Each Stage:**
- Kicker approach: Attack position, speed set, eyes on landing zone. The kicker itself is anticipated but not focused on.
- Launch moment: The kicker pitches the front up — felt as a sudden upward push through the footpegs. Legs extend slightly. The bike becomes airborne.
- Airborne phase: Weightlessness — the bike is no longer ground-referenced. Body position assessment: is the nose level? Too high? Too low?
- Pitch correction: Throttle adjustment if needed — more throttle for nose-down correction, less for nose-up. Small inputs only.
- Landing preparation: Legs bending into landing position. Arms slightly bent. Body weight slightly rearward for rear-wheel-first contact.
- Landing: Impact felt through the suspension and the legs simultaneously. The knees flex deeply to absorb. The arms flex to absorb the front landing. The bike slows slightly from impact but maintains direction.

**What Good Looks Like:**
Approaching a small kicker at 30 km/h in attack position, the bike launches 0.8 metres in the air. The rider reads the pitch (nose slightly high) and adds slight throttle to level it. The front and rear land simultaneously — absorption through bent knees and arms. Forward speed maintained. Clean landing.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Over-jumping into obstacles:** Too much approach speed — the range is longer than anticipated, and the bike lands beyond the safe landing zone. → Conservative approach speed for new kickers. Build up progressively.
- **Front-heavy landing:** Body weight too far forward in the air — front lands first and hard. → Slightly rearward weight before landing. Not dramatically back — just enough for neutral or slightly rear-first.
- **Case landing:** Too slow — the bike does not clear the kicker and the undercarriage hits the landing face. → More approach speed. Walk the kicker first to understand its minimum launch speed.
- **Throttle cut in the air:** Nose dives suddenly. → Maintain or hold throttle through the air. Do not chop throttle when airborne.

### 3c. Advanced Level
**Goal:** Navigate kickers at trail speed with instant absorb/launch decisions — landing precisely in defined zones.

**Progression Markers from Intermediate:** Rider can launch and land small kickers consistently. Pitch correction is instinctive. Landing absorption is automatic.

**Step-by-Step Execution — Full Speed with Precision:**
1. Kicker identified at 15 metres at trail speed. Decision made: absorb or launch.
2. For launch: no speed adjustment — approach at trail pace.
3. Attack position held through the kicker. Eyes hard on the landing zone.
4. Airborne: immediate pitch check. Correction executed in under 0.5 seconds.
5. Landing: targeted to the specific landing zone planned before the kicker. Arrive there.
6. Absorption: deep knee and elbow flex. Recover speed on exit.
7. If two kickers in sequence: the landing from the first is the setup for the second — no hesitation.

**What Good Looks Like:**
At full trail speed, the rider reads a kicker at 15 metres, makes an instant absorb/launch decision, executes the chosen technique, and either continues normally (absorb) or lands precisely in the clear zone ahead (launch) — all without any visible hesitation or speed reduction.

**Failure Modes and Causes:**
- **Over-jumping into rough terrain:** Too much speed, launching further than the safe landing zone. → Approach speed calibration. New kickers always explored at reduced speed first.
- **Front wash on landing:** Front wheel lands on a different surface from expected (root, edge) and washes. → Landing zone assessment before launch — know what the front wheel will contact on landing.
- **Case landing at speed:** Approach too slow for the kicker, bike cases the landing. → Know the minimum speed for clearance on a given kicker. Never approach on the boundary.

## 4. DIAGNOSTIC CUES (AI COACHING ENGINE REFERENCE)

### Visual Cues — Correct Technique
- Attack position maintained through the kicker — not weighted forward for an unexpected launch
- Airborne pitch angle approximately neutral — not dramatically nose-up or nose-down
- Landing absorption visible — knees bending deeply on impact, torso barely moving
- Rider's head height stays relatively constant through the landing — legs are doing the absorbing

### Visual Cues — Error Indicators
- Nose-up attitude in air — too much throttle or weight too far back at launch
- Nose-down attitude — throttle cut or weight too far forward in the air
- Rigid leg landing — no absorption, body slamming down on impact
- Rider sitting back down immediately after landing — no control in the air
- Front wheel landing first with visible fork dive — front-heavy airborne position

### Audio Cues
- Correct: Consistent engine note through launch and air — no abrupt throttle changes
- Correct: Landing impact absorbed — soft thud, not a crash
- Error: Engine note drops in air — throttle cut, nose-down tendency
- Error: Hard landing crash sound — no absorption, rigid landing

### Sensor Cues
- Correct: Airborne phase detected (wheel speed data) — confirmed launch
- Correct: Smooth landing deceleration — absorption detected
- Error: Hard landing spike in vertical acceleration — no absorption
- Error: Pitch angle extreme in air — poor throttle management

## 5. COACHING CUES & LANGUAGE

### Positive Reinforcement
- "Absorbed that cleanly — barely left the ground. Smart decision, good technique."
- "In the air, nose was level. Landing was rear-wheel first, knees absorbed. That's correct jump technique."
- "You read the kicker before you got to it. Decision made early — clean execution."
- "Good throttle management — I could see the nose levelling out. That's airborne awareness."

### Corrective Feedback
- "You were in attack position when you wanted to absorb — that's why you launched. For absorb, push weight into the kicker as you approach."
- "Nose was high in the air — too much throttle. Reduce slightly in the air to bring the nose down. Small input."
- "Hard landing — legs were stiff. Bend the knees in the air. You land on your legs, not your spine."
- "You chopped throttle when you went airborne — nose went straight down. Hold that throttle. The moment you leave the ground, don't change it suddenly."

### Progression Prompts
- "You've got the absorb. Now try a deliberate small launch on this kicker — attack position, accept the air, absorb the landing."
- "Next run, target that flat spot 3 metres past the kicker as your landing zone. Aim to land there. See if you can hit it."
- "Start adding throttle correction in the air — if the nose is too high, just reduce slightly. Get a feel for how little input it takes."

## 6. DRILLS & PRACTICE EXERCISES

### Drill 1: Absorb vs Launch Decision
**Purpose:** Practise making the absorb vs launch decision deliberately before the kicker.
**Setup:** A small kicker with a clear landing zone and a safe alternative absorb option.
**Execution:**
1. 5 passes: decide "absorb" before the kicker. Execute absorb — push weight forward into it.
2. 5 passes: decide "launch" before the kicker. Execute launch — attack position, accept the air.
3. Compare the outcomes. Note how the pre-decision changed the execution quality.
**Success Criteria:** Rider can execute the chosen technique reliably on 8 out of 10 passes.
**Duration/Reps:** 15 passes.
**Progression:** Make the decision later (closer to the kicker). Build decision-making speed.

### Drill 2: Pitch Correction in Air
**Purpose:** Build throttle-pitch awareness in the air.
**Setup:** A consistent small kicker with a clear landing zone.
**Execution:**
1. Launch, observe pitch angle from landing feedback.
2. If nose-high on landing: the front landed after the rear — reduce throttle slightly on next pass.
3. If nose-low on landing: the front hit first — maintain or slightly increase throttle.
4. Aim for simultaneous wheel landing (neutral pitch).
**Success Criteria:** Neutral pitch achieved on 6 out of 10 launches.
**Duration/Reps:** 15 launches.
**Progression:** Try to achieve consistent rear-wheel-first landing (slightly rearward weight plus minor throttle maintenance).

### Drill 3: Landing Zone Targeting
**Purpose:** Develop landing precision — land where you plan to land.
**Setup:** A kicker with a marked landing zone (2 metres wide initially, marked with tape or cones).
**Execution:**
1. Launch the kicker.
2. Aim to land within the marked zone. Adjust approach speed if consistently over/under.
**Success Criteria:** Landing within the zone on 7 out of 10 attempts.
**Duration/Reps:** 20 launches.
**Progression:** Narrow the zone to 1 metre. Then remove the markers — develop the targeting from feel.

### Drill 4: Consecutive Kicker Rhythm
**Purpose:** Chain two kickers in quick succession — landing as setup for the next.
**Setup:** Two kickers placed 5–7 metres apart.
**Execution:**
1. Launch kicker 1 in attack position.
2. Land and immediately prepare for kicker 2 — assess speed, position, and intent.
3. Launch kicker 2 from the correct position.
**Success Criteria:** Both kickers cleared cleanly with appropriate landing and preparation between them.
**Duration/Reps:** 15 runs.
**Progression:** Three kickers. Reduce spacing. Increase approach speed progressively.

## 8. TERRAIN & CONTEXT VARIATIONS

### Trail Kickers (Natural)
Variable and unpredictable — the approach may not be perfectly straight and the landing zone may be uneven. Requires more adaptive technique than a constructed kicker. Walk natural kickers before attempting at pace if the landing zone is unknown. Approach speed lower than on known kickers.

### Motocross Kickers (Constructed)
Consistent and well-defined — good learning environment for the technical aspects of jump technique. The approach angle, launch angle, and landing zone are predictable. Build technique here before applying to trail kickers.

### Kickers in Technical Terrain
Kickers embedded in rocky or rooted sections require instant decisions and may not allow for a clean landing zone. The absorb option is often preferred when landing terrain is unknown or the section continues immediately after the feature.

### Rain-Softened Kickers
Softer launch surface — the kicker compresses more under the tyre, reducing the effective launch angle. Approach speed calibration must account for the softer surface. The bike may not launch as steeply as expected.

### Kickers on Corners
A kicker mid-corner adds lateral forces to the vertical launch dynamics. The bike may yaw during the airborne phase. Approach alignment before the kicker and conservative speed are critical.

## 9. EXPERT INSIGHTS & SOURCES

### Key Insights
- **Absorb is underrated:** Many riders default to launching everything — it looks impressive but absorbing is often faster for small kickers in tight terrain where landing zones are uncertain. Professional coaches always emphasise developing both techniques equally. The absorb saves time when the alternative is a rough landing.
- **Rear-wheel-first landing:** The physics of rear-wheel-first landing are significantly safer than front-wheel-first. The rear suspension can absorb the initial impact before the front is loaded. Training this habit through deliberate weight positioning in the air is a significant safety improvement.
- **Throttle in air:** The most common beginner error is chopping throttle when going airborne — this pitches the nose down and creates a front-heavy landing. Maintaining or slightly holding throttle in the air is the single most important airborne habit. It must be trained explicitly because the instinct is to back off when uncertain.
- **Decision before the kicker:** Elite riders describe the absorb/launch decision as happening at 15 metres, not at 1 metre. By the time the kicker is at the front wheel, the technique is already executing. This forward decision-making is a trainable skill that dramatically improves consistency.
