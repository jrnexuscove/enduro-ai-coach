# Uphill Jump Entry

## 1. OVERVIEW

An uphill jump entry is a jump or kicker feature approached on an ascending gradient — where the slope angle of the approach is added to the launch angle of the kicker, producing a steeper-than-expected launch angle, more hang time, and different airborne dynamics than a flat approach to the same feature. In enduro, uphill jumps appear on hillside trails where a natural ledge, root cluster, or constructed feature creates a launch point mid-climb. The challenge is that the gradient reduces available approach speed (the climb is working against you), amplifies the launch angle, and means the landing terrain may be at a significantly higher elevation than a flat-approach analysis would suggest. Riders who treat an uphill jump entry like a flat jump consistently either under-speed (under-shoot) or launch at unexpected angles and distances.

## 2. CORE PRINCIPLES

### Principle 1: The Gradient Reduces Available Approach Speed
A jump approached on an uphill gradient requires more throttle to achieve the same entry speed as on flat ground. The slope is consuming energy — the rider is working against gravity. This means: more throttle than feels necessary, more gear commitment, and sometimes a speed run-up from lower on the slope. Riders who approach too slowly and attempt a jump on a climb will case the landing or stall at the lip.

### Principle 2: The Gradient Adds to the Launch Angle
A 15-degree uphill approach plus a 20-degree kicker angle produces a 35-degree effective launch angle — steeper than either individually. This means the bike launches more vertically and hangs in the air longer for the same approach speed. The rider must account for this additional hang time and the steeper airborne attitude. The nose will naturally be high — more so than expected from a flat approach to the same kicker.

### Principle 3: The Landing Is Higher Than the Take-Off
On an uphill jump, the landing zone is uphill from the launch point — not at the same elevation. The bike does not land at launch-elevation level (as on a flat jump); it lands at an elevation determined by the uphill terrain. This shortens the effective horizontal range compared to a flat jump. The rider who calculates landing range from a flat-jump mental model will consistently overshoot uphill landings.

### Principle 4: Throttle Must Be Higher Through the Launch Than on Flat Ground
The slope resistance means the engine is working harder even at constant speed. Through the launch moment, throttle must be maintained or increased to prevent the gradient from decelerating the bike as it reaches the kicker. A rider who reduces throttle at the kicker (instinctive "saving" behaviour) will have insufficient speed at the launch point.

### Principle 5: Airborne Pitch Correction Is More Important on Uphill Launches
Because the launch angle is steeper, the nose-up attitude in the air is more pronounced. The rider must make a more decisive airborne correction — throttle reduction to bring the nose down — to prevent a severe nose-up landing or a near-vertical attitude. The correction window is shorter because the trajectory is steeper and the landing comes sooner relative to peak altitude.

## 3. TECHNIQUE BREAKDOWN BY LEVEL

### 3a. Beginner Level
**Goal:** Understand the difference between flat and uphill jump entries — not attempting uphill jumps until intermediate level. Build the mental model before the physical technique.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Theoretical understanding of gradient effects on launch angle and landing elevation
- Practise on very small features on slight gradients (5 degrees maximum)
- Recognising the increased hang time and nose-high attitude on uphill launches
- Gear selection for uphill approach — understanding which gear maintains speed on a gradient

**What Good Looks Like:**
On a 5-degree slope with a small bump, the rider notices the launch angle is steeper than flat and the hang time is slightly longer. This observational experience builds the mental model before larger features are attempted. The rider can describe what happened differently from a flat jump.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Attempting significant uphill jumps before understanding the mechanics:** The increased launch angle and reduced approach speed combine to produce unexpected outcomes. → Theory and small-scale practice first. Master flat kickers before adding gradient.
- **Treating uphill kicker like a flat kicker:** Same approach speed as flat — but the gradient has reduced the effective speed at the lip. → Build in extra speed margin for uphill approaches.

### 3b. Intermediate Level
**Goal:** Execute small uphill jumps (launches below 1 metre) with correct speed management and airborne attitude correction.

**Progression Markers from Beginner:** Rider has flat-ground jump experience. Understands the uphill dynamics conceptually. Can climb technical sections with maintained momentum.

**Step-by-Step Execution:**
1. Assess the jump and gradient from below — identify the kicker angle and estimate the combined launch angle.
2. Select a gear that will maintain speed through the full approach and kicker. Second gear is usually correct.
3. Begin the approach run-up from further down the slope than feels necessary — gradient will slow the run.
4. Maintain or increase throttle all the way to the kicker lip. Do not reduce throttle.
5. Launch: the kicker pitches the front up more aggressively than expected. Allow this.
6. In the air: the nose is high — more so than a flat jump. Reduce throttle decisively to bring it down.
7. The landing is on uphill terrain — it is closer than a flat jump would be. Prepare for landing sooner.
8. Land rear-wheel-first or flat on the uphill terrain. Absorb deeply.
9. Immediately re-apply throttle after landing — on a climb, stalling after landing is a serious risk.

**What the Rider Should Feel at Each Stage:**
- Speed run-up: More throttle than feels normal — fighting the gradient. Speed building slowly.
- Kicker approach: Throttle held. No reduction at the kicker.
- Launch: A more aggressive pitch upward than expected — the gradient is adding to the kicker angle. The bike launches steeply.
- Air: Nose-high attitude more pronounced. Throttle reduction needed — earlier and more than on flat.
- Landing zone: The landing is closer (horizontally) than a flat jump — the uphill terrain is rising toward the bike.
- Landing: Impact on the uphill terrain — rear-wheel-first or flat. The slope helps cushion vertical landing speed.
- Recovery: Both wheels on uphill terrain. Throttle immediate to prevent stall on the slope.

**What Good Looks Like:**
Approaching an uphill kicker at full throttle maintaining speed against the gradient, the rider launches at a steep angle. In the air, the nose is high — a deliberate throttle reduction brings it level. The bike lands flat or slightly rear-wheel-first on the uphill terrain. Controlled recovery and continued climb.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Under-speed stall at lip:** The gradient consumed the approach speed — not enough left for the jump. → More speed, higher gear, longer run-up. The gradient is always taking from your available speed.
- **Over-speed in wrong gear:** Too much speed but the engine is screaming in a low gear — RPM too high for the load. → Correct gear selection. For uphill features, one gear higher than for a flat jump is often correct.
- **Front-up on gradient:** The steep launch angle combined with a throttle hold produces a very high nose angle. If uncorrected, the bike can approach vertical. → More aggressive throttle reduction in the air on uphill launches. Earlier than feels right.

### 3c. Advanced Level
**Goal:** Execute uphill jumps as a fluid part of climbing technique — managing the steeper launch angles and shortened landing ranges automatically.

**Progression Markers from Intermediate:** Rider can execute small uphill jumps. Speed management is understood. Airborne correction is present.

**Step-by-Step Execution — Larger Features:**
1. Walk the uphill jump section if new — identify the exact kicker, the landing zone, and the gradient angle.
2. Determine the correct gear from the walk: which gear maintains speed through the approach without bouncing off the rev limiter?
3. Approach with a committed run-up — enough distance to build speed against the gradient.
4. Attack position through the kicker. No weight shift for the launch — the kicker does the work.
5. Extended airborne: larger jumps on steeper gradients mean more airtime. Manage pitch through the full air phase.
6. The landing terrain is significantly higher than the launch point. Prepare for a shorter horizontal range.
7. Land and immediately commit throttle for the continued climb.

**Failure Modes and Causes:**
- **Under-speed stall at the lip:** The approach speed was insufficient — the bike barely cleared the kicker and is now nearly stationary on a steep climb. This can result in a fall backward. → Longer and faster approach run. Test the minimum speed with a small feature first.
- **Over-speed in wrong gear:** Revs too high at the kicker — power delivery too aggressive, wheel spin on the launch. → Higher gear for the approach. Smooth power delivery is more effective than raw RPM.
- **Front-up uncontrolled:** Steep launch plus too much throttle plus no correction = near-vertical airborne. → Throttle reduction in the air must be more aggressive and earlier than a flat jump correction. Do not wait for the nose to be very high before correcting.

## 4. DIAGNOSTIC CUES (AI COACHING ENGINE REFERENCE)

### Visual Cues — Correct Technique
- Consistent throttle approach — no speed reduction visible before the kicker on the climb
- Airborne pitch more upright than flat — expected and correct on uphill
- Throttle reduction visible in air — nose correction occurring before landing
- Landing on the uphill slope with absorption — not a flat-plane impact

### Visual Cues — Error Indicators
- Speed dropping visibly in the approach — insufficient throttle against gradient
- Extreme nose-up in air — no throttle correction being made, approach to vertical attitude
- Bike barely clearing the kicker — under-speed, nearly stalling at the lip
- Rear wheel spinning on approach — wrong gear, excessive wheelspin eating speed

### Audio Cues
- Correct: Engine note maintained or increasing through launch — gradient overcome
- Error: Engine note dropping at the kicker — throttle reduction at worst moment
- Error: Engine note too high/screaming on approach — wrong gear, too low for the gradient load

### Sensor Cues
- Correct: Speed maintained at launch point — gradient effect managed
- Error: Speed dropping at launch — insufficient approach, gradient winning

## 5. COACHING CUES & LANGUAGE

### Positive Reinforcement
- "You held throttle all the way to the lip — the gradient didn't slow you down. That commitment is what made the launch work."
- "Good nose correction in the air — you brought the nose down before the landing. Earlier than on a flat jump — that's the uphill adjustment."
- "Immediate throttle after landing on the slope. No stall. You treated the post-landing as part of the climb, not the end of the jump."

### Corrective Feedback
- "Speed was dropping as you reached the kicker — the gradient was winning. You need more throttle on the approach, not less. The slope is fighting you all the way."
- "Nose was too high in the air and you didn't correct. Throttle reduction brings it down. Don't wait until the nose is extreme — reduce earlier and more than you would on a flat jump."
- "Wrong gear for that gradient — you were screaming at the kicker. Try one gear higher next pass — more torque, less noise, smoother power."

### Progression Prompts
- "You can handle this size on this gradient. Try a steeper gradient same size — the launch angle will be even more dramatic. Larger correction needed in the air."
- "Next run, count to yourself on the approach: 'throttle, throttle, throttle.' Keep it in your head to prevent the natural instinct to reduce at the kicker."

## 6. DRILLS & PRACTICE EXERCISES

### Drill 1: Gradient Speed Maintenance
**Purpose:** Build the throttle commitment needed to maintain speed against a gradient through a kicker.
**Setup:** A gradual slope with a small bump mid-slope.
**Execution:**
1. Approach with consistent speed through the bump. No deceleration at any point.
2. Check speed before the bump and after the bump.
3. Aim for speed maintained within 3 km/h through the bump.
**Success Criteria:** Speed maintained within 3 km/h through the bump on 8 out of 10 passes.
**Duration/Reps:** 15 passes. **Progression:** Steeper slope.

### Drill 2: Nose Correction Practice
**Purpose:** Build the airborne throttle correction reflex for nose-high attitude — trained on flat before applying to uphill.
**Setup:** A flat kicker where a deliberate over-launch simulates the uphill nose-high condition.
**Execution:**
1. Deliberately launch with more throttle than usual — produces a nose-high attitude.
2. Correct the nose-high with an earlier and more decisive throttle reduction than normal.
3. Aim for neutral pitch on landing.
**Success Criteria:** Nose brought to neutral from high on 6 out of 10 launches.
**Duration/Reps:** 15 launches. **Progression:** Move to actual uphill kicker.

### Drill 3: Uphill Kicker Speed Calibration
**Purpose:** Find the minimum approach speed for a specific uphill kicker.
**Setup:** A small uphill kicker with clear landing zone.
**Execution:**
1. First pass: very conservative speed — observe if the kicker is cleared.
2. Each subsequent pass, add 2 km/h to the approach speed.
3. Identify the minimum speed for a clean launch (not casing, not under-jumping).
**Success Criteria:** Minimum speed identified and reproducible on consecutive passes.
**Duration/Reps:** 8 passes. **Progression:** Larger kicker.

### Drill 4: Full Uphill Jump Sequence
**Purpose:** Execute the complete uphill jump sequence with all elements connected.
**Setup:** A suitable uphill kicker with a clear landing zone.
**Execution:**
1. Run-up from below — speed building against gradient.
2. Throttle through launch — no reduction.
3. Nose correction in air — throttle reduction, earlier than flat jump.
4. Land on uphill terrain — absorb.
5. Immediate throttle for continued climb.
**Success Criteria:** Clean landing with no stall on 5 consecutive passes.
**Duration/Reps:** 15 passes. **Progression:** Higher gradient.

## 8. TERRAIN & CONTEXT VARIATIONS

### Moderate Gradient (10–15 degrees)
Launch angle amplification manageable. Nose-high correction needed but not extreme. Good learning gradient for the first uphill jump attempts.

### Steep Gradient (20–30 degrees)
Launch angle amplification significant. Speed loss through gradient is serious — run-up must be long. Nose-high correction must be early and decisive.

### Loose Uphill Terrain
Rear traction is reduced on loose uphill — the approach may involve wheel spin that further reduces effective approach speed. Clutch feathering on the approach to manage traction while maintaining momentum.

### Wet Uphill
Approach traction severely reduced. Kicker surface may be slippery — the launch angle and drive force are less predictable. Only attempt known features in wet conditions, and only with significantly reduced speed.

### Rocky or Root Kicker
A kicker composed of rocks or roots provides inconsistent launch surface — one side of the kicker may launch earlier than the other, creating a lateral pitch component as well as the expected vertical lift. Riders should assess whether the kicker surface is even before attempting. An uneven rocky kicker may require slight body weight correction to counteract lateral pitch on launch.

### Competition Section With Multiple Uphill Jumps
In enduro events, uphill sections sometimes feature multiple jump features on the same climb. Each launch affects the approach speed for the next — a landing that kills momentum changes the available speed for the subsequent kicker. Riders who practise sections with multiple uphill jumps learn to manage the momentum chain: the speed for the second jump is set by the recovery quality from the first landing.

### Deep Sand Uphill Approach
Sand climbs with a kicker present a particular challenge: the rear wheel is losing traction through the entire approach, limiting effective speed build. The kicker launch from sand is softer — less aggressive front pitch — because the rear traction is reduced and the launch drive is lower. The airborne phase may be shorter than expected despite the gradient. Riders accustomed to hard-surface uphill jumps must recalibrate expectations on sand: the launch angle is still elevated, but the height and range are significantly reduced.

## 9. EXPERT INSIGHTS & SOURCES

### Key Insights
- **The gradient mental model difference:** Experienced enduro coaches note that riders who lack the uphill mental model consistently misjudge uphill jump features — they either under-speed or are surprised by the launch angle. Building the specific mental model for uphill jumps is as important as the physical technique.
- **Gear selection is as important as speed:** On uphill features, gear selection is critical — a gear too low and the engine bounces off the rev limiter at the kicker; a gear too high and the engine bogs. The correct gear maintains consistent power delivery through the full approach run. Most riders use one gear higher on uphill features than they would for the same speed on flat terrain.
- **Post-landing throttle:** Many riders nail the jump but stall immediately after landing because they forget that they are still on a climb. The post-landing throttle must be immediate and deliberate — the slope gradient does not stop just because the landing was successful.
- **Visualise the full sequence before committing:** Advanced coaches recommend that riders new to uphill jumps mentally walk through the full sequence — run-up speed, throttle through the lip, nose-high correction, landing point, immediate post-landing throttle — before the first attempt. The mental rehearsal reduces surprises because each phase has already been imagined.
- **The shortened horizontal range:** One of the most counter-intuitive aspects of uphill jumps is that the landing is horizontally closer than expected. On a flat jump, the landing might be 5 metres from the lip. On the same kicker on a 20-degree uphill, the landing may be only 2.5 metres because the terrain is rising to meet the bike. Riders who calculate range from flat-jump experience consistently overshoot uphill landings, expecting to clear more distance than the terrain provides.
- **Walk before first attempt on unknown features:** Any uphill jump feature that is new should be walked on foot from below the kicker to the expected landing zone. Understanding the height of the kicker, the gradient of the approach, and the quality of the landing zone makes the first attempt significantly safer.
- **Two-stroke versus four-stroke on uphill launches:** Riders transitioning between engine types notice a significant difference on uphill jump execution. Two-strokes deliver a sharper, more sudden power band hit — the clutch-up or throttle blip at the kicker produces a more explosive front lift that requires earlier weight correction. Four-strokes have a more progressive power delivery that is more forgiving of timing errors on the kicker. Coaches note that riders trained primarily on one engine type often misjudge the launch timing when switching, with two-stroke riders commonly over-lifting on four-strokes and four-stroke riders under-lifting on two-strokes.
- **The gradient changes the landing impact force:** Landing on an uphill slope after an uphill jump is mechanically kinder than landing on flat terrain — the slope is rising to meet the bike, so the relative velocity at contact is lower. This is why uphill jump landings feel softer than flat landings at equivalent speed. Coaches use this understanding to build rider confidence: the uphill jump is not the same risk as a flat jump at identical speed, and riders who understand the physics approach the feature more confidently and consistently.
- **Momentum management before the kicker determines everything:** Coaches consistently identify the approach speed as the single variable that most affects uphill jump outcome. Riders who enter the kicker with inconsistent speed produce inconsistent launches — varying lift angle, varying airborne time, varying landing position. Building a consistent entry speed through the approach section (using landmarks and consistent braking points) is the practice habit that produces reliable uphill jump execution. Inconsistent approach speed is the root cause of most beginner uphill jump errors.
- **Post-landing throttle is part of the technique:** Many coaches note that riders separate the jump in their mental model from what comes after — they consider a clean landing to be "success" and the post-landing as a separate event. On an uphill jump, this is incorrect. The stall immediately after landing is a direct consequence of treating the landing as the end of the jump. Training the full sequence — launch, air, landing, immediate throttle — as a single continuous action prevents the post-landing stall and reinforces that the jump is one technique, not three.
