# Downhill Corner Exit

## 1. OVERVIEW

A downhill corner exit is a corner that transitions onto a descending slope immediately after the apex — the moment the bike is pointed at the exit, gravity begins accelerating it down the hill. This is significantly different from a flat or uphill corner exit, where the rider has control over exit speed through throttle alone. On a downhill exit, gravity is a constant acceleration force — if the rider applies full throttle on the exit as they would on flat terrain, speed builds rapidly beyond the desired level. Conversely, using engine braking alone on a downhill exit can cause the rear wheel to slide or the bike to feel unstable. Managing exit speed, weight position, and choosing between throttle, braking, and engine braking is the core technical challenge.

## 2. CORE PRINCIPLES

### Principle 1: Gravity Adds to Exit Speed — Plan for It
On a flat corner exit, the rider controls exit speed entirely through throttle. On a downhill exit, gravity continuously adds speed — the rider must account for this acceleration when deciding how much (if any) throttle to apply. The question on every downhill corner exit is: "how much speed will I have at the bottom of this straight if I apply X throttle?" The answer includes the gradient acceleration, not just the engine's contribution. Riders who exit downhill corners on full throttle as they would on flat terrain will find themselves going significantly faster than intended within seconds.

### Principle 2: Weight Rearward Manages the Downhill Speed Increase
As the bike accelerates down the exit slope, the rider's weight must shift rearward to prevent the front wheel from being overloaded by the downhill inertia. A rider who exits a downhill corner with weight forward will feel the front end diving and loading as speed builds — increasing the risk of front wash on the exit. Rearward weight keeps the front wheel light and steerable as gravity adds speed.

### Principle 3: Engine Braking Is Available — But Use It Judiciously
Closing the throttle on a downhill exit engages engine braking — the engine acts as a retarder, slowing the rear wheel. On loose surfaces, aggressive engine braking can cause the rear wheel to slide or skip. On hard-pack, engine braking is a controlled and effective speed management tool. On loose downhill exits, engine braking must be applied progressively — not by snapping the throttle shut.

### Principle 4: Rear Brake Supplements Engine Braking Without Causing Front Dive
If the exit speed is building too fast, the rear brake can supplement engine braking — the rear brake slows the rear wheel without transferring weight to the front (unlike the front brake). On a downhill exit, using rear brake rather than front brake for speed management is preferable — it avoids the front-loading that a front brake application would cause on a downhill.

### Principle 5: Exit Line Selection Determines the Rate of Speed Increase
A downhill corner exit on the outside of the corner (wide exit line) produces a steeper immediate descent. An exit line that hugs the inside of the corner longer before straightening reduces the initial descent angle. Line selection on a downhill corner exit affects the rate at which gravity begins its acceleration — a good line choice buys the rider time to establish control before the maximum descent begins.

## 3. TECHNIQUE BREAKDOWN BY LEVEL

### 3a. Beginner Level
**Goal:** Exit a moderate downhill corner with controlled speed — not applying full flat-ground throttle on a downhill exit.

**Step-by-Step Execution:**
1. At the corner apex, assess the exit gradient — is this a downhill exit?
2. As the bike points at the exit, deliberately do not apply full throttle.
3. Weight shifts rearward as the bike points downhill.
4. Allow the gradient to build speed gradually — observe how fast speed increases without throttle.
5. If speed is appropriate: maintain zero throttle or a very small amount.
6. If speed is building too fast: progressive engine braking (reduce throttle gradually) or light rear brake.
7. Eyes well ahead on the exit straight — not at the immediate ground.

**What the Rider Should Feel:**
- Apex: Corner is complete. Front wheel is pointing at the exit.
- Gravity engagement: The bike accelerates without any throttle input — felt as the bike pulling forward and the weight shifting toward the front.
- Rearward shift: Deliberate hips-back movement — the bars lighten, the rear pegs load.
- Speed management: The choice between holding, reducing, or adding speed, made consciously.

**What Good Looks Like:**
Exiting a moderate downhill corner, the rider holds moderate throttle (not full open) and the bike accelerates at a manageable rate. Weight is rearward. The exit speed is appropriate for the terrain ahead.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Full throttle on downhill exit:** Speed builds too fast for the exit terrain. → Less throttle. The gradient is already accelerating — throttle accelerates further. This is a fundamental mental model error.
- **Engine braking snap:** Throttle closed abruptly — rear slides or locks on downhill. → Progressive throttle reduction. The rear must maintain grip.

### 3b. Intermediate Level
**Goal:** Manage downhill exit speed precisely using the combination of throttle, engine braking, and rear brake.

**Progression Markers from Beginner:** Rider recognises downhill exit speed buildup. Weight is rearward. Throttle management is more conservative.

**Step-by-Step Execution:**
1. Identify the corner as a downhill exit before entering the corner — not at the apex.
2. Exit the corner with no throttle initially — let gravity show you how fast it will accelerate.
3. Weight shifts rearward as the front wheel clears the apex and points downhill.
4. Monitor speed buildup: is it at the correct rate? Too fast? Too slow?
5. If too fast: progressive engine braking first. If still too fast: light rear brake supplement.
6. If too slow (flat gradient): light throttle to maintain momentum.
7. At no point: front brake for speed management on a downhill exit.
8. Eyes extended far ahead — the downhill straight ahead has features that need to be visible now.

**What the Rider Should Feel at Each Stage:**
- Corner apex: The corner is complete. The front wheel is pointing at the downhill exit.
- Gravity engagement: Immediately, the bike begins to accelerate — not from throttle, but from gravity. Felt as the bike pulling forward and the weight wanting to shift forward.
- Weight rearward: Conscious rearward shift — hips back. Felt as the weight transferring from the bars toward the rear pegs. The bars lighten slightly.
- Speed management decision: Is the current rate of speed increase manageable? If yes — hold. If no — engine brake or rear brake.
- Engine braking application: Throttle reduced progressively — not snapped. Felt as the engine note dropping and a slight deceleration of the rear wheel.
- Rear brake supplement: If additional speed management needed, right foot applies light rear brake. Felt as a slight drag on the rear, reducing acceleration without front dive.
- Exit stabilisation: Speed is now appropriate. Eyes are far ahead on the exit straight.

**What Good Looks Like:**
Exiting a steep downhill corner, the rider applies zero throttle initially — engine braking manages the speed increase from gravity. As speed stabilises, a light throttle maintains the stable speed. If speed is still too high, light rear brake supplements. Weight is rearward throughout.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Too much engine braking (rear snap):** Throttle closed too quickly — rear wheel slips or locks on the downhill slope. → Progressive throttle reduction. The rear must maintain grip throughout.
- **Front diving on exit brake:** Front brake applied mid-exit — weight transfers forward dramatically on downhill. → Rear brake only for downhill exit speed management.
- **Rear snap on loose downhill:** Engine braking combined with loose surface — rear slides aggressively. → Reduce throttle even more progressively on loose downhill surfaces.

### 3c. Advanced Level
**Goal:** Manage downhill corner exits at competition pace — precise speed control enabling fast entry into the next feature.

**Progression Markers from Intermediate:** Rider manages moderate downhill exits. Weight is rearward. Speed management tools are understood.

**Step-by-Step Execution — Competition Pace:**
1. The downhill exit is identified from the corner entry line — this shapes how the corner is ridden.
2. Corner speed is managed to exit at the correct speed for the downhill — not too fast, not too slow.
3. Line selection on exit: choose the line that gives the best angle onto the downhill (delays the steepest gradient).
4. Exit weight already rearward before the apex — pre-loaded into the descent position.
5. Zero or minimal throttle on exit. Rear brake used confidently if speed builds too fast.
6. Line management down the exit straight: staying on the inside of any camber to reduce effective gradient.
7. Transition seamlessly into the next feature at the bottom of the straight.

**What Good Looks Like:**
At competition pace, the rider reads the downhill corner exit from the entry, selects the correct exit line, arrives at the apex with appropriate speed, and manages the downhill acceleration with rear brake — arriving at the bottom of the straight at exactly the right speed for the next feature.

**Failure Modes and Causes:**
- **Too much speed into downhill straight:** Throttle applied as on a flat exit — speed too high for the terrain at the bottom. → Gravity-awareness on every downhill exit. Plan the exit speed from the corner entry.
- **Front diving on exit brake application:** Front brake used for speed management — weight transfer forward is amplified by the downhill. → Rear brake for downhill speed management only.
- **Rear snap from aggressive engine braking:** Throttle snapped shut — rear slides. → Progressive. Always progressive on downhill.

## 4. DIAGNOSTIC CUES (AI COACHING ENGINE REFERENCE)

### Visual Cues — Correct Technique
- Weight clearly rearward on exit slope — hips back, arms slightly extended toward bars
- No front dive on exit — consistent front wheel height as speed builds
- Rear brake light visible if speed management occurring — not front brake
- Eyes ahead — rider's head angled forward, reading the exit straight

### Visual Cues — Error Indicators
- Weight forward on downhill exit — front diving under gravity acceleration
- Rear sliding or skipping — aggressive engine braking on loose surface
- Front brake light visible — most dangerous input on downhill exit
- Rider sitting on the downhill exit — weight too far forward and low

### Audio Cues
- Correct: Engine note moderate and stable — appropriate throttle for downhill
- Error: Sudden engine note drop — throttle snapped shut, engine braking snap risk
- Error: Rear tyre sliding sound — too much engine braking on loose surface

### Sensor Cues
- Correct: Speed building moderately and controlled on downhill exit
- Error: Speed building too rapidly — insufficient exit speed management
- Error: Rear wheel deceleration spike — rear brake or engine braking snap

## 5. COACHING CUES & LANGUAGE

### Positive Reinforcement
- "Weight was back on that downhill exit. Front stayed light as the speed built. That's correct."
- "Good rear brake use — managed the speed without loading the front."
- "You read the exit as a downhill from the corner entry. That early identification changed how you managed the whole corner."

### Corrective Feedback
- "Full throttle on a downhill exit — gravity is already accelerating. You don't need more throttle, you need less. Let the gradient do the work."
- "You snapped the throttle shut — rear skipped. Progressive reduction. The gradient is downhill, the rear traction is lower than on flat."
- "Front brake on a downhill exit — that's the highest-risk input. Rear brake only for speed management here."

### Progression Prompts
- "You've got the moderate downhill exit. Try the steep one — the rate of speed build is much higher. You'll need rear brake earlier."
- "Next time, identify whether the exit is a downhill before you enter the corner. Make it a habit: approach the corner, check the exit. It changes the whole entry strategy."

## 6. DRILLS & PRACTICE EXERCISES

### Drill 1: Gravity-Only Downhill Exit
**Purpose:** Experience the natural speed buildup from gravity alone — no throttle, so the gradient's effect is isolated.
**Setup:** A downhill corner with a clear exit straight.
**Execution:**
1. Exit the corner with zero throttle.
2. Observe the speed buildup from gravity alone over 15 metres.
3. Note the rate and compare to what you expected.
**Success Criteria:** Rider can articulate the rate of gravity-induced acceleration on this gradient.
**Duration/Reps:** 5 passes. **Progression:** Add progressive engine braking to manage the speed.

### Drill 2: Rear Brake Speed Management
**Purpose:** Use rear brake confidently as the primary downhill exit speed tool.
**Setup:** A steep downhill exit where speed builds faster than desired.
**Execution:**
1. Exit the corner.
2. Use rear brake progressively to manage speed to a target level (e.g., 25 km/h).
3. Hold that speed to the end of the straight.
**Success Criteria:** Speed maintained within 5 km/h of target using rear brake only.
**Duration/Reps:** 10 passes. **Progression:** Steeper gradient.

### Drill 3: Weight Rearward Habit
**Purpose:** Establish rearward weight as an automatic response to downhill exit.
**Setup:** Any downhill corner exit.
**Execution:**
1. Before entering the corner, tell yourself: "this is a downhill exit — weight back at the apex."
2. Execute the corner.
3. At the apex, consciously confirm weight rearward before speed builds.
**Success Criteria:** Weight rearward established before speed builds on 9 out of 10 exits.
**Duration/Reps:** 15 passes. **Progression:** Steeper gradient.

### Drill 4: Downhill Exit to Technical Feature
**Purpose:** Manage exit speed for an immediate technical feature at the bottom.
**Setup:** A downhill corner that exits into a tight section, corner, or obstacle.
**Execution:**
1. Manage speed on the downhill exit so the entry to the tight section is appropriate.
2. Rear brake or engine braking to arrive at the correct speed.
**Success Criteria:** Tight section navigated smoothly without being over-speed on 8 out of 10 runs.
**Duration/Reps:** 10 runs. **Progression:** Tighter feature at bottom, steeper approach.

## 8. TERRAIN & CONTEXT VARIATIONS

### Hard-Pack Downhill Exit
Engine braking effective and controlled. Rear brake reliable. Standard technique. Front brake supplement very light and only on firm terrain.

### Loose Downhill Exit
Engine braking must be very progressive — rear wheel is traction-limited on the loose surface. Rear brake also used carefully. Zero front brake. Accept slightly higher speed if the surface doesn't allow aggressive management.

### Steep Gradient (20%+)
Speed builds very rapidly. Rear brake must be engaged earlier and more firmly. Weight rearward is even more critical — the gradient is pushing the rider's mass forward aggressively.

### Downhill Exit Into Water or Mud
Speed must be managed carefully — the exit terrain has reduced traction. Over-speed into a water crossing or mud section is dangerous. Treat these sections as requiring significantly lower approach speed.

## 9. EXPERT INSIGHTS & SOURCES

### Key Insights
- **Gradient awareness:** This is fundamentally about understanding that gravity is always present on a downhill and planning accordingly. Riders who have this awareness built in manage downhill corners intuitively; riders who lack it consistently over-speed.
- **Rear brake as primary tool:** The preference for rear brake over front brake on downhill exits is a universal coaching principle — the front brake on a downhill can cause a forward pitch that is very difficult to manage. The rear brake manages speed without this risk.
- **Identifying the exit before the corner entry:** Advanced riders note that they identify the exit gradient before entering the corner. This changes the entry speed, the line, and the body position through the whole corner — not just at the exit. The exit awareness starts at the entry.
- **Gravity is the extra variable beginners forget:** A downhill corner exit is one of the most common places where beginners over-speed because they apply the flat-corner habit of opening the throttle on exit. Gravity is already doing the throttle's job. Coaches who work with beginners on this skill consistently describe it as a "cognitive barrier" — riders know about gravity but forget to include it in their throttle calculation. The cue "gravity is your throttle" helps.
- **The line that delays the descent:** Advanced riders choose exit lines that delay the steepest part of the descent — hugging the inside of the corner through the exit before straightening. This gives more time to establish rearward weight and assess exit speed before the maximum gradient begins.
- **Engine braking calibration:** On different bikes (two-stroke vs four-stroke, different engine characteristics), the engine braking behaviour on downhill exits varies significantly. Riders switching between bikes must recalibrate their progressive throttle reduction habit — a two-stroke may have very little engine braking, while a four-stroke with a flywheel effect may have significant engine braking on the same gradient.
- **The stutter-brake pattern for very steep exits:** On extremely steep downhill corner exits, coaches introduce a stutter-braking pattern — brief, alternating rear brake applications rather than a long progressive hold. This prevents rear brake heat build-up and rear tyre flat-spotting on long steep exits, and also helps the rear tyre maintain grip on loose surfaces better than a sustained drag. Advanced riders develop this as an instinctive rhythm on steep descents that follows the natural terrain undulation.
- **Eyes and the horizon effect:** On steep downhill corner exits, the visual horizon tilts dramatically as the bike pitches forward on the descent. Coaches observe that riders who have not trained on steep downhills tend to fix their gaze on the immediate ground in front of the wheel — a reaction to the disorienting tilt. Deliberate training to look further ahead on steep exits, finding a reference point on the descent below rather than the ground immediately in front, directly improves speed and line quality on these sections.
- **Fatigue and technique degradation:** In long enduro events, technique degradation on downhill corner exits is one of the first signs of rider fatigue. The rearward weight bias requires core engagement that diminishes as the rider tires — the rider begins sitting neutral or forward, which loads the front, increases dive, and raises front wash risk. Coaches monitoring riders in events look for forward weight position on downhill exits as a leading indicator of fatigue-driven crash risk and adjust pace accordingly.
