# Uphill Rocky Section

## 1. OVERVIEW

A rocky climb section combines the physical demands of uphill gradient with the technical demands of rock obstacle management — each rock deflects the front wheel, each loose patch threatens rear traction, and stalling mid-section means a difficult restart or worse, a fall backward. Unlike a smooth uphill where power and body position are the primary variables, a rocky climb requires continuous line reading, momentum management around individual rocks, clutch pops and feathering at specific points, and a body position that simultaneously keeps weight forward enough to hold the front wheel down and back enough to maximise rear traction. It is one of the most technically demanding skills in enduro riding.

## 2. CORE PRINCIPLES

### Principle 1: Momentum Is More Valuable Than Traction on Rocky Climbs
On a rocky climb, the rocks deflect the front wheel unpredictably — traction is constantly interrupted and resumed. Attempting to ride slowly and precisely through each rock is harder than maintaining enough momentum that the deflections are brief and manageable. Momentum carries the bike through deflections that would stop a slow-moving bike. This does not mean reckless speed — it means sufficient momentum to overcome individual rock contacts without losing forward drive.

### Principle 2: Gear Selection Before the Section — Not Mid-Section
The correct gear for a rocky climb is chosen based on the section's gradient, length, and rock size before entering — not changed during the section. Mid-section gear changes risk stalling (if mishandled) and disrupt the momentum and throttle rhythm that keeps the bike climbing. If the wrong gear is selected, the rider must work with it through the section.

### Principle 3: Front Wheel Deflection Is Normal — Allow It and Recover
Each rock the front wheel contacts will deflect the wheel — sometimes significantly. The rider must allow this deflection by having loose arms and wrists, and recover the line with subtle steering corrections between rocks. A rider who fights the front wheel deflections with stiff arms will be exhausted within seconds and will lose line consistently.

### Principle 4: Rear Wheel Traction Is the Drive Source — Protect It
The rear wheel must maintain consistent drive throughout the climb. On rocky terrain, this means: no excessive wheelspin (spins without forward drive, digs holes), no aggressive clutch pops that break traction violently, and body position that keeps some weight over the rear wheel for traction without loading the front into each rock. Clutch feathering calibrates rear wheel drive to the available traction.

### Principle 5: Line Selection Minimises Rock Contact Angle
Rocks that the front wheel hits head-on (perpendicular to the rock face) deflect the wheel straight back. Rocks hit at an angle deflect the wheel sideways. Angled approaches to rocks are more manageable — the wheel rolls over rather than slamming into. Line selection that presents rocks at an angle to the direction of travel produces smoother progress than a straight-on approach.

## 3. TECHNIQUE BREAKDOWN BY LEVEL

### 3a. Beginner Level
**Goal:** Climb a moderate rocky section without stalling — using pre-selected gear and maintained momentum.

**Step-by-Step Execution:**
1. Walk the section before riding — identify the gear needed, the major rocks, and loose patches.
2. Select 2nd gear before entering — almost always correct for rocky climbs.
3. Enter with momentum — don't slow down at the entry.
4. Body in attack position: weight forward enough to keep the front down, but not so far that the rear spins.
5. Eyes 3–4 metres ahead — not at the immediate rocks.
6. Throttle consistent — do not cut throttle on rock contact. Hold it.
7. Allow front wheel deflections — loose arms, let the bars move.
8. Continue to the top. If the engine bogs, don't cut throttle — try slight clutch slip.

**What the Rider Should Feel:**
- Entry: Momentum building into the section. Gear set, body position ready.
- Rock contacts: The bars twitch left and right. Arms absorb but don't fight.
- Throttle: Consistent — a constant sound from the engine through all the rocks.
- Rear traction: The rear wheel occasionally spinning briefly — the engine RPM rises momentarily without forward drive.

**What Good Looks Like:**
In 2nd gear on a moderate rocky climb, the rider maintains consistent throttle through deflections and rock contacts. The bike climbs slowly but continuously. Occasional rear wheel spin is managed by easing slightly. No stall.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Throttle cut on rock contact:** The natural response to a front deflection is to back off throttle — which removes drive at the worst moment. → Hold throttle through all contacts. The front deflecting is normal.
- **Wrong gear:** 1st gear causes wheel spin and lack of drive. 3rd gear bogs on the rocks. → 2nd gear is almost always correct for technical rocky climbs.

### 3b. Intermediate Level
**Goal:** Climb technical rocky sections with clutch pops on individual rocks and feathering on loose patches — continuous momentum management.

**Progression Markers from Beginner:** Rider can climb moderate rocky sections without stalling. Throttle is maintained. Attack position is established.

**Step-by-Step Execution:**
1. Walk the section — identify: where are the solid rocks (clutch pop); where are the loose patches (clutch feather); where are the problematic lines (avoid).
2. Gear selection: 2nd gear unless the section is very steep, in which case 1st gear with careful clutch management.
3. Enter with momentum. Body weight centred to slightly forward.
4. On solid individual rocks: clutch pop timed to front wheel contact — the drive spike carries the front wheel over.
5. On loose patches between rocks: clutch feather — reduce drive to prevent wheelspin that digs holes.
6. On solid rock surfaces again: full clutch engagement — maximum drive.
7. Loose arms for front deflections throughout.
8. Eyes 4–5 metres ahead — reading the next clutch technique decision before arriving.

**What the Rider Should Feel at Each Stage:**
- Pre-section: Gear set. Attack position established. Speed building for momentum.
- First rocks: Front wheel deflects — felt as the bars twitching left or right. Loose arms allow this. Throttle maintained.
- Loose patch: Rear wheel begins to spin — felt as the rear sliding slightly and the RPM rising without corresponding forward drive. Clutch feathered — drive reduces, spin stops.
- Large rock: Front wheel approaching a significant rock — clutch pop timed to the contact moment. Felt as the front wheel rising and the bike surging forward. Rear drive continues.
- Rear wheel on rock: Rear tyre contacts the same rock — throttle maintained. The rear drives over the rock. A slight rearward pitch as the rear descends off the rock's far side.
- Recovery: Eyes already on the next feature 4 metres ahead. The pattern continues.

**What Good Looks Like:**
On a complex rocky climb, the rider uses a combination of full clutch engagement on firm rock surfaces (maximum drive), clutch feathering on loose patches (controlled drive), and brief clutch pops on large individual rocks (additional drive spikes). The bike makes continuous upward progress despite many rock contacts and deflections.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Stall on rocks:** Throttle cut when facing a large rock — engine dies. → Throttle held. If needed, clutch pop to add drive. Never cut throttle facing a rock.
- **Rear wheel spin-out:** Too much throttle on loose patches — rear loses traction entirely. → Clutch feather on loose sections. Spin is wasteful; controlled drive is forward.
- **Wrong line selection:** Straight-on approach to a large rock — head-on deflection causes significant line disruption. → Approach rocks at a slight angle where possible.

### 3c. Advanced Level
**Goal:** Climb extreme rocky sections at pace — reading the entire section from below and executing with minimal hesitation.

**Progression Markers from Intermediate:** Rider can climb technical rocky sections. Clutch technique is calibrated. Line selection is active.

**Step-by-Step Execution — Extreme Rocky Climbs:**
1. Full section pre-read from below — the entire line is mentally planned before entry.
2. Enter with maximum safe momentum for the section. Speed is the primary tool.
3. Chaining clutch pops: multiple consecutive large rocks may each require a pop — timing them in sequence.
4. Managing partial stops: if momentum nearly dies mid-section, a decisive throttle and clutch pop may recover without stopping.
5. Recovery from a complete stall: controlled dismount before falling backward. Restart protocol.
6. Rocky climbs combined with ledge features or drop-offs: the section has multiple technique types within it.

**Failure Modes and Causes:**
- **Stall on rocks:** Engine dies from throttle cut or wrong gear. → Throttle committed. Gear correct. If stalled, controlled dismount before falling backward.
- **Rear wheel spin-out:** Excessive wheelspin on a critical rock — rear loses all drive and the bike slides back. → Clutch feather and correct throttle level for the surface.
- **Wrong line selection:** Committing to a line that ends with the front wheel blocked by a rock face with no exit. → Pre-read the entire section. Know the exit before committing to the entry.

## 4. DIAGNOSTIC CUES (AI COACHING ENGINE REFERENCE)

### Visual Cues — Correct Technique
- Consistent forward progress through the rocky section — no complete stops
- Front wheel deflections absorbed — loose arm visible, body not jerking with each contact
- Attack position standing throughout — no sitting
- Clutch hand visible feathering on loose patches, popping on large rocks

### Visual Cues — Error Indicators
- Stall — bike stopped mid-climb
- Rear wheel spin creating a hole — excessive wheelspin without forward drive
- Sitting on the rocky climb — seat contact visible
- Stiff arms fighting front wheel deflections — rider moving dramatically with each contact

### Audio Cues
- Correct: Engine note consistent — throttle maintained
- Correct: Brief clutch pop sounds at individual rocks — engine note rises then drops
- Error: Engine stall — loss of engine sound mid-climb
- Error: Sustained rear wheel spin sound — wheel spinning without drive

### Sensor Cues
- Correct: Consistent forward speed through climb — no zero-speed events
- Error: Speed drops to zero — stall occurred
- Error: Rear wheel speed much higher than forward speed — wheelspin without drive

## 5. COACHING CUES & LANGUAGE

### Positive Reinforcement
- "Clutch pop on the big rock — timed perfectly. I heard the engine rise and the bike carried through. That's the technique."
- "Throttle held through all the deflections. Front was bouncing around but you kept the drive. That's commitment."
- "You feathered the clutch on that loose patch — no wheelspin. Drive maintained. That's the fine control that makes the difference."

### Corrective Feedback
- "Throttle cut when the front deflected — engine died. The front deflecting is normal. Hold the throttle."
- "Rear was spinning for 2 seconds on that loose patch — no forward drive. Feather the clutch. Less spin, more drive."
- "Straight-on approach to that big rock — head-on impact. Come at it from a slight angle next time. The wheel rolls over rather than deflecting straight back."

### Progression Prompts
- "Walk the next rocky climb — identify where the loose patches are and where the solid rocks are. Plan your clutch technique for each section before riding it."
- "On your next pass, your only goal is to not cut throttle — not once. Every rock, hold it. Let the clutch do the traction management."

## 6. DRILLS & PRACTICE EXERCISES

### Drill 1: Rock Garden Flat
**Purpose:** Practise clutch pops and feathering on a flat rock garden before adding gradient — isolate the technique from the gradient challenge.
**Setup:** A flat rocky section.
**Execution:**
1. Ride through at slow pace using clutch pops on individual rocks and feathering between.
2. Focus entirely on timing — pop on rock contact, feather on loose.
**Success Criteria:** Consistent forward drive with minimal wheelspin. Rider can feel the difference between pop and feather.
**Duration/Reps:** 15 passes.

### Drill 2: Gentle Rocky Climb
**Purpose:** Apply the technique on a slight gradient — add the climb element to the flat technique.
**Setup:** A 5–10% gradient rocky section.
**Execution:**
1. Full technique application — gear, throttle, clutch pops on rocks, feather on loose.
2. Focus on maintaining momentum throughout the section.
**Success Criteria:** Section climbed without stall on 8 out of 10 attempts.
**Duration/Reps:** 10 climbs.

### Drill 3: Steep Rocky Climb
**Purpose:** Apply the full technique on a challenging gradient.
**Setup:** A 20%+ gradient rocky section.
**Execution:**
1. Full pre-read, gear selection, attack position, clutch management throughout.
2. Walk first. Identify the critical rock decisions before riding.
**Success Criteria:** Section climbed on 5 out of 10 attempts. Failure is acceptable at this level — technique quality is the assessment, not success rate.
**Duration/Reps:** 10 attempts.

### Drill 4: Recovery from Near-Stall
**Purpose:** Learn to recover momentum when nearly stalled mid-section.
**Setup:** A moderate rocky climb.
**Execution:**
1. Deliberately allow speed to drop to near-zero mid-climb.
2. Attempt to recover using a decisive throttle and clutch pop combination.
3. If recovery fails, controlled stop and restart.
**Success Criteria:** Recovery from near-zero without stall on 5 out of 10 times.
**Duration/Reps:** 10 recovery attempts.

## 8. TERRAIN & CONTEXT VARIATIONS

### Dry Rocky Climb
Standard. Maximum traction on rock surfaces. Main challenge is line and momentum management.

### Wet Rocky Climb
Traction dramatically reduced on rock surfaces. Clutch feathering even more critical — any wheelspin on wet rock is unrecoverable. Some sections may not be safely rideable wet — assess and choose alternative lines.

### Loose Dirt Between Rocks
Common combination. Transition from rock to loose dirt requires immediate clutch feathering; transition back to rock allows more engagement. Rhythm of transitions is the key skill.

### Extreme Gradient Rocky Climb
Hard enduro territory. Body position must be very forward to keep front down — knees near the tank. Every input is amplified by the gradient. Walk first on any unknown section.

### Shale and Layered Rock
Shale creates an unpredictable surface — flat plates of rock that can shift or break under the tyre load. Traction varies across individual shale pieces. Clutch feathering must be very gentle — aggressive wheelspin shatters shale and creates a moving surface. Choose lines that cross shale at a perpendicular angle where possible and maintain smooth, progressive drive throughout.

### Mixed Rock and Root Rocky Climb
Some technical climbs combine rocky terrain with exposed roots — each root crossing is a traction interruption. Brake application must stop entirely for each root. The clutch management alternates between rock-drive mode and root-crossing mode in rapid sequence. This combined terrain type is among the most technically demanding uphill scenarios and requires both root traction management (TECH-35) and rocky climb technique to be well established before attempting.

## 9. EXPERT INSIGHTS & SOURCES

### Key Insights
- **Graham Jarvis on rocky climbs:** Jarvis is known for making extreme rocky climbs look almost effortless — described by coaches as reading 5–6 metres ahead and using his clutch continuously as a fine instrument. The bike always appears to be finding traction even when there seems to be none. This is trained clutch sensitivity combined with excellent visual look-ahead.
- **Momentum as the primary tool:** Coaches universally note that riders who try to be too precise (too slow) on rocky climbs stall more often than riders who maintain momentum. Rocks are easier to manage at appropriate pace — the deflections are shorter and the forward drive overcomes them more easily.
- **The clutch as a throttle:** Advanced riders describe the clutch as a second throttle on rocky climbs — they regulate drive delivery through clutch position rather than throttle position alone. This gives much finer control of how much drive reaches the rear wheel at any moment.
- **Section walking protocol:** For any unknown technical rocky climb, the coaching protocol is: walk the full section from bottom to top, then look back down to see the line from the riding perspective (the view from the saddle is different from a walking perspective), then ride. This two-step walk provides a more accurate mental map than walking only.
- **The stall fallback:** Even expert riders stall on extreme rocky climbs. The correct fallback — not a failure mode — is a controlled dismount when the stall becomes inevitable. Riders who have practised the controlled dismount (step off the back of the bike before it falls sideways) treat stalls as restart situations rather than crashes. This is as much a skill to train as the climbing itself.
- **Rider condition matters more on rocky climbs:** Coaches note that rocky climb technique degrades significantly when riders are tired, cold, or not warmed up — the clutch sensitivity and throttle precision that the technique requires both suffer under fatigue. Rocky climbs should be attempted early in a training session when precision is highest, not at the end when fatigue has reduced fine motor control.
- **The tyre pressure variable:** Coaches who work with riders on rocky climbs consistently raise tyre pressure as a factor that dramatically affects technique. Too-high rear tyre pressure causes the tyre to bounce off rocks rather than deforming around them — grip is reduced. Slightly lower rear pressure allows the tyre to conform to rock surfaces. Most enduro coaches recommend riders experiment with tyre pressure specifically on rocky terrain and not assume road or trail pressures are correct for rock climbing.
- **Looking past the first obstacle:** Beginners on rocky climbs fix their gaze on the first large rock in front of the wheel — and are then surprised by the second rock, the third, and the subsequent sequence. Advanced coaches train riders to look through the first obstacle to the pattern beyond it: where will the front wheel be after the first rock? Where is the next opportunity to place the wheel well? The gaze must be ahead of the immediate challenge, not on it.
