# Stoppie (Front Brake)

## 1. OVERVIEW

The stoppie is a braking technique where progressive front brake application transfers weight so forcefully to the front wheel that the rear wheel lifts off the ground — the bike pivots on the front wheel contact point. In enduro riding, the stoppie is not primarily a trick — it is a functional tool for making very sharp direction changes in tight terrain, scrubbing speed dramatically in a short distance, and pivoting the bike around the front wheel when rear wheel steer or pivot turns are not applicable. The technique requires significant approach speed, confident and progressive front brake application, and the ability to release the brake and drive forward precisely at the point where the rear settles back down. Done wrong, a stoppie results in a front wheel wash (front tucks and rider goes over the front), an over-committed flip, or an abrupt rear slam that upsets the chassis. Done right, it is one of the most precise direction-change tools in the technical enduro toolkit.

## 2. CORE PRINCIPLES

### Principle 1: Weight Transfer Under Braking Is the Mechanism
Hard front brake application decelerates the bike. As the bike decelerates, inertia shifts the rider's mass (and the bike's mass) forward. This transfers load to the front tyre and simultaneously lifts load off the rear tyre. At the threshold of rear lift-off, the rear wheel becomes light and eventually lifts. Continuing to brake after rear lift-off pivots the entire bike around the front contact point.

### Principle 2: Progressive Application Controls Lift Rate
A sudden front brake grab causes the front tyre to lock or wash — there is no rear lift because the front immediately loses traction. Progressive braking — starting light and building pressure rapidly — maximises tyre grip while building weight transfer. The rear lifts smoothly because the front tyre maintains grip throughout the load transfer.

### Principle 3: Body Weight Forward Amplifies the Transfer
Moving the rider's body weight forward during the braking action amplifies the weight transfer to the front wheel. This is why the stoppie requires a forward body position — the rider's mass moving toward the front axle reduces the counterbalancing effect of the rider's weight on the rear. This does not mean throwing the body forward violently — it means allowing weight to shift forward with the bike's deceleration rather than resisting it.

### Principle 4: The Pivot Moment Must Be Read and Released
As the rear lifts and the bike pivots, there is a moment where the direction change is complete. At this point, the front brake must be released smoothly — not abruptly — to allow the rear to settle back down and forward drive to resume. Releasing too early ends the pivot prematurely. Releasing too late causes the front to over-rotate and the rider to go over the bars. Reading this moment is the core skill.

### Principle 5: Speed and Grip Are Prerequisites
A stoppie requires enough approach speed that the deceleration force can transfer sufficient weight. Too slow and the rear never lifts. The front tyre must have sufficient grip to support the full weight of the bike plus rider under hard braking — on loose or wet surfaces, the front is more likely to wash. Stoppies belong on surfaces with predictable traction, not loose gravel or mud.

## 3. TECHNIQUE BREAKDOWN BY LEVEL

### 3a. Beginner Level
**Goal:** Understand front brake weight transfer and develop progressive braking that creates significant front loading — but not necessarily rear lift.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Progressive front brake application from light to firm
- Feeling the rear wheel becoming light under hard braking
- Body weight allowing forward movement without resistance
- Understanding why abrupt braking is dangerous

**What Good Looks Like:**
Under hard progressive braking, the rider can feel the rear wheel get noticeably light — possibly even see daylight under the rear tyre for a moment. Front tyre holds. Body moves forward with the deceleration naturally. No front wash.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Abrupt front brake grab:** Front locks, tyre washes. → Progressive build always. Start light, increase over 0.5 seconds.
- **Resisting the forward weight shift:** Rider locks elbows and fights the deceleration. Rear never lifts because rider's counterbalancing mass is too far back. → Allow body to shift forward — controlled but not forced.
- **Attempting full stoppie before feeling the light rear:** Skipping steps. → Feel the rear lightening first across multiple braking efforts before seeking rear lift.

### 3b. Intermediate Level
**Goal:** Achieve controlled rear lift under progressive front braking — managing the pivot moment and settling the rear back down cleanly.

**Progression Markers from Beginner:** Rider regularly feels the rear get very light under braking. Progressive braking is consistent. No front washes in normal braking.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Building braking pressure to the point of rear lift — without crossing into front wash
- Holding the rear lift for a controlled pivot distance
- Reading the moment to release the brake and settle the rear
- Maintaining steering authority during the pivot

**What Good Looks Like:**
Approaching a tight section at 35–40 km/h, rider applies front brake progressively. Rear lifts 10–15 cm off the ground. Bike pivots slightly — perhaps 10 degrees of direction change. Rider releases front brake smoothly. Rear settles. Rider drives forward in the new direction.

**What the Rider Should Feel at Each Stage:**
- Initial brake application: Forks compress — felt as nose diving and weight flowing into the hands. Hands get heavier as front tyre loads.
- Rear wheel lightening: The rear pegs become noticeably lighter — felt as a rising sensation at the back of the bike. Weight shifting from rear pegs to front wheel.
- Rear lift-off: The rear pegs are weightless. The bike is pivoting — felt as a rotational movement around the front axle. The rear moves upward and the bike's tail swings.
- Pivot arc: The direction of the front wheel determines the pivot direction. Minor steering input at this moment changes the direction of travel significantly.
- Release and settle: Brake released progressively — rear descends. Felt as rear pegs regaining weight. The descent should be smooth, not a crash.
- Drive phase: Throttle applied as rear makes contact — felt as the familiar acceleration push through the seat.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Front wash (front tucks):** Too much brake pressure too fast, or gravel/wet surface. Rider goes over the front. Fix: reduce brake application rate. Never attempt on loose or wet surfaces.
- **Rear crashes down hard:** Brake released too suddenly — rear drops rather than settling. Fix: release brake progressively, not abruptly.
- **Over-commitment leading to flip:** Rear lifts dramatically, bike rotating beyond control. Fix: release brake immediately when rear is at desired height. Maximum rear lift for enduro use is 20–30 cm — not a full vertical stoppie.

### 3c. Advanced Level
**Goal:** Use the stoppie as a deliberate direction-change tool — pivoting the bike through 20–30 degrees in a single braking action, mid-trail.

**Progression Markers from Intermediate:** Rider can achieve consistent controlled rear lift and settle. Pivot direction is intentional. Rear settles cleanly after release.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Using stoppie for sharp hairpin entries — pivoting the bike before the turn rather than during it
- Combining with throttle for immediate drive after the pivot
- Executing on varied terrain — adapting brake pressure for different traction levels
- Chaining stoppie with rear brake slide for complex direction changes

**What Good Looks Like:**
Entering a tight switchback at moderate speed, rider uses a stoppie to rotate the bike 25 degrees before the apex — arriving at the apex already aimed at the exit. The brake is released, rear settles, throttle is applied, and the exit is clean. The overall corner entry-to-exit time is significantly faster than a conventional braking-cornering approach.

**Failure Modes and Causes:**
- **Front wash:** Most common catastrophic failure — front tyre loses grip under the full weight. Cause: too fast brake application, wet or loose surface, or over-worn tyre. Fix: terrain assessment before attempting, progressive application, check tyre condition.
- **Over-commitment with flip:** Rear lifts past vertical — bike flips. Cause: too much brake for too long. Fix: brief pivot only — release brake well before the rear reaches 45 degrees.
- **Premature release:** Brake released too early, pivot incomplete. Fix: hold brake a half-beat longer — monitor rear height before releasing.

## 4. DIAGNOSTIC CUES (AI COACHING ENGINE REFERENCE)

### Visual Cues
- Correct: Rear wheel visibly lifts — gap between tyre and ground observable
- Correct: Fork compression visible — forks clearly loaded under braking
- Correct: Rear settles smoothly after brake release — no crash-down visible
- Error: Front tyre skidding or fishtailing — front brake grabbed too hard, front wash beginning
- Error: Rear lifts and continues rising past 30 cm — brake held too long, over-commitment
- Error: Rear crashes down abruptly — brake released suddenly rather than progressively

### Audio Cues
- Correct: No tyre screech — front maintains rolling grip under progressive braking
- Error: Front tyre squeal or scrubbing sound — front tyre at or beyond grip limit
- Error: Loud rear crash-down — brake released abruptly, rear dropped rather than settled

### Sensor Cues
- Correct: Front wheel deceleration spike with rear wheel speed dropping to zero (rear in air) — confirmed rear lift
- Correct: Smooth front wheel deceleration curve — progressive braking confirmed
- Error: Front wheel deceleration interrupted by brief speed increase — front tyre skipped (partial lock)
- Error: Rear wheel speed jump after settle — rear bounced on landing, brake released too quickly

## 5. COACHING CUES & LANGUAGE

### Positive Reinforcement
- "I could see the rear lift — clean weight transfer. And the settle was smooth. That's exactly how it should look."
- "Your front brake build was progressive — no grab. The front tyre stayed loaded the whole time. That's correct."
- "You held the pivot for exactly the right duration and released cleanly. That direction change was intentional. That's the technique working."

### Corrective Feedback
- "You grabbed the front brake — that's why the front washed. Build the pressure over half a second. Start light, build firm. Never grab."
- "The rear crashed back down — you released the brake too suddenly. Release it the same way you applied it. Progressively."
- "You stopped as soon as you felt the rear lift. Hold it a half-beat longer — the pivot needs that extra moment to complete the direction change."
- "Don't attempt this on that loose section. The front tyre doesn't have the grip. Wait for a firmer surface."

### Progression Prompts
- "You can lift the rear — now try to control which direction the bike pivots. Put slight steering input on the front wheel while the rear is in the air."
- "Next step is linking the stoppie directly into a throttle-on exit. Release brake, rear settles, throttle open — all in one fluid movement."
- "Try using the stoppie as a deliberate hairpin entry tool. Approach the hairpin with more speed than usual, stoppie to rotate, and exit with the saved momentum."

## 6. DRILLS & PRACTICE EXERCISES

### Drill 1: Progressive Braking Threshold
**Purpose:** Build progressive front brake technique and feel for rear wheel lightening.
**Setup:** Flat tarmac or hard-pack, 30–40 km/h approach.
**Execution:**
1. Approach a marked stop point at speed.
2. Apply front brake starting light, building to firm over 0.5 seconds.
3. Stop before the marked point.
4. On each run, try to feel when the rear wheel gets light.
5. Note the brake pressure level at rear lightening.
**Success Criteria:** Rear wheel becomes visibly light on 5 out of 10 braking efforts. Front never washes.
**Duration/Reps:** 20 braking efforts per session.
**Progression:** Increase speed. Focus on shortening the distance to achieve the same rear lightening.

### Drill 2: Rear Lift Control
**Purpose:** Achieve controlled rear lift and hold it for 1 second before releasing.
**Setup:** Flat hard-pack, 40–50 km/h approach.
**Execution:**
1. Approach at speed.
2. Apply front brake progressively until rear lifts.
3. When rear lifts 10 cm, hold brake pressure steady — maintain the lift.
4. Hold for 1 full second.
5. Release brake progressively — rear settles.
**Success Criteria:** Rear held in the air for 1 second on 4 out of 10 attempts without front wash or hard settle.
**Duration/Reps:** 15 attempts per session.
**Progression:** Extend hold time to 2 seconds. Attempt a slight pivot direction change while rear is in air.

### Drill 3: Stoppie Direction Change
**Purpose:** Use the stoppie to make a deliberate direction change of 15–20 degrees.
**Setup:** Flat area with two marked lines — approach direction and exit direction, 20 degrees apart.
**Execution:**
1. Approach along the first line.
2. Execute stoppie at the intersection point.
3. Use front wheel steering and pivot to align with the second line.
4. Release brake, rear settles, drive along second line.
**Success Criteria:** Exit within 5 degrees of the second line on 5 out of 10 attempts.
**Duration/Reps:** 20 attempts per session.
**Progression:** Increase the direction change angle to 30 degrees. Then attempt on a trail feature.

### Drill 4: Stoppie to Drive
**Purpose:** Chain the stoppie directly into forward drive — no pause between brake release and throttle.
**Setup:** Flat area with a clear exit direction.
**Execution:**
1. Approach and execute stoppie.
2. As rear settles, simultaneously release brake and open throttle.
3. Drive forward in the new direction immediately.
4. Assess: was there a dead moment between the brake and throttle? Eliminate it.
**Success Criteria:** Seamless transition from brake to throttle — no pause, no coasting. Exit speed is meaningful (not rolling slowly away).
**Duration/Reps:** 15 attempts.
**Progression:** Increase the pivot angle. Then move to a real trail feature.

## 8. TERRAIN & CONTEXT VARIATIONS

### Hard-Pack and Tarmac
Ideal surfaces for stoppie — front tyre grip is predictable and sufficient. Most stoppie practice should begin here.

### Firm Dirt Trail
Applicable with caution — assess surface grip before attempting. Dry firm dirt usually has sufficient grip. Avoid at loose or dusty sections.

### Wet Conditions
Front tyre grip drops significantly on wet terrain. Stoppies on wet surfaces risk immediate front wash — approach with much lower speed and reduced brake pressure. Advanced riders only in wet conditions.

### Loose Gravel
Front wash is highly likely on loose gravel — the tyre rides on loose aggregate rather than gripping the surface. Stoppie is not recommended on loose gravel.

### Uphill
Stoppie is easier on a slight uphill — gravity helps pitch weight forward. However, rear lift is also more aggressive and the over-commitment risk increases.

## 9. EXPERT INSIGHTS & SOURCES

### Key Insights
- **Trials technique overlap:** In trials riding, the stoppie (called "front brake dab" at slow speeds) is used to pivot the bike for tight sections. The enduro stoppie is a higher-speed application of the same mechanic.
- **The "progressive or nothing" rule:** Every experienced coach teaching stoppie technique emphasises that grabbing the front brake is the worst thing a rider can do. The entire technique depends on progressive application — there is no compromise.
- **Direction change efficiency:** In tight enduro terrain, a 20-degree direction change from a stoppie saves significant time compared to braking, stopping, and turning. The technique is efficient, not just impressive.
- **Tyre condition matters:** A stoppie performed on a worn front tyre is significantly more dangerous than on fresh rubber. Front tyre grip is the entire foundation of the technique.
