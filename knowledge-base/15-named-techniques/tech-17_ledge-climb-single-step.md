# Ledge Climb (Single Step)

## 1. OVERVIEW

A single ledge climb is the technique of ascending a sharp, abrupt single-step obstacle — where the ledge face is vertical or near-vertical and the ledge height requires lifting the front wheel to the ledge surface before the rear wheel can drive up. Unlike a rock step that the bike rolls over at speed, the ledge climb is a deliberate, slower-speed action where the front wheel must be lifted and placed accurately on top of the ledge, then the rear must be driven up the vertical face behind it. The critical difference from a log crossing is the ledge face — a log is round and the rear wheel can roll over it; a ledge face is vertical and the rear wheel must be driven against it and up. This requires a specific combination of clutch-up timing, front wheel placement precision, and committed throttle for the rear drive phase.

## 2. CORE PRINCIPLES

### Principle 1: Front Wheel Placement on the Ledge Surface Is the First Priority
The front wheel must be placed on the flat top of the ledge — not on the ledge face (vertical surface). If the front wheel contacts the ledge face too low, the wheel cannot climb it — the bike stops. If the clutch-up lifts the front wheel too high, it overshoots the ledge and lands in empty space (the ledge may be narrow). The lift must place the front wheel on the ledge surface accurately — a placement problem, not just a height problem.

### Principle 2: The Rear Wheel Drives Against the Ledge Face and Up
Once the front wheel is on the ledge, the rear wheel must be driven into the ledge face — the contact point — and up the face using throttle. The rear tyre does not roll over the ledge like a log; it climbs the vertical face under power. This requires sufficient throttle to overcome the resistance of the ledge face, and a body position that prevents the front wheel from lifting off the ledge surface during the rear drive phase.

### Principle 3: Body Forward During Rear Drive Keeps Front Down
When the rear is being driven up the ledge face, the torque reaction tends to lift the front wheel off the ledge surface — potentially causing a wheelie and loop-out at the worst time (partially up a ledge). The rider must shift weight forward as the rear begins climbing — using body position to counteract the torque reaction and keep the front wheel pressed onto the ledge surface.

### Principle 4: Approach Speed Must Be Low but Non-Zero
Too much speed results in the clutch-up being too aggressive — front flies past the ledge and the rear hits the face too hard. Too little speed removes the momentum needed to carry the rear up after the front is placed. The approach should be at 10–20 km/h — enough for a controlled clutch-up and rear drive, not so much that placement precision is lost.

### Principle 5: The Ledge Height Determines the Clutch Pop Depth
For ledges of 25–35 cm, a moderate clutch-up is sufficient. For ledges of 40–60 cm, a more aggressive clutch-up is needed. For ledges above 60 cm, this technique reaches its limits — either additional speed or a different approach (TECH-06 full wheelie) is needed. Matching the clutch-up intensity to the specific ledge height is the calibration skill.

## 3. TECHNIQUE BREAKDOWN BY LEVEL

### 3a. Beginner Level
**Goal:** Climb ledges of 25–30 cm using a controlled clutch-up front placement and committed rear drive.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Clutch-up timing: front lift just before the ledge (not at it, not after it)
- Front wheel placement on the ledge top surface — not the face
- Maintained throttle during rear drive phase
- Body weight forward transition between front and rear phases

**What Good Looks Like:**
Approaching a 25 cm ledge at 15 km/h, the rider executes a clutch pop that places the front wheel on the ledge top. Weight shifts forward. Throttle drives the rear wheel into the ledge face and up. The rear rolls over the ledge top. The bike continues forward.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Front undershoots (wheel hits ledge face):** Clutch pop too early or too gentle — front wheel never reaches ledge height. Fix: pop timing later, or increase pre-load.
- **Rear wheels lose traction (wheel spins on ledge face):** Throttle too much — rear wheel spins rather than gripping and climbing. Fix: steady throttle, not aggressive. The rear needs grip, not spin.
- **Stall mid-ledge:** Throttle cuts as rear contacts ledge — engine dies with rear half-up. Fix: commit to maintained throttle through the entire rear drive phase.

### 3b. Intermediate Level
**Goal:** Climb ledges of 35–50 cm consistently — with precise front placement and committed rear drive.

**Progression Markers from Beginner:** Rider can climb 25–30 cm ledges consistently. Front placement is accurate. Rear drive is maintained.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Precise front wheel landing zone: targeting the inner edge of the ledge top (not too close to the edge, not too far back)
- Body weight transition: actively shifting forward as the rear approaches the ledge face
- Clutch feathering during rear drive to modulate power and prevent rear spin
- Reading ledge surface: is the ledge top wet? Rocky? Sufficient to hold the front?

**What Good Looks Like:**
Approaching a 45 cm ledge at 15 km/h, the clutch-up lifts the front wheel to approximately 50 cm — clearing the ledge by 5 cm. The front lands on the ledge, approximately 10 cm from the front edge. The rider's weight immediately shifts forward. Throttle drives the rear wheel up the ledge face — clutch feathering manages the drive to prevent spin. The rear crests the ledge top. The bike drives forward on the flat ledge surface.

**What the Rider Should Feel at Each Stage:**
- Pre-lift (1 metre from ledge): Clutch slipping, throttle pre-loading. Engine building. Felt as slight forward push from partial clutch engagement.
- Clutch pop: Sharp lift — bars become weightless. Weight on rear pegs. The ledge face is visible in the peripheral vision below the rising front wheel.
- Front placement: Front wheel descends onto the ledge top. A slight compression felt as the forks take the landing. The ledge is solid below the wheel.
- Weight forward shift: Body moves toward the bars — felt as the chest approaching the handlebars, weight distributing from rear pegs to front tyre through the ledge surface.
- Rear drive: Throttle open. The rear wheel makes contact with the ledge face — felt as a sudden resistance and the bike decelerating slightly. Then, if throttle is sufficient, the rear begins to climb. Felt as the rear pegs rising.
- Rear cresting: The rear tyre rolls over the ledge top — a slight rearward pitch as the rear descends onto the flat ledge surface. Felt as the rear pegs dropping.
- Exit: Both wheels on the ledge top. Forward drive continues.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Front wheel too far back on ledge:** Clutch pop placed the front wheel near the back of a narrow ledge — rear drive can push the front forward off the far edge. Fix: target the inner (back) edge of the ledge, not the front edge.
- **Front wheel lifts off ledge during rear drive:** Too much throttle is causing a wheelie with the front on the ledge. Extremely dangerous — front can loop off the ledge. Fix: weight forward aggressively. Reduce throttle. Use clutch feather to modulate.
- **Rear wheel spins on ledge face without climbing:** Face is too steep or smooth, or throttle is causing spin rather than grip. Fix: clutch feather to reduce wheel speed — grip rather than spin is the goal.

### 3c. Advanced Level
**Goal:** Climb ledges of 50–70+ cm at pace — as part of a hard enduro technical section.

**Progression Markers from Intermediate:** Rider can climb 45 cm ledges consistently. Front placement is precise. Body transition is automatic.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Large ledge calibration: adjusting all variables for taller, more challenging ledges
- Ledge climbing on loose terrain: front wheel placement on a ledge where the ledge top is loose
- Sequential ledge climbing: multiple ledge steps in succession
- Ledge climbs combined with corner entries or off-camber terrain

**What Good Looks Like:**
On a hard enduro course rocky climb with a series of rock ledge steps at 40, 55, and 60 cm, the rider ascends each with a precisely timed clutch-up, accurate front placement, committed rear drive, and immediate setup for the next step. The section is completed without stopping.

**Failure Modes and Causes:**
- **Front overshoots narrow ledge:** Clutch pop too aggressive — front flies past the ledge landing zone. Fix: reduce pre-load. Precision over power for placement.
- **Rear can't climb ledge face (face too steep):** The ledge is essentially vertical and smooth — rear tyre cannot grip. Fix: different approach required. A higher speed or different entry angle. Some ledges require a clutch pop onto the face and then a second push over the top.
- **Loop-out on ledge:** Front wheel is on the ledge and the rear drive causes the bike to continue rotating — front lifts off the ledge, bike loops. Fix: weight aggressively forward during the rear drive phase. This is the most dangerous failure of this technique.

## 4. DIAGNOSTIC CUES (AI COACHING ENGINE REFERENCE)

### Visual Cues
- Correct: Front wheel landing on the top surface of the ledge — not on the ledge face
- Correct: Rider weight shifting forward as rear approaches the ledge
- Correct: Rear wheel climbing the ledge face under power — not spinning on it
- Error: Front wheel hitting the ledge face and stopping — undershooting the landing zone
- Error: Front wheel lifting off the ledge during rear drive — wheelie with front on ledge, loop risk
- Error: Rear wheel spinning on ledge face with no forward progress — traction lost

### Audio Cues
- Correct: Engine note maintained through rear drive phase — continuous throttle
- Correct: Rear wheel contact sound with ledge face — brief firm contact then climbing
- Error: Engine stall mid-ledge — throttle cut during rear drive phase
- Error: Rear wheel spinning sound prolonged — traction lost on ledge face

### Sensor Cues
- Correct: Forward speed maintained through both phases — brief deceleration on ledge contact then recovery
- Correct: Pitch angle briefly elevated (nose up during front lift), then stabilised on placement
- Error: Speed drops to zero mid-ledge — stall or rear wheel spin with no climb
- Error: Pitch angle continues rising after front placement — loop-out in progress

## 5. COACHING CUES & LANGUAGE

### Positive Reinforcement
- "Front landed on the ledge top — perfect placement. Not the face, not too far back. Exactly where it needed to be."
- "Weight moved forward the moment the front landed. That's what kept the front down while the rear drove up. Good transition."
- "Committed throttle through the rear phase. No hesitation. The rear climbed clean."

### Corrective Feedback
- "Your front is hitting the face — the pop is too late. Pop the clutch when you're one metre away from the ledge face, not at it. Give the front time to reach the right height."
- "The front lifted off the ledge during the rear drive — that is a loop risk. Get your weight forward the moment the front lands. Chest toward the bars."
- "Rear is spinning on the face — all noise, no progress. Feather the clutch to reduce wheel speed. Grip climbs, spin doesn't."
- "You stalled mid-ledge. You need to commit to maintained throttle through the entire rear drive. Don't cut it when you feel resistance — that resistance is normal."

### Progression Prompts
- "You've got the 30 cm ledge consistently. Try the 40 cm one. The only difference is a slightly more aggressive clutch pop — everything else is the same."
- "Next, practise on a ledge with a loose surface on top. The front placement is the same but the front can slip if you load it awkwardly."
- "Try a series of two ledges in quick succession — as soon as you clear the first, set up for the second. The exit of one is the approach of the next."

## 6. DRILLS & PRACTICE EXERCISES

### Drill 1: Front Placement Accuracy
**Purpose:** Develop precise front wheel landing on the ledge top — not the face.
**Setup:** A 25 cm wooden platform with a marked 20 cm landing zone on top (20 cm from the front edge).
**Execution:**
1. Approach at 15 km/h.
2. Execute clutch-up.
3. Aim front wheel at the marked zone.
4. Assess landing position — was it within the zone?
**Success Criteria:** Front wheel lands within the marked zone on 7 out of 10 attempts.
**Duration/Reps:** 20 attempts.
**Progression:** Narrow the landing zone to 10 cm.

### Drill 2: Rear Drive Commitment
**Purpose:** Build the habit of maintained throttle through the rear drive phase.
**Setup:** A 25 cm ledge.
**Execution:**
1. Front wheel placed on ledge manually (assisted — no clutch pop).
2. Focus only on the rear drive phase: throttle committed until rear is on the ledge.
3. Practice this phase independently.
**Success Criteria:** Rear successfully climbs the ledge on 8 out of 10 attempts with no stall.
**Duration/Reps:** 15 attempts.
**Progression:** Combine with the front clutch-up.

### Drill 3: Full Ledge Climb — Graduated Height
**Purpose:** Build confidence across a range of ledge heights.
**Setup:** Ledges of 25, 35, and 45 cm.
**Execution:**
1. 25 cm ledge — 5 successful climbs.
2. 35 cm ledge — 5 successful climbs.
3. 45 cm ledge — 5 successful climbs.
**Success Criteria:** All climbs completed cleanly at each height.
**Duration/Reps:** 15 total climbs across all heights.
**Progression:** Add a 55 cm ledge.

### Drill 4: Sequential Ledge Climb
**Purpose:** Chain multiple ledge climbs without stopping.
**Setup:** Two ledges of 30 cm and 40 cm, spaced 3 metres apart.
**Execution:**
1. Climb the first ledge.
2. Immediately set up for the second.
3. Climb the second without pausing.
**Success Criteria:** Both ledges climbed in one continuous motion without stopping between.
**Duration/Reps:** 10 runs.
**Progression:** Add a third ledge. Reduce spacing to 2 metres.

## 8. TERRAIN & CONTEXT VARIATIONS

### Clean Rock Ledge
Standard application — predictable ledge face and top. Most practice should occur on clean ledges.

### Loose Top Surface
Front wheel can slide forward on placement if the ledge top is loose or sandy. Place front wheel gently, not aggressively — allowing it to settle before rear drive begins.

### Wet Rock Ledge Face
Rear wheel loses traction on wet vertical rock face more easily. Lower throttle, more clutch feather. Very wet rock may make some ledge heights unclimbable — be willing to walk them.

### Narrow Ledge Top
A ledge narrower than 30 cm provides very little front wheel landing space. Precision is extreme. Overshooting is dangerous — front wheel can go off the far side. Approach at lower speed.

### Ledge at Angle to Trail
Approaching a ledge at a slight angle (not perpendicular) requires the front wheel to be aimed at the angle — the clutch-up lifts on the approach angle, not perpendicular to the ledge. The technique adapts the aim, not the execution.

## 9. EXPERT INSIGHTS & SOURCES

### Key Insights
- **Cody Webb ledge technique:** Webb is noted for consistently clean ledge climbs on extreme terrain — his front wheel placement accuracy is particularly studied. His approach combines moderate speed with a very calibrated clutch-up rather than high-speed brute force.
- **The "placement over power" principle:** Most coaches teaching ledge climbing emphasise that precision of front wheel placement is more important than clutch-up power. An accurate placement at moderate power is more reliable than an aggressive pop that may overshoot.
- **The rear drive commitment:** Coaches consistently flag that the most common ledge failure is throttle hesitation during the rear drive phase. Teaching riders to commit to the throttle — even when they feel resistance — is the primary correction for this technique.
- **Loop-out risk is highest here:** Ledge climbs with the front wheel on the ledge represent one of the highest loop-out risk scenarios in enduro — the bike is already partially up, the torque from the rear drive is rotating the bike forward, and the rider may be in a position from which they cannot escape the loop safely. Forward weight transition is the critical safety mechanism.
