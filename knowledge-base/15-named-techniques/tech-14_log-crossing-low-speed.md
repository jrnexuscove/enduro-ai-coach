# Log Crossing (Low Speed)

## 1. OVERVIEW

Log crossing at low speed is the technique of riding over a static log obstacle — typically 15–40 cm in diameter — at speeds below 25 km/h. It is one of the most fundamental obstacle techniques in off-road riding and appears constantly on enduro trails. The technique has two distinct phases: lifting the front wheel over the log, and driving the rear wheel over the log. Each requires a specific rider input. At low speed, the front wheel cannot rely on momentum to carry it over the log — it must be actively lifted using either a manual front wheel lift (TECH-05) or a clutch-up (TECH-06). The rear wheel must be driven over with maintained or increased throttle. Understanding both phases and the transition between them is the core of low-speed log crossing.

## 2. CORE PRINCIPLES

### Principle 1: Two-Phase Obstacle Crossing — Front Then Rear
Every log crossing has two distinct phases: the front wheel crossing phase and the rear wheel crossing phase. Each phase has different requirements. The front phase is about height and placement. The rear phase is about drive and momentum. Conflating the two phases — trying to do everything at once — leads to poor technique for both. Thinking of the obstacle as two separate events executed sequentially produces better results.

### Principle 2: Front Wheel Height Must Clear the Log Fully
If the front wheel contacts the top of the log rather than clearing it, the wheel either bounces back (front deflects off) or climbs up the log at an uncontrolled angle (bike pitches forward). The front wheel must clear the top of the log by at least a few centimetres — not scraping over. This means the lift must be calibrated to the log's height, not just "up."

### Principle 3: Throttle Must Be Maintained During the Rear Drive Phase
The most common error in rear wheel crossing is reducing throttle as the rear wheel approaches the log. This causes the rear wheel to decelerate and hit the log with reduced drive momentum — resulting in the rear either bouncing off or getting stuck. The rear wheel must be driven over with maintained or slightly increased throttle, allowing it to climb the log under power.

### Principle 4: Landing the Front Wheel on the Far Side Controls Pitch
When the front wheel clears the log and lands on the far side, the bike pitches slightly forward. If the front landing is too hard (front drops sharply), the bike pitches aggressively and the rider is thrown forward. The front landing should be controlled — the front wheel height at the moment of crossing the log should allow it to settle naturally rather than dropping sharply.

### Principle 5: Approach Speed Determines Technique Choice
At very slow speeds (below 10 km/h), a clutch feather and front wheel placement (walking the bike over) works. At moderate low speeds (10–20 km/h), a manual lift or light clutch pop is appropriate. The technique adapts to the speed — there is no single universal approach for all low-speed log crossings.

## 3. TECHNIQUE BREAKDOWN BY LEVEL

### 3a. Beginner Level
**Goal:** Cross a small log (15–20 cm) at very low speed using a clutch pop for the front wheel, and maintained throttle for the rear.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Approaching the log in 1st or 2nd gear at walking pace
- Using a clutch pop to lift the front wheel over the log
- Maintaining throttle as the rear wheel approaches
- Keeping body weight centred — not leaning forward as the front crosses

**What Good Looks Like:**
The rider approaches at 10 km/h, executes a small clutch pop that lifts the front wheel 5 cm above the log height. The front lands cleanly on the far side. The rider maintains throttle. The rear wheel contacts the log and drives over it. The bike continues forward without stopping.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Front bounces back off log:** The front wheel hit the log rather than clearing it — lift was insufficient or mistimed. → Ensure the lift occurs before the wheel reaches the log, not when it is already in contact.
- **Rear loops up over front:** Too much speed combined with rear wheel hitting the log — rear pitches the bike forward. → Reduce approach speed. The rear wheel should roll over the log steadily, not hit it.
- **Throttle cut when rear hits log:** Rider panics and reduces throttle as the rear contacts the log — rear gets stuck. → Hold throttle through the rear crossing. The bike needs drive to get the rear over.

### 3b. Intermediate Level
**Goal:** Cross logs of 25–35 cm diameter at various speeds within the low-speed range — adapting the front lift technique to the log height.

**Progression Markers from Beginner:** Rider can cross small logs consistently. Front lift is controlled. Throttle is maintained through both phases.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Calibrating clutch pop depth to log height — more height for taller logs
- Manual lift option for logs where speed makes a clutch pop too aggressive
- Body position: weight slightly rearward during front phase, shifting forward during rear phase
- Timing the front lift so the wheel clears the log by 3–5 cm — not more, not less

**What Good Looks Like:**
Approaching a 30 cm log at 15 km/h, the rider executes a clutch pop that lifts the front wheel 35–40 cm. The front clears the log and lands cleanly on the far side. The rider's weight was slightly rearward during the front phase. As the rear approaches the log, weight shifts forward slightly, and throttle is maintained — the rear rolls cleanly over the log. The crossing is smooth and forward speed is maintained.

**What the Rider Should Feel at Each Stage:**
- Pre-lift setup: Clutch begins to slip while throttle holds steady. Engine builds RPM. Felt as slight forward pressure from the rear wheel beginning to receive more drive.
- Front lift (clutch pop): Clutch snaps off. Front wheel lifts — felt as the weight leaving the front tyre contact patch, the bars becoming weightless. Body weight felt entirely through the rear pegs.
- Front descent: The front wheel is at peak height and begins to drop toward the far side. The bike tips slightly forward. Felt as weight slowly returning to the front.
- Front landing: Front wheel contacts the ground on the far side. A slight compression felt through the bars as the forks absorb the landing. If too hard, the rider is pitched forward noticeably.
- Rear approach: The rear wheel is now near the log. Throttle is maintained — engine driving the rear wheel forward and into the log contact point.
- Rear crossing: The rear wheel contacts the log and rolls over it. Felt as a brief rearward pitch — the back of the bike rises slightly as the rear tyre rolls over the log top. Then settles as the rear descends to the far side.
- Exit: Both wheels on the far side. Normal forward drive resumes.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Front too high — overshooting the log:** Clutch pop too aggressive — front clears the log by a large margin but drops quickly and hard on the far side. Fix: lighter pre-load. The front only needs to clear by 5 cm.
- **Rear loops up over front:** Too much speed or too much throttle as rear contacts log — rear climbs aggressively and pitches the bike nose-down. Fix: moderate throttle, appropriate approach speed.
- **Loss of forward drive:** Throttle reduced as rear contacts log — rear stalls on log and the bike stops. Fix: hold throttle through the rear crossing phase.

### 3c. Advanced Level
**Goal:** Cross logs of all sizes at the maximum appropriate speed — incorporating the crossing into a continuous technical rhythm.

**Progression Markers from Intermediate:** Rider can cross 30 cm logs consistently at 15 km/h. Both phases are distinct and smooth.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Integrating log crossings into technical trail sections without stopping
- Combining the crossing with the terrain immediately before and after
- Reading log crossings from further away and preparing early
- Multiple logs in quick succession — rhythmic crossing technique

**What Good Looks Like:**
On a trail with three logs within 20 metres, the rider navigates all three without pausing — each crossing is a brief front lift and rear drive, and the momentum carries through. The crossings are barely distinguishable from the rest of the trail riding from the outside.

**Failure Modes and Causes:**
- **Front bounces back (front wheel hits log face):** Lift timing was late — the wheel was already at the log when the clutch popped. Fix: pop 0.5–1 metre before the log. The lift needs time to happen before contact.
- **Rear jumps over log awkwardly:** Too much speed combined with steep log — rear hits log and the rear end of the bike launches. Fix: moderate approach speed for taller logs. The rear should roll, not launch.
- **Loss of traction on rear crossing:** Rear wheel spins on log surface (slippery bark, wet wood). Fix: lower tyre pressure for more contact patch. Lower approach speed. Maintain steady, not excessive, throttle.

## 4. DIAGNOSTIC CUES (AI COACHING ENGINE REFERENCE)

### Visual Cues
- Correct: Front wheel clearly clears the top of the log — daylight visible between wheel and log
- Correct: Rear wheel rolling over the log under throttle — no sudden rear pitch
- Correct: Rider body weight slightly rearward during front phase, returning to centre during rear phase
- Error: Front wheel contacts the log face and stops or deflects — insufficient lift or wrong timing
- Error: Rear end launches — too much speed or throttle for the log height
- Error: Bike stops on the log — throttle cut during rear phase

### Audio Cues
- Correct: Engine note maintained through both phases — no throttle reduction audible
- Correct: Brief front suspension compression sound on landing (front cleared cleanly)
- Error: Sudden engine note drop as rear hits log — throttle cut
- Error: Front wheel impact sound — wheel hit the log face rather than clearing it

### Sensor Cues
- Correct: Front wheel speed briefly drops to zero (front in air) then resumes — clean front lift
- Correct: Rear wheel speed data shows brief acceleration event at log contact — throttle maintained
- Error: Speed data drops to zero and does not resume — bike stopped on log
- Error: Rear wheel speed spike after log contact — rear launched, not rolled

## 5. COACHING CUES & LANGUAGE

### Positive Reinforcement
- "Front cleared cleanly, rear rolled over smoothly — two phases, both done right. That's the technique."
- "I can hear the engine holding through the rear crossing. You didn't panic-throttle-off when the rear hit. That's the habit we're building."
- "Good timing on the lift — the front was already in the air when it reached the log. You anticipated the log, not reacted to it."

### Corrective Feedback
- "Your front hit the log face — the lift was too late. Pop the clutch when you're a metre away from the log, not when you're at it."
- "Throttle off as the rear hit — bike stopped. Hold throttle through both phases. The rear needs drive to roll over the log."
- "Your front is going way too high. That big lift causes a hard front landing on the other side. The front only needs to clear by 5 centimetres. Lighter pre-load."
- "You're sitting down. Stand up. You need your legs to absorb the rear crossing. Seated means the suspension handles everything — not ideal for rear pitch management."

### Progression Prompts
- "You've got the 20 cm log. Now try the 30 cm one. Same technique — just needs slightly more throttle pre-load on the clutch pop for the extra height."
- "Next run, approach 5 km/h faster. At higher speed you can use a manual lift instead of a clutch pop — try it and see which feels more controlled."
- "When you can do three consecutive logs without pausing, you're ready for trail application. Let's set up a three-log sequence."

## 6. DRILLS & PRACTICE EXERCISES

### Drill 1: Front Phase Only — No Log
**Purpose:** Isolate the front lift technique before combining with a log.
**Setup:** Flat ground, a marked line representing the log position.
**Execution:**
1. Approach the marked line.
2. Execute a clutch pop — front lifts to 20 cm.
3. Let front come down on the far side of the line.
4. Rear crosses the line normally.
**Success Criteria:** Front lifts cleanly and lands on the far side of the line on 8 out of 10 attempts.
**Duration/Reps:** 20 attempts.
**Progression:** Add a 5 cm foam noodle at the line to simulate the log.

### Drill 2: Small Log Crossing
**Purpose:** Apply the full two-phase technique to a real low log.
**Setup:** A log 10–15 cm diameter on flat ground.
**Execution:**
1. Approach at 10 km/h.
2. Clutch pop for front lift.
3. Maintained throttle for rear crossing.
**Success Criteria:** Clean crossing without contact on front, smooth rear roll-over.
**Duration/Reps:** 20 passes.
**Progression:** Increase log diameter to 20 cm, then 30 cm.

### Drill 3: Speed Variation Log
**Purpose:** Learn to adapt technique to different approach speeds.
**Setup:** A 25 cm log on flat ground.
**Execution:**
1. First 5 passes: 10 km/h approach.
2. Next 5 passes: 15 km/h approach.
3. Next 5 passes: 20 km/h approach.
4. Note technique changes required at each speed.
**Success Criteria:** Clean crossing at all three speeds. Rider can articulate the technique difference.
**Duration/Reps:** 15 passes total.
**Progression:** Add a second log 3 metres from the first.

### Drill 4: Multi-Log Rhythm
**Purpose:** Cross multiple logs in sequence without pausing — building rhythm and flow.
**Setup:** Three logs placed 4–6 metres apart on flat ground.
**Execution:**
1. Approach the first log at moderate speed.
2. Cross it cleanly.
3. Maintain speed and cross the second.
4. Cross the third.
5. Assess: was speed maintained through all three? Did technique break down on the later logs?
**Success Criteria:** All three logs crossed cleanly without stopping.
**Duration/Reps:** 10 runs through all three logs.
**Progression:** Reduce spacing to 3 metres. Then add a corner between two of the logs.

## 8. TERRAIN & CONTEXT VARIATIONS

### Flat Ground Crossing
Standard application. No gradient complicates the technique. Front lift height is consistent and predictable.

### Uphill Approach
The gradient slows the approach and reduces available momentum. More throttle pre-load needed for the front lift. The rear crossing on uphill is easier — gravity keeps the rear from launching.

### Downhill Approach
More speed available — the technique may shift toward manual lift. The front landing is harder on the downhill side — suspension must absorb more. Body weight rearward is critical.

### Wet Log
A wet log surface reduces rear wheel traction during the crossing — the rear tyre may spin on the log rather than rolling over it. Approach at lower speed. Maintained steady throttle (not excessive) is more important than ever on wet wood.

### Angled Log
A log that crosses the trail at an angle requires the rider to approach at an angle — front crosses first, then rear. The crossing angle changes the effective width of the log contact — a well-angled approach reduces effective log height by riding up the log's slope rather than its face.

## 9. EXPERT INSIGHTS & SOURCES

### Key Insights
- **The universal obstacle:** Log crossings are so ubiquitous in enduro that coaches refer to the two-phase technique as fundamental literacy — not optional knowledge. A rider who cannot cross a log consistently will struggle across all technical terrain types.
- **Rear phase importance:** Most coaching focus on log crossings is on the front lift because it is the more dramatic action. However, experienced coaches note that the rear phase failure (throttle cut, rear stopping on log) is equally common and more momentum-costly.
- **The height calibration principle:** A common coaching observation is that beginner riders over-lift the front dramatically — 50–60 cm for a 20 cm log. This creates a hard front landing and unnecessarily aggressive technique. The goal is precise clearance, not maximum height.
- **Wet wood risk:** Wet log crossings are disproportionately common crash scenarios in enduro — the rear tyre spins on wet bark and the bike either stops or the rider high-sides as the rear exits the log and suddenly grips. Lower speed and careful throttle management on wet logs.
