# Body Positioning Through Corners

## 1. OVERVIEW

Off-road cornering body position is fundamentally different from road riding. In road racing, the rider leans aggressively with the bike, hanging off to reduce lean angle. In off-road and enduro, the principle is inverted: the bike leans into the corner while the rider's body stays relatively upright. This means the bike leans more than the rider, creating a lean angle differential that dramatically increases available traction. The off-road cornering position also involves the inside foot forward on the peg, outside peg heavily weighted, and a hip offset toward the inside of the corner. These elements work together to reduce the lean angle the bike needs to generate the required cornering force — and every degree of lean angle saved is a degree of traction margin preserved. Getting body position right in corners is not about style; it is about physics.

## 2. CORE PRINCIPLES

### Principle 1: Bike Leans, Rider Stays Upright
The defining characteristic of off-road corner technique is that the bike leans significantly more than the rider. While the bike may be at 25-35 degrees of lean, the rider's torso may be at only 10-15 degrees. This differential is what gives the system its traction advantage. When the combined centre of mass (rider plus bike) is lower than what a fully-leaned-together system would create, the system requires less lean angle to achieve the same cornering radius. Less lean angle means a larger tyre contact patch and more grip. Road riders "hang off" to achieve a similar effect but in the opposite direction — enduro riders achieve it by staying upright while the bike leans beneath them.

### Principle 2: Outside Peg Weighting Is the Engine of the Technique
Pushing down through the outside footpeg is the physical mechanism that keeps the rider upright while the bike leans. When the rider stands and loads the outside peg heavily, the bike is pushed downward on the outside, encouraging it to lean into the corner. The rider's centre of mass moves toward the inside relative to the bike — creating the lean differential. This is not a passive position: it requires deliberate, sustained downward pressure on the outside peg throughout the corner. Riders who understand this cue — "push the outside peg through the corner" — improve dramatically and quickly.

### Principle 3: Inside Foot Forward Provides Leverage and Emergency Balance
In enduro and off-road cornering, the inside foot moves forward on the peg — sometimes to the ball of the foot, sometimes the toes, with the heel back. This position provides two benefits. First, it gives the rider a mechanical lever — the forward foot position allows the hip to drop toward the inside of the corner, offsetting the centre of mass inward. Second, if the front wheel washes out or the bike begins to fall in the corner, the inside foot can be dabbed on the ground without the rider being caught wrong-footed. The foot-out technique at slow speeds (dragging the inside foot on the ground) is an extreme version of this, used in very tight corners.

### Principle 4: The Inside Elbow Drop Is a Consequence, Not a Technique
Coaches often cue "drop the inside elbow" in corners. This is correct but slightly misleading — the dropped inside elbow is a result of correct body position, not a separate action. When the rider's hips offset toward the inside and the bike leans beneath them, the inside arm naturally shortens and the inside elbow drops. Trying to drop the elbow deliberately without the hip offset tends to twist the upper body incorrectly. Cue the hip and foot position first; the elbow will follow.

### Principle 5: Head Position — Looking Through the Exit
The head must lead the body through the corner. As the bike enters the corner, the rider's head should already be rotating toward the apex; as the bike reaches the apex, the head should already be pointed at the exit. This serves two functions: it maintains the visual lead system established in CORNER-01, and it keeps the upper body from over-rotating into the corner. A rider whose head is looking correctly at the exit will naturally have a more upright, stable upper body position. A rider who looks down at the front wheel or at the inside of the corner tends to lean too far with the bike.

### Principle 6: Standing vs Sitting Through Corners Changes the Mechanics
Standing through a corner provides maximum adjustability — the rider can shift weight dynamically, respond to grip changes, and manage fore/aft balance in real time. In the standing corner position, the outside peg weighting is more effective because there is no seat contact distributing the force. Sitting through corners reduces the traction advantage because the rider's weight is split between seat and pegs — outside peg pressure is lower. Sitting corners work in certain conditions (smooth, bermed, low speed) but the standing corner position is the default for technical enduro terrain.

## 3. TECHNIQUE BREAKDOWN BY LEVEL

### 3a. Beginner Level
**Goal:** Learn the inside foot forward, outside peg weighted position and understand why it differs from road riding.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Move the inside foot forward on the peg as you enter the corner
- Deliberately push down on the outside peg throughout the corner
- Keep the head upright and eyes looking through the corner, not at the inside edge
- Allow a small gap to remain between the seat and the rider's body

**What Good Looks Like:**
From a rear or slight side camera, the rider's shoulders are noticeably more upright than the bike's lean angle. The inside foot is clearly forward. The outside peg has visible weight on it — the outside leg is slightly compressed and loaded. The rider's head is tracking through the corner toward the exit, not looking at the apex. The bike leans more than the rider.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Leaning with the bike:** Rider matches the bike's lean angle — both bike and body lean the same amount. Why it happens: road riding habit or instinctive matching of the bike's movement. → Push the outside peg down. Actively resist leaning with the bike.
- **Inside foot stays back on the peg:** Foot does not move forward in the corner. Why it happens: rider is focused on braking and turning, not foot position. → Set the foot before the corner — as part of the entry checklist.
- **Looking at the inside of the corner:** Head turns toward the inside edge rather than through the corner. Why it happens: target fixation on the apex. → "Look where you want to go, not at what you want to avoid."
- **Sitting through every corner:** Rider stays seated, reducing the effectiveness of all peg-weighting techniques. → Stand up through technical corners. Make it a rule.

### 3b. Intermediate Level
**Goal:** Make the off-road corner position automatic — foot, peg, and head all correct without conscious thought — and begin adjusting position dynamically to terrain and grip.

**Progression Markers from Beginner:** Rider consistently places inside foot forward. Outside peg weighting is habitual. Head leads through the corner without coaching prompt.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Hip offset toward the inside — not just foot and peg, but actively pushing the hip inside the bike's centreline
- Maintaining outside peg pressure through the full arc of the corner, including while throttle rolls on at the exit
- Adjusting foot position for tight corners (further forward, possibly foot toward the ground) versus sweeping corners
- Decoupling upper body from the bike — body upright, bike leaning, without feeling of fighting the bike

**What Good Looks Like:**
The lean angle differential between bike and rider is clearly visible — the bike is at 25+ degrees and the rider's shoulders are at 10 degrees or less. The hip is offset toward the inside. The corner looks effortless — the rider is not fighting for balance or clinging to the bars. Through a sweeper, the body position remains stable for the duration of the arc. Through a tight corner, the inside foot is almost to the ground.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Correct position at apex only:** Rider sets up the correct position but releases it too early — leaning with the bike before the exit is clear. → Hold the outside peg pressure until throttle is fully open and the bike is upright.
- **Hip doesn't move:** Foot goes forward but the hip stays centred. Why it happens: rider learned foot position but not the hip component. → Think "push the hip to the inside, not just the foot."
- **Over-rotating upper body:** Rider twists toward the inside so aggressively that shoulders point into the corner — creating tension in the inside arm. → Head leads, body follows minimally. The lean differential is hip-driven, not shoulder-driven.

### 3c. Advanced Level
**Goal:** Use body position as an active traction management tool — adjusting in real time based on grip feel, corner shape changes, and what follows the corner.

**Progression Markers from Intermediate:** Lean differential is automatic and consistent. Hip offset is habitual. Body decoupled from bike through the full arc.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Micro-adjustments of outside peg pressure to manage rear slide — if rear slides, momentarily increasing outside peg load can stabilise
- Adjusting the degree of body uprightness based on grip: better grip allows more aggressive lean, poor grip demands more body uprightness
- Transitioning body position through corner shape changes — a corner that tightens mid-arc requires more outside peg pressure as it tightens
- Pre-positioning: setting foot and hip position earlier on the approach, not at the entry point
- Managing the exit — as lean reduces, how the body returns to neutral without unweighting the pegs prematurely

**What Good Looks Like:**
From a chase camera or aerial view, the rider looks completely still — the bike works underneath them. Through a corner with a grip change (loose patch in the middle), the rider's outside peg pressure visibly increases at that point without any loss of line. The position transitions are invisible — the rider is not "setting up" the corner, they are already in the right position. Exit re-centering is smooth and simultaneous with throttle opening.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Forgetting body position in fast sweepers:** At high speed, riders sometimes default back to a neutral or road-style lean. → The higher the speed, the more valuable the lean differential — do not abandon it on sweepers.
- **Using bars instead of pegs to manage slides:** When the rear steps out, gripping the bars harder instead of adjusting peg pressure. → Pegs stabilise the bike; bars steer it. Separate the actions.
- **Early release of outside peg:** Releasing the outside peg pressure as the throttle opens — but before the bike is upright. → Maintain peg load until the bike has fully straightened.

## 4. DIAGNOSTIC CUES (AI COACHING ENGINE REFERENCE)

### Visual Cues — Correct Technique
- From a rear or side camera: measurable lean angle differential visible — bike lean angle is clearly greater than rider torso lean angle
- Inside foot is forward on the peg — toes or ball of foot leading, heel back, visible when viewed from behind or side
- Outside leg is compressed and loaded — slight bend visible, contrasting with the inside leg which may be more extended
- Rider's shoulders are roughly level or very slightly inclined toward the outside — not matching the bike's lean
- Head is upright and rotated toward the exit of the corner, not looking at the inside edge
- In tight corners: inside foot approaches or touches the ground — foot-out body position visible from front or side camera
- Rider's hips are offset toward the inside of the corner relative to the bike's centreline

### Visual Cues — Error Indicators
- Rider and bike lean at identical angles — no lean differential (road-style lean)
- Inside foot remains back on the peg — both feet parallel rather than inside foot forward
- Rider's head is looking at the inside of the corner or down at the wheel — vision not leading
- Upper body twisted aggressively into the corner — inside shoulder dropping too far, creating bar tension
- Rider sitting through the corner — visible seat contact reducing peg effectiveness
- Arms extended and stiff — rider using bars as a support structure rather than a steering input

### Audio Cues
- Smooth, sustained throttle roll-on from the apex to the exit: consistent with correct body position allowing earlier throttle
- Rear wheel slide sound mid-corner: may indicate body position shifted to the inside too much (overloaded lean) or outside peg pressure insufficient
- Front wheel wash sound (front tyre scrubbing): may indicate rider weight too far forward or inside — check body position for lean matching

### Sensor Cues
- IMU lean angle: correct technique shows bike lean angle significantly greater than rider lean angle (if measured separately via helmet or vest IMU vs chassis IMU)
- Footpeg pressure sensor (if available): outside peg should show significantly higher load than inside peg throughout the corner arc
- Handlebar load cells: correct technique shows lower handlebar force than poor technique — body position is managing cornering load through pegs, not bars
- GPS trace: correct body position correlates with narrower, more controlled lines — consistent apex selection rather than varying, reactive lines

## 5. COACHING CUES & LANGUAGE

### Positive Reinforcement
- "Perfect — the bike is leaning but your body is upright. That's traction in the bank. You gave yourself a grip buffer every corner."
- "I can see the lean differential. The bike's doing its job, your body's doing its job — they're two separate things working together."
- "That inside foot forward, outside peg down — that's the position. Keep that going for the whole corner and you'll never run out of grip."

### Corrective Feedback
- "You're matching the bike's lean angle. You're road riding. Push that outside peg down and feel the bike separate from you — you stay up, it leans."
- "Your inside foot isn't moving. Before the corner even starts, slide that foot forward. It's part of the setup, not an in-corner adjustment."
- "You're looking at the inside of the corner — that's why your body is diving in. Head up, eyes forward. Look at where you want to be, not where you are."
- "You sat down through that corner. You lost your peg leverage the moment you sat. Stand it up and push that outside peg — that's where your control comes from."
- "Your elbow is dropping but your hip isn't moving. Elbow drop is a consequence, not the cause. Push the hip to the inside first — the elbow will follow."

### Progression Prompts
- "This next lap, I want you to set your inside foot forward before the corner starts — on the approach, not at the apex. Make it part of your entry routine."
- "Concentrate on the outside peg for the next three laps. Push it like you're trying to push the peg through the footpeg mount. The bike will feel like it's on rails."
- "Now try this: as you hold the corner position, keep the outside peg pressure even as the throttle opens. Don't release the peg when you go for gas — hold both together."

## 6. DRILLS & PRACTICE EXERCISES

### Drill 1: The Figure-Eight Body Position Drill
**Purpose:** Build the inside foot forward and outside peg weighted position at low speed with no terrain pressure — pure body position focus.
**Setup:** Flat open area (parking lot or field), two cones placed about 10 metres apart.
**Execution:**
1. Ride a slow figure-eight around the two cones.
2. As you enter each arc, move the inside foot forward on the peg.
3. Push the outside peg down — feel the bike lean under you while your body stays relatively upright.
4. Hold the position for the entire arc.
5. Transition to the other direction, swapping foot positions.
6. After five minutes, have someone observe or film from behind: is the lean differential visible?
**Success Criteria:** Clear lean differential visible from rear camera. Inside foot forward on both arcs. Body upright relative to the bike. Rider can hold this for the full figure-eight without losing it mid-arc.
**Duration/Reps:** 20 minutes continuous.
**Progression:** Reduce the cone spacing (tighter arcs). Increase speed. Add throttle roll-on at each exit.

### Drill 2: Foot Forward Habit Builder
**Purpose:** Make inside foot forward automatic before the corner, not during it.
**Setup:** A familiar corner on a practice loop — medium radius, clear entry point.
**Execution:**
1. Approach the corner standing.
2. On the straight before the corner, deliberately move the inside foot forward — before the entry point.
3. Enter the corner with the foot already in position.
4. Exit and reset on the return.
5. After ten passes, try moving the foot earlier and earlier on the approach — eventually setting it before the braking zone begins.
**Success Criteria:** Inside foot is consistently forward before turn-in. No mid-corner foot shuffling. The foot position becomes a trigger, not an afterthought.
**Duration/Reps:** 15 minutes on a single corner.
**Progression:** Apply to a sequence of corners, switching feet quickly between corners. Then increase pace to 80%.

### Drill 3: Lean Differential Check with Observer
**Purpose:** Get direct feedback on lean differential — feel versus reality calibration.
**Setup:** A medium-radius corner on a practice trail. Observer with a camera positioned to film from behind or slightly to the side.
**Execution:**
1. Rider makes five passes at moderate pace, attempting maximum lean differential (body upright, bike leaning).
2. Observer films each pass and provides immediate feedback after each one.
3. Rider adjusts based on feedback — often they believe they are leaning correctly when they are not.
4. Make another five passes after the feedback.
5. Compare the two sets for improvement.
**Success Criteria:** Lean differential is clearly visible in the second set of passes and measurably greater than the first set. Rider can feel the difference and match the observation.
**Duration/Reps:** 30 minutes including review.
**Progression:** Take the technique to a different corner type (off-camber, bermed) and repeat the filming exercise.

### Drill 4: Peg Pressure Corners
**Purpose:** Consciously maximise outside peg pressure through a full corner arc to understand and internalise its effect.
**Setup:** A moderate-radius, well-known corner — the rider should not need to focus on line selection.
**Execution:**
1. Ride the corner with deliberate focus on the outside peg only — push it as hard as possible throughout.
2. Note how the bike feels: how much lean it wants to take, how stable the corner feels.
3. On the next pass, relax the outside peg and ride normally.
4. Compare the two feelings — the peg-pressure version should feel more planted and stable.
5. Repeat the peg-pressure version until it feels normal.
**Success Criteria:** Rider can consistently feel the difference between peg-weighted and non-peg-weighted cornering. Describes the peg-weighted version as more planted, more controlled, or more confident.
**Duration/Reps:** 20 minutes.
**Progression:** Apply peg pressure in corners where a rear slide previously occurred and note the stability improvement.

## 8. TERRAIN & CONTEXT VARIATIONS

### Hard Pack / Dry Dirt
Classic body position applies — bike leans, body stays upright, outside peg loaded. Grip is predictable enough to trust the lean differential fully. The position can be held confidently through the full arc. Entry speed allows a more dynamic hip offset. This is the ideal surface for drilling the position.

### Loose over Hard
Grip is unpredictable at the front, particularly through the apex. The body uprightness becomes even more important — reducing lean angle protects front grip. Outside peg pressure should be maximised. If the front begins to wash, the upright body position provides more recovery options than a leaned-in body would. Inside foot further forward and closer to the ground as insurance.

### Mud
Mud cornering often involves ruts that guide the bike's path. In a rut corner, the body position shifts slightly — the rut provides lateral support that replaces some of the lean differential. However, outside peg weighting is still critical to keep the bike tracking in the rut. Inside foot is often close to the ground or dragging gently. Head position — looking out of the rut toward the exit — is critical.

### Sand
In deep sand corners, lean angle must be reduced even more aggressively than on hard surfaces — the sand collapses under high lean, dropping grip suddenly. Body uprightness is maximised. Outside peg pressure is high. Speed is managed down on entry to reduce lean demand. The corner is often wider and slower than equivalent hard-pack corners. The inside foot technique is less useful here because foot contact with sand provides minimal stability.

### Off-Camber
Off-camber corners are where this body position has the highest impact. The surface tilts away from the corner, effectively reducing available lean angle. Body uprightness counteracts this — keeping the rider and combined CoM as upright as possible reduces the effective lean angle the bike needs. Outside peg pressure is maximum. Entry speed is significantly reduced. The inside foot is forward and close to the ground. Throttle application at the apex must be smooth — any rear slip on an off-camber apex can be catastrophic.

### Bermed Corners
In a berm, the banking provides lateral support that the body position technique is designed to replicate on flat ground. In a berm, the rider can be slightly more aggressive with inside lean — the berm compensates. However, outside peg weighting is still beneficial because it pushes the tyre into the berm wall, increasing contact patch pressure. Speed through berms is higher, so the body position must be set up earlier.

## 9. EXPERT INSIGHTS & SOURCES

### Key Insights
- **Cody Webb** (multiple SuperEnduro Champion): Describes the off-road corner position as "letting the bike fall in while you stay up." The mental image of the bike falling away from the rider (leaning) while the rider remains over their feet (upright) is a useful model for riders struggling with the lean differential concept.
- **Jonny Walker** (Red Bull KTM Factory Racing): Noted for his ability to maintain cornering position on extreme off-camber terrain in hard enduro events — the body uprightness is visible and consistent even under extreme conditions where most riders collapse into the corner.
- **Bret Tkacs** (ADVMotoSkills): Explicitly coaches the outside peg as the primary cornering control — "the outside peg is your steering wheel in a corner." This contradicts the instinctive road-riding approach and requires deliberate relearning for most riders transitioning from tarmac.
- **Motocross heritage:** The off-road body position in corners is directly derived from motocross — MX riders have used the inside foot forward, outside peg weighted position since the early development of the sport, because the physics of loose-surface cornering have always demanded it. Enduro riders inherit this directly.
- **Trials influence:** In trials, the foot-out technique (inside foot touching or dragging) is common at very low speeds. Extreme enduro has absorbed this technique for hairpins and technical slow corners, extending the range of the cornering body position concept across speed ranges.
