# Entry, Apex, Exit Theory

## 1. OVERVIEW

Every corner in off-road riding can be broken into three distinct phases: entry, apex, and exit. Understanding these phases is the geometric foundation of fast, safe, and consistent cornering. Entry is where braking is completed and the rider commits to the turn. The apex is the point of closest approach to the inside of the corner — the pivot around which the line is built. The exit is where the bike straightens out and acceleration begins. In road racing, the apex is selected based on pure geometry. In enduro and off-road riding, the apex is selected based on grip, surface, and what the terrain allows. Mastering this three-phase model allows a rider to plan corners in advance rather than react to them, and it underpins every advanced cornering concept that follows.

## 2. CORE PRINCIPLES

### Principle 1: The Three Phases Are Sequential and Each Depends on the Previous
A poor entry creates a poor apex, which creates a poor exit — errors compound. Arriving at the apex with too much speed means the bike will run wide on exit. Arriving at the apex from the wrong angle means the exit line is compromised. Riders who understand this sequence stop trying to fix problems mid-corner and instead focus on setting up each phase correctly before it arrives. The best riders in the world are solving exit problems at the moment they enter the corner.

### Principle 2: The Late Apex Is the Default in Off-Road Riding
The geometric apex — the mathematical midpoint of the corner's inside edge — is rarely the correct apex off-road. The late apex means turning in later than the geometric centre, which produces two critical advantages: it allows the bike to be pointed toward the exit before the tightest part of the corner, and it creates a wider, more forgiving exit line. A late apex means that at the exit, the bike is already tracking straight while there is still road or trail left to use. An early apex forces the rider to hold the lean angle longer and risk running out of trail on the outside of the exit.

### Principle 3: Off-Road Apex Is Grip-Driven, Not Geometry-Driven
On tarmac, the ideal apex is geometric because grip is relatively consistent. In enduro, the grip varies dramatically — inside ruts, hard pack, loose over hard, embedded rocks, roots. The apex in off-road is wherever the grip is best, even if that is not the geometric inside of the corner. A rider might apex on a rut that happens to be two metres from the inside edge, or avoid the geometric apex entirely because it sits on a slippery root cluster. Reading the apex for grip is a higher skill than simply hitting the inside of every corner.

### Principle 4: Vision Drives the Entire System
The entry, apex, and exit sequence is driven almost entirely by where the rider is looking. To hit a late apex, the rider must identify it before reaching the entry point — which means looking through the corner from well before turn-in. Riders who look at the entry point are already behind; their vision has to be at the apex as they enter, and at the exit as they apex. This eye movement is the cognitive engine that runs the three-phase system. Without correct vision, no amount of mechanical skill produces consistent corners.

### Principle 5: Corner Shape Determines Apex Strategy
Three corner shapes exist in off-road riding, and each demands a different approach. A constant-radius corner — one that maintains the same arc throughout — can be apexed at the geometric centre. A tightening-radius corner — one that gets tighter as it progresses — demands a late apex and deliberate slowing before turn-in; early apex here is dangerous. An opening-radius corner — one that opens up on exit — rewards an early-ish apex because the exit widens naturally. Most enduro corners are tightening-radius, which is why the late apex is the dominant off-road strategy. Identifying the corner shape on the approach is a core reading skill.

### Principle 6: The Exit Sets Up Everything That Follows
A correct exit — bike pointed at the next section, lean angle already reducing, throttle opening — does more than just end the corner cleanly. It sets up the braking point for the next corner, the line through the next feature, and the time available to plan the following section. In enduro, where corners chain rapidly together, riders who execute poor exits spend the next several seconds recovering instead of planning. The exit is where lap time and stage time are built or lost.

## 3. TECHNIQUE BREAKDOWN BY LEVEL

### 3a. Beginner Level
**Goal:** Understand the three phases conceptually and begin applying a consistent entry-apex-exit pattern on simple corners.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Identify the entry point before reaching it — where braking must be completed
- Find the inside of the corner as the visual target for the apex
- Look for the exit before reaching the apex
- Avoid turning in too early (early apex is the default beginner error)

**What Good Looks Like:**
The rider approaches a corner wide, brakes in a straight line before the entry point, then turns toward the inside of the corner. At the apex, the bike is close to the inside edge and the rider's eyes are already on the exit. The bike begins to straighten and throttle opens before the corner is finished. The exit is wide — the bike has room to track straight without running off the trail edge.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Turning in too early:** Rider cuts across the corner at the earliest opportunity. Why it happens: the inside of the corner is visible and instinctively attractive. → Delay turn-in. Count to two after the instinct fires, then turn.
- **Looking at the front wheel:** Rider's vision is two metres ahead during the corner. Why it happens: focus on the immediate trail surface. → Force eyes to the apex as you enter, and to the exit as you apex. Vision leads the bike.
- **Braking through the corner:** Speed still being scrubbed inside the corner. Why it happens: rider arrives too fast and uses the corner to slow down. → All braking must be done before the entry point.
- **Not identifying corner shape:** Rider treats every corner identically. Why it happens: lack of conceptual model. → Before each run, walk the section and name the corner shapes out loud.

### 3b. Intermediate Level
**Goal:** Consistently apply late apex technique and adapt apex selection to grip conditions rather than geometry.

**Progression Markers from Beginner:** Rider correctly enters wide and exits wide on standard corners. Vision is at the apex during entry. No braking inside corners under normal conditions.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Deliberately delaying turn-in past the geometric apex for late apex technique
- Scanning apex zone for grip before committing to the entry point
- Identifying tightening versus opening radius corners on approach
- Timing throttle application precisely to the apex and exit direction

**What Good Looks Like:**
The rider approaches a corner on the outside edge, delays turn-in noticeably (the bike tracks wide for slightly longer than a beginner would expect), then cuts late to an apex that is in the second half of the corner. By the time the bike is closest to the inside, it is already pointing at the exit and the rider is opening the throttle. On a tightening corner, the rider's entry speed is visibly lower — they have read the shape and adjusted.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Compromising late apex under time pressure:** In race situations, reverting to geometric apex. Why it happens: early apex feels faster because entry speed is higher momentarily. → It is not faster — it costs more on exit than it saves on entry.
- **Apexing geometrically on bad grip:** Hitting the geometric inside even when grip is poor there. Why it happens: habit, not reading. → Look at the apex zone surface before committing to turn-in.
- **Missing tightening radius:** Entering too fast because corner shape was not read. Why it happens: vision focus is on entry, not through the corner. → Practise identifying corner shapes from further back — 5 seconds minimum before entry.

### 3c. Advanced Level
**Goal:** Automatically select the optimal apex based on grip, shape, and what follows the corner — and chain multiple corners together without losing line.

**Progression Markers from Intermediate:** Late apex is default on all corner types. Corner shape identification is automatic. Throttle application timed precisely to apex.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Reading grip zones from the saddle at race speed (mud, sand, hard pack, loose)
- Adjusting entry point in real time based on surface changes (wet, dry, churned)
- Linking corners — using each exit to set up the entry of the next corner
- Selecting alternative lines (ruts, berms, offcamber) based on real-time traction data
- Managing the risk-reward trade-off: tighter late apex for more exit drive versus risk of apex error on loose

**What Good Looks Like:**
From a chase or aerial camera, the rider's line through a sequence of corners looks deliberate and geometric — each entry is wide, each apex is in the rear third of the corner, each exit is straight. The transition from one corner to the next is seamless — the exit of corner one is already the correct width for the entry of corner two. Throttle is applied at exactly the apex in each corner. In changing grip, the apex point shifts without the rider slowing their overall pace.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Tunnel vision on a single corner:** Advanced riders sometimes optimise one corner but lose the line for the next. → Always solve corner N+1 while riding corner N.
- **Rigid late apex in corners that reward earlier apexing:** Opening-radius corners in enduro sometimes benefit from slightly earlier apex. → Adapt — late apex is a default, not a rule.
- **Racing the entry instead of the exit:** Carrying maximum speed to the entry point but losing time on exit. → Exit speed is what matters — protect it.

## 4. DIAGNOSTIC CUES (AI COACHING ENGINE REFERENCE)

### Visual Cues — Correct Technique
- Aerial or high-mounted side camera: rider enters on the outside edge of the trail, apex is clearly in the second half of the corner (late apex visible), exit is wide with room to spare
- Rider's eyes and head are visibly rotated toward the exit before the apex is reached — head leading the bike through the corner
- Lean angle peaks and begins to reduce before the apex — the bike is already straightening as it passes the closest point to the inside
- Throttle application (visible via rear wheel traction event or audio) begins at or just after the apex, not before
- Smooth, connected line from entry through apex to exit — no abrupt direction changes

### Visual Cues — Error Indicators
- Rider turns in early — the apex is in the first half of the corner and the bike is wide on exit (running out of trail visible)
- Rear wheel slide or front wash mid-corner — suggests throttle applied before the apex with too much lean angle
- Rider's head is looking at the front wheel or the entry point during the corner — vision has not moved ahead
- Braking visible inside the corner (brake light, forward pitch, speed scrub) — braking not completed before entry
- Line across multiple corners shows no width management — straight line through corners regardless of shape

### Audio Cues
- Throttle application sound starting mid-corner before the apex indicates premature throttle — high traction-loss risk
- Consistent throttle roll-on starting after the apex, matching exit straightening: correct technique
- Brake sound inside the corner confirms late braking — entry not set up correctly

### Sensor Cues
- GPS trace overlaid on map: shows the actual line through corners — late apex produces a characteristic swept-wide entry, tight apex, wide exit shape
- IMU lateral G-force: lean angle should peak and begin reducing before the geometric midpoint of the corner on a late apex line
- Throttle position sensor: opening should begin at or after lean angle starts reducing — throttle and lean angle should not be increasing simultaneously

## 5. COACHING CUES & LANGUAGE

### Positive Reinforcement
- "That's exactly it — you turned in late and had a full exit lane. That's what late apex looks like. Every corner should feel like that."
- "Great shape through that sequence. I could see you solving the second corner while you were still in the first one. That's the level."
- "You read that tightening radius correctly — you slowed at the entry and had a perfect exit. Most riders get caught out by that corner."

### Corrective Feedback
- "You're turning in too early — I can see you running out of room on the exit. Delay the turn-in. It feels wrong at first because you're pointing at the outside of the corner for longer, but trust the late apex."
- "Your vision is on the entry. I need your eyes at the apex as you enter, and at the exit as you apex. The bike goes where your eyes go — stop looking at the front tyre."
- "That corner gets tighter and you went in at the same speed as a constant-radius corner. Walk it again — see how it tightens? That needs a slower entry and a later apex."
- "You're braking inside the corner. All your braking has to be done before you turn in. If you're braking in the corner, your entry speed was wrong."

### Progression Prompts
- "Next time through, I want you to identify the corner shape before you enter — constant, tightening, or opening. Call it out loud as you approach. Then pick the apex based on that."
- "Pick a physical landmark for your apex before you ride it. A rock, a root, a marking on the ground. Aim for that specific point, not a general inside area."
- "On this next run, focus entirely on the exit. Wide exit, throttle open, room to spare. Work backwards from there to set up your entry."

## 6. DRILLS & PRACTICE EXERCISES

### Drill 1: Three-Phase Walk-Through
**Purpose:** Build the cognitive habit of planning all three phases before riding a corner, not during it.
**Setup:** A single corner on a practice trail or enduro loop — a moderate-speed corner with clear inside and outside edges.
**Execution:**
1. Walk the corner on foot before riding it.
2. Identify and physically mark (or memorise) the entry point, apex point, and exit point.
3. Note the corner shape: constant, tightening, or opening.
4. Choose the apex based on grip, not geometry — look at the surface in the apex zone.
5. Ride the corner five times, hitting the three marked points each time.
6. After each pass, review: did you hit all three? What caused deviation if not?
**Success Criteria:** Rider consistently hits the planned entry, apex, and exit on at least four of five passes. Can articulate which corner shape it is and why that apex was chosen.
**Duration/Reps:** 20 minutes on a single corner. Repeat on a different corner shape.
**Progression:** Move to a sequence of two connected corners. The exit of the first must be the correct width for the entry of the second.

### Drill 2: Late Apex Delay Drill
**Purpose:** Break the early turn-in habit by practising deliberate turn-in delay.
**Setup:** A wide, open corner with clear inside edge — ideally marked with a cone or natural feature at the geometric apex and another cone 3-4 metres further along as the late apex target.
**Execution:**
1. Ride the corner at moderate speed.
2. On the first pass, apex at the first cone (geometric apex) — feel where that puts the exit.
3. On the second pass, apex at the second cone (late apex) — note the wider, cleaner exit.
4. Repeat the late apex version five times.
5. Progressively increase entry speed while maintaining the late apex point.
**Success Criteria:** Rider consistently apexes at the second cone. Exit lane is noticeably wider than the geometric apex pass. Rider can feel (not just see) the exit difference.
**Duration/Reps:** 15-20 minutes.
**Progression:** Remove the cone and replicate the late apex by feel. Then move to a corner without a clear inside edge (open terrain).

### Drill 3: Corner Shape Identification Ride
**Purpose:** Train automatic corner shape identification at trail speed.
**Setup:** A trail loop containing at least five corners of different shapes — ideally a familiar section where the rider can focus on classification rather than navigation.
**Execution:**
1. Ride the loop at 70% pace.
2. For each corner, call out the shape (constant, tightening, opening) before turning in — out loud.
3. Adjust entry speed based on the shape: tightening = slower entry, opening = normal entry.
4. After the loop, review: which corners were misread? Which entry speeds were wrong?
**Success Criteria:** Rider correctly identifies corner shape on all five target corners. Entry speed adjusts appropriately on tightening corners (measurably slower than opening corners of similar appearance).
**Duration/Reps:** 30 minutes, multiple laps.
**Progression:** Increase pace to 85% and repeat. Add a new, unfamiliar section with different corner shapes.

### Drill 4: Vision Lead Drill
**Purpose:** Train the eyes to lead the body through the corner — apex vision at entry, exit vision at apex.
**Setup:** A corner with a distinct visual target at both the apex and the exit (a tree, rock, or placed cone at each).
**Execution:**
1. Before turning in, fix vision on the apex target.
2. As the bike reaches the apex, fix vision on the exit target immediately — do not look at the apex as you pass it.
3. Hold vision on the exit as the bike straightens.
4. Note how early the throttle can be opened when vision is ahead of the bike.
**Success Criteria:** Rider's head is visibly rotated toward the exit before the apex is reached (visible from a chase camera or observer behind). Throttle opens earlier than in previous runs.
**Duration/Reps:** 15 minutes, alternating vision-focused and normal passes to feel the difference.
**Progression:** Move vision commitment one step further — fix on the exit target before reaching the entry point.

## 8. TERRAIN & CONTEXT VARIATIONS

### Hard Pack / Dry Dirt
Classic late apex applies cleanly. Grip is predictable and the geometric apex is often close to the best-grip apex. Vision lead and late turn-in are the primary focus. Entry speed can be higher and the braking zone is shorter and more precise.

### Loose over Hard (Typical Enduro Surface)
The geometric apex is frequently the worst-grip point — loose material collects on the inside of corners. The best grip is often found just off the inside line, in the slightly scraped zone where tyres have displaced loose material. Apex selection is dominated by grip reading. Entry speed is reduced to manage the unpredictable front-end response on the loose surface.

### Mud
Corner shape changes as the session progresses — ruts form, lines deepen, new grip zones emerge. The apex in mud is wherever the rut provides lateral support, which may not be the inside of the corner at all. An existing rut that curves through the second half of the corner becomes the apex even if it is offset from the inside edge. Entry speed is dramatically reduced. The three-phase model still applies but the apex definition shifts to rut-following.

### Rocky / Technical Terrain
Speed is low enough that the late apex principle becomes less about line speed and more about picking the safest and most navigable path. The entry point is where forward momentum can be managed without balance loss. The apex is the rock or gap that the front wheel must thread. The exit is the clear zone beyond the technical section. Vision lead is even more critical — looking at rocks causes hitting them.

### Berms
A berm provides a built-in late apex — the banked wall guides the bike through the second half of the corner naturally. Entry should be wide to maximise use of the berm's banking angle. The apex is at the base of the berm wall, in the rear half of the berm. Throttle can be applied earlier in a berm than on a flat corner because the banking provides lateral support. Treat the berm entry and exit as the critical phases — the berm itself does the apex work.

### Off-Camber
Off-camber corners tilt the surface away from the turn, eliminating the natural lateral support. The correct apex on an off-camber corner is often higher on the slope (closer to the uphill edge) to gain more level ground and reduce the effective lean angle. Entry speed must be reduced. The risk of the front washing out on an off-camber apex is significantly higher than on a flat or bermed corner — the late apex principle is even more important here, as an early apex on an off-camber corner risks running to the low side of the slope on exit.

## 9. EXPERT INSIGHTS & SOURCES

### Key Insights
- **Taddy Blazusiak** (six-time SuperEnduro World Champion): Describes cornering as "always solving the exit" — the entry is just preparation for the exit, and stage time is built by driving hard off every corner, which requires a correct apex to be possible.
- **Graham Jarvis** (multiple Erzbergrodeo and Red Bull Romaniacs winner): Known for reading grip and selecting non-geometric apexes in extreme conditions — in hard enduro, the apex is entirely grip-driven, and Jarvis's ability to find traction where others cannot is a direct application of grip-based apex selection.
- **Road racing application (Keith Code, "A Twist of the Wrist II"):** The late apex principle is documented in road racing coaching as producing both faster exit speeds and greater safety margin on corner exit. While the off-road application differs in grip management, the geometric principle is identical and well-documented.
- **Enduro coaching principle:** The most common single error in club-level enduro riders observed by professional coaches is the early apex — riders turn in too early on nearly every corner type. Correcting this single error produces measurable improvement in stage times without any other technique change.
- **Vision research in motorsport:** Studies of expert versus novice motorcycle riders consistently show that experts fixate on further-ahead points — the apex before entry, the exit before the apex. Novices fixate on the immediate terrain in front of the wheel. This is the primary cognitive difference between fast and slow corners.
