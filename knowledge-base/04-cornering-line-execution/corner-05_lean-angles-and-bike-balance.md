# Lean Angles and Bike Balance

## 1. OVERVIEW

Lean angle is the physical mechanism through which a motorcycle generates cornering force. When a bike leans, gravity and centripetal acceleration combine to keep the rider and bike in a circular arc — and the lean angle is the angle at which those two forces are balanced. The relationship is precise: for a given speed and corner radius, there is a required lean angle. Exceeding the tyre's grip at that lean angle causes the bike to slide or crash. The goal of every cornering technique in this domain — body position, late apex, trail braking — is to manage lean angle: either reducing the lean angle required (through body positioning and late apex) or expanding the lean angle available (through berm banking or tyre selection). Understanding lean angle at a mechanical level gives riders a framework for making decisions about speed, line, and technique that goes beyond instinct and allows deliberate, calibrated cornering across all terrain types.

## 2. CORE PRINCIPLES

### Principle 1: Lean Angle Is Determined by Speed and Radius
The fundamental lean angle equation is: tan(lean angle) = v² / (r × g), where v is speed, r is the corner radius, and g is gravitational acceleration. This means lean angle increases with the square of speed — doubling your speed through a corner requires four times the cornering force, and therefore a dramatically steeper lean angle. It also means that tighter radius corners require more lean at the same speed. Riders do not need to calculate this — but understanding the relationship makes it clear why carrying extra speed into a corner demands significantly more traction, and why tighter corners are disproportionately harder than they appear.

### Principle 2: Lean Angle and Traction Are Directly Linked
At zero lean angle (straight line), the tyre's full contact patch is on the ground — maximum grip available. As lean angle increases, the tyre rolls onto its shoulder — a smaller area, often a different rubber compound, and a different profile. At the extreme of lean angle, the tyre is operating on its very edge and the available grip is at its minimum. The traction required for cornering is highest at the maximum lean angle — precisely when grip availability is lowest. This intersection of high demand and low supply defines the lean angle limit. Riders who understand this relationship approach their lean angle limit with deliberate care rather than surprise.

### Principle 3: Body Inside Technique Reduces Required Lean Angle
The combined centre of mass (bike plus rider) is what determines the lean angle required for a given speed and radius. If the rider shifts their body mass toward the inside of the corner — maintaining an upright body while the bike leans beneath — the combined CoM shifts inward. A lower, more inside combined CoM requires less lean angle from the bike to achieve the same cornering radius at the same speed. This is the mechanism behind the off-road body position technique in CORNER-02. Every degree of body separation from the bike's lean angle is a degree of bike lean angle saved — and a degree of traction margin preserved.

### Principle 4: Berms Change the Lean Angle Equation
A berm — a banked corner wall — introduces a third force into the lean angle equation: the lateral reaction force from the berm surface itself. On a flat corner, gravity and centripetal acceleration must be balanced purely by lean angle. On a banked corner, the bank contributes a lateral force that partially replaces lean angle. The result is that a bermed corner allows significantly higher speed for the same lean angle, or equivalently, the same speed at significantly less lean angle. This is why riders accelerate aggressively into and through berms — the berm effectively increases the traction budget available. The physics of berms are the same as banked roads and velodrome tracks: banking replaces lean.

### Principle 5: Off-Camber Corners Reduce Available Lean Angle
Off-camber corners slope the surface away from the turn, creating a geometry where the bike must lean further to maintain the same cornering radius on the same speed. The banked surface effectively reduces the traction force available by tilting the normal force away from the ideal direction. The practical result: off-camber corners require more lean angle for the same speed and radius as a flat corner, on a surface that may also provide less grip because it is sloped. The double effect — more lean required, less grip available — is why off-camber corners are among the most challenging in enduro. The correct response is dramatically reduced entry speed and maximised body uprightness.

### Principle 6: The Feeling of the Traction Limit
Experienced riders develop a tactile awareness of the lean angle traction limit — a subtle feedback from the tyre through the chassis, pegs, and bars before full grip loss occurs. This is described as a "vagueness" in the front end, a slight "float" in the steering, or a "squirm" from the rear tyre. These signals precede full grip loss by a small margin — small enough that snap throttle or sudden steering input at this point will exceed the limit, but large enough that smooth, settled riding can continue. Developing this feel requires deliberate exposure to near-limit cornering in controlled environments, not avoidance. Riders who never approach the limit have no calibration for it and crash unpredictably when they accidentally reach it.

## 3. TECHNIQUE BREAKDOWN BY LEVEL

### 3a. Beginner Level
**Goal:** Not the primary focus at beginner level — beginners should focus on CORNER-01 and CORNER-02. However, beginners benefit from understanding the basics of why lean angle matters before approaching intermediate technique.

**Key Focus Areas for Awareness:**
- Understand that the bike must lean to corner — this is not adjustable
- Understand that body position can reduce how much lean the bike needs
- Understand that speed directly determines lean angle — more speed equals more lean
- Understand that some surfaces (berms, hard pack) allow more lean than others (mud, loose, off-camber)

**What Good Looks Like at Awareness Level:**
The rider makes speed adjustments before corners with some understanding of why — "this is tight so I need less speed." The rider stays more upright than the bike through corners even if imperfectly — beginning to separate body lean from bike lean. On a berm, the rider carries more speed than on a flat corner with some intuitive sense that the berm allows it.

**Common Mistakes at Awareness Level:**
- **Carrying the same speed through all corner types:** Beginner does not differentiate lean angle demand. → Point out after the ride: "that tight corner at the same speed meant a much steeper lean — that's why it felt sketchy."
- **Matching bike lean (road habit):** Rider leans with the bike regardless of surface. → Introduce the body-inside principle from CORNER-02 with the lean angle reduction explanation.

### 3b. Intermediate Level
**Goal:** Consciously manage lean angle through speed and body position — understanding where the limit is and using body technique to create a margin.

**Progression Markers from Awareness:** Rider understands the lean-speed relationship. Body position separates rider lean from bike lean on standard corners. Entry speed adjustments are conscious and deliberate.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Calibrating entry speed to the corner type: tight corners at lower speed, sweepers at higher, berms higher still
- Maximising body uprightness to preserve lean angle margin on loose and off-camber corners
- Beginning to feel the traction limit — identifying the sensation of the tyre approaching its limit
- Understanding the visual lean angle differential between correct technique and road-style lean (visible from camera footage)
- Adjusting for berm versus flat versus off-camber corners — different lean angle equations, different speed choices

**What Good Looks Like:**
The rider's speed varies meaningfully between corner types — a tight off-camber corner is approached significantly slower than a bermed sweeper. Body position creates a visible lean differential on flat corners. When shown camera footage, the rider can identify correct versus incorrect lean differential and articulate why. The rider describes the feeling of approaching the limit and can slow down before reaching it rather than after.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Over-slowing on bermed corners:** Rider carries too much caution into berms because they do not trust the banking. → Progressive berm speed-building drill — experience teaches trust.
- **Under-slowing on off-camber corners:** Rider underestimates the lean demand on off-camber surfaces. → Walk the corner and physically see the surface slope before riding it.
- **No traction limit awareness:** Rider has not experienced near-limit cornering in a controlled context. → Dedicated drills on a soft surface (grass or sand) at moderate speed to explore and feel the front and rear limit.

### 3c. Advanced Level
**Goal:** Operate confidently at or near the lean angle traction limit across all terrain types — using speed, body position, berm exploitation, and tyre selection as precision tools.

**Progression Markers from Intermediate:** Speed selection is automatic and appropriate. Lean differential is consistent. Traction limit feel is reliable. Camera footage shows consistent correct technique.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Operating consistently at 85-90% of lean angle traction limit — not 60-70% (leaving time on the table) and not 100% (no margin for error)
- Real-time lean angle adjustment based on surface grip changes mid-corner — stepping down lean if grip deteriorates
- Exploiting berms maximally — driving into the berm wall at high speed, using the banking to maintain minimum lean at maximum speed
- Managing lean angle through corner shape changes (opening radius versus tightening radius) — as the radius changes, lean angle demand changes, requiring immediate speed or body position adjustment
- Tyre selection for lean angle performance — understanding how enduro tyre profile affects lean angle grip

**What Good Looks Like:**
At race speed through a familiar section, the rider's line through corners is consistent and the lean differential is visible. Through a berm, the speed is noticeably higher than equivalent flat corners. On off-camber sections, the speed is noticeably lower but the body is maximally upright. If a grip change occurs mid-corner (a mud patch, a wet root), the rider's response is proportionate and smooth — not a panic reaction. Lap times improve because more of the available lean angle budget is being used, not because more traction was available.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Complacency on familiar sections:** On well-known terrain, riders sometimes carry excess speed without sufficient lean angle margin because they "know" the corner. → A corner changes with weather, traffic, and wear — always maintain a margin.
- **Under-using berms:** Treating bermed corners the same as flat corners — not taking advantage of the banking. → Berms are traction gifts — use them aggressively.
- **Inconsistent lean angle after a crash:** After a lean-angle crash, riders often overcorrect and leave too much margin. → Systematic rebuilding with filmed sessions to restore calibration.

## 4. DIAGNOSTIC CUES (AI COACHING ENGINE REFERENCE)

### Visual Cues — Correct Technique
- From a side or rear camera: bike lean angle is measurably greater than rider torso lean angle — a consistent differential across all corners
- On bermed corners: high corner speed with low apparent lean angle — the berm banking is visible and the rider is using it
- On off-camber corners: visibly reduced speed and maximally upright rider body — safety margin is visible in both speed and position
- Rider's outside peg is loaded, creating the lean differential — outside leg compressed slightly relative to inside leg
- No sudden direction changes mid-corner — lean angle is managed smoothly, not adjusted in steps

### Visual Cues — Error Indicators
- Rider and bike lean at identical angles — road-style lean, no lean differential, maximum lean angle required
- Bike runs wide on exit — entry speed too high for the available lean angle and surface grip combination
- Front wheel wash mid-corner — lean angle demand exceeded front tyre grip capacity (speed too high, grip too low, or lean too great)
- Rear wheel step-out at lean angle — traction budget exceeded at the lean angle present (throttle input, bump, or grip loss)
- Rider leans aggressively into a berm but carries low speed — not exploiting the berm's banking advantage
- Rider matches speed on off-camber to flat corners — not adjusting for the reduced lean angle availability

### Audio Cues
- Front tyre scrub sound at lean angle: tyre approaching the lateral limit — lean angle near maximum for the surface
- Rear tyre slide sound mid-corner: traction budget exceeded at lean angle — either too much lean, too much throttle, or surface loss
- Smooth, consistent cornering with no scrub or slide sound: lean angle within the traction budget throughout

### Sensor Cues
- IMU lean angle: peak lean angle should be measurably lower than would be required if the rider leaned with the bike — confirms the body-inside technique is reducing effective lean
- IMU lean angle vs GPS speed: plot should show that as lean angle increases, speed is consistent or reducing — if lean increases and speed also increases (mid-corner acceleration at lean), traction budget is being stressed
- Accelerometer lateral G: in a bermed corner, lateral G should be higher than equivalent-lean-angle flat corners — the berm is generating additional cornering force
- GPS trace: corners on off-camber terrain should show lower approach speeds than equivalent radius corners on flat terrain — confirming the rider is adjusting for the reduced lean angle budget

## 5. COACHING CUES & LANGUAGE

### Positive Reinforcement
- "You slowed right down for that off-camber section and stayed upright. That's exactly the right call — the surface can't handle the lean, so you gave yourself a margin."
- "Perfect berm technique — you trusted the bank and carried the speed. That's how berms should be used. You were three bike lengths faster through that section."
- "I can see the lean differential clearly on the footage — your body is upright and the bike is doing the leaning. You've got traction margin in the bank."

### Corrective Feedback
- "You're matching the bike's lean angle — that means you're demanding maximum lean from the bike when you don't need to. Push the outside peg, stay upright, and give yourself some traction margin."
- "That off-camber corner — you went in at the same speed as the flat section before it. Off-camber means the surface is working against you. Half the speed, twice the attention."
- "You're leaving the berm unused. You slowed down and rode through it like a flat corner. The berm is giving you grip — take the higher speed. Trust the wall."
- "You didn't feel the front starting to push before it went — you need to be feeling for that. Next session, explore the limit deliberately on a soft surface at low speed. Build the feeling."

### Progression Prompts
- "On the next run, I want you to identify the three cornering types on this section — flat, bermed, off-camber — and adjust your speed for each one deliberately. Talk through it before you go."
- "Pick a bermed corner and progressively add 5 km/h per pass until you feel the limit. That's your berm speed calibration. Do the same on a flat corner — feel the difference."
- "Try this: on any corner where you felt uncertain, stop and look at the surface. Ask yourself: is it flat, bermed, or off-camber? What does that mean for lean angle? Then ride it again with that knowledge."

## 6. DRILLS & PRACTICE EXERCISES

### Drill 1: Berm vs Flat Corner Speed Comparison
**Purpose:** Demonstrate the lean angle advantage of berms through direct speed comparison — builds trust in bermed corners.
**Setup:** Two corners of similar radius — one flat, one bermed. Ideally at the same location or known practice track.
**Execution:**
1. Establish a comfortable corner speed on the flat corner — note the speed at which the corner feels at the limit of comfort.
2. Ride the bermed corner at the same speed. Note how much easier it feels.
3. Increase speed on the bermed corner in 5 km/h increments until the limit is felt.
4. Compare the two limit speeds — the berm should accommodate significantly more.
5. Discuss: why does the berm allow more speed? What does that tell you about where traction comes from?
**Success Criteria:** Rider can consistently carry 20-30% more speed through the bermed corner than the flat corner of similar radius. Can articulate the banking mechanism in simple terms.
**Duration/Reps:** 30 minutes including multiple comparison passes.
**Progression:** Add throttle application in the berm to understand how early the throttle can open versus the flat corner.

### Drill 2: Off-Camber Speed Reduction Drill
**Purpose:** Build calibration for the increased lean angle demand on off-camber surfaces — eliminate the tendency to carry flat-corner speed into off-camber.
**Setup:** An off-camber corner on a practice trail. The surface should tilt away from the turn by at least 5-10 degrees.
**Execution:**
1. Approach at normal flat-corner speed and ride the off-camber corner. Note whether it feels difficult, sketchy, or if any grip loss occurs.
2. Reduce entry speed by 20% and ride the corner again. Note the improvement in confidence and control.
3. Reduce again by another 10%. Find the speed at which the corner feels controlled and sustainable.
4. Set that as the working speed for this corner type and practise at it five times.
**Success Criteria:** Rider identifies and commits to a controlled working speed for the off-camber corner. No grip events at the working speed. Rider can articulate why off-camber requires less speed.
**Duration/Reps:** 20 minutes.
**Progression:** Practise body uprightness maximisation at the working speed. Then attempt to recover 5-10% speed by improving body position rather than simply accepting the reduced speed.

### Drill 3: Lean Angle Limit Exploration — Soft Surface
**Purpose:** Introduce the rider to the feel of the traction limit in a low-consequence environment.
**Setup:** A large, flat, soft surface — grass, sand, or a muddy field. Open enough to corner freely. No hazards.
**Execution:**
1. Ride gentle, wide circles at low speed. Gradually tighten the radius and increase speed.
2. Focus entirely on feeling the tyres. Look for: front "float" or vagueness in steering, rear squirm or slight lateral movement.
3. When a subtle signal is felt, maintain that state for 2-3 seconds — do not immediately reduce speed. Learn the feeling.
4. Gradually reduce speed and note when the signals disappear.
5. Repeat with slightly higher speed. The goal is repeated exposure to the limit, not exceeding it.
**Success Criteria:** Rider can describe the specific sensation they feel as the traction limit is approached. Can identify the front and rear limit signals separately. Can maintain near-limit cornering for 2-3 seconds without crashing.
**Duration/Reps:** 30 minutes.
**Progression:** Transfer the calibration to a harder surface. Apply the same awareness in practice trail corners.

### Drill 4: Lean Differential Assessment with Video
**Purpose:** Give the rider objective visual feedback on their lean angle differential — correcting the common gap between "what I think I'm doing" and "what I'm actually doing."
**Setup:** A medium-radius corner on a practice trail. A fixed camera positioned to film from behind or slightly to the side.
**Execution:**
1. Rider makes five passes at comfortable pace.
2. Review footage immediately: measure the visual lean differential — is the rider clearly more upright than the bike?
3. If the differential is small or absent, the rider is matching the bike's lean. Instruct: "push outside peg harder, let the bike fall away."
4. Make another five passes with this specific cue.
5. Review footage again and compare the two sets.
**Success Criteria:** Second set of passes shows clearly measurable lean differential compared to the first set. Rider agrees the second set looks and feels different. Lean angle from bike chassis is greater than rider torso lean angle.
**Duration/Reps:** 30 minutes including filming and review.
**Progression:** Repeat on an off-camber corner, where the lean differential is even more critical. Then on a bermed corner to see how the berm changes the visual picture.

## 8. TERRAIN & CONTEXT VARIATIONS

### Hard Pack / Dry Dirt
Maximum lean angle is available — the tyre can be pushed to its full profile edge. Body position provides meaningful traction margin but the limit is high enough that intermediate riders rarely approach it at moderate pace. This is the ideal surface for exploring lean angle capability and building feel for the limit. Tyre profile matters most here — a round-profile tyre has more usable lean angle than a square-profile tyre.

### Loose over Hard
Lean angle limit is significantly lower than hard pack — the loose layer can shift under the tyre, causing sudden grip loss. Body uprightness must increase proportionally to maintain equivalent traction margin. Approach speeds must be lower. The lean angle limit in loose over hard is unpredictable and can change within a corner — a patch of loose material at the apex can halve the available grip instantly. Building lean angle awareness on this surface is critical for enduro racing.

### Mud
Lean angle available in mud varies with mud depth and type. Shallow mud over hard base can be nearly as grippy as hard pack at lean. Deep mud or slick clay provides very little lean angle grip — the tyre floats on the surface rather than cutting through. In mud, the lean angle limit is difficult to feel in advance — grip loss can be sudden. Minimum lean angle strategy (slower entry, body maximally upright) is the standard approach.

### Berms
As covered in the principles section: berms expand the effective lean angle budget by providing a lateral reaction force from the banking. On a perfect berm, riders can operate at very high lean angles because the banking surface is providing a significant proportion of the cornering force. The limit in a berm is the point where the berm surface itself loses grip — usually at the top of the berm or on a poorly-formed berm that does not have enough bank angle.

### Off-Camber
Off-camber corners represent the most challenging lean angle environment. The effective lean angle available is reduced by the surface tilt — on a 15-degree off-camber surface, the rider effectively loses 15 degrees of lean angle budget compared to a flat corner. This means a corner that would be comfortable at 40 km/h on a flat surface may only be safe at 25 km/h on a 15-degree off-camber. The visual trick of off-camber corners is that they often do not look as steep as they are from the approach — the tilt is only visible when the corner is entered.

### Sandy Terrain
Sand provides low, soft lean angle limits — the tyre sinks into the sand rather than getting a firm lateral grip surface. Corner lean angles in sand are typically very low — riders corner at what would be trivially small lean angles on hard pack. The correct approach to sand corners is wide, slow, and upright. Any lean angle event in sand (rear slide or front wash) tends to be progressive — the sand gives away slowly rather than suddenly — which provides more recovery time than hard-surface events.

## 9. EXPERT INSIGHTS & SOURCES

### Key Insights
- **Marc Coma** (four-time Dakar Rally winner, enduro and rally background): Discusses lean angle management in desert and mixed terrain as a calculated risk management problem — "you carry exactly the lean angle the surface allows, not the lean the corner demands." This principle of matching lean angle supply to demand rather than demand to supply is the core of advanced lean angle management.
- **Tyre engineering perspective (Pirelli and Michelin enduro tyre development teams):** Enduro tyres are designed with specific shoulder profiles that attempt to balance lean angle grip (for cornering) with straight-line traction (for drive). The round profile favours lean angle range; the square profile favours straight-line traction. Intermediate enduro riders benefit from understanding which design their chosen tyre prioritises.
- **Physics confirmation:** The lean angle formula (tan θ = v²/rg) is confirmed by every motorcycle dynamics textbook. Its practical implication — speed matters more than radius at moderate lean — is often counterintuitive for riders. Knowing the formula is not necessary; understanding that "twice the speed means four times the cornering force demand" is.
- **Off-camber research:** Trail and enduro track design deliberately uses off-camber corners to create challenge and require skill differentiation. Elite riders identify off-camber corners from the approach and adjust speed before the corner, not during it. This early recognition and adjustment is the primary technical differentiator on off-camber terrain.
- **Berm exploitation as a separating skill:** Race data from enduro events consistently shows that riders who exploit berms fully are fastest through bermed sections by margins disproportionate to their absolute speed advantage. The berm section is a multiplier — good technique produces much more gain than modest technique.
