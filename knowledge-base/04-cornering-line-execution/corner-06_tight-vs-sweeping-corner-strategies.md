# Tight vs Sweeping Corner Strategies

## 1. OVERVIEW

Not all corners are the same — and riding them all with the same strategy is one of the most common efficiency losses in enduro. Tight corners (hairpins, switchbacks, and sharp slow-speed turns) require a completely different approach from sweeping corners (long-arc, high-speed bends). In tight corners, the goal is rotation — completing the direction change quickly and efficiently, minimising the time spent at slow speed, and exiting with forward momentum. In sweeping corners, the goal is speed — maintaining maximum velocity through the arc, protecting exit drive, and generating time advantage on the linking straight. The techniques used in each type diverge significantly: tight corners use rear brake for rotation, foot-out body control, slow-speed balance, and deliberate momentum management; sweeping corners use late apex to maximum effect, body position for lean angle management, and sustained throttle from the apex. This topic covers the strategic and technical differences between the two corner types and how to recognise and respond to each.

## 2. CORE PRINCIPLES

### Principle 1: Tight Corners Are About Rotation, Not Speed
In a tight corner — anything from a 90-degree hairpin to a full switchback — there is no way to maintain significant speed through the arc. The turning radius is too small. The correct goal is to complete the direction change as efficiently as possible, spending minimum time at low speed, and exiting with as much forward momentum as the corner allows. This means the correct entry into a tight corner is slow — much slower than instinct suggests — so that the exit can be handled with early throttle and forward drive. Riders who enter tight corners too fast spend a long time navigating the apex, cannot get on the throttle, and exit slowly. Riders who enter correctly (slower) rotate quickly through the apex and exit fast.

### Principle 2: Rear Brake Is the Rotation Tool in Tight Corners
In tight corners, the rear brake is used simultaneously with partial throttle — a technique that loads the rear tyre, shortens the wheelbase dynamics, and tightens the turning radius. This is not trail braking in the CORNER-04 sense — it is a specific technique where the engine is providing forward drive while the rear brake is absorbing that drive, creating a stable, loaded, slow-speed rotation. The effect is a controlled, tight arc at low speed that the bike could not achieve on throttle alone. This technique is also called the "brakestand turn" or "pivot technique" in some coaching contexts. It requires a significant amount of clutch and throttle feel to avoid stalling or spinning the rear excessively.

### Principle 3: Foot-Out Technique Provides Low-Speed Balance
In very tight, very slow corners — switchbacks and hairpins — the inside foot leaves the peg and is held ready to touch the ground. At the apex, the foot may drag lightly on the ground, providing a stability reference and emergency support. This is not a failure of balance — it is a deliberate technique that allows the rider to maintain a tighter, slower arc than would otherwise be possible. The foot does not carry weight except in a momentary dab — the bike is still balanced and controlled. The foot is a safety net that enables commitment to a tighter, slower line.

### Principle 4: Sweeping Corners Are Managed for Maximum Exit Speed
In a sweeping corner, the entry speed is high, the arc lasts several seconds, and the exit feeds onto a significant straight or linking section. Every technique in CORNER-01 through CORNER-05 is most relevant here: late apex maximises exit speed; body position manages lean angle; trail braking sets up the entry; throttle timing at the apex drives the exit. The sweeping corner is where the full suite of cornering skills integrates. The primary error in sweepers is an early apex — which forces the rider wide on exit, costs exit drive, and sometimes causes running off the trail edge. Late apex in a sweeper is even more important than in a tight corner because the exit consequences of an early apex are more severe at high speed.

### Principle 5: Surface Type Changes the Strategy Within Each Corner Type
A tight corner on hard pack versus loose surface requires completely different rear brake application — on hard pack, the rear brake creates a clean pivot effect; on loose, the rear can spin and dig, requiring more modulation. A sweeping corner in mud versus hard pack requires completely different entry speeds and body uprightness. The corner type (tight vs sweeping) determines the strategy; the surface determines the calibration of that strategy. Riders who apply tight-corner technique correctly on hard pack and then apply the same unmodified technique in mud will find the rear brake doing more harm than good on the loose surface.

### Principle 6: Linked Corners — The Exit of One Sets Up the Entry of the Next
In enduro, corners rarely occur in isolation — they chain together in sequences. The exit of the first corner is simultaneously the approach for the second corner. In a sequence, the most important corner is the last one before a long straight — because exit speed from that corner multiplies onto the straight. Working backwards through a sequence: the last corner's exit is most important, so the second-to-last corner's exit must set up the last corner's entry correctly. This forces riders to plan backwards through corner sequences rather than forward — "what do I need at the exit of the last corner?" determines how all previous corners in the sequence should be ridden.

## 3. TECHNIQUE BREAKDOWN BY LEVEL

### 3a. Beginner Level
**Goal:** Recognise the two corner types and begin applying the basic correct strategy for each — slower entry in tight corners, higher speed through sweepers.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Identify a corner as tight or sweeping before riding it — not during
- Tight corners: slow the entry speed more than feels comfortable; exit is the priority
- Sweeping corners: apply late apex from CORNER-01; protect the exit speed
- Avoid using the same entry speed for both corner types

**What Good Looks Like:**
On a tight hairpin, the rider visibly slows significantly before the apex — the entry looks slow. But the exit is clean and the throttle opens early. On a sweeping corner, entry speed is maintained and the apex is in the second half of the corner. The rider makes a visible distinction between the two corner types in their approach and technique.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Same speed for all corners:** Beginner approaches a tight switchback at sweeper speed. Why it happens: no distinction between corner types made. → Walk the section first and identify: is this tight or sweeping? Adjust speed accordingly.
- **Trying to carry speed through a tight corner:** Rider resists slowing for a hairpin, resulting in a wide, slow, uncomfortable exit. → Accept the slow entry. The exit speed is the goal, not the entry speed.
- **Overthinking the sweeper:** Rider slows excessively for a sweeper because "corners are dangerous." → Sweepers reward speed and late apex. Slow entries in sweepers waste exit opportunity.
- **Inside foot stays on peg in tight slow corners:** Rider does not use foot-out technique in hairpins. → Allow the foot to come forward and down — it is an asset, not a sign of poor balance.

### 3b. Intermediate Level
**Goal:** Apply the rear brake rotation technique in tight corners and consistently execute late apex with maximum exit drive in sweepers.

**Progression Markers from Beginner:** Rider consistently adjusts entry speed for corner type. Tight corner exits are clean. Sweeping corners show visible late apex on camera review.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Rear brake plus throttle in tight corners: smooth clutch control preventing stall, rear brake preventing wheelie, resulting in tight controlled rotation
- Foot-out apex technique in switchbacks: inside foot held ready, touching or dragging at the apex, returning to peg at exit
- Sweeper late apex with full throttle drive at exit: connecting the late apex to maximum throttle from CORNER-03
- Recognising linked corner sequences and planning the exit of the last corner first
- Surface calibration: adjusting rear brake pressure for loose versus hard-pack tight corners

**What Good Looks Like:**
Through a tight hairpin, the rider enters at a noticeably low speed, uses rear brake with controlled throttle to rotate tightly around the apex, inside foot briefly touching the ground, and exits with early throttle — the exit speed is higher than expected given the entry speed. Through a sweeping section, the rider maintains high entry speed, apexes late, and drives hard off each exit. Before a corner sequence, the rider can identify and ride the most important corner (the last one before the straight) correctly even if it means compromising earlier corners in the sequence.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Rear brake causing rear spin in tight corners:** Too much rear brake relative to throttle — the rear spins rather than creating a controlled rotation. → Reduce rear brake pressure, increase throttle slightly. Find the balance where the rear drags but does not spin.
- **Stalling in tight corners:** Too much rear brake and insufficient throttle — engine stalls at the apex. → Clutch feathering is the solution — keep engine rpm up, clutch partially engaged.
- **Early apex in sweepers (under time pressure):** At race pace, reverting to early apex. → The early apex feels instinctively faster because the entry is smooth — resist it. Late apex produces more exit drive.
- **Not planning the corner sequence:** Rider optimises each corner individually without considering how they link. → Before riding a sequence, identify the "most important" corner and plan backwards.

### 3c. Advanced Level
**Goal:** Execute tight and sweeping corner techniques at race pace — automatically and consistently across all surface types and in corner sequences.

**Progression Markers from Intermediate:** Rear brake rotation is smooth and consistent. Sweeper late apex is automatic. Corner sequences are planned and ridden backwards.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Maximising pivot speed in tight corners — the entry may look slow but the apex rotation is fast
- Using throttle and rear brake simultaneously at the limit of rear tyre grip — controlled power oversteer through tight corners
- Pushing sweeper late apex to the extreme — entry speed very high, apex tight and late, exit width fully used
- Adapting tight corner technique for surface: loose tight corners require different throttle/brake balance than hard-pack
- Managing energy in tight corners during a long enduro stage — the rear brake rotation technique is physically demanding; pacing it
- Exploiting alternative lines in tight corners: inside rut, outside bermed section, foot-out dirt bank pivot

**What Good Looks Like:**
Through a switchback sequence, the rider appears to almost stop, pivots tightly around the apex with the inside foot skimming the ground, and accelerates cleanly out — the elapsed time in the switchback is minimised despite the low speed. Through a sweeper at race pace, the entry is fast, the apex is extremely late and tight, and the exit is full throttle from the apex point — the bike is pointed straight before the exit straight even begins. The rider can describe the strategy for each corner type without thinking about it.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Rear brake pivot on extremely loose terrain:** The pivot technique that works on hard pack can cause the rear to dig or wash on very loose surfaces. → Reduce rear brake, use more body weight rearward to anchor the rear.
- **Not using alternative lines in tight corners:** Defaulting to the "correct" geometric apex in a tight corner when a rut line or dirt bank would be faster. → Survey the corner before each lap or stage and update the line plan.
- **Over-commitment to sequence plan:** If the sequence changes mid-ride (new rut, fallen obstacle), the pre-planned order may be wrong. → Plans are defaults, not rules — adapt if the terrain changes.

## 4. DIAGNOSTIC CUES (AI COACHING ENGINE REFERENCE)

### Visual Cues — Correct Technique
- Tight corners from side/overhead: entry speed is visibly low; inside foot is forward and close to the ground at the apex; the bike pivots tightly with lean angle changing rapidly through the arc; exit has clear forward momentum and early throttle visible
- Rear wheel light drag smoke or minimal roost at the apex of a tight corner: rear brake drag with throttle — correct rotation technique
- Sweeping corners from overhead: wide entry, late apex in the second half of the corner arc, wide exit with the bike pointing straight before the exit straight begins
- In corner sequences: rider's line on the last corner before the straight is cleanest and has the most exit drive — visible priority allocation
- Inside foot skimming or touching the ground at switchback apex: deliberate foot-out technique, not an uncontrolled dab

### Visual Cues — Error Indicators
- Tight corner: entry speed too high — rider runs wide through the apex, exits on the wrong line; exit speed is low despite high entry speed
- Tight corner: inside foot never leaves the peg at very slow-speed apex — rider is riding too stiff and not using available balance tools
- Rear wheel spinning with no forward drive in tight corners: too much rear brake and not enough throttle — engine is overcoming the brake but the rotation is ragged
- Sweeper: apex is in the first half of the corner arc — early apex visible; bike runs wide on exit
- Corner sequence: rider optimises the first corner and has poor line for the last corner before the straight — sequence not planned

### Audio Cues
- Rear brake drag sound with simultaneous engine note in tight corners: correct rotation technique — brake and throttle together
- Engine stall in tight corner: throttle-rear brake balance lost — insufficient engine management
- Smooth engine note build from the second half of a sweeping corner: correct late apex throttle timing
- Throttle snap sound at the first corner apex in a sequence followed by throttle-off for the next: rider not linking corners — treating each as isolated

### Sensor Cues
- GPS trace on tight corner: correct technique shows a very tight radius at the apex — a sharp, clean pivot shape. Poor technique shows a larger, slower arc.
- Rear brake pressure in tight corners: should show sustained moderate pressure simultaneously with throttle position — not fully off or fully on
- GPS speed trace in corner sequence: correct technique shows each corner's exit speed maintained or recovering quickly; the last corner before the straight has the highest exit speed
- IMU lean angle in tight corners: lean angle changes rapidly — from entry lean to apex lean and back — over a short time period. Sluggish lean change indicates slow, wide rotation.

## 5. COACHING CUES & LANGUAGE

### Positive Reinforcement
- "Perfect pivot — I could see the foot touch, the rear dragging, and then you were already on the throttle before I expected it. That's how you turn a hairpin into nothing."
- "That sweeper line was textbook — you went in deep, hit the apex late, and drove the whole exit. You had three bike lengths of width on exit. That's stage time right there."
- "You planned the sequence backwards — the last corner was your cleanest and you drove the straight perfectly. That's the thinking that wins races."

### Corrective Feedback
- "You carried too much speed into that hairpin. I know it feels faster — it isn't. Slow down at the entry, pivot fast at the apex, and you'll be faster out. Test it."
- "The rear is spinning rather than dragging. Too much brake, not enough throttle. You want the rear to load, not spin. Try this: more gas, less brake, until the drag is controlled."
- "Your foot stayed on the peg through that entire switchback. At that speed, the foot should be out and ready to touch. It's not a balance failure — it's a tool. Use it."
- "Early apex on that sweeper — you turned in at the geometric apex and ran out of room on the exit. Wait longer before turning in. The entry approach feels wrong but the exit proves the technique."
- "You're riding each corner in that sequence individually. The last corner before the straight should be your best corner. Work backwards — what does the entry of the last corner need? Set that up from the corner before it."

### Progression Prompts
- "Next run through the switchbacks, deliberately try to make the arc tighter than last time. Use the rear brake and throttle together. Find where the rotation gets too tight."
- "Pick the sweeper that feeds onto the longest straight. Ride that one perfectly — wide entry, late apex, full drive at exit. Let the other sweepers be warm-ups for that one."
- "Take this sequence and walk it backwards: start at the exit straight, work to the last corner, then the second-to-last, then the entry. Tell me what the priority order is, then ride it."

## 6. DRILLS & PRACTICE EXERCISES

### Drill 1: Hairpin Pivot Practice with Rear Brake
**Purpose:** Develop the rear-brake-plus-throttle rotation technique for tight corners from a controlled, low-speed starting point.
**Setup:** A flat, open area with a cone or natural feature as the pivot point. No trail navigation required — pure technique focus.
**Execution:**
1. Approach the cone at walking pace.
2. Apply rear brake and simultaneously keep the engine running with light throttle — the throttle prevents stalling; the rear brake prevents forward drive.
3. Turn the front wheel fully toward the cone and rotate around it — the rear wheel should drag, not spin.
4. Complete the 180-degree direction change while maintaining the brake-throttle balance.
5. Release the rear brake as the direction change completes and drive away.
6. Increase approach speed gradually — maintaining the same brake-throttle rotation.
**Success Criteria:** Rider can complete a clean 180-degree pivot around the cone without stalling or losing balance. The pivot radius should be less than one bike length. Rear wheel drags rather than spins.
**Duration/Reps:** 30 minutes. Minimum 20 successful pivots before introducing speed or terrain variation.
**Progression:** Move to a natural hairpin corner on a trail. Replicate the same brake-throttle balance. Then add the foot-out technique.

### Drill 2: Foot-Out Switchback Drill
**Purpose:** Make the foot-out balance technique habitual in very slow-speed tight corners.
**Setup:** A switchback on a practice trail or a tight cone corner in an open area — speed must be low enough that the foot can reach the ground comfortably.
**Execution:**
1. Approach at very low speed.
2. As the corner begins, move the inside foot forward off the peg and hold it 10-15cm from the ground.
3. At the apex, allow the foot to touch or drag lightly on the ground — do not support weight, just reference.
4. As the exit begins, return the foot to the peg and open throttle.
5. Repeat until the foot movement is automatic — not something to think about.
**Success Criteria:** Foot leaves the peg on every pass without conscious decision. Foot touches at the apex naturally. Return to peg is smooth and does not delay throttle opening. Rider describes the foot as an asset, not a crutch.
**Duration/Reps:** 20 minutes.
**Progression:** Increase the corner speed slightly. At some point the foot is no longer touching — this is correct. Find the speed threshold where foot-out transitions to foot-ready-but-not-touching.

### Drill 3: Sweeper Speed-Building Drill
**Purpose:** Build confidence and speed in sweeping corners through progressive, controlled speed increases with attention to late apex technique.
**Setup:** A sweeping corner with a known safe exit — must have run-off space in case of line error.
**Execution:**
1. Ride the corner at 60% pace with a deliberate late apex. Film or have observer confirm the apex position.
2. Increase to 70% pace — late apex maintained? Confirm.
3. Increase to 80% — note where the technique begins to require more focus.
4. Increase to 90% — does the late apex hold? Is there still exit width?
5. If exit width disappears at any speed, the apex has moved forward — identify why and correct.
**Success Criteria:** Rider can demonstrate late apex at 85% pace with measurable exit width remaining. Exit speed at 85% is faster than exit speed at 60% despite earlier impressions.
**Duration/Reps:** 40 minutes including review.
**Progression:** Apply the same drill to a sweeper that connects directly to a tight corner — the sweeper exit sets up the tight corner entry.

### Drill 4: Linked Corner Sequence Drill — Backwards Planning
**Purpose:** Train the habit of planning corner sequences from the last corner backwards — ensuring the correct priority allocation.
**Setup:** A sequence of three to four corners ending in a long straight. The corners should include at least one tight and one sweeping corner.
**Execution:**
1. Walk the sequence backwards: start at the exit straight, identify the last corner, determine what exit line is needed.
2. Work back: what entry does the last corner need? What exit from the second-to-last corner sets that up?
3. Continue backwards to the entry of the first corner in the sequence.
4. Write down or verbalise the priority order: which corner matters most, which can be compromised.
5. Ride the sequence with this plan. Debrief: did the last corner produce the expected exit drive?
**Success Criteria:** Rider can articulate the priority order of the sequence before riding. The last corner before the straight is the cleanest and produces the highest exit drive. Earlier corners are "good enough" — not perfect, but setting up the next correctly.
**Duration/Reps:** 30 minutes on one sequence.
**Progression:** Move to a longer sequence with five or more corners. Apply to a full stage section. Timer comparison: planned sequence versus intuitive approach.

## 8. TERRAIN & CONTEXT VARIATIONS

### Hard Pack / Dry Dirt — Tight Corners
The rear brake rotation technique works cleanly on hard pack. The rear tyre grips predictably and the drag effect creates a tight, controlled pivot. Entry speed can be slightly higher than on loose surfaces and the rotation still achieves a tight radius. The foot-out technique is less critical on hard pack because the bike is stable — it is a safety tool rather than a balance necessity.

### Loose over Hard — Tight Corners
The rear brake must be applied more gently on loose surfaces. Too much pressure causes the rear to spin and dig rather than drag and rotate. The technique requires more sensitivity — less brake, more throttle feel, slower rotation. The foot-out technique becomes more important on loose tight corners because balance at low speed over loose material is less predictable. Some riders add more weight to the rear by sitting or semi-sitting in very loose tight corners to prevent the rear from digging.

### Mud — Tight Corners
Mud tight corners are among the most demanding. The rear brake may lock the wheel in deep mud without producing a clean drag effect. Throttle must be used to keep the wheel turning. The pivot technique becomes more about momentum management than brake-and-throttle rotation. Inside foot almost always touches in mud tight corners — the surface is unpredictable and foot contact provides critical stability feedback.

### Hard Pack — Sweeping Corners
Classic sweeper technique applies. Late apex, high entry speed, maximum exit drive. The rider has full access to all lean angle available. Body position management is the differentiating technique. Tyre grip is predictable enough to push to 85-90% of the traction limit consistently.

### Loose over Hard — Sweeping Corners
Entry speed must be moderated — the loose surface reduces lean angle grip. Late apex is even more important because early apex on loose over hard means the bike is at peak lean angle over the loose surface at the apex — the highest-risk moment. Body uprightness is maximised. Exit throttle is applied more gently to avoid spinning the rear at lean angle. The sweeper technique still produces the most exit speed of any approach, but the absolute speed is lower than hard pack.

### Off-Camber Sweeping Corners
An off-camber sweeping corner is one of the most demanding combinations in enduro. The surface tilts away from the turn, the corner lasts several seconds, and the lean angle demand is increased by the camber. The correct response is: maximum body uprightness (reducing the bike's required lean angle), entry speed reduced significantly from flat-surface sweeper speed, and a very deliberate late apex to ensure the exit does not force the bike to the low side of the slope. Throttle at the apex is smooth and careful — any rear spin on an off-camber sweeper apex can send the bike downhill.

### Linked Tight-Sweeping Combinations
The most common enduro corner sequence involves a tight corner immediately followed by, or immediately preceding, a sweeping corner. The exit of the tight corner is the entry of the sweeper — meaning the tight corner exit must be positioned correctly for the sweeper entry. If the tight corner is ridden correctly (slow entry, fast pivot, early throttle), the exit position is usually wide — which is correct for the sweeper entry. If the tight corner is ridden incorrectly (carried speed in, slow rotation, late apex), the exit is often on the wrong line for the sweeper.

## 9. EXPERT INSIGHTS & SOURCES

### Key Insights
- **Taddy Blazusiak** (six-time SuperEnduro World Champion): SuperEnduro tracks are almost entirely tight corners — hairpins, switchbacks, and slow-speed technical turns. Blazusiak's technique is the definitive reference for tight corner rotation: slow entry, rear brake pivot, foot-out, immediate throttle at exit. His corner times in tight sections are measurably faster than competitors despite apparently lower entry speeds — the rotation technique converts slow entry into fast exit.
- **Antoine Meo** (three-time Enduro World Champion): Known for exceptional sweeping corner speed in outdoor enduro. His lines show consistent late apex in sweepers, with exit speed measurably higher than competitors who apex earlier. The late apex effect in sweepers is most visible in data from GPS tracking — Meo's trace shows the characteristic entry-wide, apex-late, exit-wide pattern across entire stages.
- **Rally Raid application:** In desert rally raid racing (Dakar, Africa Eco Race), sweeping corners are navigated at very high speed over loose surfaces. Co-drivers call the corner type and radius before the entry, allowing the rider or driver to select entry speed based on the corner classification. This systematic corner typing and speed selection is applicable in enduro race preparation — riders who pre-ride a stage classify each corner and set the strategy.
- **Tight corner coaching principle:** The universal coaching observation is that riders resist slowing for tight corners because "it feels slower." Timed comparison drills consistently show that the slow-entry-fast-exit approach is faster — by 0.5 to 2 seconds per corner on a typical enduro hairpin. Riders who accept this counterintuitive finding and apply it improve their tight corner performance more rapidly than riders who continue trying to carry speed.
- **Sequence planning research:** Rally and circuit racing strategy research confirms that corners feeding long straights are worth more optimization effort than corners between short sections. The principle that the last corner before the long straight is the most important corner in a sequence is well-established in racing strategy and applies directly to enduro section analysis.
