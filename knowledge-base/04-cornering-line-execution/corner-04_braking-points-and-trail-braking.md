# Braking Points and Trail Braking

## 1. OVERVIEW

Braking point selection and trail braking are the techniques that control corner entry — the first of the three phases from CORNER-01. The braking point is not just "where you start braking" — it is the result of calculating approach speed, sight distance, surface grip, corner radius, and what follows the corner. A correct braking point arrives at the entry point with exactly the speed required for the chosen apex. Trail braking extends this further: rather than completing all braking before turn-in, the rider maintains partial brake pressure past the turn-in point and progressively releases it through the early corner arc. In off-road riding, trail braking primarily means the rear brake — dragging the rear into the corner shifts weight forward, loads the front tyre for increased turning grip, and gives the rider precise speed control deep into the corner entry. Front trail braking is used selectively, only on high-grip surfaces where the risk of front-end loss is acceptable. This topic covers how to select braking points, how trail braking works, and why the progressive brake release is the core skill of the technique.

## 2. CORE PRINCIPLES

### Principle 1: The Braking Point Must Be a Physical Landmark
A braking point defined as a distance ("I brake 30 metres before the corner") fails under race conditions because distance perception degrades with speed, fatigue, and adrenaline. A braking point defined as a physical feature — a tree, a rock, a root crossing the trail, a change in surface colour — is reproducible under all conditions. Selecting a landmark before the run and committing to it removes the decision from the heat of the moment. The landmark does not need to be before the corner — it can be the corner entry itself, a trail marker, or a feature in the corner zone. The key is that it exists as a physical trigger, not a mental calculation.

### Principle 2: Braking Zone Length Is Set by Approach Speed and Surface
The braking zone is the distance required to reduce from approach speed to corner entry speed. This length is determined by: approach speed (higher speed = longer zone), surface grip (loose = longer zone), front-to-rear brake balance, and sight distance. Riders who do not know their braking zone length for common speeds are estimating — and estimation under pressure tends toward optimism, resulting in arriving hot at the corner. Knowing the braking zone length allows the braking landmark to be selected accurately. This is a practised skill, not an innate ability — it develops through deliberate repeated braking at measured distances.

### Principle 3: Trail Braking Loads the Front for Turn-In Grip
The central mechanism of trail braking is weight transfer. When braking, weight shifts forward, loading the front tyre. If all braking is released at turn-in, that forward weight load is also released — the front tyre unloads at exactly the moment it needs maximum grip for turning. Trail braking maintains partial brake pressure (and therefore partial forward weight) past the turn-in point. The front tyre enters the corner already loaded, already generating grip, and the turn-in response is sharper and more precise. This is why riders who trail brake can turn in harder and later than those who do not — the front is working, not unloaded.

### Principle 4: Off-Road Trail Braking Is Primarily the Rear Brake
In road racing, trail braking uses the front brake — the front is the primary braking tool on tarmac, and the loss is recoverable with skill. In off-road riding, front brake loss is rarely recoverable — a front wash at lean angle and speed usually results in a crash. The rear brake is therefore the primary trail braking tool in enduro. Dragging the rear brake into the corner loads the front (through chassis geometry — rear brake application pitches the bike forward), maintains speed control, and tightens the turning radius by loading the rear tyre and adding a slight rear-wheel drag effect. If the rear slides, it can be caught. If the front washes, it usually cannot be.

### Principle 5: The Progressive Release Is the Skill
Trail braking is not simply "leave the brake on in the corner." The brake pressure must decrease progressively as lean angle increases — because the combined slip capacity of the tyre cannot accommodate both high braking force and high cornering force simultaneously. As the bike leans into the corner, braking force must reduce precisely in proportion to the increasing cornering demand. The rider who learns to feel this trade-off — releasing brake in direct proportion to lean angle — is performing trail braking correctly. The rider who holds constant brake pressure through increasing lean angle will exceed the traction budget and lose the rear.

### Principle 6: Common Error — Full Brake Release at Turn-In
The most common single error in corner entry is releasing all brake pressure precisely at turn-in. This is often taught as the "correct" approach and is safe — but it abandons the weight management benefit of trail braking. At the moment of release, the front tyre unloads, the bike's attitude changes, and the rider loses the weight transfer advantage for turn-in grip. The result is a slightly vague, slightly slower turn-in with reduced confidence in the front tyre. Many intermediate riders who feel their corners are "not sharp enough" are experiencing the consequence of this error. The fix is not to brake harder — it is to maintain lighter but consistent rear brake pressure past the turn-in point.

## 3. TECHNIQUE BREAKDOWN BY LEVEL

### 3a. Beginner Level
**Goal:** Not applicable — this topic is rated intermediate to advanced. Beginners should focus on CORNER-01 and completing all braking before turn-in as a foundation. This section provides context for the baseline all intermediate work builds from.

**Key Focus Areas for Baseline:**
- Complete all braking before the entry point — no braking mid-corner
- Select a physical landmark as the braking trigger
- Arrive at the entry point at the correct speed for the apex
- Understand that front brake is used primarily in the straight braking zone, not past turn-in

**What Good Looks Like at Baseline:**
The rider brakes firmly on the straight, completes the speed reduction before the entry point, and enters the corner with no brake pressure applied. The entry speed is correct — no running wide, no excessive caution. The braking landmark is used consistently on repeated passes. Brake pressure is not touched again until the exit straight.

**Common Mistakes at Baseline:**
- **No defined braking landmark:** Rider decides to brake at different points each pass. → Select a physical feature and commit to it before riding.
- **Carrying speed to the corner and braking through it:** Entry speed is too high, rider uses the corner to slow down. → Set the braking point earlier — a corner is not a braking zone.
- **Front brake past turn-in:** Rider applies front brake inside the corner. → Front brake only on the straight. Rear brake only, if anything, inside the corner.

### 3b. Intermediate Level
**Goal:** Develop consistent braking landmark selection and introduce rear brake trail braking — maintaining light rear brake pressure past the turn-in point and releasing it progressively through the early corner arc.

**Progression Markers from Baseline:** Rider consistently completes braking before turn-in. Braking landmark is used on every pass. Entry speed is predictable and correct.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Deliberately maintaining light rear brake pressure (10-20%) past the turn-in point
- Feeling the front tyre load under this trailing brake — the turn-in should feel sharper and more confident
- Releasing the rear brake progressively as lean angle increases in the early corner
- Completing the brake release before reaching the apex — no brake pressure at the apex
- Selecting more aggressive (later) braking landmarks as confidence and technique develop

**What Good Looks Like:**
The rider brakes firmly on the approach, transitions to a light rear-only trailer as they turn in, and the bike enters the corner with a slight forward pitch maintained — weight forward, front loaded. The turn-in is clean and confident. As the lean angle builds, the brake releases in proportion — finished entirely before the apex. Entry speed is slightly higher than the fully-pre-apex-released version but the corner is still controlled.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Holding too much rear brake through the corner:** Rear brake pressure stays at 30-40% through the apex. Why it happens: rider finds the drag sensation reassuring. → The brake must finish releasing before the apex — it is entry management, not mid-corner speed control.
- **Rear wheel lock-up at lean angle:** Too much rear brake applied at turn-in with lean increasing. Why it happens: brake pressure not reduced as lean increases. → Progressive release means reduce pressure continuously — not hold then release suddenly.
- **Not feeling the difference from baseline technique:** Trail braking seems to produce the same result as pre-releasing. → Have an observer or camera confirm the turn-in point — trail braking should allow a later, sharper turn-in.

### 3c. Advanced Level
**Goal:** Use trail braking as a precision entry management tool — selecting braking landmarks aggressively, modulating front and rear simultaneously on appropriate surfaces, and using the entry phase to optimise the entire corner.

**Progression Markers from Intermediate:** Rear trail braking is consistent and the brake releases completely before the apex. Turn-in is sharper than baseline technique. Entry speed is measurably higher.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Combining front trail brake (light) and rear trail brake on high-grip, hard-pack surfaces
- Adjusting braking landmark aggressively — pushing the braking zone later as track knowledge develops
- Using the trail brake to actively steer the entry — more rear brake tightens the radius, less opens it
- Managing the brake-to-throttle transition — no gap, no overlap: brake releases as throttle picks up at the apex
- Trail braking as a line correction tool — if the entry is slightly wrong, trail brake adjustment can recover the apex

**What Good Looks Like:**
The rider brakes very late — the braking landmark appears too close to the corner for most riders but the controlled deceleration and trail technique means the entry speed is correct. The front forks are compressed from combined braking and cornering load at turn-in — this is the correct chassis attitude. The brake releases smoothly as lean increases, finishing just before the apex. The transition from brake to throttle at the apex is seamless — no coasting phase between them. The exit is clean and aggressive.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Front trail brake on loose surface:** Using front brake past turn-in on loose over hard. Why it happens: technique from hard pack applied without surface adjustment. → Front trail brake only on known high-grip surfaces. Rear only on loose.
- **Brake-throttle overlap at apex:** Rider opens throttle before the brake is fully released — combined longitudinal forces exceed traction budget. → One ends, then the other begins. No simultaneous maximum application.
- **Over-reliance on trail brake to compensate for wrong entry speed:** Using trail brake to rescue too-hot entries rather than selecting a better landmark. → Trail brake is for marginal adjustments, not hot-entry recovery at speed.

## 4. DIAGNOSTIC CUES (AI COACHING ENGINE REFERENCE)

### Visual Cues — Correct Technique
- From a side or chase camera: the front forks remain slightly compressed as the bike turns in — a visible chassis squat at turn-in confirms forward weight transfer from trail braking
- Brake light (if visible): remains illuminated briefly past the turn-in point, then extinguishes progressively through the early corner arc
- Turn-in appears sharp and confident — the bike changes direction cleanly without a hesitation or "float" at the entry
- No rear wheel lock or skip at turn-in — progressive release is working
- Transition from braking to corner arc is smooth — no abrupt chassis attitude change at turn-in

### Visual Cues — Error Indicators
- Abrupt chassis pitch change at turn-in — forks extend suddenly as brake releases: brake released all at once at turn-in, losing forward weight load
- Brake light on deep into the corner arc (past the apex zone): too much brake maintained too long — trail braking becoming mid-corner braking
- Rear wheel hop or skip at turn-in: rear brake applied too aggressively at lean angle — lock-up risk
- Wide exit after apparently controlled entry: trail brake pressure not released before apex — residual braking force corrupted the apex phase
- Entry speed varies significantly between passes on the same corner: braking landmark not physical, rider estimating — inconsistent braking zones

### Audio Cues
- Rear tyre scrub sound at turn-in (controlled, brief): rear trail brake creating light drag — correct technique
- Rear tyre lock-up squeal or chatter at turn-in: too much rear brake at lean angle — immediate risk
- Abrupt engine note change at turn-in (from braking deceleration to engine idle): brake released suddenly — loss of weight management
- Smooth, quiet trail through the early arc followed by gradual engine note rise at apex: correct trail brake release transitioning to maintenance/throttle

### Sensor Cues
- Brake pressure sensor (rear): should show a high-pressure braking phase on the straight, transitioning to a lower but sustained pressure through turn-in, progressively releasing to zero before the apex — a ski-slope shape on the trace
- IMU front fork travel: should show extended compression at turn-in (weight forward from trail brake) compared to a full pre-release entry — the difference confirms trail braking is loading the front
- Lateral G-force vs brake pressure overlay: as lateral G increases (lean increasing), brake pressure should decrease in proportion — this inverse correlation is the signature of correct trail braking
- GPS speed trace at entry: trail braking should show a later, sharper deceleration peak closer to the corner compared to a fully pre-apex braking approach

## 5. COACHING CUES & LANGUAGE

### Positive Reinforcement
- "That turn-in was sharp — you kept the rear brake on just past the entry and the front loaded up perfectly. That's exactly what trail braking feels like."
- "Good braking landmark. Same point every time, same result every time. That's how you build consistency under pressure."
- "You released the brake smoothly as the lean came in. That's the progressive release working. The tyre had both jobs — cornering and braking — managed separately, not simultaneously."

### Corrective Feedback
- "You're releasing the brake right at turn-in — I can see the fork extend the moment you turn. Keep a light rear brake on, even as you start to lean. Release it slowly, not suddenly."
- "The rear hopped — you had too much rear brake at lean angle. You need to be releasing pressure as the lean increases, not holding it constant."
- "Your braking landmark changes every lap. Pick a physical thing — that root, that rock, that trail edge — and commit to it. Your entry speed varies because your trigger point varies."
- "You're braking too deep and using trail brake to save a hot entry. Trail brake should be a precision tool, not a rescue tool. Move the braking point earlier and use trail brake deliberately, not urgently."
- "No front brake past turn-in on this surface — it's loose over hard. If the front washes in a corner you don't get a second chance. Rear only inside the corner."

### Progression Prompts
- "Next run, I want you to hold 10% rear brake past the turn-in point. Not hard — just enough to feel it. Notice how the front tyre turns in sharper."
- "Pick a braking landmark that feels slightly too late. Ride to it five times. If the entries are controlled, that's your new landmark. Keep pushing it forward."
- "Try this sequence out loud: 'brake — turn — release — apex.' Each word is an action. The 'release' should take the whole early corner arc, not a single moment."

## 6. DRILLS & PRACTICE EXERCISES

### Drill 1: Braking Landmark Commitment Drill
**Purpose:** Break the habit of estimating braking points and replace it with a physical landmark that is used consistently.
**Setup:** A single corner with a clear, well-known approach. Choose a physical feature within the braking zone — a specific rock, root, tree, or trail marking.
**Execution:**
1. Walk the approach and select a braking landmark. Mark it mentally or physically (bright tape if possible).
2. Ride the approach at 70% pace and brake at the landmark — no earlier, no later.
3. Evaluate the entry: was the speed correct? Too fast? Too slow?
4. Adjust the landmark position one step earlier or later accordingly.
5. Once the correct landmark is found, repeat ten passes using only that landmark — do not adjust, just repeat.
**Success Criteria:** Entry speed is consistent on all ten passes. Rider can articulate exactly what the braking landmark is. Entry does not vary between passes.
**Duration/Reps:** 30 minutes on one corner.
**Progression:** Move the landmark progressively later (more aggressive) as confidence builds. Repeat on a different corner with a less obvious landmark.

### Drill 2: Progressive Brake Release Exercise
**Purpose:** Develop the physical habit of releasing the rear brake proportionally to lean angle increase — the core skill of trail braking.
**Setup:** A medium-radius corner on a practice loop. The rider should be able to focus entirely on brake pressure, not on navigation.
**Execution:**
1. Enter the corner with moderate speed and apply moderate rear brake on the straight.
2. At turn-in, do not release the brake — hold it at 50% pressure.
3. As the lean angle increases through the first half of the arc, consciously reduce brake pressure to match: at 25% lean, 40% brake; at 50% lean, 20% brake; at 75% lean, 5% brake.
4. Brake should be fully released before the apex.
5. Repeat until the progressive release feels smooth and natural — not stepped or sudden.
**Success Criteria:** Brake releases completely before the apex on every pass. No rear wheel hop or lock at any point in the arc. Rider can describe the feeling of the progressive release and distinguish it from a sudden release.
**Duration/Reps:** 20 minutes.
**Progression:** Increase turn-in speed. Then apply on a tighter-radius corner where the lean angle change is more rapid.

### Drill 3: Turn-In Sharpness Comparison
**Purpose:** Make the benefit of trail braking tangible — comparing turn-in quality with and without the technique.
**Setup:** A corner with a clear turn-in point. Film from a fixed camera if possible.
**Execution:**
1. Make five passes with all braking completed before turn-in (standard beginner technique). Film or have observer assess turn-in sharpness.
2. Make five passes with deliberate light rear trail brake maintained 2-3 metres past the turn-in point, then progressively released.
3. Compare: is the turn-in sharper? Does the front feel more planted? Is the entry line tighter?
4. Discuss or note the subjective feel difference.
**Success Criteria:** Rider can clearly feel and describe the difference between the two techniques. Trail braking version produces sharper, more confident turn-in in at least four of five passes.
**Duration/Reps:** 30 minutes including comparison and discussion.
**Progression:** Extend the trail braking distance past turn-in progressively — 3m, 5m, 8m. Find the limit where excess rear brake degrades the apex rather than improving the entry.

### Drill 4: Aggressive Braking Landmark Progression
**Purpose:** Build the ability to brake later without losing entry control — compressing the braking zone while maintaining correct entry speed.
**Setup:** A familiar corner with a measured approach. Place markers (cones or tape) at 5-metre intervals from 40 metres before the entry to 10 metres before the entry.
**Execution:**
1. Start braking at the 40-metre marker. Confirm entry speed is correct and controlled.
2. Move the braking point to 35 metres. Repeat five times and confirm control.
3. Move to 30 metres. Repeat.
4. Continue moving the braking point in until the entry becomes marginally too fast — that is the limit.
5. Set the race braking landmark 5 metres earlier than the limit for a safety margin.
**Success Criteria:** Rider identifies their actual braking zone limit (not their comfort limit) and sets a race braking point with a controlled margin. Entry speed is consistent at the race landmark.
**Duration/Reps:** 40 minutes.
**Progression:** Repeat on a wet or loose surface and compare the braking zone length difference. Apply the landmark method to a previously difficult corner.

## 8. TERRAIN & CONTEXT VARIATIONS

### Hard Pack / Dry Dirt
Optimal surface for practising trail braking — predictable grip allows both front and rear trail brake use. The front trail brake is most viable here: firm, predictable grip means the front wash risk is manageable. Rear trail brake is also effective. Braking zones are shorter than loose surfaces. This is the environment where the technique is drilled before being transferred to more challenging surfaces.

### Loose over Hard
Rear-only trail braking. Front brake use past turn-in is too risky — loose material over the hard base gives unpredictable front traction. The rear brake provides the weight management effect of trail braking while the rear wheel is more forgiving — a rear slide is manageable, a front wash is not. Braking zone is longer due to reduced grip. Braking landmarks must be set further back. Trail brake release must be slightly earlier in the arc than hard pack.

### Mud
Trail braking in mud is almost entirely rear-only and at very light pressure. The rear tyre may spin under trail brake in deep mud — a slight loss of braking effectiveness — but the weight management effect still applies. Braking zones are dramatically longer. Deep mud can cause the rear brake to lose feel — the brake pedal may feel the same but the actual deceleration varies. Conservative braking points are essential. Trail braking in mud is used more for directional control than speed management.

### Rocky / Technical
In rocky technical terrain at low speed, braking is more about momentum management than high-speed deceleration. The braking point in a technical section is the last stable surface before a feature — braking on rocks is unpredictable (the tyre can deflect off a rock under braking load). Trail braking is less useful here because corner radius is typically defined by obstacles, not line selection. The rear brake is used for momentum control and engine braking management, not classic trail braking.

### Sand
Sand cornering rarely involves aggressive trail braking — speeds are lower and the surface deformation under braking is unpredictable. Braking in sand requires longer zones than equivalent hard-pack speeds. Front brake in sand compresses the front into the sand, potentially causing the wheel to dig. Rear brake is preferred. "Trail braking" in sand is usually just maintaining rear brake engagement through the corner for directional stability rather than speed management — a different application of the same tool.

### Steep Downhill Approach
On a steep descent leading into a corner, the approach speed can build rapidly and the braking zone extends dramatically. Braking landmark selection is critical — underestimating the approach speed on a downhill leads to seriously hot entries. The rear brake is used extensively on downhill approaches to manage speed, but engine braking (lower gear) is combined with physical braking to avoid overheating the rear brake. Trail braking on a downhill entry must be calibrated to the increased speed and the weight-forward bias already present from the descent.

## 9. EXPERT INSIGHTS & SOURCES

### Key Insights
- **Valentino Rossi (MotoGP, now relevant via his off-road training foundation):** Widely cited as the master of trail braking in motorsport — his principle of "I slow down when others are already slowing down" applies directly: trail braking allows the same entry speed with more control, or higher entry speed with the same control. The principle translates from tarmac to off-road when adjusted for front/rear brake priorities.
- **Jimmy Lewis** (elite off-road coach): Teaches rear trail braking as a cornering control tool specifically in the enduro and adventure riding context — noting that the weight management effect is equally important as the speed management effect. The loaded front at turn-in is the reason the technique exists.
- **Enduro coaching observation:** Riders who are told to "complete braking before the corner" and follow the instruction precisely often have vague, slow turn-ins. Riders who learn trail braking report that their corners feel "clicked in" — the front tyre commits to the turn decisively. This subjective feel difference motivates intermediate riders to invest in the technique.
- **Front vs rear brake doctrine:** The off-road coaching community is consistent on this point — front trail brake is a tarmac technique that does not transfer safely to loose off-road surfaces. Every major off-road coaching school (Jimmy Lewis, Bret Tkacs, Nick Burson) teaches rear-only trail braking for enduro. Front trail brake is only introduced in controlled conditions on very high grip surfaces.
- **Braking landmark research:** Sports psychology research on performance under pressure confirms that physical landmark-based triggers outperform distance-based estimation for speed-based decisions. The same principle used in rally co-driving ("brake at the gate") applies to solo enduro braking points.
