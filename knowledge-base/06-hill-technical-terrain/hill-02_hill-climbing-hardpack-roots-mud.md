# Hill Climbing (Hard-Pack, Roots, Mud)

## 1. OVERVIEW

Hard-pack, root, and mud climbs present a fundamentally different challenge from loose surface climbing. Where loose terrain demands sustained smooth throttle and clutch feathering to stay in a traction window, these surfaces offer intermittent high grip (hard-pack rock, embedded roots) and intermittent low grip (wet roots, mud), often within the same climb. The rider must read each section in advance, apply power when traction is available, back off when it is not, and manage obstacles mid-climb without losing the momentum the climb demands. Gear selection — chosen before entry — determines whether the engine has enough torque to pull through the hardest point, and the wrong gear choice on these surfaces cannot be corrected mid-climb under load. The rhythm of root climbing, in particular, is a distinct skill that separates riders who consistently clean technical climbs from those who stall or wash the front mid-section.

## 2. CORE PRINCIPLES

### Principle 1: Gear Selection Determines the Outcome Before the Climb Starts
On hard-pack and mixed climbs, the gear determines both the available torque and the throttle sensitivity. Too low a gear means excessive sensitivity — a small throttle increase causes a large power spike that spins the rear on roots or wet patches. Too high a gear means the engine is outside its powerband at the hardest section, bogs, and stalls. The correct gear provides smooth, linear power in the engine's torque peak throughout the climb. On most modern enduro bikes, this is second gear for hard-pack technical climbs, and third or even fourth on longer sustained hard-pack gradients.

### Principle 2: Root Climbing Has a Rhythm — Throttle Between, Ease Over
Roots across a steep climb require a specific alternating input pattern. Between roots, throttle drives the bike forward and upward. As each root approaches, reduce throttle slightly and allow the wheel to roll over the root rather than spin against it — a spinning tyre on a wet root provides zero traction and will slide sideways. After the wheel crosses the root, throttle again to drive to the next. This on-off rhythm, repeated for every root in the sequence, is what root climbing is. Riders who apply steady throttle throughout spin on every root and stall or wash out.

### Principle 3: Mud Climbs Require Entry Momentum and Minimum Input
In mud, the rear wheel creates its own rut on the first rotation, and every additional rotation deepens it until the wheel is buried. The correct technique is: maximum entry momentum, then minimum throttle input to maintain forward progress without digging the wheel. Clutch feathering is critical — the goal is to keep the wheel turning just fast enough to clear mud from the tyre pattern (self-cleaning) without spinning so fast that it digs. Higher-than-instinct gearing reduces torque peaks and makes the tyre more manageable. Aggressive throttle in mud is self-defeating.

### Principle 4: Hard-Pack Climbing Is About Line, Not Power
On hard-pack, traction is abundant — the limiting factor is line quality and gear selection. The rider should be looking for the smoothest path that avoids the loose material that accumulates on hard-pack climbs (leaf litter, loose gravel deposited by rain). On hard-pack with embedded rocks, the correct technique is to roll directly over rocks — not around them — because rocks provide superior grip compared to the surrounding surface. The obstacle avoidance instinct that serves riders on flat terrain is counterproductive on a hard-pack climb.

### Principle 5: Rock Steps Mid-Climb Require Momentum and Front-Wheel Unloading
A rock ledge or step encountered mid-climb cannot be treated as a slow obstacle — the bike must have forward momentum to roll over it, and the front wheel must be slightly unloaded to lift and roll over the lip rather than deflect off it. The technique is a brief throttle pulse as the front wheel approaches the step, which shifts weight rearward momentarily, allowing the front to rise and roll over the ledge. Immediately after, weight shifts forward again to maintain traction and forward drive. This is a micro wheelie on demand, mid-climb.

### Principle 6: Failed Climb Recovery Differs by Surface
On hard-pack, a failed climb is usually straightforward — the bike stops but holds position on the surface. Front brake holds, and the rider can dismount, turn the bike across the slope, and push or ride back down. On mud, a stopped bike begins to sink immediately, and the rider must act quickly to prevent the bike from settling into the mud. On root climbs, a stopped bike may be on or between roots, changing the balance point and making controlled recovery harder. Each surface has a specific recovery sequence that should be understood before the climb is attempted.

## 3. TECHNIQUE BREAKDOWN BY LEVEL

### 3a. Beginner Level
**Goal:** Successfully climb moderate hard-pack inclines using correct gear and body position, and begin to understand the basic root climbing rhythm on gentle rooted slopes.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Gear selection before entry — stop below the hill, choose the gear, then go
- Body position: standing, slight forward lean
- Looking ahead to the top of the hill, not at the ground immediately ahead
- On a simple rooted slope: feel the front wheel crossing each root, sense the throttle reduction needed

**What Good Looks Like:**
The rider stops briefly before the climb, selects gear, stands, and enters with committed throttle. The bike drives cleanly up a hard-pack slope with no wheel spin. On a gentle rooted slope, the rider's throttle is noticeably less smooth — there are slight reductions at each root crossing — and the bike tracks without significant weave. The rider crests the top in control.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Changing gear mid-climb under load:** Attempting to upshift or downshift when the engine is working against gravity damages the gearbox and usually results in a missed shift and stall. → Choose the gear at the bottom, before loading the engine.
- **Standing bolt upright on hard-pack — no forward lean:** The front wheel stays light and wanders. → Lean forward and push the front wheel into the surface.
- **Constant throttle over roots:** Rear spins on each root and the bike weaves off line. → Practice the feel: throttle between roots, ease over each one.
- **Looking down at feet or front wheel:** Vision is at 1m ahead instead of 5-10m. → Eyes up, look at where you want to exit.

### 3b. Intermediate Level
**Goal:** Execute root climbs with deliberate rhythm, manage mud climbs with entry momentum and minimum input, and handle rock steps mid-climb.

**Progression Markers from Beginner:** Rider climbs hard-pack consistently in correct gear. Root rhythm is developing — fewer stalls and washes on easy rooted slopes.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Root rhythm: deliberate throttle-off at each root, throttle-on between — making it intentional, not accidental
- Mud entry: assess the mud depth and surface before entry, then commit with maximum entry speed
- Rock steps: learn the throttle pulse timing for front-wheel lift over ledges
- Body position adapting to surface: forward more on hard-pack, slightly back on mud to prevent digging

**What Good Looks Like:**
On a rooted climb, the rider's throttle hand shows a visible rhythm of on-and-off corresponding to each root crossing. The rear wheel tracks straight across roots without sliding sideways. On a mud climb, the rider enters fast, reduces throttle quickly to minimum sustaining speed, and the wheel self-cleans rather than digging. The bike exits the top of the mud section still moving, not stalled in the deepest point.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Root rhythm too early or too late:** Throttle reduction happens while the wheel is between roots (wasting drive) or after it hits the root (too late to prevent spin). → The reduction should happen as the front wheel approaches the root — matching the rear wheel crossing.
- **Mud throttle too aggressive after entry:** High entry momentum is correct, but maintaining high throttle digs the wheel. → Entry fast, then immediately reduce to sustaining throttle.
- **Failing to recognise when mud depth exceeds technique:** Sometimes the mud is too deep and walking is correct. → Assess depth on foot before committing a bike to it.

### 3c. Advanced Level
**Goal:** Clean technical climbs combining roots, mud patches, rock steps, and gradient changes in a single section, with automatic gear selection and adaptive throttle rhythm throughout.

**Progression Markers from Intermediate:** Root rhythm is consistent and automatic. Mud climbs are managed correctly under pressure. Rock step technique is reliable.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Reading a complex mixed climb before entry: identifying each traction-demand zone and pre-planning the throttle response
- Continuous body position adaptation within a single climb: forward on hard-pack, managing roots mid-section, rearward on mud patches
- Wet roots on steep gradient: the worst-case scenario — minimum throttle, maximum grip from the centre of the tyre, weight forward
- Recognising when a mixed climb exceeds the traction budget and walking is the correct decision

**What Good Looks Like:**
The rider pre-walks or scans the complex climb, identifies the key sections, and enters with a clear plan. Mid-climb, body position is continuously adjusting — visible forward and rearward shifts as the surface changes. The root rhythm is automatic; the throttle pulses are barely visible but effective. On wet roots, the rider is slower but controlled, using the root edges for grip. The bike reaches the top cleanly, often faster than riders who used more aggressive throttle.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Committed line without plan for the hardest section:** Rider cleans 80% then stalls at a wet root section because no technique was prepared. → Walk the climb; plan the hardest section specifically.
- **Speed-compensating for lack of technique on wet roots:** More speed does not help on wet roots — it makes the slide more violent. → Technique, not speed, solves wet roots.
- **Forgetting gear choice when focused on roots:** Rider is thinking about roots and forgets they're in the wrong gear. → Make gear selection habitual and automatic before the climb occupies mental bandwidth.

## 4. DIAGNOSTIC CUES (AI COACHING ENGINE REFERENCE)

### Visual Cues — Correct Technique
- On hard-pack: rider standing, slight forward lean, consistent throttle — bike tracks a clean straight line up the slope
- On roots: visible rhythm in throttle hand — small reductions at each root crossing, power between — rear wheel tracks straight across each root
- On mud: high entry speed, immediate throttle reduction, wheel showing mud self-cleaning spray rather than a rooster of wet mud
- Rock step technique: brief front-wheel lift before the step, smooth roll-over, immediate body weight forward after
- Gear selection behaviour: rider pauses before climb base, shifts gear, then enters — not shifting under load

### Visual Cues — Error Indicators
- Rear wheel sliding sideways at each root crossing: throttle not reduced over roots — constant throttle applied
- Bike weaving left and right on hard-pack: body weight too far rearward, front wheel wandering
- Deep rut forming under rear wheel on mud: wheel spinning and digging — too much throttle after entry
- Bike stalling mid-climb: wrong gear (too high), engine outside powerband
- Front wheel deflecting off rock step rather than rolling over: no throttle pulse, front wheel fully loaded

### Audio Cues
- Even engine load on hard-pack climb: good gear selection, wheel in traction
- Rhythmic engine note variation on root climb: throttle rhythm correct — load, ease, load, ease pattern audible
- High-rpm screaming on mud with minimal forward progress: wheel digging, too much throttle
- Engine note dropping suddenly to near-stall: gear too high, engine bogging — missed gear selection
- Tyre impact sound on root crossing: a "thump" without wheel slip indicates clean crossing; a "squeal" or "slap" indicates sidewall slide on wet root

### Sensor Cues
- Consistent rear wheel speed with small periodic reductions at root spacing: correct root rhythm detected
- Rear wheel speed spike followed by lateral IMU movement: wheel spun on root, bike pushed sideways
- Throttle position high, forward acceleration low: wheel spinning in mud — throttle reduction needed
- Engine RPM drop below powerband mid-climb: gear too high — missed pre-climb gear selection

## 5. COACHING CUES & LANGUAGE

### Positive Reinforcement
- "That root rhythm is there — I can see the throttle coming off and back on at every crossing. That's the technique. Keep that exact pattern."
- "Good gear choice — the engine never sounded strained and you had power all the way to the top. That's what correct pre-climb selection gives you."
- "Your mud entry was committed and the throttle was restrained after entry. The wheel self-cleaned and kept moving. Exactly right."

### Corrective Feedback
- "You're holding the throttle steady over every root. That's why the rear is sliding sideways. Ease it off as the front wheel approaches each root, then back on between them. It's a rhythm."
- "You changed gear mid-climb and missed it. Everything after that was fighting the bike. Gear goes on before the climb — stop, select, go."
- "You entered the mud too slowly and then added throttle to compensate. That's backwards. Enter fast, then reduce throttle immediately. The momentum carries you; the throttle maintains it."
- "Your weight drifted back on that hard-pack section. The front started wandering — that's why you came off line. Push your chest forward and pin the front wheel."

### Progression Prompts
- "Next pass: I want you to narrate the root positions in your head — 'root, between, root, between.' Let that narration drive your throttle hand."
- "Before the next mud section, tell me what entry speed you're planning and why. Then we'll see if the execution matches the plan."
- "Try a rock step section at low speed first — slow enough that the only thing getting the front up is the throttle pulse. Once you can do it slowly, speed will follow."

## 6. DRILLS & PRACTICE EXERCISES

### Drill 1: Root Rhythm Flat Training
**Purpose:** Develop the root crossing throttle rhythm on flat ground before adding gradient, so cognitive load is minimal when the hill is introduced.
**Setup:** Flat area with 4-6 evenly spaced roots, logs, or constructed 50-75mm obstacles — enough to require the throttle adjustment but not so large they require a wheelie.
**Execution:**
1. Ride through the obstacle line in second gear at moderate speed.
2. As the front wheel approaches each obstacle, deliberately reduce throttle by 30%.
3. As the rear wheel clears the obstacle, restore full throttle.
4. Focus on the timing: front approaches → reduce → rear clears → restore.
5. Increase speed on each pass until the rhythm is automatic at riding speed.
**Success Criteria:** Rear wheel crosses each obstacle without lateral slip. Throttle rhythm is deliberate and consistent across all obstacles.
**Duration/Reps:** 20 minutes on the flat section.
**Progression:** Move the drill to a gentle uphill slope. Then use real roots of varying size.

### Drill 2: Gear Selection Protocol Drill
**Purpose:** Build the habit of deliberate gear selection before the climb, rather than reactive mid-climb adjustment.
**Setup:** Three different climbs of varying length and gradient — one short steep, one long moderate, one mixed.
**Execution:**
1. Before each climb, stop the bike at the base.
2. Assess the gradient and expected hardest section.
3. Explicitly say (out loud or in your head): "This climb needs [gear]. I am selecting it now."
4. Select the gear, then enter the climb.
5. Do not change gear during the climb regardless of outcome.
6. After each attempt, assess: was the gear choice correct? What would you change?
**Success Criteria:** Rider can correctly predict and pre-select the appropriate gear for each climb type. Mid-climb gear changes are eliminated.
**Duration/Reps:** 5 attempts per climb, three climbs.
**Progression:** Introduce a mixed climb requiring gear assessment across multiple sections. Try the same climb in different weather conditions and reassess gear choice.

### Drill 3: Mud Entry Speed Calibration
**Purpose:** Find the minimum entry speed that allows a mud section to be cleared without wheel dig-in.
**Setup:** A short mud section, 5-10m long, on a slight gradient.
**Execution:**
1. Walk the mud to assess depth and consistency.
2. First pass: low entry speed. Observe whether the wheel cleans or digs.
3. Second pass: 20% more entry speed. Reduce throttle immediately after entry.
4. Third pass: continue increasing until the wheel self-cleans and forward progress is maintained.
5. Note the entry speed at which the mud section is consistently cleared.
**Success Criteria:** Rider identifies the minimum entry speed for this mud type. Can reproduce a consistent self-cleaning throttle level after entry.
**Duration/Reps:** 8-10 passes.
**Progression:** Test in heavier mud. Test the same section after a rain event and recalibrate entry speed.

### Drill 4: Rock Step Throttle Pulse
**Purpose:** Develop the timing and feel of the throttle pulse needed to unload the front wheel over rock ledges mid-climb.
**Setup:** A flat or very gently inclined rock step of 150-250mm height — no gradient required initially.
**Execution:**
1. Approach the step slowly in second gear.
2. As the front wheel is 0.5m from the step, give a brief throttle pulse (half a second, 30% increase).
3. Feel the front wheel lift slightly.
4. Roll the front wheel over the ledge, then shift weight forward immediately after.
5. Repeat, increasing approach speed until the pulse timing is instinctive.
**Success Criteria:** Front wheel consistently rolls over the ledge without deflecting or stopping. Rider can feel the weight shift during the pulse.
**Duration/Reps:** 15 passes at varying speeds.
**Progression:** Add the step mid-way on an actual climb. Increase step height progressively.

## 8. TERRAIN & CONTEXT VARIATIONS

### Hard-Pack Rocky Climbs
Pure hard-pack with embedded rocks is the most grip-friendly climbing surface. The primary technique variable is line choice: seek rocks for maximum grip and avoid the loose material that collects around them. Body position can remain consistently forward throughout. Gear selection tends toward a higher gear than on other surfaces — the traction supports using the engine's power more aggressively.

### Root Network Climbs
Root climbs in steep woodland are the most technically demanding of the three primary surfaces. Root density, root diameter, and root orientation all change the difficulty. Roots running across the slope are manageable with the rhythm technique. Diagonal roots require the rider to adjust line so the wheel crosses the root as close to perpendicular as possible — crossing at a shallow angle makes sliding far more likely. In wet conditions, root climbs should be approached with maximum caution and minimum wheel spin.

### Mud Over Hardpack
When mud is shallow over hard substrate, the mud provides some lubrication but the hard substrate provides grip through the mud layer. Higher entry speed is viable and the mud will self-clear more readily. The bike tends to be less likely to dig in because the hard layer prevents deepening ruts.

### Deep Mud With No Substrate
Deep mud with no hard substrate is where technique alone often cannot substitute for momentum. If the mud exceeds a depth where the tyre can reach a hard surface, wheel spin digs the bike until it is buried. In competition, riders walking this type of section is often the correct call. If attempting it, maximum entry speed and minimum throttle after entry is the only viable technique.

### Wet Roots on Steep Gradients
The worst-case climbing scenario in woodland enduro. Every wet root is a near-zero traction surface. The technique is: lower speed than on dry, use the edges of roots rather than the top surface (the edge provides more mechanical grip), keep the wheel perpendicular to the root, and accept that some slip will occur. The goal is controlled slip — the wheel crosses the root while moving, not stalling on it. Clutch feathering reduces the power spike as the wheel crosses.

## 9. EXPERT INSIGHTS & SOURCES

### Key Insights
- **Alfie Cox** (multiple ISDE Enduro World Championship competitor and coach): Identifies root rhythm as the single most common technical failure point in woodland enduro — "Riders know they need to ease off over roots but their hands don't do it. It has to become automatic, and that takes specific drilling."
- **Billy Bolt** (Hard Enduro World Championship podium, extreme enduro specialist): Notes that mud climbing technique is fundamentally about accepting that some forward progress will feel uncontrolled — "In mud, the bike is going to do things. Your job is to keep it pointed uphill and keep the wheel from burying. That's it."
- **Enduro GP technical analysis:** Video analysis of elite MXGP and EnduroGP riders on root climbs consistently shows micro-throttle variations at each root crossing — the rhythm is present in elite riders even when they are unaware of executing it consciously.
- **Two-stroke vs four-stroke difference on roots:** Two-stroke engines produce a sharper power delivery curve, meaning the throttle rhythm must be more precise — a small overinput causes a larger spike than on a four-stroke. Four-stroke riders can use slightly more throttle over roots without the same spin risk.
- **Gear selection research:** Post-race analysis of failed technical climbs in enduro consistently identifies wrong gear selection as the primary cause — more often than throttle control or body position errors.
