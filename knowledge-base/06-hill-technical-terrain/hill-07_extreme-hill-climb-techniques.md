# Extreme Hill Climb Techniques

## 1. OVERVIEW

Extreme hill climbing — found in hard enduro, cross-country enduro, and extreme off-road competition — operates at the outer boundary of what a motorcycle and rider can achieve on terrain that seems impossibly steep, loose, rocky, or rutted. Gradients exceeding 45 degrees, loose scree faces, rocky near-vertical walls, and deep-rutted steep climbs are the defining challenges of events like Erzbergrodeo and the Hard Enduro World Championship. What separates riders who clean these sections from those who do not is rarely raw power or fitness — it is a combination of complete pre-commitment, body position held at the absolute physical limit from the first metre, and the precise use of clutch and throttle as a coordinated unit to maintain traction in a slip zone that is centimetres wide. Half-commitment on an extreme climb is not a failed attempt — it is the most dangerous outcome, leaving the rider perched on a steep, loose slope with no momentum and a heavy bike. The decision to attempt must be made fully before the front wheel touches the hill.

## 2. CORE PRINCIPLES

### Principle 1: Full Commitment From the First Metre Is Non-Negotiable
On an extreme climb, the bike enters a traction-deficit environment immediately. There is no gradual increase in difficulty — the hardest part may be the first five metres. This means the correct body position, throttle level, and mental state must all be fully established before entry, not developed mid-climb. A rider who enters at 60% commitment will reach the first hard section with insufficient momentum, incorrect body position, and a hesitant throttle — a combination that ensures failure at the worst possible location on the climb.

### Principle 2: Body Position Is at Maximum Forward Extent From Metre One
On an extreme climb, the rider's chest should be at or near the tank from the very first metre. Many riders adopt this position as the climb steepens — but on a genuine extreme gradient, steepening happens immediately or not at all. There is no warm-up period. The technique is to assess the gradient from the base, determine that it is an extreme climb, and adopt the maximum body position before the throttle opens. Knees against the tank, chest low, elbows bent — held from first metre to top.

### Principle 3: The Clutch Is a Precision Tool, Not a Binary Switch
On extreme climbs, the clutch is worked continuously and with fine precision. The productive traction window — the narrow range where the rear tyre is driving without free-spinning — is smaller on extreme terrain because surface pressure and gradient interact to reduce it. The clutch must feather within that window continuously. Binary clutch operation (fully open or fully closed) cannot maintain the tyre in its optimal slip zone on extreme terrain. The clutch hand must remain active throughout the climb.

### Principle 4: Wheelie Management Requires the Rear Brake, Not Throttle Reduction
On extreme gradients, the combination of full body weight forward and high throttle output is sometimes still insufficient to prevent the front wheel from rising. When the front wheel lifts during an extreme climb, the correct response is not to close the throttle — which reduces drive and immediately collapses momentum. Instead, a brief application of the rear brake brings the front wheel down while maintaining engine output. The rear brake creates a momentary forward pitch force that overrides the wheelie tendency without losing the drive that is carrying the bike up the hill.

### Principle 5: Pre-Plan the Failure Exit Before Every Extreme Attempt
On every extreme climb attempt, the rider should identify the failure exit before entering. Where on the slope is it possible to turn the bike across the slope to arrest a slide? Is there a natural berm or flat rock that provides a safe stopping point? If the attempt fails, the rider has a plan rather than improvising on a steep, loose slope under fatigue and adrenaline. In competition, this plan includes whether the bike needs to be moved to allow the next rider through, and how a damaged bike will be recovered.

### Principle 6: Multiple Attempt Learning — Each Failure Is a Data Point
Elite extreme enduro riders typically attempt the hardest climbs multiple times, adjusting technique with each attempt based on what was learned. This is not persistence for its own sake — it is systematic learning. After each failed attempt: where did momentum run out? What did the clutch feel like at that point? What was the rear wheel doing? Did body position hold? Each of these answers informs the next attempt. Riders who view failed extreme climb attempts as defeats rather than measurements improve much more slowly than those who treat each attempt as a calibration run.

## 3. TECHNIQUE BREAKDOWN BY LEVEL

### 3a. Beginner Level
**Goal:** Not applicable. Extreme hill climbing is not a beginner skill and should not be attempted without solid foundations in HILL-01, HILL-04, HILL-05, and HILL-06.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Build proficiency on loose climbs (HILL-01) to the point of consistency on moderate-gradient loose terrain
- Develop body position on inclines (HILL-05) to the point of automatic maximum forward positioning on steep terrain
- Build momentum management (HILL-04) to the point of accurate entry speed calibration
- Master gear selection (HILL-06) to the point of immediate, correct selection under pressure

**What Good Looks Like:**
A rider ready to progress toward extreme climbing can consistently clean 70-80% of moderate technical climbs, has automatic forward body position on gradients above 25 degrees, and can execute a controlled failure recovery independently.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Attempting extreme terrain before foundations are solid:** The consequence of technique failure on extreme terrain is significantly higher than on moderate terrain. → Prerequisites are not advisory — they are safety requirements.

### 3b. Intermediate Level
**Goal:** Successfully attempt and clean steep (35-45 degree) loose and mixed climbs using pre-commitment, maximum body position, and precision clutch management.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Maximum forward body position from the first metre — not building into it mid-climb
- Continuous clutch feathering as the primary traction management tool
- Rear brake wheelie control: when and how to apply briefly to bring the front wheel down
- Failure exit planning: identifying the safe turn-across-slope point before each attempt
- Reading the worst traction zone and setting entry speed to carry through it with momentum reserve

**What Good Looks Like:**
The rider walks the climb and identifies the worst surface section, sets entry speed to that section, and adopts maximum body position before the throttle opens. Mid-climb, the clutch hand is visibly active — small continuous adjustments. If the front wheel begins to lift, a brief rear brake tap brings it down without throttle reduction. If the attempt fails, the rider stops cleanly and turns across the slope using the pre-planned recovery point.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Body position building gradually:** Rider starts at moderate forward position and leans further forward only as the gradient increases. → The maximum position must be established at the bottom. There is no time to build it.
- **Reacting to front wheel lift with throttle reduction:** Throttle reduction kills momentum immediately. → Rear brake, not throttle. Short tap, not sustained application.
- **No pre-planned failure exit:** When the attempt fails, the rider freezes and the bike begins to slide backward. → Walk the climb and mark the safe recovery point before attempting.

### 3c. Advanced Level
**Goal:** Clean near-vertical extreme climbs on varied surfaces (scree, rock faces, deeply rutted slopes) with full technical and mental commitment, and make rapid go/no-go decisions under race conditions.

**Progression Markers from Intermediate:** Steep loose climbs are consistent. Body position is at maximum without conscious effort. Clutch feathering is a sensory feedback loop, not a calculated action. Failure recovery is calm and practiced.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Rocky face climbing: sequential rock-by-rock technique — each rock surface is a micro-traction zone requiring individual management
- Spotter use: communicating with a spotter effectively, following guidance on foot placement and line
- Competition strategy — go or walk: calculating the expected time cost of the climb attempt vs walking, considering bike condition, fatigue state, and remaining race distance
- Energy conservation on extreme climbs: the physical cost of multiple attempts, and the recovery management between them
- Near-vertical gradient management: at gradients approaching 60 degrees, body position alone is insufficient — rock selection and precise foot peg loading become the primary techniques

**What Good Looks Like:**
From a chase camera, the rider's body appears to be lying on the bike — chest on the tank, extremely low. The rear wheel shows consistent, even traction output — not spiking or disappearing. The clutch hand makes tiny, continuous movements. The bike tracks straight (or follows the planned line on a rocky face) without oscillating. The rider reaches the top and controls the cresting moment — not catapulted over the top by excess speed.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Over-committing in competition on walks: ego-driven attempt that costs 5-10 minutes:** → When a climb would take more than 2-3 times as long to ride as to walk, walking is correct race strategy.
- **Energy depletion from repeated failed attempts:** Each attempt costs significant physical and fuel resources. → Set a maximum attempt number before racing: if not cleaned in X attempts, walk. Stick to the plan.
- **Losing technique under fatigue during a race:** Extreme climbs late in a race are attempted with compromised body position and clutch sensitivity. → Recognise fatigue as a technique degrader. Add 20% more recovery time before extreme climb attempts late in race.

## 4. DIAGNOSTIC CUES (AI COACHING ENGINE REFERENCE)

### Visual Cues — Correct Technique
- Rider's chest at or near the tank from the very first metre of the climb — not building position mid-section
- Clutch hand showing visible continuous micro-movements throughout the climb
- Rear wheel showing a consistent, moderate roost — even spray volume throughout, not erratic
- Body position maintained through to the top — not collapsing as the climb reaches maximum gradient
- If front wheel lifts: brief rear brake application, front comes down, throttle unchanged
- Cresting point: rider controls speed over the top — no catapulting or front-wheel-first landing

### Visual Cues — Error Indicators
- Body position at standard attack position at entry — not maximally forward
- Rider building forward position only as the climb progresses — reactive, not pre-committed
- Rear wheel showing large roost spikes followed by quiet periods: spin-and-recover cycling, not sustained traction
- Front wheel lifting: rider closes throttle, momentum collapses
- Bike stopping above halfway with no controlled recovery: failure without plan
- Rider looking down at the front wheel, not at the climb ahead

### Audio Cues
- High-pitch engine screaming through the entire climb: wheel in free spin — clutch needs more feathering, throttle too high
- Even engine load with subtle variation: clutch feathering maintaining productive slip correctly
- Engine bog mid-climb followed by stall: clutch feathered too far, too little drive reaching the wheel — release clutch slightly
- Rear brake application sound (subtle): brief wheelie correction technique in use

### Sensor Cues
- Rear wheel speed consistently 10-20% higher than front wheel speed throughout: productive slip maintained — correct
- Rear wheel speed 50%+ above front wheel speed: free spin — clutch needs further feathering
- Wheelie sensor (pitch angle) rapidly increasing: front wheel lifting — rear brake intervention required
- Throttle position data shows stable position throughout: committed throttle hold confirmed
- IMU pitch increasing over time beyond gradient: body position drifting rearward — forward commitment failing

## 5. COACHING CUES & LANGUAGE

### Positive Reinforcement
- "That was committed from the first metre. Your chest was on the tank before you even hit the gradient. That's the level of pre-commitment extreme climbs demand."
- "Your clutch hand didn't stop moving — I could see it working the whole way up. That's precision technique. The wheel was in the traction zone throughout."
- "When the front came up you went straight to the rear brake and held the throttle. That's exactly right. The wheel came down and you kept climbing. Most riders kill the throttle and lose the climb at that moment."

### Corrective Feedback
- "You built your body position as the hill got steeper. By the time you were forward enough, the worst section was already here and your momentum had dropped. Maximum position before you touch the hill — not during it."
- "When the front came up, you closed the throttle. I understand the instinct but it killed your drive. The fix for a front wheel lift on a climb is rear brake — a short tap — not throttle reduction. Hold the throttle and use the rear brake to bring the nose down."
- "You entered this climb with a maybe mindset. I could see it — entry speed too low, body position not fully committed. This hill demands a yes or a no. If it's yes, it's full yes from the first metre. If it's a no, walk it and don't look back."
- "You had no plan for when it failed. The bike was on a steep slope sliding backwards and you froze. Before the next attempt, walk the climb and show me where you're going to turn the bike across the slope if it fails. Know that point before you enter."

### Progression Prompts
- "Walk the climb right now. Show me the three worst sections. Now tell me where you need to arrive with the most momentum, and what that means for your entry speed."
- "Before the next attempt, I want you to walk the failure recovery point. Stand there. Look at the angle across the slope. Know that spot like it's a friend. Then enter the climb."
- "On the next attempt: the only thing I want you thinking about is keeping the clutch moving. Not the throttle, not the body position — trust that those will be there. Just make sure the clutch hand never stops."

## 6. DRILLS & PRACTICE EXERCISES

### Drill 1: Maximum Position Before Entry Protocol
**Purpose:** Eliminate the progressive body position error — build the habit of maximum forward commitment established before the first metre of the extreme climb.
**Setup:** A moderate steep climb the rider can consistently clean.
**Execution:**
1. Stop 5 metres before the climb base.
2. Adopt the most extreme forward position possible while stationary: chest on tank, knees forward, elbows bent.
3. Hold the position for 3 seconds before moving.
4. Enter the climb from this position without adjusting it.
5. Maintain the position through to the top.
**Success Criteria:** Body position at the top matches the position established at the bottom. No mid-climb position building. Front wheel does not lift.
**Duration/Reps:** 8 passes.
**Progression:** Apply the same protocol to a steeper climb. Then apply it to a genuine extreme gradient, using the now-habitual pre-position behaviour.

### Drill 2: Rear Brake Wheelie Control on a Moderate Climb
**Purpose:** Build the rear brake wheelie correction reflex on manageable terrain, so that on extreme terrain the response is automatic.
**Setup:** A hard-pack moderate climb where wheelie tendency can be induced by body position adjustment and throttle.
**Execution:**
1. Begin climbing in correct body position.
2. Deliberately allow the front wheel to begin rising by sitting back slightly and adding throttle.
3. As the front rises, apply rear brake briefly — 0.5 seconds, moderate pressure.
4. Feel the front wheel return to the ground without throttle change.
5. Repeat: allow rise → rear brake → down → continue climbing.
**Success Criteria:** Front wheel rise is corrected with rear brake alone — no throttle reduction. Response happens within 0.5 seconds of front wheel rising.
**Duration/Reps:** 15 repetitions.
**Progression:** Move to a steeper climb where the tendency to wheelie is stronger and the correction requires more confidence.

### Drill 3: Pre-Planned Failure Recovery Practice
**Purpose:** Make the failure recovery a practiced, calm procedure rather than a panic improvisation.
**Setup:** A moderate steep climb with a planned mid-section stopping point.
**Execution:**
1. Identify a safe stopping point at 40-50% of the climb height.
2. Ride to that point and stop deliberately.
3. Apply front brake to hold position.
4. Turn the bike 90 degrees across the slope without the bike sliding.
5. Walk or ride the bike back to the base.
6. Repeat from a different height.
**Success Criteria:** Rider can stop at the planned point and execute the across-slope turn without the bike sliding. Recovery is calm and methodical.
**Duration/Reps:** 8 recoveries from varied heights.
**Progression:** Perform the recovery on a looser surface. Practise from higher on the climb.

### Drill 4: Multiple Attempt Calibration Sequence
**Purpose:** Train the analytical mindset of treating each attempt as a measurement, adjusting one variable per attempt.
**Setup:** An extreme climb the rider cannot yet clean.
**Execution:**
1. Attempt 1: at normal entry speed, normal body position. Note failure point.
2. Attempt 2: increase entry speed by 20%. Hold everything else constant. Note new failure point.
3. Attempt 3: maintain new entry speed, add more aggressive clutch feathering. Note result.
4. Attempt 4: if body position is the limiting factor, make that the adjustment.
5. Continue until the climb is clean or a clear limiting factor is identified.
**Success Criteria:** Rider can identify which variable is currently the limiting factor on the extreme climb. Changes between attempts are deliberate, not random.
**Duration/Reps:** 6-8 attempts with specific adjustment logging.
**Progression:** Apply the calibration approach to a new unfamiliar extreme climb where variables are unknown from the start.

## 8. TERRAIN & CONTEXT VARIATIONS

### Loose Scree Faces
Scree presents the maximum challenge to traction management because the entire surface moves under the tyre. Each wheel rotation displaces material, reducing the effective traction available for the next rotation. The technique is to keep the rear wheel spinning at just above the surface movement rate — the wheel must turn fast enough to generate drive but not so fast that it digs through the scree to the substrate below. On long scree faces, even expert riders may need to carry forward momentum from a very high entry speed to compensate for the traction that disappears as the scree is displaced.

### Rocky Near-Vertical Faces
On near-vertical rock faces, the technique shifts from traction management to sequential rock selection. Each rock surface is a micro-traction platform — the rider must land the wheel on the highest-grip surface available on each rock, use that grip to generate drive, and move to the next surface before grip is lost. The clutch becomes less about spin prevention and more about timing — power is applied in bursts as the wheel lands on each secure surface, then feathered as the wheel transitions between rocks. A spotter is essential on true rocky near-vertical climbs: they can identify the best rock-landing sequence from a vantage point the rider cannot access.

### Deeply Rutted Extreme Climbs
A deeply rutted steep climb constrains the line completely — the bike must follow the rut. This removes line choice as a variable but creates a new challenge: rut walls can trap the footpegs and panels, and the rut surface at the bottom may be smoother and lower-grip than the rut edges. The technique is to ride the rut walls — keeping the tyre on the upper edges of the rut rather than the base, which provides better traction and prevents the bike from grounding out. Body position remains maximally forward but the feet must be positioned to avoid the rut walls trapping the pegs.

### Competition Context (Race Strategy)
In extreme enduro competition, extreme climbs impose a time penalty whether ridden or walked. The strategic calculation is: expected ride time (including failed attempts and restarts) vs walk time. For professional riders, this calculation is made in seconds. For amateur competitors, the calculation is often skewed by ego — the attempt is made when the walk is objectively faster. The correct approach is a maximum attempt number set before the climb (typically 2-3 attempts) after which walking is the committed choice. Changing the walk decision mid-attempt costs the time of the walk AND the failed attempts.

## 9. EXPERT INSIGHTS & SOURCES

### Key Insights
- **Graham Jarvis** (most successful Erzbergrodeo finisher in history): "The bike doesn't care about the gradient. It cares about traction. Your job is to manage traction. The gradient is just background information." Jarvis is known for his technical calmness on extreme terrain — his clutch feathering is described by coaches as continuous and invisible.
- **Taddy Blazusiak** (multiple Erzbergrodeo and Hard Enduro Champion): Emphasises pre-commitment above all — "You decide before you go. Once you commit to a climb, your brain should go quiet. No more decisions. Just execute what you already decided."
- **Alfie Cox**: "The rear brake for wheelie control on a climb is one of the most important techniques most riders never learn. They're trained to close the throttle when the front comes up. On a steep climb, that's the wrong reflex. Rear brake is the tool."
- **Spotter technique in hard enduro:** The spotter's role on extreme rocky climbs is not simply to encourage — it is to call out the specific rock landing targets the rider cannot see from behind the bars. This requires the spotter to understand the technique: "land on the flat face of the rock on your left, then the ledge at 2 o'clock." This communication skill is trained in the hard enduro community as a specific discipline.
- **Energy management:** Studies of hard enduro race data show that extreme climbs account for a disproportionate share of total race time and energy expenditure — a single Erzbergrodeo rocky climb can cost more energy than 30 minutes of trail riding. Energy conservation strategy for these sections is as important as technical technique.
