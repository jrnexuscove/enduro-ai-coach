# Technical Descents

## 1. OVERVIEW

Technical descents are among the highest-consequence skill areas in off-road riding because gravity is continuously working against the rider's attempts to control speed, and errors compound quickly — a bad braking input that would be recoverable on flat terrain can cause an endo (front flip) or slide on a steep descent. The fundamental challenge is managing deceleration across two separate systems: engine braking and mechanical braking. Most riders who struggle on descents make the same error: they do not trust engine braking, they over-rely on the front brake, and their body position remains too far forward, which loads the front wheel and makes the very endo they fear more likely. Correct descent technique is a system of interconnected decisions — gear selection before descent, engine braking as primary deceleration, body weight rearward throughout, and a progressive and measured front brake — that must all work together.

## 2. CORE PRINCIPLES

### Principle 1: Weight Rearward Is the Primary Structural Rule of Descents
Gravity on a descent constantly tries to push the rider's weight forward over the front wheel. If the rider's centre of mass is above or forward of the standard position, the front wheel is overloaded, front brake application becomes immediately dangerous, and the endo risk is real at relatively modest brake inputs. The correct body position on a technical descent is significantly rearward from normal — hips behind the seat, arms extended, weight through the rear pegs. The steeper the descent, the more extreme this rearward shift must be. This is a constant, maintained position throughout the descent — not a reactive adjustment when things go wrong.

### Principle 2: Engine Braking Is the Primary Deceleration Tool
The correct gear before a descent is the gear that produces enough engine braking to maintain a controlled speed without heavy mechanical braking input. This means selecting a lower gear before the descent begins — while the bike is still on flat or mild terrain and the gearbox is not under descent load. On a steep technical descent, the engine's compression resistance slows the bike continuously, allowing the rider to modulate speed with minor throttle additions rather than relying on brakes. Riders who descend in too high a gear eliminate most engine braking and must compensate with heavy brake use, which is less stable and less controllable.

### Principle 3: Front Brake Must Be Progressive — Never Grabbed
The front brake is extraordinarily powerful on a descent because forward weight transfer adds to what is already a front-loaded situation. Any sharp front brake input on a steep descent dramatically increases endo risk and front wheel washout risk simultaneously. The correct technique is progressive front brake application: squeeze gradually, allow the weight to transfer and the suspension to compress, and hold at a consistent pressure. If more deceleration is needed, increase pressure gradually — never in a pulse or grab. On very steep descents, many expert riders minimise front brake use entirely and rely on engine braking plus rear brake for deceleration.

### Principle 4: Rear Brake Adds Stability on Descents
The rear brake is the stability tool on descents. Light but consistent rear brake pressure keeps the rear wheel tracking, prevents the rear from stepping out sideways on loose descents, and gives the rider tactile feedback about surface traction. Unlike the front brake, the rear brake cannot cause an endo — the worst outcome of rear brake overuse is rear wheel lock and a straight-line slide, which is far more recoverable than a front wheel wash or endo. Using both brakes in a coordinated way — light rear continuous, front progressive and measured — is the correct descent braking system.

### Principle 5: Vision Extends Further on Steep Descents
On a steep descent, vision must extend further ahead than on flat terrain. At walking pace down a steep slope, the visual information needed is 10-15m ahead. At trail speed on a technical descent, it is 20-30m. The reason is reaction time — a rider looking only 5m ahead at descent speed cannot react to obstacles quickly enough to make smooth control inputs. Short vision on descents causes rider panic as obstacles arrive faster than expected, leading to reactive harsh braking. Extend vision deliberately to the exit of the descent, scanning for braking points, obstacles, and traction changes.

### Principle 6: Two-Stroke vs Four-Stroke Descent Strategy Differs Significantly
Four-stroke engines produce strong engine braking through compression, which is a significant deceleration force on descents. Two-stroke engines produce almost no engine braking — the characteristic of a two-stroke is that the engine freewheels on the overrun. This means two-stroke riders must carry far more of the descent deceleration through the disc brakes. Two-stroke enduro riders should understand that their braking system carries a heavier load than a four-stroke rider's system, and front brake technique must be even more precise. Some two-stroke riders use a partially engaged clutch to create artificial drag as a rudimentary engine braking substitute.

## 3. TECHNIQUE BREAKDOWN BY LEVEL

### 3a. Beginner Level
**Goal:** Establish the correct body position and braking sequence for moderate technical descents. Understand engine braking as a primary control system.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Body position: standing, weight actively shifted rearward before the descent begins
- Gear selection: choose the gear before descent — lower than instinct suggests
- Front brake: squeeze, do not grab — practise on gentle slopes first
- Rear brake: maintain light but consistent pressure throughout
- Vision: look to the exit of the descent, not at the ground immediately in front

**What Good Looks Like:**
The rider stops before the descent begins, selects a lower gear, shifts weight rearward, and begins descent at a controlled speed without heavy braking. The bike moves down the slope at a steady pace — the engine braking doing most of the work. Front brake is used briefly and progressively at the steepest point. The rider's arms are extended forward (because body is behind the seat), and the rear of the bike is visibly lower than on flat ground — the rider's weight is clearly rearward.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Body weight too far forward on descent:** Rider's weight stays over the standard attack position. Front wheel is overloaded, braking is dangerous. → Push hips behind the seat. Arms extended. Feel your weight on the rear pegs.
- **Descending in too high a gear:** No engine braking, heavy brake use required. → Select a lower gear before starting. Engine braking should be doing the majority of the work.
- **Grabbing front brake reactively:** A harsh obstacle or rut causes a grab response. → Practise deliberate squeeze on gentle slopes until it is habitual.
- **Sitting during the descent:** Weight concentrated on the seat, cannot shift rearward effectively. → Stand throughout all descents. Sit only when the descent is fully complete.

### 3b. Intermediate Level
**Goal:** Manage technical descents including rutted, rooted, and loose sections using correct braking sequence and adaptive body position.

**Progression Markers from Beginner:** Rider descends moderate slopes with correct body position and measured braking. Engine braking is used as the primary control. Does not grab the front brake.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Rutted descents: tracking in the rut, understanding when to stay in and when to exit
- Rear brake as a steering tool on loose descents: light rear brake straightens the bike if the rear begins to drift
- Progressive front brake development: learning to squeeze to 70-80% capacity on a steep descent without endo risk
- Partial clutch use on very steep sections to reduce engine braking intensity when it is causing rear wheel lock

**What Good Looks Like:**
The rider selects the correct rut line and tracks through it cleanly. On a loose section mid-descent, the rear shows a slight drift but the rider corrects with rear brake without adding front brake. When engine braking is too aggressive and the rear skips, the rider slightly opens the clutch to reduce engine braking, maintaining wheel roll and stability. Vision is consistently forward — the rider is not looking at the ground below them.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Switching ruts mid-descent:** Rut transitions at speed cause front wheel to deflect and washout. → Commit to a rut before entry. If the rut changes mid-descent, adapt gradually, not sharply.
- **Over-relying on rear brake to the point of rear wheel lock:** Rear wheels locks and bike slides. → Rear brake should not lock the wheel — modulate it like a dimmer switch.
- **Neglecting engine braking by riding in too high a gear:** All deceleration comes from disc brakes. → If brakes feel overworked on a descent, the gear is too high.

### 3c. Advanced Level
**Goal:** Execute extreme technical descents — near-vertical, rocky faces, loose shale, muddy gradients — with full engine braking integration, precise braking balance, and adaptive line choice throughout.

**Progression Markers from Intermediate:** Rutted descents are clean. Engine braking management is consistent. Front brake is confident and progressive even on steep slopes.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Near-vertical descent technique: body position at maximum rearward extent, engine braking at maximum, front brake minimal
- Rocky face descent: tyre selection matters; ride the shallowest line available, accept that the bike will deflect, absorb deflections rather than fighting them
- Loose shale or scree descent: the surface moves under the wheels — do not brake hard on scree, use engine braking almost exclusively to prevent surface displacement
- Mud descent: engine braking is the only reliable deceleration; brake inputs into deep mud cause the wheel to lock and the bike to slide unpredictably
- Speed management through obstacles mid-descent: brake, absorb, accelerate out — treating technical obstacles as rhythm points within the descent

**What Good Looks Like:**
From a chase camera, the rider appears to be sitting behind the seat — arms extended, body very low and very rearward. The bike descends at a controlled pace without heavy brake involvement. Over obstacles mid-descent, the rider absorbs the hit through bent knees without their body position changing significantly. On a loose section, the bike slides slightly but the rider neither panics nor brakes — they allow the slide and steer through it.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Locking the front brake on rocky face descent:** Front wheel stops, bike pitches forward. → On rocky descents, front brake is almost never used in hard application; engine braking and rear brake only.
- **Braking on loose shale mid-corner:** The surface shifts under brake load and the bike slides. → On loose descents, brake in straight lines only — no brake input in corners.
- **Failing to absorb mid-descent obstacles:** Rider stiffens at impact, weight transfers forward. → Descend with knees slightly flexed continuously — ready to absorb, not static.

## 4. DIAGNOSTIC CUES (AI COACHING ENGINE REFERENCE)

### Visual Cues — Correct Technique
- From side/chase camera: rider's body is significantly behind the seat — arms extended forward, clear gap between hips and seat
- Rear of the bike appears lower than the front relative to the slope — weight correctly rearward
- Consistent slow speed on descent without heavy visible braking — engine braking doing the work
- Front brake lever: slow, deliberate squeeze motion — no quick grabs
- Rear brake foot slightly pressed continuously — not pumping
- Vision: rider's head is directed forward and down the slope, not at the ground immediately ahead

### Visual Cues — Error Indicators
- Rider's body in standard attack position on a steep descent — weight too far forward
- Rider sitting during descent — seat contact visible, weight cannot be properly rearward
- Rapid head movement or "bobbing" — reactive braking causing instability
- Front wheel compressing sharply mid-descent — hard front brake grab detected
- Rear wheel locked and sliding — rear brake over-application or engine braking too strong for current surface
- Rider looking down at front wheel — short vision, reactive riding

### Audio Cues
- Consistent engine note in compression (overrun): engine braking active, correct gear selected
- Engine spinning freely with no compression sound: gear too high, engine overrunning — no engine braking
- Sustained brake squeal: brakes heavily overloaded — gear may be too high
- Intermittent tyre squeal: front or rear wheel locking briefly — brake input too harsh

### Sensor Cues
- IMU pitch data showing rider CoM forward of correct position: body weight not sufficiently rearward
- Front wheel speed approaching zero while rear speed maintained: front brake approaching lock — reduce input
- Engine RPM significantly below redline on overrun: engine braking active and appropriate
- Engine RPM at or near maximum on overrun: gear too high, engine not producing meaningful braking force
- Rear wheel speed dropping to zero while front maintained: rear brake lock — modulate

## 5. COACHING CUES & LANGUAGE

### Positive Reinforcement
- "That's exactly it — your weight is behind the seat, arms extended, and the engine is doing the braking. I could barely see any brake use. That's how you descend."
- "Good recovery on that loose section — you felt the rear drift, kept the front brake off, and let it track through. That's trust in the technique."
- "Your vision is forward. I can see your head angled toward the exit, not down at the wheels. That's what puts you in control of the section, not behind it."

### Corrective Feedback
- "Your weight is in the wrong place for a descent. Your hips need to be behind the seat, not over it. Push them back. Your arms should feel long — almost stretched forward. If they feel comfortable and short, you're too far forward."
- "You're using the front brake too hard too quickly. Squeeze it — squeeze like you're squeezing water from a sponge, not grabbing a door handle. The harder you grab, the more likely you are to go over the front."
- "You're descending in too high a gear. There's no engine braking. You're carrying all the deceleration in the brakes and they can't sustain that. Go down a gear before you start the descent, not halfway down."
- "You're looking at the front wheel. That's five metres of vision. You need twenty. Look to the bottom of the descent. Your body will adjust — but your eyes have to lead."

### Progression Prompts
- "Next descent, no front brake at all. Use engine braking and rear brake only. It will feel terrifying at first, then it will feel right. This will teach you how much engine braking is doing."
- "Descend the same section again but shift your weight so far rearward that you feel like you might fall off the back. That's probably about right for this gradient."
- "Try the descent at 20% slower than last time. Use only engine braking. No disc brakes unless absolutely necessary. Then tell me how it felt compared to your normal approach."

## 6. DRILLS & PRACTICE EXERCISES

### Drill 1: Engine Braking Only Descent
**Purpose:** Isolate engine braking as the primary deceleration tool, removing disc brake dependency and building understanding of how much control the engine provides.
**Setup:** A moderate, clean descent with consistent gradient — no tight corners or obstacles.
**Execution:**
1. Select one gear lower than normal before the top of the descent.
2. Begin descent with no brake input — engine braking only.
3. Control speed using only throttle addition (to reduce braking) or throttle removal (to increase braking).
4. No disc brake use unless an emergency requires it.
5. Note the controlled speed the engine maintains on this gradient in this gear.
**Success Criteria:** Rider descends the full section without disc brake use. Speed is controlled and consistent. Rider can identify what gear produces controlled descent speed on this gradient.
**Duration/Reps:** 5 passes.
**Progression:** Try the same drill one gear higher — note how the engine braking drops and more disc braking is needed. This teaches the relationship directly.

### Drill 2: Rearward Body Position Lock
**Purpose:** Build muscle memory for the extreme rearward body position required on steep descents.
**Setup:** A gentle-to-moderate slope, clean surface.
**Execution:**
1. Stop at the top of the slope.
2. Before moving, shift hips as far rearward as possible — behind the seat, arms nearly fully extended toward the bars.
3. Hold this position for 3 seconds before starting descent.
4. Descend maintaining exactly this position.
5. At the bottom, check: are your hips still behind the seat? Are your arms still extended?
**Success Criteria:** Body position at bottom matches the locked-in position at the top. Arms remain extended throughout. Rider reports that it "felt too rearward" — this is usually the correct amount.
**Duration/Reps:** 10 passes.
**Progression:** Increase gradient. Check if the rearward position is maintained under greater gravitational load.

### Drill 3: Progressive Front Brake Squeeze
**Purpose:** Train the progressive front brake application pattern, removing the grab reflex, on a gentle slope where the consequence of error is low.
**Setup:** A very gentle descent — 5-10 degrees — clean surface.
**Execution:**
1. Descend slowly with body weight slightly rearward.
2. Squeeze the front brake from zero to maximum over a 3-second count.
3. Feel the weight transfer forward as pressure increases.
4. Hold maximum pressure for 1 second, then release equally slowly.
5. Repeat, reducing the squeeze time progressively until the progressive technique is fast.
**Success Criteria:** Rider can apply heavy front brake in under 1 second without grabbing — the squeeze is always progressive, even when fast.
**Duration/Reps:** 20 repetitions.
**Progression:** Move to a slightly steeper slope and repeat. Add a braking point marker — brake at marker, release 5m later.

### Drill 4: Rutted Descent Line Selection
**Purpose:** Develop the skill of identifying, entering, and committing to a descent rut, and managing the transition between ruts when necessary.
**Setup:** A descent with 2-3 visible rut options of varying depth and line.
**Execution:**
1. Walk the descent and identify each rut — which offers the cleanest line to the bottom?
2. On the first pass, enter the best rut at low speed and track it all the way to the bottom without switching.
3. On the second pass, enter a different rut to feel how each one affects speed and stability.
4. On subsequent passes, practise rut transitions at the safest point — where the ruts merge or where the angle is shallowest.
**Success Criteria:** Rider can track a rut cleanly from top to bottom. Can identify the safest transition point between ruts. No front wheel deflection during transitions.
**Duration/Reps:** 8-10 passes.
**Progression:** Increase descent speed. Add a mid-descent braking point within the rut.

## 8. TERRAIN & CONTEXT VARIATIONS

### Rocky Face Descents
On a rocky descent where the surface is broken and irregular, the front brake must be used with extreme caution — rocks can deflect the front wheel instantly and brake application at that moment locks and flips. The technique is to descend using engine braking only, absorbing each rock with flexed legs, and using the rear brake lightly for additional speed control. Choose a line that rolls over rocks rather than through them — the bike's wheels should stay on the rock tops, not drop into gaps between rocks.

### Loose Shale and Scree Descents
Scree and shale move under the wheels, making any braking force unpredictable. As soon as brake pressure is applied, the surface begins to shift and the wheel's tracking becomes uncertain. The technique is engine braking as the exclusive deceleration tool, with speed managed by gear selection before descent. Body weight must be rearward to prevent the front wheel from digging into the moving surface.

### Mud Descents
In deep mud on a descent, brake inputs are largely ineffective — the wheel locks but slides rather than gripping. The mud's lubricating layer removes traction from both wheels simultaneously. Engine braking provides the most reliable deceleration. If the bike begins to slide, do not fight it — allow it to slide while steering with subtle weight shifts. A braked, sliding descent in mud is actually fairly controlled if the rider remains loose and rearward.

### Rutted Descents
Ruts on a descent can be friends or enemies. A rut that runs directly down the slope channels the bike cleanly and removes steering workload — a useful aid. A rut that curves or ends mid-descent, or that is inconsistent in depth, requires active management. The key rule: commit to a rut before entry. Attempting to exit a rut mid-descent at speed is one of the highest-risk manoeuvres in trail riding.

### Switchback Descents
A tight downhill switchback requires a completely different approach from a straight descent. Speed must be reduced to near-walking pace before the turn. The front brake must be used on the approach to the corner, then fully released mid-corner. Body weight shifts from rearward (on approach) to slightly inward on the corner itself. This is covered in detail in CORNER-04 (Hairpin and Tight Turn Technique) but the descent-specific element is: any braking must happen before the corner, not during it.

## 9. EXPERT INSIGHTS & SOURCES

### Key Insights
- **Chris Birch** (KTM global off-road ambassador, ADV and enduro coach): Emphasises the engine braking principle extensively — "Most riders are scared of engine braking. They don't trust it. Once you learn to trust it, descending becomes a completely different experience."
- **Jonny Walker** (Hard Enduro World Championship): Describes rearward weight position as automatic for him — "The steeper it gets, the further back I am. It's like a reflex. If you have to think about it, you haven't practised it enough."
- **Two-stroke descent physics:** Academic studies on off-road braking have shown that two-stroke riders typically carry 30-40% more front brake load than four-stroke riders on equivalent descents, due to absence of engine braking — a significant factor in two-stroke rider front-wheel incidents.
- **Endo mechanics:** The physics of endos show that the front wheel needs to decelerate to near zero while the rear still has forward momentum. The primary cause is not front brake force alone but the combination of forward weight and sudden front brake. Managing weight rearward is more effective than reducing brake force in preventing endos.
- **Vision research:** Studies on motorcycle safety consistently identify target fixation and short vision as contributing factors to crashes on descents — the rider is looking too close, sees an obstacle too late, and makes a reactive sharp brake input.
