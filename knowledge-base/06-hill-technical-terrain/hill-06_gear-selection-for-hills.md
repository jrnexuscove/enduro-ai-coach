# Gear Selection for Hills

## 1. OVERVIEW

Gear selection for hills is one of the highest-leverage decisions in technical terrain riding, and one of the most commonly made incorrectly. The gear determines two critical things simultaneously: available torque for climbing, and engine braking intensity for descending. These are opposing requirements — the lower the gear, the more torque is available but the more aggressive the engine braking; the higher the gear, the smoother the power delivery but the less engine braking. Getting the gear wrong on a climb usually results in either a spin-out (too low, too much torque for the surface) or a stall (too high, insufficient torque at the climb's hardest point). Getting it wrong on a descent means either a rear wheel hop and skip (too low, engine braking too aggressive for the surface) or over-reliance on disc brakes to compensate for insufficient engine braking (too high). Critically, gear selection for hills must happen before the hill — there is almost no opportunity to safely change gear under full load on a steep incline or with a loaded front wheel on a descent.

## 2. CORE PRINCIPLES

### Principle 1: Gear Determines Torque on Climbs — Match It to the Surface
Lower gears multiply engine torque, which is useful when traction is abundant (hard-pack rock) and the engine needs to produce maximum force to overcome gravity. On loose surfaces, however, too much torque overcomes the tyre's grip before it produces forward motion — the wheel spins rather than drives. On loose surfaces, the gear should provide enough torque to maintain forward progress in productive slip without exceeding the traction ceiling. This often means a higher gear than instinct suggests on loose terrain — counterintuitive but mechanically correct.

### Principle 2: Gear Determines Engine Braking on Descents — Match It to the Surface
Engine braking force increases as gear decreases. On a hard-pack descent with abundant traction, strong engine braking (second gear, four-stroke) is excellent — it slows the bike reliably with minimal disc brake use. On a loose descent (gravel, sand, scree), aggressive engine braking causes the rear wheel to lock intermittently as the tyre breaks traction on each rotation, causing instability. On loose descents, a higher gear reduces engine braking intensity, allowing the rear wheel to roll more freely and track the surface more smoothly. This is counterintuitive — the descent that feels most dangerous may actually require a higher gear, not a lower one.

### Principle 3: Gear Selection Happens Before the Hill — Not During It
On a climb, the gearbox is under significant load — the engine is pushing against gravity through the drivetrain. Attempting to change gear under this load either misses the shift (engine flare, loss of drive) or causes gearbox stress. On a descent, the engine braking is loading the gearbox in the opposite direction — a downshift mid-descent creates a massive engine braking spike that can lock the rear wheel instantly. The rule is absolute: select the gear at the base of the climb or top of the descent, in a position where the gearbox is unloaded, then proceed with the selected gear.

### Principle 4: Two-Stroke and Four-Stroke Gear Strategies Differ Significantly
Four-stroke engines produce strong engine braking through compression resistance, and their power delivery is relatively linear with throttle input. Two-stroke engines produce almost no engine braking (freewheeling on overrun) and have a pronounced powerband — a sudden large increase in power as rpm enters the powerband range. Two-stroke riders must compensate for absent engine braking with more disc brake use on descents. On climbs, two-stroke riders should stay within the powerband — which often means a slightly lower gear than a comparable four-stroke would use — because the power above and below the powerband drops off sharply and the torque curve is narrow.

### Principle 5: The Clutch Is a Gear Ratio Modifier in Practice
The clutch allows the rider to effectively vary the power reaching the rear wheel within a given gear. Partial clutch engagement reduces effective torque delivered to the wheel, functioning similarly to a higher gear. This means: if the climb demands slightly less torque than the selected gear provides, a partially engaged clutch can compensate without requiring a gear change. Similarly on descents, partial clutch engagement reduces engine braking effectively — a one- or two-gear reduction in engine braking without the instability risk of an actual downshift mid-descent.

### Principle 6: Specific Gear Recommendations by Terrain Type
This is the practical application of the principles above. Loose climb: first or second, depending on gradient and power delivery — higher on longer climbs to avoid wheel spin. Hard-pack climb: second or third — enough pull without excessive spin risk. Mud climb: higher than instinct (often third) — reducing torque spike prevents immediate wheel dig. Rocky descent with high traction: second — engine braking reliable, disc brakes as supplemental. Loose descent: third or fourth — reduced engine braking prevents rear wheel skip. Mud descent: often third or fourth — same reasoning as loose. These are starting points, not absolutes — the rider must calibrate from these baselines for their specific bike and terrain.

## 3. TECHNIQUE BREAKDOWN BY LEVEL

### 3a. Beginner Level
**Goal:** Establish the habit of pre-selecting gear before hills, and understand that gear is the primary pre-hill decision — not entry speed or body position.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Stop before the climb or descent and consciously select the gear
- For climbs: use second gear as a default starting point on most surfaces
- For descents: use second gear as a default starting point on hard-pack, third on loose
- After each attempt, diagnose: did the gear feel right? Too much spin or not enough torque? Too much engine braking or not enough?

**What Good Looks Like:**
The rider approaches a climb, stops briefly at the base, selects second gear, then enters the climb with committed throttle. On a descent, the rider selects a lower gear at the top before beginning. After each hill, the rider can articulate whether the gear choice felt correct and why, demonstrating awareness of the gear-outcome relationship.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Carrying whatever gear was being used on the approach:** Rider arrives at the climb in fourth gear from fast flat riding and never changes. → Stop, assess, select. The approach gear is not the hill gear.
- **Downshifting mid-climb when power feels insufficient:** This is the most common dangerous mistake. → Select the correct gear before entry. If the climb stalls, deal with the stall and restart with the correct gear next attempt.
- **Selecting first gear for every loose climb:** Too much torque for most surfaces — wheel spins immediately. → First gear is reserved for very steep, very slow climbs. Most loose climbing is done in second.

### 3b. Intermediate Level
**Goal:** Select gear accurately for varied terrain types, understand the two-stroke vs four-stroke gear difference, and use partial clutch as a torque modifier.

**Progression Markers from Beginner:** Pre-selection habit is established. Default gears (second on most climbs, second or third on descents) are producing better outcomes. Beginning to understand why different surface types require different gears.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Surface-specific gear selection: loose (higher gear), hard-pack (lower gear), mud (higher gear counter-intuitively)
- Understanding powerband: staying in the powerband on two-stroke for consistent power
- Partial clutch as a within-gear adjustment: feathering the clutch when the gear provides slightly too much torque
- Post-climb diagnosis: relating gear choice to outcome systematically

**What Good Looks Like:**
Before a loose climb, the rider selects second or third (depending on gradient), uses partial clutch feathering to prevent wheel spin from the first metre, and maintains productive slip throughout. On a loose descent, the rider selects third or fourth before the top, and the bike descends at controlled speed with minimal disc brake use. When the gear feels slightly wrong, the rider reaches for the clutch as a modifier rather than stopping to change gear.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Mud climbing in first gear:** Maximum torque digs the tyre immediately. → Higher gear on mud, then finesse with clutch.
- **Rigid adherence to one gear per terrain type:** Every gravel climb in second regardless of gradient and bike characteristics. → Calibrate from the recommendations. A steeper climb may need first; a longer climb may need third.
- **Ignoring engine characteristics:** Recommendations differ between a high-revving enduro single and a torquey trail bike. → Learn the powerband and torque characteristics of the specific bike.

### 3c. Advanced Level
**Goal:** Gear selection is immediate, automatic, and terrain-specific with no need for stop-and-select procedure — assessed and decided in the seconds of approach.

**Progression Markers from Intermediate:** Surface-specific gear selection is reliable. Clutch-as-modifier technique is consistent. Post-climb analysis is fast and accurate.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Rapid gear assessment: reading the terrain while approaching and selecting the correct gear without stopping
- Competition gear strategy: gear selection decisions under race pressure and time constraint
- Bikes with different characteristics: adapting recommendations to the specific torque and powerband of the bike being ridden
- Two-stroke-specific strategies: managing engine braking absence on descents, powerband management on climbs
- Gear selection for combination terrain: a climb that begins on hard-pack, transitions to loose, and ends on rock — single gear or mid-section management?

**What Good Looks Like:**
Approaching an unfamiliar technical climb, the rider assesses in 2-3 seconds and selects the gear without stopping — perhaps briefly rolling off the throttle to allow an unloaded shift, but not stopping. The first attempt of an unfamiliar climb is in the correct gear bracket. Competition riding: gear selection is integrated into the section-planning process — no mid-section gear decisions.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Rapid assessment errors under race pressure:** Speed of decision compromises quality. → Build a fast-assessment habit deliberately — not just faster, but faster AND accurate.
- **Forgetting to adjust for conditions:** Same climb, different gear in wet vs dry conditions. → Weather state changes the optimal gear selection. Recalibrate each session.
- **Two-stroke riders using four-stroke recommendations:** The powerband-specific nature of two-stroke selection is different. → Know your engine type and apply the appropriate framework.

## 4. DIAGNOSTIC CUES (AI COACHING ENGINE REFERENCE)

### Visual Cues — Correct Technique
- Rider pauses or briefly rolls off throttle before the climb and shifts gear — visible gear selection before entry
- No gear change visible during the climb itself — committed to the pre-selected gear
- On descent: gear selected at top before any slope begins — descent in one gear throughout
- Consistent rear wheel traction on climb — gear choice not producing excessive spin or stall
- No rear wheel skip or hop on descent — engine braking appropriate for the surface

### Visual Cues — Error Indicators
- Gear change visible mid-climb (foot movement, engine note change): missed pre-selection, dangerous reactive adjustment
- Rear wheel spinning freely immediately from the bottom: gear too low for this surface — too much torque
- Bike stalling mid-climb with throttle open: gear too high — engine outside powerband at the hardest point
- Rear wheel skipping rhythmically on descent: engine braking too strong for the surface — gear too low
- Heavy disc brake use on descent: engine braking insufficient — gear too high for this gradient

### Audio Cues
- Engine revving high with minimal forward progress on climb: first or second gear, wheel spin — may need higher gear
- Engine note dropping rapidly on climb, bike stalling: too high a gear, engine bogging — needs lower gear
- Engine braking sound too aggressive on descent (rhythmic rear wheel skip sound): too low a gear for this surface
- Smooth, consistent engine note on descent without heavy brake sounds: correct gear for engine braking level

### Sensor Cues
- Rear wheel speed significantly above front wheel speed on climb: wheel spin — likely too low a gear for this surface
- Engine RPM below powerband on climb with full throttle: gear too high — engine cannot produce peak torque
- Rear wheel speed dropping intermittently on descent: engine braking causing wheel lock — gear too low
- Throttle position high with consistent acceleration on climb: correct gear, engine in powerband, traction maintained

## 5. COACHING CUES & LANGUAGE

### Positive Reinforcement
- "Good gear call — the engine was in the powerband all the way up. I didn't hear it struggling or over-revving. That's exactly the gear choice that cleans climbs."
- "You selected the gear before the descent and held it all the way down. Engine braking was doing the work. That's how you protect your brakes on long descents."
- "Good adjustment on the second attempt — you went one gear higher on that loose section and the spin immediately dropped. You read the surface and changed the variable."

### Corrective Feedback
- "You're carrying your flat-trail gear into the climb. You're in fourth and the engine is bogging — you don't have enough torque. Stop before the next attempt, drop to second, and then commit."
- "You downshifted mid-climb. I heard the missed shift and then the stall. That gear change has to happen before the climb starts, when the gearbox isn't loaded. Once you're on the hill, you ride whatever gear you're in."
- "The engine braking is making the rear skip on that descent. You're in too low a gear for this loose surface. Try third instead of second — let the rear wheel roll more freely and use your rear brake to supplement."
- "You're in first gear on that mud climb. First gear is too much torque for mud — it just digs the wheel. Try second or even third. I know it sounds counterintuitive, but reduce the torque and use the clutch to manage it."

### Progression Prompts
- "Before the next attempt, stop at the base, look at the surface, and tell me what gear you're going to select and why. Say it out loud. Then we'll see if the execution matches the reasoning."
- "Try the same descent in both second and third gear — one pass each. Tell me which one felt more controlled and why. That's your gear for this surface."
- "On the next climb attempt, if you feel like the gear is wrong, don't change it — just use the clutch to adjust. Feather the clutch if there's too much torque. Hold the clutch slightly open if you need more drive. No gear changes on the hill."

## 6. DRILLS & PRACTICE EXERCISES

### Drill 1: Systematic Gear Comparison on a Single Climb
**Purpose:** Directly experience the effect of different gears on the same climb, building the connection between gear choice and outcome.
**Setup:** A consistent loose or mixed climb that the rider is familiar with.
**Execution:**
1. Attempt the climb in first gear. Note: how much spin? Did it clean or stall?
2. Attempt in second gear. Note: same questions.
3. Attempt in third gear. Note: same questions.
4. If possible, attempt in fourth. Note: same questions.
5. Identify which gear produced the best result (most traction, cleanest climb, best momentum carry).
**Success Criteria:** Rider can articulate why one gear outperformed the others in terms of torque, spin, and powerband engagement.
**Duration/Reps:** 2 attempts per gear, minimum.
**Progression:** Repeat on a different surface type (wet, dry, mud). Note how the optimal gear changes with conditions.

### Drill 2: Pre-Selection Protocol Habit Build
**Purpose:** Establish the automatic habit of stopping before a hill to select gear — eliminating mid-climb gear changes.
**Setup:** Any approach to a series of hills or technical sections.
**Execution:**
1. Before every hill, dismount or stop the bike completely.
2. Say aloud: "This hill needs gear [X] because [reason]."
3. Select the gear. Remount (if dismounted). Enter the hill.
4. No gear changes during the hill regardless of outcome.
5. After 20 hills with this protocol, the selection process should be verbal-free but still automatic.
**Success Criteria:** Zero mid-climb gear changes across 20 hill attempts. Verbal reasoning becomes internal and faster.
**Duration/Reps:** 20 hills across 2-3 sessions.
**Progression:** Move to rolling pre-selection: assess and select while still approaching, without stopping — only rolling off the throttle to allow an unloaded shift.

### Drill 3: Clutch-as-Gear-Modifier Drill on a Slope
**Purpose:** Train the clutch feathering technique as a within-gear torque adjustment on hills.
**Setup:** A moderate loose climb in a gear that produces slight wheel spin.
**Execution:**
1. Select the gear. Begin climbing.
2. When wheel spin starts, instead of adjusting throttle, feather the clutch (25% reduction in engagement).
3. Feel the wheel regain bite.
4. Hold the clutch at this position throughout the climb — it should reduce effective torque just enough to prevent spin.
5. Repeat with different clutch positions to find the optimal feather point for this climb.
**Success Criteria:** Rider can maintain productive slip using clutch feathering alone, without throttle adjustment. Can identify the correct clutch position for this gear on this surface.
**Duration/Reps:** 10 passes.
**Progression:** Apply the same technique on a steeper climb. Test on different surface types.

### Drill 4: Two-Surface Descent Comparison
**Purpose:** Demonstrate the surface-specific engine braking difference, building the intuition for loose vs hard-pack descent gear selection.
**Setup:** Two descents of similar gradient: one hard-pack, one loose/gravel.
**Execution:**
1. Descend the hard-pack section in second gear. Evaluate: controlled? Too fast? Rear skipping?
2. Descend the loose section in second gear. Evaluate: same questions.
3. Descend the loose section in third gear. Evaluate: how does this compare to second on the loose?
4. Identify which gear is optimal for each surface type.
**Success Criteria:** Rider can articulate the difference in engine braking effect between the two surfaces and select the optimal gear for each.
**Duration/Reps:** 3 passes on each surface in each gear.
**Progression:** Introduce a mixed descent (hard-pack into loose) and determine whether one gear or a mid-descent change is more appropriate.

## 8. TERRAIN & CONTEXT VARIATIONS

### Sand Climbs
Sand is the surface where the highest gear on a climb is most often appropriate — the tyre needs to skim over the surface rather than dig into it, and too much torque causes immediate dig-in and momentum loss. First gear is almost never correct for sand. Second or third on steep sand dunes, third or fourth on more gradual sand slopes. The combination of a higher gear plus partial clutch feathering provides the most control over torque delivery in sand.

### Mud Climbs
Mud is counterintuitively the other surface where a higher gear than instinct suggests is appropriate. The wheel needs to turn fast enough to self-clean (throw mud off the tyre pattern) but not so fast that it digs. First or second gear produces too much torque spike on each power stroke, causing the wheel to dig rather than clean. Third gear, with careful clutch management, allows the tyre to rotate at a mud-shedding speed without excess torque.

### Rock Descents
On hard rock with excellent traction, engine braking can be maximised — second gear or even first on very steep rocky descents is appropriate. The rock surface provides enough traction to withstand strong engine braking without the rear wheel skipping. Disc brakes are supplemental. The risk of engine braking on rock is minimal.

### Two-Stroke Descents
Without engine braking, two-stroke riders must shift the entire engine braking load to disc brakes. On steep or long descents, disc brakes can overheat with sustained application — brake fade becomes a real risk on two-stroke bikes on extended technical descents. Managing disc brake temperature by using intermittent application rather than continuous drag is more important on two-stroke bikes. Some two-stroke riders use partial clutch engagement on descents to create artificial drag — this reduces brake load but increases clutch wear.

### Race Conditions
In competition, gear selection decisions happen in milliseconds of visual assessment. The pre-selection habit must be deeply automatic. Under race pressure, the common error is defaulting to the same gear regardless of surface because the cognitive bandwidth for surface-specific assessment is consumed by navigation and competitor management. Building the habit to the point of automaticity is the only solution.

## 9. EXPERT INSIGHTS & SOURCES

### Key Insights
- **David Knight** (multiple ISDE and EnduroGP champion): Identifies wrong gear selection as the most common mechanical reason for failed climbs — "The climb doesn't fail in the middle — it fails at the bottom, when the wrong gear was selected. The middle is just where you notice it."
- **Two-stroke enduro coaching (Alfie Cox curriculum):** The powerband management principle is fundamental to two-stroke enduro technique — "On a two-stroke, the powerband is your friend and your enemy. You need to be in it for traction on a climb, but you must enter it gently. If you hit the powerband on a loose surface, the wheel goes instantly."
- **Engine braking physics:** The engine braking force in first gear can be 3-5x greater than in third gear on the same four-stroke engine. This is a dramatic difference — equivalent to changing from light to heavy braking with the lever. Understanding this scale helps riders appreciate why gear selection matters so much on descents.
- **Motocross influence:** MX gear selection for hills is often lower than enduro (more traction on prepared surfaces), and this MX habit carried into enduro by crossover riders creates a common error — too low a gear on loose terrain producing wheel spin. Enduro-specific gear recalibration is often needed.
- **KTM enduro team technical notes:** Race preparation for extreme enduro events includes specific gearing ratio changes (front/rear sprockets) for terrain type — events with predominantly loose climbs use larger rear sprockets (effectively lowering gearing) while descent-heavy events may use smaller rear sprockets to reduce engine braking.
