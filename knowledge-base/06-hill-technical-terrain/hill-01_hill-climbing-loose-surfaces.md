# Hill Climbing (Loose Surfaces)

## 1. OVERVIEW

Loose hill climbing — over sand, loose dirt, gravel, and scree — is one of the most technically demanding skills in off-road riding, and also one of the areas where poor technique has the most immediate and dramatic consequences. The fundamental challenge is that loose surfaces provide little traction just when the engine is demanding maximum grip to fight gravity. The result is a narrow band of productive wheel slip where the rear wheel is generating drive without spinning freely, and the rider must use throttle, clutch, and body position simultaneously to stay inside that band from the bottom of the climb to the top. Half-hearted attempts are more dangerous than walking — an aborted climb mid-way leaves the rider perched on a loose, steep slope with no momentum and a heavy bike. The decision to attempt must be made before entry, and once made, it must be executed with full commitment.

## 2. CORE PRINCIPLES

### Principle 1: Commitment Before Entry Is Non-Negotiable
The climb outcome is largely determined before the front wheel touches the hill. The rider must assess the climb — length, gradient, surface type, line — and decide fully before the entry. A committed rider enters with the correct speed, body position, and throttle intent already set. A hesitant rider enters too slowly, opens throttle reactively, and spins the wheel before traction is lost. Half-committed is not "safer" — it produces the worst outcome: no momentum, full wheel spin, and a stalled bike on a steep loose slope with no easy escape.

### Principle 2: Weight Forward Against Gravity
On a steep climb, gravity constantly tries to tip the bike rearward, which unloads the front wheel and overloads the rear. A rising rear wheel means the rider's weight has shifted back — often without awareness. The correct response is an aggressive forward body position: chest toward the tank, elbows bent and pulled in, hips pushed forward over the pegs. This keeps weight distribution balanced and prevents the front wheel from lifting. If the front comes up during a climb, it is almost always because body position has drifted rearward.

### Principle 3: Smooth Sustained Throttle — Not Progressive Throttle
On loose terrain, smooth throttle from the entry is more effective than building throttle as the climb progresses. The moment throttle is increased sharply mid-climb, the rear wheel breaks free and spins. The correct approach is to establish the throttle position that keeps the rear in productive slip — where the wheel is turning slightly faster than ground speed but still generating drive — and hold that position all the way to the top. There is no waiting to "see how it goes" partway up. Commit to a throttle position and execute.

### Principle 4: Clutch Feathering Maintains the Productive Slip Zone
The clutch is the fine-tuning tool on loose climbs. When the rear wheel begins to spin freely (too much power), feathering the clutch slightly reduces power delivery without a throttle change, allowing the wheel to regain bite. When the wheel is gripping but the engine is labouring (not enough power), opening the clutch restores drive. This feathering happens in small, continuous adjustments — not binary grab-and-release. The clutch becomes a second throttle on loose terrain.

### Principle 5: Standing Is Mandatory on Loose Climbs
A seated rider on a loose climb loads the rear axle directly through the seat, compressing the rear suspension and reducing its ability to manage surface variations. More critically, a seated rider cannot shift weight forward effectively — the pelvis is anchored to the seat. Standing on the pegs allows the rider to push weight forward, flex the legs to absorb surface irregularities, and respond to wheel spin with micro weight shifts that a seated rider simply cannot make.

### Principle 6: Failed Climb Recovery Is a Technique, Not a Failure
Every rider who climbs loose terrain will eventually fail a climb. The correct recovery procedure is: as soon as momentum is lost and the rear is spinning, close the throttle, apply the front brake firmly to arrest the bike's slide, and hold position. From there, manoeuvre the bike across the slope — never let it slide straight down. Turn it perpendicular to the fall line, then walk or ride it back down with the front brake holding. A controlled failed recovery is a skill, and riders should practise it deliberately.

## 3. TECHNIQUE BREAKDOWN BY LEVEL

### 3a. Beginner Level
**Goal:** Successfully climb moderate loose inclines using correct body position and smooth throttle, and execute a controlled failure recovery on easier slopes.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Body position forward before the climb begins — not as a reaction mid-climb
- Consistent throttle from bottom to top — no waiting, no surging
- Standing on the pegs throughout the entire climb
- Identifying the sound and feel of productive wheel slip vs free spin

**What Good Looks Like:**
The rider approaches with committed entry speed, stands upright with a slight forward lean, and opens throttle smoothly as the front wheel begins the incline. The rear wheel shows a slight roost but is not flinging material wildly. The bike tracks straight up the slope without weaving. The rider's chest is clearly forward — not upright or leaning back. The bike reaches the top and the rider releases the throttle smoothly before cresting.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Sitting on the approach and during the climb:** Weight loads the rear, front lifts, grip is lost. → Stand before the slope begins. Never sit on a loose climb.
- **Throttle too late — waiting to "feel" the hill:** Rider enters slowly then attempts to add throttle mid-climb when traction is already compromised. → Set throttle intent before entry. Commit from the first metre.
- **Grabbing throttle sharply when feeling the rear slip:** Natural panic response causes a worse spin. → Feather or hold throttle; smooth beats reactive.
- **Looking at the ground immediately in front of the wheel:** Short vision causes body to tense and wobble. → Look at the top of the hill or the exit point.

### 3b. Intermediate Level
**Goal:** Read loose climbs accurately before entry, select the correct line, manage clutch feathering on marginal traction surfaces, and confidently attempt steeper or longer loose climbs.

**Progression Markers from Beginner:** Rider consistently tops out moderate loose climbs without spinning. Body position is forward automatically. Entry speed decisions are mostly correct.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Clutch feathering as an active throttle management tool
- Line selection within the climb — choosing the firmest surface available
- Momentum calibration: setting entry speed to the hardest point of the climb, not the easiest
- Controlled recovery when the climb fails past the halfway point

**What Good Looks Like:**
The rider walks or scans the climb before entry, identifies the worst traction section, and sets entry speed accordingly. Mid-climb, the rider's clutch hand makes small, continuous adjustments — visible as subtle lever movement. When the rear begins to spin briefly, the rider feathers the clutch and recovers forward drive without stopping. If the climb fails, the rider applies the front brake deliberately, turns the bike across the slope, and dismounts cleanly.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Clutch as an on/off switch:** Grabbing the clutch fully rather than feathering it kills all drive and stalls the bike. → Practice clutch feathering on flat loose surfaces first.
- **Misjudging entry speed — too slow:** Rider runs out of momentum at 70% of the climb. → The toughest section dictates entry speed, not the easiest section.
- **Choosing the rut or dug-out line:** Previous riders' tracks often dig to soft, loose substrate. → Look for the undisturbed, compressed surface alongside the rut.

### 3c. Advanced Level
**Goal:** Clean technically demanding loose climbs with variable traction, including scree, deep gravel, and mixed surfaces, with precise clutch and throttle management throughout.

**Progression Markers from Intermediate:** Rider manages clutch feathering consistently. Entry speed decisions are accurate on familiar terrain. Body position is automatic.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Reading surface variation mid-climb and adapting throttle and clutch in real time
- Managing the front-to-rear traction split on surfaces that change gradient mid-climb
- Body position at maximum forward extent on near-vertical loose climbs
- Energy management in competition — choosing which loose climbs to attempt vs walk based on consequence and bike condition
- Pre-planning the failure recovery point before the attempt

**What Good Looks Like:**
From a chase camera, the rider's body barely moves — legs are absorbing surface variation while the rider's chest stays low over the tank. The rear wheel leaves a consistent roost that is even in volume — not spiking or disappearing — showing the rider is in the productive slip zone continuously. Throttle and clutch adjustments are invisible from the outside; the bike tracks straight and true. On scree, the rider finds purchase quickly and does not allow the wheel to spin freely even momentarily.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Momentum overcorrection — entering too fast:** On scree or deep gravel, too much entry speed causes wheel spin before traction loads. → Entry speed on loose surfaces is lower than instinct suggests; traction must build before momentum peaks.
- **Failing to pre-plan the recovery:** When the climb fails, the rider freezes. → Identify the recovery point (across-slope turn zone) before the attempt begins.
- **Ignoring rear suspension sag under load:** As the bike labours up the climb, rear suspension compresses; if spring rate is too soft, the suspension bottoms and traction disappears. → Sag and spring rate matters specifically on steep loose terrain.

## 4. DIAGNOSTIC CUES (AI COACHING ENGINE REFERENCE)

### Visual Cues — Correct Technique
- Rider's chest visibly forward over the tank from a side camera — torso lean angle roughly parallels the slope
- Rider is standing, with a gap between seat and body throughout the climb
- Rear wheel produces a consistent, moderate roost — even in spray, not erratic
- Bike tracks straight or with minor controlled weave — not oscillating
- Throttle hand position remains relatively stable — no visible jerking
- Rider crests the top and releases throttle smoothly before the bike becomes airborne

### Visual Cues — Error Indicators
- Rider's torso upright or leaning back — body position has drifted rearward
- Rider seated on the approach or during the climb — seat contact visible
- Rear wheel producing large, uneven roost spikes — free spin followed by sudden traction
- Bike weaving side to side on the slope — front wheel unloaded due to weight rearward
- Throttle hand movements visible and jerky — reactive rather than planned
- Rider stops mid-climb — commitment failure or spin-out without recovery

### Audio Cues
- Even engine note with slight load variation: productive slip zone maintained
- Sudden high-rev engine scream with no forward progress: rear wheel in free spin — throttle or clutch overcorrection needed
- Engine labouring (low rpm, bogging): insufficient throttle or clutch slipping too much
- Tyre-to-surface sound: a rhythmic "bite-bite-bite" indicates the wheel is gripping intermittently — correct for loose terrain

### Sensor Cues
- Rear wheel speed significantly higher than front wheel speed: wheel spin detected — check throttle and clutch input
- High throttle position with no forward acceleration increase: wheel spin confirmed
- IMU pitch angle increasing during climb with no rider weight adjustment: body position drifting rearward — coaching intervention needed
- Clutch sensor (if fitted): rapid continuous modulation = good; binary on/off = technique fault

## 5. COACHING CUES & LANGUAGE

### Positive Reinforcement
- "That's it — chest forward, throttle smooth. I can see you're holding the rear in the bite zone. That's the technique right there."
- "Good recovery on that one. You felt it spin, held the throttle, and let the clutch do the adjustment. That's experienced riding."
- "Your entry speed was perfect on that last run — you had momentum through the loose section and didn't need to add mid-climb. That's what assessment looks like."

### Corrective Feedback
- "You sat down just before the hill. That's the problem — your weight went straight to the rear axle and unloaded the front. Stand before the slope, not on it."
- "You're waiting to add throttle until you're halfway up. By then the wheel is already spinning and you've lost momentum. Set the throttle before entry, not during."
- "Your clutch is on or off — I need you feathering it, like a volume dial, not a light switch. Small continuous adjustments, not grab-and-release."
- "You're looking at the front wheel. Look at the top of the hill. Your body will follow your eyes, and right now your eyes are pulling you down."

### Progression Prompts
- "Next attempt: set your body position before the hill, and don't change it until you're over the top. Commit to the position from the bottom."
- "When you feel the rear spinning, try closing the clutch 20% — just 20% — and see if the wheel bites again. Don't close it all the way."
- "Walk the climb before the next attempt. Find the firmest surface. That's your line. Ignore where everyone else has ridden."

## 6. DRILLS & PRACTICE EXERCISES

### Drill 1: Body Position Commitment Drill
**Purpose:** Build habit of correct forward body position before the slope begins — removing the reactive body movement that causes front-wheel lift.
**Setup:** A gentle loose slope, 10-15m long, maximum 15-degree gradient.
**Execution:**
1. Stop the bike 5m before the base of the slope.
2. Adopt full climbing body position: stand, chest forward over tank, elbows bent.
3. Hold the position for 3 seconds before moving.
4. Ride the slope maintaining exactly that position — do not adjust mid-climb.
5. Stop at the top, check body position has not drifted rearward.
**Success Criteria:** Position at top matches position at bottom. Front wheel does not lift. Rear shows controlled roost, not free spin.
**Duration/Reps:** 10 passes on the same slope.
**Progression:** Move to a steeper slope. Add the clutch feathering element on the next progression.

### Drill 2: Clutch Feathering on Flat Loose Surface
**Purpose:** Develop clutch sensitivity before applying it on a slope, where the consequence of errors is higher.
**Setup:** Flat loose surface (gravel, sand, or loose dirt).
**Execution:**
1. Ride in second gear at moderate speed.
2. Find the clutch engagement point — the range where the clutch is neither fully open nor fully closed.
3. Deliberately cause slight wheel spin by increasing throttle, then recover it using only the clutch (no throttle change).
4. Repeat: spin → feather → bite → hold → spin → feather → bite.
5. Goal is to maintain a consistent speed using clutch feathering alone.
**Success Criteria:** Rider can induce and recover wheel spin using clutch only. Response time decreases with repetition.
**Duration/Reps:** 15-20 minutes on the flat before taking it to a slope.
**Progression:** Add slight incline. Then move to actual loose climb with clutch as primary management tool.

### Drill 3: Momentum Calibration Runs
**Purpose:** Train accurate entry speed assessment by systematically testing different entry speeds on the same climb.
**Setup:** A consistent loose climb of 20-30m that the rider has failed or barely cleaned before.
**Execution:**
1. Attempt the climb at what feels like comfortable entry speed. Note where momentum runs out.
2. On the next attempt, add 15% more entry speed. Note result.
3. On the third attempt, add 25% more entry speed. Note result.
4. Identify the minimum speed at which the climb is consistently cleaned.
5. That speed becomes the calibrated entry for that gradient and surface.
**Success Criteria:** Rider identifies the correct entry speed bracket for this surface type and gradient. Can consistently reproduce it.
**Duration/Reps:** 5-8 attempts per session.
**Progression:** Test the calibration on different surface conditions (wetter, drier). Note how surface state affects the required entry speed.

### Drill 4: Controlled Failure Recovery
**Purpose:** Practise failed climb recovery deliberately so that it becomes a calm, automatic procedure rather than a panic situation.
**Setup:** A short, moderate loose slope where stopping mid-climb is safely possible.
**Execution:**
1. Begin the climb with deliberately insufficient entry speed so that the bike stops mid-climb.
2. As forward progress stops, close throttle immediately.
3. Apply front brake firmly to hold position on the slope.
4. With front brake held, manoeuvre the bike 90 degrees across the slope.
5. Walk or ride the bike back down the slope with the front brake controlling speed.
**Success Criteria:** Rider executes the recovery calmly without the bike sliding straight down. Can perform it smoothly within 5 attempts.
**Duration/Reps:** 5-10 recovery exercises per session.
**Progression:** Perform on a steeper or longer slope. Add a failed attempt from higher on the climb.

## 8. TERRAIN & CONTEXT VARIATIONS

### Sand
Deep sand climbing requires slightly lower entry speed than gravel because wheel spin is more damaging — the wheel digs rather than skims. Body position must be even further forward to keep the front wheel skimming rather than ploughing. Use a slightly higher gear than instinct suggests to reduce available torque and prevent digging. If the front wheel begins to push into the sand, shift weight forward and reduce throttle marginally.

### Loose Dirt / Loam
Loose dirt over hard substrate is the most common loose climb surface in enduro. The key variable is depth: shallow loose over hardpack allows more traction than deep loose over soft substrate. Read the surface before entry — a scuffed line from previous riders often reveals the substrate. Pick the undisturbed surface if the revealed substrate is soft; follow the rut if it goes to hard substrate.

### Gravel and Scree
Gravel and scree provide almost no predictable traction — every contact patch is rolling on top of other loose material. Entry speed must be high enough that momentum carries through any traction gaps. Clutch feathering is most critical here: any spike in power delivery causes the entire surface to shift, destroying traction instantly. Use a smooth, sustained throttle at lower engine rpm to avoid shock loading the surface.

### Wet Loose Surfaces
Wet loose terrain — wet gravel, wet sand — is fundamentally harder than dry. Surface particles bind slightly when wet (counterintuitively), but the wet layer over clay or rock is treacherously slippery. Reduce entry speed, use higher gears, and accept more wheel spin as the normal operating state. Body position becomes even more critical: the slightest rearward shift collapses the front-end traction completely.

## 9. EXPERT INSIGHTS & SOURCES

### Key Insights
- **Graham Jarvis** (multiple Erzbergrodeo and Hard Enduro World Championship winner): Emphasises that on extreme loose climbs, the clutch is the primary traction tool — "The throttle sets the power level, the clutch manages what actually reaches the wheel." This is counterintuitive for riders trained primarily on hard-pack.
- **Jonny Walker** (Hard Enduro World Champion): Stresses that the decision to attempt a loose climb must be absolute — "If you're not fully committed, you've already lost. The hill can feel your hesitation."
- **Enduro GP coaching consensus:** Entry speed calibration is consistently identified as the most undertrained skill in loose climbing — riders practise technique but rarely practise setting entry speed systematically.
- **Trials riding influence:** The clutch feathering technique on loose climbs comes directly from trials, where the clutch is used to modulate power delivery on near-zero-traction surfaces. Riders who cross-train in trials show dramatically faster improvement in loose climb technique.
- **Physics of productive slip:** Research in off-road tyre dynamics shows that maximum drive force is generated at approximately 10-20% slip ratio — the tyre turning 10-20% faster than ground speed. Above this, traction collapses and wheel spin is unproductive. The "sound and feel" of productive slip is a trained perception skill.
