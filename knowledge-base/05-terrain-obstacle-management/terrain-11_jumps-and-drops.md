# Jumps and Drops

## 1. OVERVIEW

Jumps and drops are two distinct aerial techniques that are often grouped together but require different approaches. A jump is a projectile launched from a ramp or natural feature — the bike becomes airborne through forward momentum and the geometry of the launch point. A drop is a controlled fall from an edge — the bike leaves the surface and falls rather than being projected. Jumps require specific take-off technique to achieve neutral air attitude; drops require weight management and controlled approach to land safely. Both share the requirements of correct landing technique (both wheels simultaneously or rear-slightly-first, with absorbing knees and bent arms) and the awareness that landing position is determined at take-off — errors at the lip determine landing problems. The primary safety rule for gaps and larger jumps is that tabletops (with a safe landing plateau) must be mastered before gaps (where under-jumping creates a cliff landing). Air control — using throttle and rear brake to adjust pitch during flight — is an advanced technique that allows mid-air attitude correction.

## 2. CORE PRINCIPLES

### Principle 1: Jumps vs Drops — Distinct Technique Requirements
A jump (projectile from a ramp) requires the rider to maintain throttle through the lip — closing the throttle at the lip raises the rear of the bike and produces a nose-high, unstable trajectory. A drop (leaving an edge) requires the rider to slow before the edge and allow the front wheel to fall naturally off the lip, then absorb on landing. Jumps need speed and maintained throttle; drops need speed control and weight management. Applying jump technique to a drop (arriving too fast, maintaining throttle) produces dangerous overshoot. Applying drop technique to a jump (closing throttle at lip) produces a nose-high flight attitude and rear-wheel-first landing.

### Principle 2: Take-Off — Neutral Body Position, Consistent Speed, Throttle Maintained Through Lip
At the take-off lip of a jump, three variables determine the air trajectory: entry speed (determines jump distance), body position (determines initial pitch attitude), and throttle (determines rear wheel behaviour). The correct combination is: consistent approach speed appropriate to the intended landing zone, neutral body position (weight central, not leaned forward or back), and throttle maintained through the lip without closing. Closing the throttle at the lip causes the rear wheel to decelerate and the rear of the bike to rise (creating a nose-low attitude). Adding throttle aggressively at the lip causes the front to rise (nose-high attitude). Neutral, consistent throttle through the lip is the foundation.

### Principle 3: Landing — Both Wheels Simultaneously or Rear-Slightly-First
A correct jump landing contacts both wheels at near-simultaneously, or the rear wheel slightly before the front. This distributes the impact force through the chassis and allows the suspension to absorb symmetrically. A front-wheel-first landing is the most dangerous outcome — the forks absorb the full impact, the front tyre may buckle, and the rider's weight pitches forward into a potential endo. A rear-wheel-only landing (very nose-high) causes rear wheel slam and pitches the front down violently. The landing attitude is determined primarily at take-off: correct take-off produces correct landing attitude. Landing absorption requires bent arms and bent knees, compressing through impact.

### Principle 4: Drops — Approach Slow, Weight Back, Front Drops Off
A drop is approached more slowly than a jump because the goal is a controlled fall, not a projection. The front wheel reaches the edge and falls below the rim — the rider allows this by maintaining slight rearward weight. As the front drops, the body remains upright and the legs absorb the initial front-fall through bending. The rear wheel follows over the edge and the bike begins to fall as a unit. The landing is absorbed through both wheels simultaneously with knees and arms compressing. The height of the drop determines the landing impact force — small drops absorb naturally; larger drops require significant leg and arm compression. Weight rearward prevents the bike from pitching forward during the drop.

### Principle 5: Air Control — Throttle and Rear Brake for Pitch Adjustment
During flight, the rider has two controls for adjusting pitch attitude (nose up or down): throttle (adding throttle accelerates the rear wheel and the chain-drive effect raises the rear, pitching the nose down); and rear brake (applying the rear brake slows the rear wheel, which through gyroscopic effect pitches the nose up). These corrections are small — subtle inputs that adjust rather than dramatically change attitude. Air control is an advanced technique used to correct minor take-off errors. A rider who is slightly nose-high (common) uses rear brake gently to bring the nose down. A rider who is slightly nose-low uses a brief throttle pulse to raise the rear and bring the nose up.

### Principle 6: Safety Hierarchy — Tabletop Before Gap, Assessment Before Commitment
The safety hierarchy for jumps is: tabletop (a jump with a plateau landing — under-jumping lands safely on the flat) before gap (a jump where the landing is below the take-off — under-jumping means landing on the edge or face of the landing). Any gap jump that cannot be safely attempted at minimum clearing speed should not be attempted until the required speed and technique are confirmed. Pre-flight check: assess the landing zone before committing. Look for: landing slope (downhill is better — it reduces impact), landing surface (soft is better), and clearance required (how far the rider must jump). Never jump blind — unknown landing zones are avoided.

## 3. TECHNIQUE BREAKDOWN BY LEVEL

### 3a. Beginner Level
**Goal:** Jumps and drops are not entry-level enduro techniques. Beginners should focus on natural terrain jumps — rises and compressions that produce minimal air — and learn drop technique from very small drops (30–50cm) before progressing.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Maintaining throttle through small natural rise take-offs — no closing
- Landing absorption — bent knees, bent arms, compressing through impact
- Small drops — slow approach, weight back, front drops off
- Assessment habit — look at the landing zone before committing

**What Good Looks Like:**
The rider rolls a small natural rise and maintains throttle — the bike gets brief air and lands flat with both wheels together. The rider absorbs through bent knees and arms. On a small drop, the rider approaches slowly, weight back, allows the front to drop, and absorbs the landing.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Closing throttle at a natural take-off:** Instinct lifts off the throttle when the ground falls away. → Hold the throttle — closure at lip produces tail-high, dangerous attitude.
- **Stiff legs on landing:** Impact is absorbed by arms and spine. → Active leg compression through landing — treat it like a standing jump landing.
- **Approaching a drop too fast:** The front projects off the edge rather than dropping. → Drops require slow approach; jumps require committed speed. Do not confuse them.

### 3b. Intermediate Level
**Goal:** Jump small-medium natural jumps cleanly (correct attitude, controlled landing). Execute drops from moderate heights. Begin developing air control awareness.

**Progression Markers from Beginner:** Small natural rises handled with maintained throttle. Small drop technique established. Landing absorption is consistent.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Consistent approach speed — not variable between attempts
- Neutral body position at the lip — not leaned forward or back
- Tabletop progression before attempting gaps
- Air control introduction — noticing attitude in the air, beginning to adjust

**What Good Looks Like:**
The rider approaches a moderate tabletop at consistent speed, takes off with neutral body and maintained throttle, achieves a flat, controlled trajectory, and lands both wheels together with compressed absorption. The rider can correct minor attitude issues (slight nose-high) with subtle rear brake.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Inconsistent approach speed:** Different distances on each attempt — indicates inconsistent commitment or throttle. → Count approach markers and replicate the speed each time.
- **Leaning forward at the lip:** Common fear response — leans into the jump. → Neutral position at lip; neither forward nor back.
- **Skipping tabletops to attempt gaps:** The gap lands severely if technique is imperfect. → All gap jumps must be successfully completed as tabletops first.

### 3c. Advanced Level
**Goal:** Jump race-pace natural and constructed jumps. Execute large drops. Use air control confidently. Manage jump sequences and timing.

**Progression Markers from Intermediate:** Tabletops mastered. Moderate drops executed confidently. Air control beginning. Consistent landing attitude.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Race-speed jump assessment and commitment
- Air control — both throttle and rear brake for attitude adjustment
- Jump sequences — timing multiple jumps in rhythm
- Landing on varied surfaces (loose, hard, rutted)

**What Good Looks Like:**
The rider jumps at race pace — assessment is rapid, commitment is decisive, take-off is consistent. In the air, attitude is flat and controlled. Any minor adjustment is made with subtle rear brake or throttle. Landing is absorbed cleanly and speed is maintained or rebuilt immediately after landing.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Over-correcting with air control:** Large throttle or brake input in the air causes overcorrection — nose drops too far or rises too far. → Air control inputs must be small — gentle adjustments.
- **Landing on unprepared surfaces:** Hard pack landing after a soft-take-off adjustment. → Always assess the landing surface, not just the jump geometry.
- **Jump sequence timing errors:** Jumping the second jump at the wrong speed because the first jump altered speed. → Speed recovery between jumps in a sequence must be planned.

## 4. DIAGNOSTIC CUES (AI COACHING ENGINE REFERENCE)

### Visual Cues — Correct Technique
- Neutral body position at take-off: rider not leaned forward or back, weight central
- Both wheels leaving the take-off point simultaneously (for ramp jumps)
- Flat trajectory in the air — bike parallel to the ground at mid-flight
- Both wheels landing simultaneously or rear fractionally first
- Visible knee and arm compression at landing — absorption happening
- Front wheel not slamming down first (from chase or side camera: rear and front contact near-simultaneously)

### Visual Cues — Error Indicators
- Nose-high attitude in the air (rear higher than front): throttle closed at lip or aggressive throttle before lip
- Nose-low attitude (front lower than rear): too much throttle at lip or weight too far forward
- Front wheel landing first with dramatic fork compression: nose-low issue — dangerous landing
- Stiff legs and arms on landing — body rigid rather than absorbing
- Rider leaning forward aggressively at the lip (visible from side camera)
- Closing throttle visible from rear camera (brake light activating) at the lip of a jump

### Audio Cues
- Consistent engine note through take-off lip: correct throttle maintenance
- Engine note dropping at the lip: throttle closed — error
- Engine note spike just before or at the lip: throttle added at lip — too aggressive
- Clean dual-impact on landing (two close simultaneous thumps): wheels landing together
- Single large impact (one thump): front or rear wheel landing alone — incorrect attitude
- Rear brake squeal in the air: air control being applied — check if attitude correction is visible

### Sensor Cues
- Accelerometer showing smooth upward arc then downward arc (projectile flight): correct jump
- Sudden large accelerometer spike on landing: hard landing — likely stiff absorption or front-first
- Gyroscope pitch data showing nose tilting down during flight: nose-low — either throttle correction needed or take-off error
- Gyroscope pitch showing nose tilting up: nose-high — rear brake correction or reduce throttle at lip
- GPS speed showing consistent approach speed across multiple attempts: good technique consistency

## 5. COACHING CUES & LANGUAGE

### Positive Reinforcement
- "Perfect take-off — you held the throttle through the lip and the bike was flat in the air. That's the neutral attitude we're looking for."
- "Good absorption on landing — I could see your knees compressing. The bike landed, you went with it, and you were back on the throttle immediately. That's how to flow through a jump."
- "Nice drop — slow approach, weight back, front dropped off cleanly, and you absorbed through bent knees on landing. That's the technique."

### Corrective Feedback
- "You closed the throttle at the lip and the rear came up. That's why you were nose-high and landed rear-first. Hold the throttle through the lip — it feels counterintuitive but it keeps the attitude flat."
- "Your legs were straight on landing. That's all the impact going into your spine and arms. Bend the knees when you see the ground coming — compress into the landing."
- "You leaned forward at the lip. That pitches the nose down. Neutral position at take-off — not forward, not back. Stand straight and let the bike take off under you."
- "You approached that drop at jump speed. Drops need slow approach — the front needs to drop off the edge, not project off it. Slow down for drops."

### Progression Prompts
- "Before the next jump, tell me the landing zone: where is it? What's the surface? Then commit to clearing it. No blind jumps."
- "Try this: on the next take-off, consciously think 'hold the throttle' as you hit the lip. Say it out loud if necessary. That single cue will fix the nose-high problem."
- "Practice landing absorption separately — ride off a 30cm kerb or step and focus only on compressing through landing. Get the leg absorption reflex before adding jump complexity."

## 6. DRILLS & PRACTICE EXERCISES

### Drill 1: Throttle-Through-Lip Habit
**Purpose:** Build the reflex of maintaining throttle through a jump take-off.
**Setup:** A small natural rise or constructed tabletop, minimal height, maximum 30cm of air.
**Execution:**
1. Approach at slow pace and deliberately close throttle at the lip. Observe the tail-high attitude.
2. Approach again and hold throttle constant through the lip. Observe the improved attitude.
3. Approach at the same speed but add a small throttle increase at the lip. Observe nose-high result.
4. Repeat the constant-throttle approach 15 times until it is automatic.
**Success Criteria:** Rider maintains consistent throttle through the lip on every attempt. No throttle closure reflex on take-off.
**Duration/Reps:** 25 jump attempts on the small take-off.
**Progression:** Increase height slightly. Apply the same throttle discipline to the larger jump.

### Drill 2: Landing Absorption Isolation
**Purpose:** Develop knee and arm absorption reflex separate from jump complexity.
**Setup:** A very small drop (30cm or less) or a curb.
**Execution:**
1. Ride off the small drop at walking pace.
2. At landing, consciously bend knees and compress downward — exaggerate the flex.
3. Feel the difference between landing with stiff legs (jarring impact) and bent legs (absorbed impact).
4. Repeat 20 times, focusing only on the landing compression quality.
**Success Criteria:** Rider absorbs every landing with visible knee compression. No stiff-leg landings in 20 attempts.
**Duration/Reps:** 30 landing absorption repetitions.
**Progression:** Increase drop height gradually. Add forward speed. Introduce jumps once absorption reflex is consistent.

### Drill 3: Drop Technique Progression
**Purpose:** Develop confident drop technique from small to moderate heights.
**Setup:** Three drops at increasing heights: 30cm, 60cm, 1m.
**Execution:**
1. Start at 30cm drop. Approach very slowly. Allow front to drop off the edge. Absorb landing.
2. Repeat 10 times at 30cm until it feels controlled and automatic.
3. Move to 60cm. Repeat the process — slow approach, weight back, front drops.
4. Only progress to 1m when 60cm is consistently controlled.
**Success Criteria:** Rider can execute all three heights with controlled approach, correct weight position, and absorbed landing. No rushing the progression.
**Duration/Reps:** 10 repetitions per height before progressing.
**Progression:** Add a corner after the drop. Increase height only when lower height is consistently clean.

### Drill 4: Air Control Introduction
**Purpose:** Develop awareness of pitch attitude in the air and introduce gentle rear-brake correction.
**Setup:** A reliable tabletop (safe landing plateau) where the take-off can be reproduced consistently.
**Execution:**
1. First 5 jumps: neutral take-off with no air control. Observe natural attitude in the air.
2. Next 5 jumps: at the mid-point of flight, gently apply rear brake for 0.5 seconds. Observe nose rising.
3. Next 5 jumps: at mid-flight, gently blip throttle. Observe rear rising (nose-down tendency).
4. If nose-high is the natural error: use gentle rear brake at mid-flight to correct.
5. If nose-low is the natural error: use gentle throttle blip at mid-flight to correct.
**Success Criteria:** Rider can identify their natural air attitude and apply the correct subtle correction to achieve a flatter trajectory.
**Duration/Reps:** 20 jumps with conscious air control practice.
**Progression:** Apply air control at higher speeds. Introduce correction timing earlier in the flight arc.

## 8. TERRAIN & CONTEXT VARIATIONS

### Natural Dirt Jump (Mound / Rise)
The most common enduro airborne scenario — a natural rise with no constructed lip. Variable take-off angle depending on terrain shape. Approach speed calibration critical — the take-off angle changes the required speed. Standing, neutral body, throttle through.

### Constructed Tabletop
Defined take-off lip and plateau landing. Safest jump format — under-jumping lands on the flat. Use tabletops to practice technique before progressing to gaps. The defined lip makes throttle-through-lip easier to practice than natural terrain.

### Gap Jump
Take-off and landing separated by a gap. Under-jumping lands on the face or edge of the landing — potentially catastrophic. Must be mastered as a tabletop at the same speed before the gap version is attempted. Always assess minimum clearing speed before committing.

### Step-Down Drop
A drop where the landing is significantly lower than the take-off. The bike falls further after the edge than a flat drop. Weight significantly rearward — the front must stay up during the fall, not dive. Absorb a proportionally larger landing impact.

### Jumps on Descents
The approach speed is gravity-assisted and the landing slope reduces the impact. But mis-judging the landing zone angle can cause a front-first impact if the approach and landing slopes don't align. Descents also mean post-landing braking is on a slope — increased care.

## 9. EXPERT INSIGHTS & SOURCES

### Key Insights
- **Motocross heritage:** Jump technique in enduro is inherited directly from motocross, where jumps are central to the sport. MX coaches universally teach throttle through the lip as the most important single cue — and the throttle-closure reflex as the most common error. Enduro's natural jumps follow the same physics but have less predictable geometry.
- **Taddy Blazusiak** (five-time X-Games gold medallist, extreme enduro champion): Blazusiak is noted for using natural terrain features — logs, rocks, erosion lips — as jump ramps in ways that other riders simply do not see. This identifies natural launch features is itself an advanced perceptual skill, layered on top of the execution technique.
- **Drop height and force:** The force of landing from a drop increases with the square of the height. A drop from 1 metre produces approximately 4 times the landing force of a drop from 0.5 metres. This quantifies why height progression in drop practice must be gradual — the physical demands increase non-linearly.
- **Air time and traction windows:** Airborne time removes the tyre from the ground, eliminating traction. Brake inputs during airborne phases apply to wheel rotation only — they affect landing attitude but do not produce vehicle deceleration. Riders must understand this: the bike does not slow in the air from braking — it pitches.
- **Pre-landing checklist:** Professional training frameworks for jumps include a mental pre-flight checklist: correct speed, landing zone confirmed, neutral body, throttle ready. This brief mental preparation (executed in the second before take-off) reduces unplanned inputs at the lip and improves consistency.
