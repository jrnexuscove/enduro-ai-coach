# Rock Gardens

## 1. OVERVIEW

A rock garden is a section of trail or terrain covered by dense, irregular rocks requiring continuous navigation decision-making across a sustained area — not a single rock but a field of them. Rock gardens demand two distinct but simultaneous skill sets: macro line selection (choosing the overall path through the garden before entering) and micro absorption (managing each individual rock within the chosen line while maintaining momentum). These operate at different levels of attention — the macro line is identified before and at the start of the section, the micro absorption is near-unconscious, handled by active legs and body position while the brain focuses on vision 5+ rocks ahead. Speed consistency is often more important than perfect line selection — a consistent pace prevents compression packing into individual rocks and allows the suspension to work optimally. Rock gardens separate riders who have mastered basic rocky terrain technique from those who can apply it continuously under sustained complexity.

## 2. CORE PRINCIPLES

### Principle 1: Macro Line Selection Before Entering — Plan Then Execute
Before entering a rock garden, identify the overall path through it. This is the macro line: it threads through the densest rock concentrations to find the route with the most manageable rocks, the best approach angles, and the clearest exits. The macro line is not necessarily the shortest path. It may take the rider to one side of the garden, curve through a relatively open section, and aim for a specific exit point. This selection happens before entering — either by stopping to scan, or by identifying the line at approach speed from sufficient distance. Once inside a complex rock garden at speed, there is insufficient time to identify a macro line. It must exist before entry.

### Principle 2: Speed Consistency Is More Important Than Perfect Line
Once committed to the macro line, consistent speed through the rock garden matters more than small deviations from the ideal line. A rider who maintains consistent speed handles each rock with the same suspension response — the tyres, chassis, and body have found a rhythm. A rider who accelerates and decelerates within the rock garden packs into rocks during deceleration and launches off them during acceleration, losing both traction and control. Speed consistency allows the rock garden to be ridden rhythmically rather than reactively.

### Principle 3: Vision 5+ Rocks Ahead — Continuous Forward Scanning
Looking down at the rock under the front wheel is the most common and most limiting rock garden error. By the time a rock is visible directly in front of the wheel, there is no time to react meaningfully. Vision must be directed at least 5 rocks ahead — identifying the path, preparing for the next significant obstacle, and keeping the brain upstream of the body's actions. This requires genuine discipline because the rocks directly ahead are visually alarming. Experienced rock garden riders appear calm because their brain is working on rocks they haven't reached yet, while their body handles current rocks on autopilot through trained position and suspension absorption.

### Principle 4: Throttle Modulation Between Rocks — The Rock Garden Rhythm
In a rock garden, throttle is not constant. Brief throttle pulses in the clearer sections between rocks maintain speed and drive; throttle is neutral or slightly reduced during rock contact. This creates a rhythmic cadence across the rock garden rather than a constant drive that loads the bike against each rock. The rhythm becomes automatic with practice — experienced riders feel the cadence rather than thinking about it. Disruption of this rhythm (a large individual rock, an unexpected depth change) must be absorbed and the rhythm re-established quickly.

### Principle 5: Body Weight Balanced — Legs Active Throughout
In a rock garden, body weight is central to slightly rearward — enough to allow the front wheel to roll over rocks rather than deflect off them, but not so rearward that front tyre tracking is compromised. Critically, the legs must be continuously active — not held statically in any position but continuously absorbing individual rocks and variations, keeping the torso relatively stable while the bike moves beneath it. Passive standing (standing but with stiff legs) in a rock garden provides no suspension benefit. The legs must be a functioning part of the suspension system throughout the section.

### Principle 6: Rock Garden Exit — Re-Establishing Normal Pace
The rock garden exit is where riders lose time through over-caution or poor preparation. The exit is the transition from rock garden pace (managed, rhythmic, moderate speed) to trail pace (faster, more aggressive). This transition should be smooth — the rider identifies the exit zone before reaching it (macro planning), maintains speed into the exit rather than decelerating at the end of the rock garden, and accelerates smoothly on the clear surface beyond. An abrupt change of pace at the exit (sudden brake or sudden acceleration) is both unnecessary and disruptive.

## 3. TECHNIQUE BREAKDOWN BY LEVEL

### 3a. Beginner Level
**Goal:** Rock gardens are not appropriate for beginners who have not mastered basic rocky terrain navigation (TERRAIN-03). This section addresses the transition from individual rock technique to continuous rock sections for early intermediate riders.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Identify a clear macro line before entering — stop if needed
- Maintain a consistent, moderate pace through the garden — no stopping mid-section
- Vision forward — specifically avoid looking at the wheel
- Let the bike work — trust suspension and active legs

**What Good Looks Like:**
The rider stops briefly before the rock garden, identifies a clear path, enters at a committed moderate speed, and maintains that speed through the section. They look ahead, not down. There are bumps and deflections but the bike keeps moving. The section is completed in one continuous motion.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Entering without a macro line:** Making directional decisions inside the rock garden at speed. → Stop before, identify the path, then enter.
- **Stopping mid-section:** Once stopped in a rock garden, restarting is much harder. → Commit to the macro line before entering; do not stop unless unavoidable.
- **Vision on the wheel:** Rider staring at rocks directly beneath. → Consciously force eyes ahead — pick a feature 10+ metres forward and look at it.

### 3b. Intermediate Level
**Goal:** Identify and ride the macro line through complex rock gardens, maintain speed consistency, and apply active leg absorption throughout.

**Progression Markers from Beginner:** Rider can complete simple rock gardens without stopping. Basic rocky terrain technique is established. Vision is beginning to extend forward.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Macro line that threads around, not through, the densest rock concentrations
- Speed calibration — finding the correct pace for a specific rock garden
- Throttle rhythm — drive in gaps, neutral on rocks
- Managing individual large rocks within the macro line

**What Good Looks Like:**
The rider scans the rock garden from distance, identifies a non-obvious line that avoids the worst clusters, enters at a calibrated speed, and maintains it with throttle rhythm. Legs absorb individual rocks while the torso stays relatively stable. The exit is clean and controlled.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Macro line that goes directly through the worst rocks:** Taking the obvious (direct) line rather than the manageable one. → The macro line should actively avoid the largest and most irregular rocks where the surrounding route is viable.
- **Speed inconsistency:** Accelerating between groups of rocks and decelerating into the next group. → Find a consistent speed and hold it with throttle management.
- **Rigid legs:** Standing but not absorbing — legs acting as rigid struts. → Exaggerate the leg flex — actively compress and extend with each rock.

### 3c. Advanced Level
**Goal:** Race-pace rock garden execution — fast macro line identification, high-speed micro absorption, consistent speed throughout, and partner coordination in multi-rider scenarios.

**Progression Markers from Intermediate:** Rider navigates complex rock gardens at moderate pace. Throttle rhythm is established. Leg absorption is active. Macro line selection is consistent.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- High-speed macro line identification at approach speed — no stopping to scan
- Managing large square-edge rocks within the flowing macro line
- Partner and group riding through rock gardens — maintaining space, avoiding line conflicts
- Rock garden in varied light — dappled forest, late day shadows hiding rock features

**What Good Looks Like:**
The rider reads the rock garden at approach speed, commits to a macro line without stopping, maintains a high, consistent pace through the section with active absorption and throttle rhythm, and exits at the same pace rather than decelerating. In multi-rider situations, the rider leaves appropriate spacing and does not follow directly in a partner's wheel track (which may not be the optimal line for their bike).

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Following another rider's line without independent assessment:** The other rider's line suits their technique, bike, and body weight — it may not suit yours. → Always identify your own macro line.
- **High-speed rock garden in poor light:** Shadows hide rock depth and shape. → Reduce speed in rock gardens when lighting reduces surface reading capability.
- **Over-fixating on a single large rock:** Staring at one difficult rock causes the rider to miss the macro line and approach at a sub-optimal angle. → Mark the large rock in peripheral vision; eyes stay on the exit line beyond it.

## 4. DIAGNOSTIC CUES (AI COACHING ENGINE REFERENCE)

### Visual Cues — Correct Technique
- Rider pausing or scanning before the rock garden entry (from forward camera or observer)
- Consistent bike speed through the section — no dramatic deceleration or acceleration within the garden
- Rider's head up and looking forward — not tracking the wheel or looking down
- Legs visibly active: continuous compression and extension with individual rock contacts
- Torso remaining relatively stable while the bike moves beneath — upper/lower decoupling visible
- Line threading around major obstacles rather than directly over them

### Visual Cues — Error Indicators
- Rider stopping mid-rock-garden — momentum lost, restart required
- Speed oscillating dramatically: acceleration then braking as the rider reacts to individual rocks
- Rider's head visibly tracking the front wheel — looking down
- Legs static while standing: passive standing with no absorption
- Bike repeatedly deflecting off rock faces rather than rolling over them — wrong approach angles from poor macro line
- Rider taking the centre/direct line straight through the densest concentration of rocks

### Audio Cues
- Rhythmic, consistent engine note with brief throttle variations: correct speed consistency and throttle rhythm
- Harsh individual impacts without corresponding engine note changes: rider absorbing poorly — large impacts reaching frame level
- Engine note varying dramatically (high then low then high): speed inconsistency — rider accelerating and braking within the garden
- High-frequency, consistent low-level impact sounds: correct rolling-over technique with manageable rock contact

### Sensor Cues
- GPS speed consistent through rock garden (±15% variation): correct speed management
- GPS speed showing large oscillations within the rock section: acceleration-deceleration problem
- Accelerometer showing high-frequency moderate-amplitude signals: rolling over rocks correctly — suspension working rhythmically
- Accelerometer showing occasional large spikes: individual large rock impacts — manageable if not frequent
- Frequent high-amplitude accelerometer spikes: wrong approach angles, deflection events

## 5. COACHING CUES & LANGUAGE

### Positive Reinforcement
- "Good macro line — you went around the big cluster on the left and threaded through the cleaner section. That's reading the garden."
- "Consistent speed the whole way through. I can see the throttle rhythm — drive in the gaps, soft on the rocks. The bike is working for you."
- "Your legs are working. The bike is bouncing underneath you but your head is staying level. That's what active absorption looks like."

### Corrective Feedback
- "You went straight through the middle of the worst bit. The manageable line was to the right — you just had to see it before you entered. Stop first, look for the cleaner path."
- "Your speed is all over the place in there. You're accelerating to the rocks and then braking into them. Find one speed and hold it. The suspension does better with consistency."
- "Your head is down — you're watching the wheel. I need your eyes 5 rocks ahead minimum. Pick a reference point beyond the rock garden and keep your eyes on that, not the rocks under your wheel."
- "Your legs are rigid — you're standing but not absorbing. Every rock impact is going straight into your arms. Bend the knees and let the rock come up into you rather than pushing down against it."

### Progression Prompts
- "Before the next rock garden, stop at the edge and point to your macro line. Tell me specifically where you're going to go. Then ride exactly that."
- "Next run, count the rocks ahead. At any point in the garden, you should be able to tell me which rock you're currently managing, and which one you're looking at. If you can't, your vision is too close."
- "Try riding the garden at 20% slower than your instinct. Notice how the suspension has more time to work. Then build speed back up from there."

## 6. DRILLS & PRACTICE EXERCISES

### Drill 1: Macro Line Identification and Execution
**Purpose:** Develop the habit of identifying and committing to a macro line before entering a rock garden.
**Setup:** A natural or constructed rock garden, 30–50m in length.
**Execution:**
1. Stop at the entry point. Scan the entire section.
2. Identify the macro line verbally — "I'll go left around the big cluster, through the middle section, exit on the right."
3. Enter and execute exactly that line.
4. After exiting, compare what was planned with what was ridden. Did the execution match the plan?
5. Repeat, each time identifying and then executing a specific line.
**Success Criteria:** Execution matches the planned macro line in 8 out of 10 passes. Rider can describe the line before and after riding it.
**Duration/Reps:** 20 passes with verbal planning.
**Progression:** Identify the macro line without stopping — from 20m approach distance at moderate speed. Identify, commit, execute.

### Drill 2: Speed Consistency Calibration
**Purpose:** Find and maintain the consistent speed that works best for a specific rock garden.
**Setup:** A rock garden section, 20–30m.
**Execution:**
1. First pass: very slow. Feel the individual rock impacts and the difficulty of maintaining balance.
2. Second pass: faster. Note the smoothing effect of speed on suspension response.
3. Third pass: moderate "sweet spot" speed. Identify the pace where the bike flows rather than deflects.
4. Subsequent passes: maintain exactly that speed with throttle management. No acceleration or braking within the section.
**Success Criteria:** Rider identifies the speed sweet spot and can maintain it with throttle for 5 consecutive passes.
**Duration/Reps:** 20 passes at calibrated speed.
**Progression:** Apply speed calibration to a different rock garden with different characteristics.

### Drill 3: Throttle Rhythm Practice
**Purpose:** Build the rock garden throttle rhythm — drive in gaps, neutral on rocks.
**Setup:** A moderately dense rock section where rock-to-gap alternation is visible.
**Execution:**
1. Ride the section consciously applying throttle in gaps and reducing to neutral on rock contacts.
2. The rhythm should feel like: "drive... rock... drive... rock..." — a consistent alternation.
3. Exaggerate the rhythm first — make the throttle changes obvious to feel them clearly.
4. Gradually reduce the exaggeration until the rhythm is subtle but present.
**Success Criteria:** Rider can sustain the throttle rhythm for the full section. Speed through the section is more consistent than without the rhythm.
**Duration/Reps:** 20 minutes on the throttle rhythm.
**Progression:** Increase rock garden complexity. Apply the rhythm to irregular rocks where the gap-to-rock timing varies.

### Drill 4: Active Leg Absorption Comparison
**Purpose:** Feel the difference between passive standing and active leg absorption in a rock garden.
**Setup:** A known rock garden section.
**Execution:**
1. First pass: stand with deliberately stiff legs — hold them rigidly. Feel how impacts reach the upper body and arms.
2. Second pass: stand with actively flexing legs — compress on each rock hit, extend in the gaps. Feel how impacts are absorbed before reaching the torso.
3. Third and subsequent passes: maximise leg activity — exaggerate the flex to reinforce the muscle memory.
**Success Criteria:** Rider can clearly feel the difference between passive and active leg absorption. Active absorption becomes the default setting.
**Duration/Reps:** 15 comparative passes.
**Progression:** Practice active absorption at increasing rock garden speeds.

## 8. TERRAIN & CONTEXT VARIATIONS

### Rounded River Rock Gardens
Excellent grip potential when dry — rounded shapes allow roll-over at manageable speeds. When wet, very slippery surfaces. The rolling line is easier to find because rounded rocks have clear high-points to aim for. Main skill: speed calibration and leg absorption.

### Angular Quarried Rock Gardens
Maximum technical demand. Square-edged rocks require precise approach angles. Macro line selection is critical — avoiding face-on approaches to square edges. More likely to see significant wheel deflection events. Approach angles and micro throttle timing more critical than in rounded rock gardens.

### Rocky Descents (Rock Garden on Slope)
Weight significantly rearward. Rear brake primary. Speed management before the descent — cannot brake on the rocks. Front wheel must remain light but tracking — not heavily loaded. The macro line on a descent is chosen for braking and exit capability, not just obstacle avoidance.

### Rocky Climbs
Weight forward. Throttle maintained to prevent rear wheel spin and stall. Momentum approach — cannot restart from zero on a steep rocky climb. Macro line targets sections of most consolidated rock for maximum rear tyre grip.

### Mixed Rock and Mud Gardens
The worst combination. Mud between rocks is zero grip; rocks are grip. Weight and throttle adjustments happen within single bike lengths. Vision must be active to identify whether the next surface is rock or mud. Speed lower than pure rock gardens.

## 9. EXPERT INSIGHTS & SOURCES

### Key Insights
- **Wade Young and Taddy Blazusiak** (multiple Hard Enduro World Championships): Both riders are noted for extraordinary composure in extreme rock gardens. Analysis of their technique consistently reveals the same pattern: they scan far ahead, their speed is consistent, and their body absorbs independently of the bike. The composure is not natural ability — it is the result of macro-line planning combined with learned micro-absorption.
- **Erzbergrodeo Hare Scramble (Austria):** The most demanding rock garden enduro in the world. The Eisenerz iron ore mountain terrain is almost entirely large angular rock gardens. Only approximately 5% of starters finish. Analysis of successful finishers shows consistent macro-line selection at each major section as the distinguishing factor.
- **WESS (World Enduro Super Series) rock sections:** Rock garden technique is identified as the primary differentiator between top-15 and top-5 finishers in high rock-content stages. Speed consistency and vision are cited in athlete interviews as the primary skills.
- **Tyre setup for rock gardens:** Tyre inserts (foam mousse) are standard in hard enduro rock gardens — the risk of pinch flats on sharp angular rocks is very high with conventional tubes. Mousse allows lower effective pressures for better tyre deformation and grip.
- **The rhythm concept:** Professional rock garden riders describe the experience as "rhythm terrain" — when the technique is right, the garden has a cadence, like riding a rhythm in motocross. This rhythm is speed-consistent, absorption-active riding. When the rhythm breaks (wrong speed, wrong line), the garden becomes chaotic and tiring.
