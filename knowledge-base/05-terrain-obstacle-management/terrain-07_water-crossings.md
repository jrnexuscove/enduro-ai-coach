# Water Crossings

## 1. OVERVIEW

Water crossings are one of the most distinct skill sets in enduro riding — they require a stop-and-assess discipline before entry, then absolute commitment once the decision to cross is made. Hesitation in the middle of a water crossing is one of the most reliable ways to stall the engine or lose balance, so the correct rhythm is assess thoroughly, decide, commit fully, and execute. The depth of the water, the nature of the bottom (rocky, muddy, sandy), and the presence of current all affect technique. Post-crossing brake care is often overlooked by beginners — wet brakes provide dramatically reduced stopping power and require a brief drying procedure before the rider is again safe at speed. Knowing when not to cross is also a skill — some crossings exceed safe limits and require an alternate route or walking.

## 2. CORE PRINCIPLES

### Principle 1: Stop and Assess Before Every Unknown Crossing
Never enter an unknown water crossing at speed. Stop before the entry point, assess depth (use a stick, wade if necessary, observe bottom visibility), identify the bottom type (rock, mud, sand, gravel), check for current direction and strength, and identify the exit point. This assessment determines technique: shallow with a rocky bottom (ride through confidently), moderate depth with a muddy bottom (slower approach, careful balance), deep with current (very different approach or do not cross). The information gathered in 30 seconds of assessment prevents the problems that arise from uninformed entry.

### Principle 2: Commitment Once Entered — Hesitation Stalls in Water
Once the entry decision is made and the bike is moving into the crossing, hesitation is the enemy. Slowing or stopping mid-crossing allows water pressure against the wheel to build, increases the depth relative to the bike's mass, and dramatically increases stall risk. If the engine stalls mid-crossing, recovery is difficult and time-consuming. A committed, consistent throttle carries the bike through crossings that hesitation would stop. This does not mean fast — it means unbroken forward progress at a pace matched to the crossing conditions.

### Principle 3: Consistent Throttle — Not Slow, Not Fast, but Unbroken
The throttle in a water crossing must be maintained continuously once movement begins. A two-stroke engine is especially susceptible to stalling in water — the reduced air intake (if water reaches the airbox level) combined with any hesitation in throttle creates the conditions for a stall. A four-stroke is more forgiving but still requires unbroken throttle to maintain momentum against water resistance. The correct pace is slower than normal trail riding but faster than hesitation — a committed walking pace to slow jogging pace appropriate to depth.

### Principle 4: Wet Brakes — Apply Gently to Dry After Every Crossing
After any water crossing, front and rear brakes are functionally wet. Wet brake pads have dramatically reduced friction against wet rotors — braking distance increases by as much as 200-300% in some conditions. The drying procedure is to apply both brakes gently while riding at low speed on the exit from the crossing — the friction generates heat that evaporates the water from the pad and rotor surfaces. This should be done for 20–30 metres after every crossing before resuming normal speed. Failing to dry brakes after a crossing is a safety risk that many riders overlook.

### Principle 5: Depth Reference Points — Footpeg vs Seat vs Air Box
Understanding depth reference points allows the rider to make go/no-go decisions at assessment. Footpeg level: normal crossing, no engine concern. Footpeg level with current: significant water resistance, maintain throttle. Engine cases (mid-bike height): water beginning to reach mechanical components, short crossing at committed pace. Seat height (approximately 90cm on most enduro bikes): airbox risk on many bikes — this is approaching the limit of safe crossing depth. Above seat height without specialist preparation: do not cross without specific preparation (high-air snorkel, experience, and extreme commitment).

### Principle 6: Bottom Type Determines Technique
Rocky bottom crossings allow confident throttle and committed speed — the rocks provide stable footing and the bike rolls over them. Muddy bottom crossings require more caution — the wheels can sink into soft mud and momentum is critical to maintain. Sandy bottom crossings are similar to sand riding — momentum-critical, throttle maintained. Invisible bottom crossings (turbid water) require a physical assessment (wading, stick test) before entry — the risk of a deep hole or large submerged obstacle is real and the consequences of discovery at speed are severe.

## 3. TECHNIQUE BREAKDOWN BY LEVEL

### 3a. Beginner Level
**Goal:** Establish the stop-and-assess habit, develop committed throttle through shallow crossings, and practise post-crossing brake drying.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Always stopping to assess depth and bottom before entry
- Identifying a clear entry and exit line before committing
- Maintaining consistent throttle once moving — no hesitation
- Applying brakes gently after the crossing to dry them

**What Good Looks Like:**
The rider stops before the crossing, checks depth (visually or physically), identifies an entry and exit, then enters with a consistent throttle and crosses without hesitation or mid-crossing speed changes. After exiting, they apply gentle brakes for 20–30 metres before resuming normal speed.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Entering at speed without assessment:** The bottom is unknown — hazards include depth changes, large rocks, and holes. → Always stop and check first.
- **Hesitating mid-crossing:** Speed drops, water resistance increases, stall probability rises. → Once moving, commit. Hesitation is worse than whatever you're hesitating about.
- **Not drying brakes after crossing:** First hard braking after crossing produces dramatically reduced force — potential front-wheel-on, rear-nothing mismatch. → Dry brakes immediately every time.
- **Lifting feet off pegs:** Instinct in deep water is to lift feet. This raises the centre of mass and destabilises the bike. → Feet on pegs unless water is approaching seat height.

### 3b. Intermediate Level
**Goal:** Cross varied crossings confidently — rocky bottoms at pace, muddy bottoms with commitment, mild current management. Handle a stall-in-water recovery.

**Progression Markers from Beginner:** Rider consistently crosses shallow known crossings. Assessment habit established. Brake drying is automatic.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Rocky vs muddy bottom technique adjustment
- Mild current management — approaching at an angle or accounting for drift
- Stall-in-water recovery — quick restart without losing balance
- Managing multiple crossings in a stage — cumulative brake drying

**What Good Looks Like:**
The rider approaches a varied crossing — rocky entry transitioning to muddy mid-section — adjusts throttle slightly for the bottom change, maintains momentum through both sections, and exits cleanly. If the engine stalls, the rider quickly grounds a foot, avoids tilting the bike, and restarts promptly.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Throttle reduction at muddy section of rocky crossing:** The muddy section needs equal or more throttle — not less. → Identify the bottom change before entry and pre-plan the throttle response.
- **Incorrect current angle:** Entering directly against a current rather than angling slightly downstream. → Approach at an angle 15–20 degrees off perpendicular, pointing slightly upstream, to use current for forward progress rather than fighting it sideways.
- **Panic stall recovery:** Dropping the bike sideways in an attempt to dismount after stalling. → Foot down, stay balanced, restart before water starts entering the airbox.

### 3c. Advanced Level
**Goal:** Race-pace crossing management — rapid assessment, fast committed crossing, minimal time loss. Handle deep crossings, significant current, and unknown-bottom crossings.

**Progression Markers from Intermediate:** Rider handles varied crossings consistently. Current management established. Stall recovery is practiced.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Rapid assessment at race pace — quick go/no-go decision
- Deep crossings (at footpeg level and above) — lifted feet technique, airbox management
- Submerged obstacle management — recovery from unexpected impact mid-crossing
- Race crossing sequencing — multiple crossings in a short stage

**What Good Looks Like:**
The rider makes a rapid assessment (5–10 seconds rather than 30), executes a fast, committed crossing with appropriate bottom technique, exits with minimum time loss, and dries brakes in the shortest effective distance. Deep sections are handled with raised legs if appropriate, and the rider maintains balance and throttle through depth changes.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Skipping assessment under race pressure:** Time pressure causes entry without proper assessment. → The time lost in recovery from an unknown-bottom incident vastly exceeds the assessment time. Even at race pace, 5 seconds of assessment is non-negotiable.
- **Insufficient throttle in deep water:** Reducing throttle in deep sections because the water feels intimidating. → Deeper water requires at least equal and often more committed throttle to maintain momentum against resistance.
- **Failing to anticipate submerged obstacles:** Mid-crossing impact from a submerged rock or log without prepared body position. → Standing position in crossings, legs active and ready to absorb; weight slightly rearward for obstacle absorption.

## 4. DIAGNOSTIC CUES (AI COACHING ENGINE REFERENCE)

### Visual Cues — Correct Technique
- Rider stopping at the crossing edge before entry — visible assessment pause
- Clear, deliberate entry line selection
- Consistent, unbroken speed through the crossing — no hesitation or mid-crossing speed drop
- Standing position with active legs and feet on pegs (unless water is very deep)
- Controlled exit with no abrupt acceleration on exit
- Visible brake application on exit — both hands and foot applying gently for 20-30m

### Visual Cues — Error Indicators
- Rider entering at full trail speed without stopping to assess depth
- Speed reduction or stop mid-crossing — hesitation event
- Rider lifting feet off pegs in shallow to moderate water — destabilising response
- Bike visibly tilting or wobbling mid-crossing from loss of balance
- Rider departing crossing immediately at full speed without brake drying
- Large rooster tail in water: throttle too aggressive — excess wheel spin in water reduces drive

### Audio Cues
- Engine note consistent and maintained through crossing: correct throttle management
- Engine note dropping or stuttering mid-crossing: throttle hesitation — stall risk increasing
- Engine stopping mid-crossing: stall event — recovery procedure required
- High-revving engine in water with reduced forward progress: wheel spinning — too much throttle for the traction available on the bottom

### Sensor Cues
- GPS speed consistent through crossing: correct momentum management
- GPS speed dropping sharply mid-crossing: hesitation event
- GPS speed dropping to zero mid-crossing: stall or fall event
- Accelerometer showing large lateral spike mid-crossing: impact with submerged obstacle or loss of balance

## 5. COACHING CUES & LANGUAGE

### Positive Reinforcement
- "Good assessment — you stopped, you checked the depth, you identified your exit. That 30 seconds of preparation is what makes the crossing easy."
- "Perfect commitment through there. Once you were in, you maintained your throttle and never hesitated. That's how water crossings should look."
- "Good brake drying on exit — I can see you applying gentle pressure for the 20 metres after the crossing. You're safe to build speed again now."

### Corrective Feedback
- "You entered that crossing without stopping. The water was shallow today, but you didn't know that when you committed. Stop and assess every time — the crossing that catches you is the one that looks just like the last one."
- "You hesitated mid-crossing and nearly stalled. Once you're committed to crossing — keep the throttle on. If it was a mistake to enter, you deal with that on the other side. Mid-crossing is the worst place to make decisions."
- "You came off that crossing and immediately accelerated to full speed. Your brakes are wet — they won't work properly right now. Apply gentle pressure for 30 metres after every crossing, every time."
- "You lifted your feet off the pegs in the water. Feet stay on the pegs unless you're in swim territory — lifting your feet raises your centre of mass and makes you less stable, not more."

### Progression Prompts
- "Next crossing, before you enter, tell me the depth and the bottom type. Assessment is a skill — practise narrating it until it becomes automatic."
- "Try a crossing where you enter slightly faster than feels comfortable. Find the speed where committed momentum makes the crossing easier than hesitant caution."
- "Practice your brake drying drill after this crossing — apply light pressure both front and rear from the exit for 30 metres. Feel the bite return as they warm up."

## 6. DRILLS & PRACTICE EXERCISES

### Drill 1: Assessment and Entry Protocol
**Purpose:** Build a consistent stop-and-assess habit before every unknown crossing.
**Setup:** A shallow stream crossing with a visible rocky bottom, in a safe practice environment.
**Execution:**
1. Approach the crossing and stop at the bank.
2. Verbally identify: approximate depth, bottom type, current direction and strength, entry point, exit point.
3. Identify any visible hazards (large rocks, deep sections).
4. Only after completing the verbal assessment, enter and cross.
5. Repeat with artificially varied assessments (instructor obscures part of the crossing, changes visible cues).
**Success Criteria:** Rider consistently stops and completes a verbal assessment before every crossing. Can accurately identify bottom type and depth from assessment.
**Duration/Reps:** 20 crossings, each with full assessment.
**Progression:** Reduce assessment time while maintaining accuracy — 30 seconds → 15 seconds → 10 seconds.

### Drill 2: Brake Drying Practice
**Purpose:** Build the automatic brake-drying habit after water crossings.
**Setup:** A stream crossing with a clear, flat exit section of at least 50m.
**Execution:**
1. Cross the stream and exit.
2. Immediately apply both brakes gently — not hard enough to brake strongly, just enough to generate friction.
3. Maintain this gentle application for 30 metres.
4. At 30 metres, apply the brakes at normal force — feel if they have dried (normal bite) or are still wet (reduced bite).
5. Continue drying if necessary, then test again.
**Success Criteria:** Rider restores normal brake performance within 30m of crossing exit consistently. The procedure becomes automatic — no prompting needed.
**Duration/Reps:** 15 crossings with brake drying practice.
**Progression:** Integrate brake drying into continuous riding — not stopping, just applying the drying pressure while riding through the exit section.

### Drill 3: Commitment Speed Test
**Purpose:** Find the committed throttle level that produces unbroken crossing without hesitation.
**Setup:** A known, safe shallow crossing (assessed and confirmed bottom type).
**Execution:**
1. First crossing: very slow — almost walking pace. Note the water resistance and balance difficulty.
2. Second crossing: slightly faster — moderate walking pace. Note improvement.
3. Third crossing: committed moderate pace — enough that forward momentum is clearly maintained.
4. Identify the speed at which crossing felt easiest and most controlled — this is the committed crossing pace for this depth.
**Success Criteria:** Rider identifies the crossing pace that provides easiest balance and most forward momentum. Can reproduce it consistently.
**Duration/Reps:** 10 crossings at varying speeds.
**Progression:** Apply the same speed-finding process to crossings with different bottom types (rocky, muddy, sandy).

### Drill 4: Stall Recovery Practice
**Purpose:** Build confidence and correct procedure for recovering from an engine stall mid-crossing.
**Setup:** A very shallow, safe crossing (ankle depth or less) where the rider can practice stalling and restarting without risk.
**Execution:**
1. Enter the crossing at slow pace.
2. Deliberately stall the engine mid-crossing by releasing the clutch in a high gear.
3. Immediately: foot down to stabilise, remain standing, assess water depth at current position.
4. Restart the engine immediately (electric start) — do not delay.
5. Continue the crossing.
**Success Criteria:** Rider can stall, stabilise, restart, and continue within 5 seconds in a shallow crossing.
**Duration/Reps:** 10 deliberate stalls and recoveries.
**Progression:** Practice with the crossing slightly deeper. Add time pressure to the restart (simulate an event situation).

## 8. TERRAIN & CONTEXT VARIATIONS

### Rocky Bottom Crossings
Most confidence-inspiring — the stable base allows committed speed. Main risk: large individual submerged rocks can deflect or trap the front wheel. Scan for visible rock outlines before entering. Standing with active legs ready to absorb unexpected impacts.

### Muddy Bottom Crossings
The most commitment-demanding. The bottom yields under tyre load and momentum is critical. Lower entry speed than rocky crossings but must be compensated by more committed throttle throughout. Risk of rear wheel sinking if momentum stops.

### Sandy Bottom Crossings
Similar to sand riding with water above. Forward weight, consistent throttle. The sand bottom may ripple or have inconsistent depth. Probe before entry in unfamiliar sandy crossings.

### Significant Current Crossings
Approach at a slight angle into the current. Current pushing sideways requires counterbalancing with body weight. At high current speeds, the force against the bike's side panels and wheels is significant — entry angle and speed must account for drift. Very high current: do not cross without specific training and preparation.

### Deep Crossings (Near or Above Footpeg)
Legs may be lifted off pegs and raised forward (feet in front of axle) if water approaches seat height — this keeps feet clear while maintaining some control. Throttle maintained throughout — stalling at depth is very difficult to recover from. Assess carefully and do not enter if depth exceeds bike's safe crossing limit.

## 9. EXPERT INSIGHTS & SOURCES

### Key Insights
- **Dakar Rally water crossings:** Desert rallies including the Dakar regularly feature water crossings that range from ankle-deep to potentially submerging bikes. Teams use specific preparation (raised airbox intakes, waterproofed electronics) for deep crossings. The commitment principle is well-established: stopping in deep water is catastrophic, so assessment before entry is non-negotiable.
- **ISDE water management:** The International Six Days Enduro's special tests and cross-country stages regularly include stream crossings, and riders' brake management after crossings is a notable performance differentiator — riders with warm, dry brakes descend post-crossing sections faster and more safely.
- **Adventure touring application:** Water crossings are one of the most frequently discussed topics in adventure motorcycling communities. The consistent advice from experienced travellers mirrors competitive technique: assess first, commit fully, dry brakes after.
- **Airbox submersion risk:** Different bikes have airboxes at different heights. The specific depth limit for any bike's airbox should be known to the rider — hydrolock (water in the cylinder through the airbox) can destroy an engine. Manufacturers publish crossing depth recommendations.
- **Waterproofing and recovery:** Modern enduro bikes have increasingly capable waterproofing. However, electronics at footpeg level and below are rarely fully waterproof. Repetitive deep crossings in a stage can cause electrical issues — a factor in race preparation planning.
