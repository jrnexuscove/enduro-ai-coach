# Log Crossings

## 1. OVERVIEW

Log crossings are a fundamental obstacle type in forest and woodland enduro, appearing in three distinct categories that require different techniques: small logs that can be rolled over with momentum, medium logs requiring a timed front lift and rear drive, and large logs requiring a full front-lift technique. The critical timing skill is throttle application at the face of the log — applying drive as the front wheel contacts the log (not before, not during the approach) is what powers the wheel up and over rather than into the face. Getting the throttle timing wrong is the most common log crossing error and the cause of most stalls and falls at log obstacles. Log crossings also vary significantly with moisture — wet logs are near-zero grip (similar to wet roots), while dry logs offer reasonable traction. Understanding which technique to deploy based on log size and moisture condition is the core log crossing skill.

## 2. CORE PRINCIPLES

### Principle 1: Log Size Determines Technique
Logs below approximately 15cm diameter can be rolled over with maintained speed and no specific technique adjustment — the tyre momentum carries the wheel over without front wheel lift. Logs 15–35cm diameter require a timed throttle pulse at contact to lift the front wheel over the top of the log, followed immediately by driving the rear over. Logs above 35cm diameter typically require a full front-lift technique where the front wheel is actively lifted (using combined throttle and body weight rearward) before the log face, then the rear is driven over once the front is clear. Understanding which category applies prevents the error of using large-log technique on a small log (over-riding it) or small-log technique on a large log (slamming into it).

### Principle 2: Throttle at the Face — Not Before, Not After
The timing of the throttle input at a log is precise. Applied too early (before the front wheel reaches the log face), the power lifts the front wheel in the wrong position — away from the log rather than over it. Applied too late (after the front wheel is against the log face), the power is wasted against the obstacle rather than lifting over it. The correct timing is exactly as the front wheel contacts the log face — a brief, sharp throttle pulse that converts the contact energy into upward lift rather than forward impact. This timing must be practiced until it is reflexive. Experienced riders describe it as "firing the throttle at the log face."

### Principle 3: Drive the Rear Over Immediately After the Front Clears
Once the front wheel has crested the log, the rear must be driven over before it loses momentum against the log face. This is done with a continuation of throttle — not a gap in drive. The sequence is: front contacts log → throttle at face (front lifts) → front wheel over the log → continuous throttle drives the rear up and over. If the throttle is reduced between the front clearing and the rear arriving at the log, the rear hits the log face without drive energy and the bike stalls against it. The crossing must be one continuous, committed motion.

### Principle 4: Body Weight Rearward as Front Lifts
As the front wheel contacts the log face and the throttle is applied, body weight must shift rearward. This is the counterforce to the front lifting — if weight remains forward at the moment of front lift, the lift energy goes into compressing the forks rather than elevating the wheel. A brief rearward weight shift (hips back, arms slightly extended) at the moment of contact allows the front wheel to rise freely. After the front clears, weight shifts forward again to help drive the rear over. This fore/aft weight movement is rapid — it happens within the span of the crossing, not over seconds.

### Principle 5: Wet vs Dry Log Grip — Fundamental Technique Change
Dry log wood provides similar grip to packed dirt — reasonable traction for the rear wheel to drive over without excessive spin. A wet log is near-zero grip (similar to wet root technique). On a wet log, the rear wheel spinning loses all drive and the bike stalls against the log. The technique adjustment for wet logs: slightly more approach speed (to carry momentum through without relying on rear drive), minimal throttle at the face (just enough to lift the front, not to drive the rear — momentum does that work), and smooth, controlled rollover. Over-revving on a wet log is extremely counter-productive.

### Principle 6: Multiple Logs in Sequence
Log staircases or multiple logs in close succession require maintaining the crossing rhythm — one continuous motion from the first log to the last without stopping between them. If the approach speed and throttle timing are correct on the first log, the exit speed from that crossing provides the entry speed for the next. Stopping between logs in a sequence means restarting the obstacle sequence from zero momentum, which dramatically increases difficulty. Pre-plan the sequence before entry and commit to riding it as one continuous motion.

## 3. TECHNIQUE BREAKDOWN BY LEVEL

### 3a. Beginner Level
**Goal:** Learn to negotiate small logs with momentum and practise the basic throttle-at-face timing on medium logs.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Approaching small logs at sufficient speed to roll over without stalling
- Standing position with weight slightly rearward on approach
- Practicing throttle timing on a single medium log until it becomes consistent
- Identifying log size category before approaching

**What Good Looks Like:**
The rider approaches a small log at moderate speed and rolls over it smoothly. On a medium log, the rider applies throttle as the front wheel contacts the face — the front lifts over, the rear is driven over continuously. The bike crosses the log in one clean motion without stopping against it.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Closing throttle on log approach:** The instinct is to reduce speed before an obstacle. On a log, this removes the momentum needed for the roll-over. → Hold or add throttle on approach.
- **Throttle before the log face (premature lift):** Front wheel lifts before reaching the log, comes down short or hits awkwardly. → Time the throttle to front wheel contact, not to visual log proximity.
- **Stalling against the log face:** No throttle at contact, front pushes against the log and stops. → Practice the throttle-at-face reflex on small logs before increasing size.
- **Sitting on approach:** Sitting reduces the ability to shift weight rearward. → Stand on approach to all logs.

### 3b. Intermediate Level
**Goal:** Correctly cross medium and large logs consistently. Manage wet logs with technique adjustment. Handle multiple logs in sequence.

**Progression Markers from Beginner:** Rider crosses small logs confidently. Basic throttle-at-face timing established. Medium logs are possible with some inconsistency.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Large log technique — deliberate front lift before contact, then rear drive
- Wet log technique — momentum approach, minimal throttle
- Multiple logs — maintaining rhythm without stopping between obstacles
- Log on an incline — technique adjustments for slope-mounted logs

**What Good Looks Like:**
The rider approaches a large log and executes a deliberate front lift — the front wheel rises before reaching the log face, contacts the top of the log, and the rear is driven over in one continuous motion. On a wet log, approach speed is higher and throttle is controlled — not over-revved. Multiple logs are crossed in a single continuous motion.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Insufficient front lift on large logs:** Front wheel hits the face of a large log rather than the top. → Front lift must begin slightly before the log face for large obstacles.
- **Over-revving on wet logs:** High throttle at face of wet log causes immediate rear wheel spin with no drive. → Wet log = momentum-dependent crossing. Less throttle, more entry speed.
- **Stopping between multiple logs:** Momentum lost after first log, second log approached from zero. → Plan the sequence as one motion; cross the first log with enough carry speed for the second.

### 3c. Advanced Level
**Goal:** Race-pace log management — rapid size assessment, correct technique selection, consistent execution including on inclines, in sequence, and in varied moisture conditions.

**Progression Markers from Intermediate:** Large logs crossed correctly. Wet log technique is established. Multiple log sequences navigated.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Race-speed log assessment — correct technique choice at approach pace
- Logs in corners or at awkward angles — navigation with compromised approach
- Logs on steep descents — the most dangerous configuration
- Log hop technique — two-wheel hop for small logs at high speed

**What Good Looks Like:**
The rider identifies log size and moisture condition at approach speed, executes the correct technique without hesitation, and maintains race pace through and after the obstacle. Logs at awkward positions or angles are handled with adaptations — slightly slower, adjusted line — but without full stops.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Applying medium-log technique to large logs under time pressure:** Under-estimating log size leads to front hitting the log face rather than the top. → Log assessment must be accurate — when uncertain, treat as the larger category.
- **Aggressive throttle on a log on a descent:** A log on a descent combined with throttle can cause the rear to drive over too fast, pitching the rider forward. → On descent logs, controlled momentum is the tool; throttle is reduced compared to flat.
- **Misjudging a wet log as dry:** Applying dry-log throttle to a wet-log surface. → When in doubt, assume wet technique — excess caution is better than rear wheel spin against a log.

## 4. DIAGNOSTIC CUES (AI COACHING ENGINE REFERENCE)

### Visual Cues — Correct Technique
- Front wheel visibly lifting as it contacts the log face — not pushing against the face
- Continuous, unbroken crossing motion — no mid-crossing pause
- Rider's weight briefly shifting rearward as front lifts, then forward to drive rear over
- Rear wheel driving up and over the log without visible spinning or stalling
- Standing position maintained throughout with active legs absorbing the crossing motion
- Approach speed maintained to the log face — no visible deceleration on approach

### Visual Cues — Error Indicators
- Front wheel pushing against the log face and compressing rather than lifting — stall event beginning
- Rider sitting on approach — no rearward weight shift available
- Throttle applied at distance from log — front wheel lifts prematurely and comes down before the log
- Rear wheel spinning against the log face (especially on wet logs) — no forward progress
- Rider stopping mid-crossing with front wheel on top of log and rear against the face
- Approach speed dropping significantly before the log — throttle reduced

### Audio Cues
- A sharp throttle response as front contacts log face: correct timing — engine note rises briefly
- Engine note dropping then stuttering: stall beginning — front pushing against log face without lift
- High engine rev with no forward progress: rear wheel spinning against log face (wet log or over-gearing)
- Clean, brief engine acceleration through the crossing: correct throttle timing and drive
- Thump followed by rear drive sound: two-part crossing — front contacted then drove over and rear then drove (acceptable technique, slightly less smooth than one continuous motion)

### Sensor Cues
- GPS speed showing brief acceleration at log crossing: correct throttle pulse timed to log contact
- GPS speed dropping to zero mid-crossing: stall against log face
- Accelerometer showing vertical spike (upward): correct front wheel lift
- Accelerometer showing sharp forward (horizontal) spike: front wheel impact against log face at speed — wrong technique

## 5. COACHING CUES & LANGUAGE

### Positive Reinforcement
- "That's it — you fired the throttle exactly as the front wheel hit the log. Front went up, rear drove over. One clean motion. That's the technique."
- "Good weight shift — you moved back as the front lifted and forward to drive the rear. The bike crossed that log like it wasn't there."
- "Nice timing on the wet log — you used your entry speed rather than fighting with the rear wheel, and the bike floated straight over. Correct read of the conditions."

### Corrective Feedback
- "You're closing the throttle on approach and running into the log face. I need you to hold the throttle and fire it at the moment the front wheel makes contact. Not before the log — at the log."
- "Your front lifted too early — you applied throttle when you saw the log, not when you hit it. Walk up to a log and tap it — that's the moment. Fire the throttle at the tap, not at the sight."
- "You're spinning the rear on that wet log. The wood is slick — you can't drive over it with throttle alone. Carry more entry speed and use less throttle. Let the momentum do the work."
- "You stopped between the first and second log and lost all your momentum. That sequence has to be one motion — commit to crossing both before you reach the first one."

### Progression Prompts
- "Start on the smallest log you can find and perfect the throttle-at-face timing on that. When you can cross it 10 times in a row cleanly, move to the next size up."
- "Practice the crossing with your eyes closed for the last 2 metres of approach — trust your timing and feel. If you're looking at the log, your throttle timing will be late."
- "Next time you approach a multiple-log section, plan the whole sequence in your head first. Visualise crossing all of them in one continuous motion, then execute exactly that."

## 6. DRILLS & PRACTICE EXERCISES

### Drill 1: Throttle Timing Isolation
**Purpose:** Develop precise throttle-at-face timing without the complication of large obstacles.
**Setup:** A single small log or wooden plank (5–10cm height) on flat, safe ground.
**Execution:**
1. Ride toward the log at slow walking pace.
2. Watch the front tyre closely. At the exact moment it touches the log, snap a brief throttle pulse.
3. The front should rise slightly over the log and the rear drive over.
4. Repeat 20 times, focusing only on the timing — not the speed.
5. Gradually increase approach speed while maintaining timing precision.
**Success Criteria:** Front wheel lifts at contact point on 18 out of 20 passes. Timing is consistent regardless of speed.
**Duration/Reps:** 30 minutes on one log.
**Progression:** Move to a larger log (15cm, then 25cm). Maintain the same precise timing as the consequence of mistiming becomes more obvious.

### Drill 2: Size Category Assessment Drill
**Purpose:** Build the habit of accurately assessing log size category (small/roll/medium/large) before choosing technique.
**Setup:** Three logs of different sizes set up on a practice course — one small (< 15cm), one medium (15–35cm), one large (> 35cm).
**Execution:**
1. Instructor points to a log from distance — rider calls the category (small/medium/large) before approaching.
2. Rider then applies the correct technique for that category.
3. Rotate through logs in random order.
4. When all three are consistent, add a fourth unknown log (instructor-selected size) for real-time assessment.
**Success Criteria:** Rider correctly categorises log size at approach distance with 90% accuracy. Executes correct technique for each category.
**Duration/Reps:** 20 minutes, multiple size assessments.
**Progression:** Add approach speed. Categorise and select technique at moderate trail speed rather than slow approach.

### Drill 3: Wet Log Protocol
**Purpose:** Build the momentum-based wet log technique and calibrate throttle reduction for slippery surfaces.
**Setup:** A single medium log, wetted thoroughly (water or a shaded log after rain).
**Execution:**
1. First pass: use normal (dry-log) throttle timing. Observe rear wheel spin on the wet surface.
2. Second pass: increase entry speed by 20%, reduce throttle at face by 50%. Note the difference.
3. Third pass: further reduce throttle, relying on momentum for the rear to follow the front over.
4. Find the minimum throttle / maximum entry-speed combination that crosses the wet log cleanly.
**Success Criteria:** Rider crosses the wet log without rear wheel spin. Can articulate the difference between wet and dry technique.
**Duration/Reps:** 15 wet log passes.
**Progression:** Introduce multiple wet logs. Apply the wet-log adjustments to an entire wet section.

### Drill 4: Log Sequence Rhythm
**Purpose:** Build the ability to cross multiple logs in sequence as one continuous, committed motion.
**Setup:** Three medium logs, approximately 1.5–2 metres apart, on flat ground.
**Execution:**
1. First pass: stop between each log. Cross each individually. Note the difficulty of restarting from zero each time.
2. Second pass: target one continuous motion from first log to last. Carry exit speed from each crossing into the approach of the next.
3. Subsequent passes: increase approach speed until the sequence becomes fluid.
4. Goal: cross all three logs in a single unbroken motion.
**Success Criteria:** Rider crosses all three logs without stopping between any two. Speed maintains or increases through the sequence.
**Duration/Reps:** 20 minutes on the sequence.
**Progression:** Add a fourth log. Reduce spacing between logs. Introduce one wet log into the sequence.

## 8. TERRAIN & CONTEXT VARIATIONS

### Small Log (< 15cm Diameter)
Roll over with maintained speed. No specific technique required beyond not braking on approach. At race pace, these are typically ignored as significant obstacles.

### Medium Log (15–35cm Diameter)
Throttle-at-face timing is the primary skill. Weight shift rearward at contact. Standard enduro log crossing technique. Most common log obstacle size in woodland enduro.

### Large Log (> 35cm Diameter)
Full front-lift technique. Front lift begins slightly before the face — the front wheel must clear the top of the log. Weight significantly rearward during lift. Then continuous throttle drives the rear over. This requires pre-planned technique, not reactive.

### Log on an Ascent
The log is approached with additional kinetic energy from the slope below. This helps front lift. The complication: after the front clears, the rear must drive over while still climbing — requires sustained throttle. The log acts as a step on an already difficult climb.

### Log on a Descent
Most demanding configuration. Approach speed may be high (gravity-assisted) but control is critical. The rear driving over a log on a descent can tip the weight balance forward — potential for an endo if throttle is too aggressive. Controlled momentum, less throttle than flat crossing.

## 9. EXPERT INSIGHTS & SOURCES

### Key Insights
- **Jonny Walker** (Hard Enduro World Championship): Log and stump crossings are a signature technique in extreme enduro. Walker's approach to large obstacles involves reading the log diameter from distance and selecting the correct entry angle to maximise lift efficiency. "Every log has a best crossing point — the lowest point or the narrowest angle. You find that before you arrive."
- **Trials technique inheritance:** Trials riding involves continuous log and step crossings — the technique of "firing the throttle at the face" comes directly from trials technique where precision timing of power delivery is the foundation of all obstacle navigation.
- **Log hop technique:** At race pace, some riders cross small to medium logs with a two-wheel hop — simultaneously lifting both wheels over the log. This requires front and rear lift coordination (similar to small jump technique) and is faster than the roll-over on logs that would otherwise cause a speed reduction.
- **Dry vs wet log grip data:** Laboratory tests of rubber on dry wood show friction coefficients of approximately 0.6–0.8. Wet wood drops this to 0.1–0.2 — a 75–85% reduction. This quantifies why wet log technique is fundamentally different from dry.
- **Peril of the inclined log:** A log angled diagonally across the trail (not perpendicular to travel) creates an asymmetric crossing where one wheel reaches the log before the other. This is one of the most common causes of mid-crossing falls — the bike tilts sideways as the higher wheel crosses first. Cross perpendicular to the log's axis where possible; if not possible, reduce speed significantly.
