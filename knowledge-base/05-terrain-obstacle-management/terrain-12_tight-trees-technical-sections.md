# Tight Trees / Technical Sections

## 1. OVERVIEW

Riding through tight trees and narrow technical sections exposes the most fundamental challenge in off-road riding: target fixation. The brain instinctively focuses on obstacles — the trees, the rocks, the narrow gaps — and then steers directly toward them. This is not a technique failure; it is a wired survival response that must be actively overridden through training and conscious redirection of attention. The gap between the trees is the target, not the trees themselves. Combined with this vision challenge, tight sections demand precise clutch control for slow-speed management, awareness of handlebar width (typically 80–85cm on enduro bikes), and a speed window that is low enough for control but not so low that balance is compromised. Wet autumn woodland conditions add slippery surfaces to the mix, compounding every other technical demand. Tight trees and technical sections are where vision, slow-speed bike control, and composed decision-making are tested simultaneously.

## 2. CORE PRINCIPLES

### Principle 1: Target Fixation Is the Primary Hazard — The Gap, Not the Trees
Target fixation is the brain's tendency to steer toward what the eyes focus on. In a section of tight trees, the obstacles are visually dominant — they are large, close, and threatening. The instinctive visual response is to look at the trees, which directs steering toward them. The correct override is to deliberately look at the gap between the trees — the space you want to pass through. Research on target fixation in motorsport and cycling consistently shows that where the eyes go, the bike follows. This is not metaphor; it is literal motor function. Retrain the eyes to fixate on the exit of each gap, not on the tree trunks. This single skill shift transforms tight tree riding.

### Principle 2: The Gap Is the Target — Look Through, Not At
An extension of the target fixation principle: the rider's vision must be directed through the gap (at the next obstacle or opening beyond it), not at the gap itself. Looking at the gap treats it as an endpoint; looking through it treats it as a waypoint toward the next position. This means that while navigating a specific gap, the rider's eyes should already be scanning the trees beyond — identifying the next gap, the next turn, the next obstacle. This continuous forward scanning prevents the tunnel-vision of reacting to one obstacle at a time and instead builds a flowing, ahead-of-the-bike mental picture.

### Principle 3: Handlebar Width Awareness — Know Your Geometry
Most enduro bikes run handlebars between 80 and 87cm wide. Knowing this width precisely and having a calibrated sense of the minimum gap the bike can pass through is a practical skill. Riders who know their handlebar width make go/no-go decisions on tight gaps rapidly and accurately. Riders who do not know their width misjudge gaps in both directions — refusing passable gaps (lost time) and attempting impassable ones (impact). Some riders mark their bar ends with tape as a visual reference. The minimum comfortable gap is approximately bar width plus 10–15cm of margin — narrower is possible but requires precise line alignment.

### Principle 4: Clutch Feathering Through the Entire Section
Tight technical sections require the slowest speed compatible with maintaining balance — typically 5–15 km/h. At this speed, the clutch must be feathered at the friction point for the entire section to provide drive without lurching or stalling. Releasing the clutch fully at these speeds produces a speed surge and reduces fine control. Holding the clutch fully results in stalling from insufficient drive. The friction-point feathering that was introduced in mud riding applies here in a precise, sustained form — holding the bike at the exact speed that allows control through the narrowest gaps. This requires finger endurance and fine motor coordination.

### Principle 5: Pre-Planning the Section Before Entry
Tight technical sections reward riders who pause before entry to plan the path. Unlike fast terrain where planning happens at speed, a tight tree section can and should be walked mentally (or physically for extreme sections) before the bike enters. Identify: the entry angle, the positions of trees that will require steering inputs, the handlebar-clearance points, and the exit direction. Once inside a tight section at slow speed, there is time to make steering decisions — but the overall framework of the path should already exist. Pre-planning also reveals impassable points before commitment, allowing a different line to be chosen.

### Principle 6: Speed Window — Slow Enough for Control, Fast Enough for Balance
Balance on a two-wheeled vehicle requires forward momentum — below a minimum speed, the bike falls regardless of steering input. In tight trees, the speed must be reduced to the minimum compatible with the gap width and cornering needed, but not reduced below the balance threshold. This minimum balance speed on a modern enduro bike is approximately 3–5 km/h when the rider has both feet available for grounding. The practical riding window for tight sections is typically 6–15 km/h — slow enough for precise navigation, fast enough to maintain balance. Finding this window for each section's complexity is a key skill.

## 3. TECHNIQUE BREAKDOWN BY LEVEL

### 3a. Beginner Level
**Goal:** Understand target fixation and actively look at gaps rather than trees. Develop basic clutch-feathering control at slow speed. Learn to pre-plan short sections before entering.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Actively directing eyes to the gap — conscious override of target fixation
- Clutch at friction point for slow-speed control
- Pre-planning a short section before entering
- Standing for balance and slow-speed control

**What Good Looks Like:**
The rider approaches a 3–4 tree section at slow pace, looks at the gaps not the trees, enters with clutch feathered, steers through each gap in sequence, and exits with control. There is no contact with trees. Speed is slow but consistent. The rider does not freeze mid-section.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Looking at the trees:** The instinctive response — and it steers directly toward them. → Consciously redirect every time. Say "look at the gap" out loud if necessary.
- **Releasing clutch mid-section:** Speed lurches, bike rushes toward a tree. → Clutch stays at friction point throughout the section.
- **Stopping mid-section in panic:** Balance lost, foot down, no space to restart. → Pre-plan the section as one committed motion. Stopping is harder than continuing slowly.
- **Too much speed — losing control of direction:** Over-confident approach, cannot steer precisely at speed. → Reduce to the window where fine steering is possible.

### 3b. Intermediate Level
**Goal:** Navigate complex tight tree sections smoothly — multiple gaps, direction changes, obstacles on the floor (roots, mud). Manage sight lines through sections with limited preview distance.

**Progression Markers from Beginner:** Rider consistently navigates simple tight sections without contact. Target fixation override is developing. Clutch feathering is controlled.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Looking 3 trees ahead rather than only the next tree
- Managing direction changes within a tight section (S-bends through trees)
- Combining mud/root technique with tight trees
- Foot-down balance recovery in tight sections

**What Good Looks Like:**
The rider threads through a complex 10–15 tree section with multiple direction changes, looking ahead through each gap to the next, adjusting speed with clutch, and managing floor obstacles (roots, mud patches) without stopping. If balance wavers, a foot touches down briefly and the rider continues — no full stop.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Looking only one tree ahead:** Reacting to each tree rather than flowing through the sequence. → Force vision 3 trees ahead — read the pattern, not each tree individually.
- **Freezing at a direction change:** Mid-section S-bend stops the rider momentarily. → Pre-plan S-bends before entering. Look at the exit of the S before reaching its start.
- **Target fixation recovery failure:** Rider catches themselves looking at a tree and over-corrects away from it — creating a large swerve. → Small, smooth corrections. The swerve is worse than the original error.

### 3c. Advanced Level
**Goal:** Race-pace tight tree navigation — reading sections at speed, making rapid go/no-go decisions on gaps, and flowing through sections that slower riders stop or walk.

**Progression Markers from Intermediate:** Complex tight sections navigated consistently. Vision 3+ trees ahead is established. No full stops in tight sections.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Race-pace gap assessment — go/no-go decision at approach speed
- Elbows in adaptation for narrow gaps without losing steering leverage
- Wet autumn woodland — slippery roots and leaves within a tight section
- Managing a tight section after a physical effort (climbing, mud) — fatigue-impaired vision

**What Good Looks Like:**
The rider reads a tight section from distance, makes a rapid go/no-go assessment, enters at the correct speed, and flows through without visible hesitation. Vision is consistently forward. Elbows adjust subtly to narrowest gaps without losing control. Exits are clean and immediate pace resumes.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Racing speed into a tight section:** Arriving too fast to steer precisely through gaps. → Tight sections require speed reduction. Speed after, not before.
- **Elbows out in very narrow gaps:** Elbows contact tree trunks if too wide. → Practise riding with elbows slightly inward — reduces effective width by 5–10cm without losing significant control.
- **Fatigue-induced target fixation regression:** After physical effort, vision quality drops and target fixation returns. → Conscious over-ride of target fixation must be intensified during tired sections.

## 4. DIAGNOSTIC CUES (AI COACHING ENGINE REFERENCE)

### Visual Cues — Correct Technique
- Rider's head visibly looking ahead through gaps — not tracking the trees laterally
- Bike threading precisely through gaps with consistent, moderate slow speed
- No contact with trees or obstacles — centimetre-level clearance is correct technique
- Clutch hand visibly active — lever not in or out, feathered at friction point
- Smooth, continuous forward motion — no stopping and restarting mid-section
- Elbows slightly inward compared to open terrain position in narrowest gaps

### Visual Cues — Error Indicators
- Bike drifting toward trees rather than threading through gaps — target fixation
- Speed surge mid-section from clutch release — clutch feathering lost
- Rider stopping mid-section and putting foot down to re-plan
- Head tracking individual trees (visible from forward camera: head turns toward obstacle)
- Front wheel contacting a tree trunk — target fixation error
- Excessive speed on entry followed by overcorrection — arrival speed incompatible with section

### Audio Cues
- Consistent, low engine note at clutch friction point: correct feathering
- Engine note surging then dropping: clutch released and re-grabbed — losing the friction zone
- Engine stalling mid-section: clutch over-held or speed dropped below balance threshold
- Crack or impact sound: handlebar or wheel contact with a tree
- Smooth, consistent idle-above-idle engine note with no surges: correct sustained feathering

### Sensor Cues
- GPS speed consistent at 6–15 km/h through tight section: correct speed window
- GPS speed surging and dropping: clutch feathering not sustained — speed control lost
- GPS speed dropping to zero mid-section: stop event — balance or planning failure
- Accelerometer showing smooth lateral movements within section: correct smooth steering
- Accelerometer showing sudden lateral spike: contact event or aggressive over-correction

## 5. COACHING CUES & LANGUAGE

### Positive Reinforcement
- "Good eyes — you were looking through the gaps, not at the trees. I can see it because you threaded every one of them without a wobble. That's the vision skill."
- "Your clutch control through that whole section was excellent. Consistent friction point the whole way — the speed never surged."
- "Good pre-planning — you paused at the entry, looked the section over, and then rode it exactly as you mapped it. That preparation is what made it look easy."

### Corrective Feedback
- "You're looking at the trees and riding toward them. Every single time, your head turns toward a tree trunk and the bike follows. Force your eyes to the gap — the space between the trees. The gap is the target."
- "You released the clutch mid-section and the bike surged. In tight trees, the clutch stays at the friction point the whole time — not in, not out. Feather it through the entire section."
- "You arrived at that section too fast to steer precisely. Tight trees need slow speed — reduce before you enter. Speed can wait until you're out."
- "You stopped mid-section and then couldn't restart in the gap. Next time, if you feel uncertain, keep moving — slow is fine, stopped is not. Forward movement is balance."

### Progression Prompts
- "Before the next section, walk up to it, stand at the entry, and point to where your eyes should be looking at each gap. Talk through the path. Then ride it exactly as you described."
- "Practise looking 3 trees ahead — count them. At the first tree, your eyes should be on the third. When you get to the second, look to the fourth. Build the habit with counting."
- "Next tight section, ride it twice. First pass: look at the trees (do this deliberately — experience the target fixation result). Second pass: look at the gaps. Compare what happens."

## 6. DRILLS & PRACTICE EXERCISES

### Drill 1: Target Fixation Override — Cone or Pole Exercise
**Purpose:** Build the reflex of looking at gaps rather than obstacles in a controlled, repeatable environment.
**Setup:** 5–6 cones or poles set up in a forest-gap-like pattern, approximately 1.5–2m apart.
**Execution:**
1. First pass: deliberately look at a cone as you approach. Note where the bike goes.
2. Second pass: deliberately look at the gap beside the cone. Note where the bike goes.
3. Repeat 20 passes, alternating: look at cone vs look at gap. Build awareness of the visual-steering relationship.
4. Subsequent passes: look at gap only — build this as the automatic response.
**Success Criteria:** Rider navigates all cones without contact in 15 consecutive passes while looking at gaps only.
**Duration/Reps:** 30 minutes on the cone pattern.
**Progression:** Move cones closer together. Introduce corners between poles. Replicate with trees in a real forest section.

### Drill 2: Sustained Clutch Feathering
**Purpose:** Develop the endurance and precision for sustained clutch friction-point control.
**Setup:** A flat, open area.
**Execution:**
1. Find the clutch friction point (where the bike just begins to move from idle).
2. Ride at that friction point at approximately 5 km/h in a straight line for 30 seconds without varying speed.
3. Rest hands for 30 seconds.
4. Repeat for 10 minutes total of clutch-point riding.
5. Introduce a long slow loop (using the clutch to maintain speed through bends without the bike surging).
**Success Criteria:** Rider can sustain clutch friction-point control for 2 minutes without the speed varying by more than 2 km/h.
**Duration/Reps:** 10 minutes of sustained feathering practice.
**Progression:** Transfer the sustained feathering to a short (5 tree) tight section. Apply the friction-point control under the additional visual and navigation demands.

### Drill 3: Section Pre-Planning Protocol
**Purpose:** Build the habit of assessing and mapping a tight section before entry.
**Setup:** A real or constructed 10–15 tree section.
**Execution:**
1. Stop at the section entry. Do not enter immediately.
2. Visually trace the path through the section — identify each gap, each direction change, each obstacle on the floor.
3. Narrate the path aloud: "Gap left of the big birch, right of the two close ones, left of the root cluster, exit right."
4. Then ride exactly the described path.
5. After exiting, compare what was planned with what was executed.
**Success Criteria:** Execution matches the pre-planned path in 8 out of 10 attempts. Rider can narrate the path before riding it accurately.
**Duration/Reps:** 20 minutes of plan-then-ride.
**Progression:** Reduce planning time — 30 seconds → 15 seconds → 10 seconds. Then attempt at approach speed.

### Drill 4: Width Awareness Calibration
**Purpose:** Build accurate sense of handlebar width and minimum passable gaps.
**Setup:** An open area with two poles or trees at measured widths: 100cm, 90cm, 80cm, 75cm.
**Execution:**
1. At 100cm gap: ride through at slow speed. Comfortable clearance.
2. At 90cm gap: ride through. Note the tighter clearance feeling.
3. At 80cm gap: ride through (approximately handlebar width). Feel the minimal clearance.
4. At 75cm: riders with 80cm bars cannot pass cleanly — stop and confirm minimum width.
5. Repeat each gap 10 times to calibrate the visual sense of passable vs impassable.
**Success Criteria:** Rider can accurately identify passable gaps from 3 metres away at 8 out of 10 attempts.
**Duration/Reps:** 30 minutes width calibration.
**Progression:** Cover the gap measurement labels. Judge width by eye only. Test accuracy.

## 8. TERRAIN & CONTEXT VARIATIONS

### Summer Dry Woodland
Most manageable tight-tree condition. Dry ground, good grip, fallen leaves not yet a hazard. Vision is better — the tree canopy is full but light penetrates. The target fixation challenge is the same regardless of conditions.

### Autumn Wet Woodland
Most demanding condition. Wet leaves on top of wet roots on top of wet ground. Near-zero grip on multiple surface types simultaneously. Speed must be reduced further than dry conditions. Target fixation risk is amplified by the added concern about surfaces. Every tight-tree technique is compounded by low grip demands.

### Winter Tight Trees with Ice or Frost
Requires extreme caution — frosted roots and frozen mud patches within the section can cause instant loss of traction. Often better to dismount and walk these sections. The clutch feathering discipline and zero throttle on potentially iced surfaces. Risk is very high.

### Dense Pine Plantation Sections
Often very regularly spaced trees with predictable gaps. Consistent width allows faster rhythm development than mixed deciduous woodland. However, fallen branches on the ground are a constant hazard. Visibility can be surprisingly good — the regular geometry helps vision planning.

### Narrow Rocky Technical Sections (Not Trees)
The same target fixation and slow-speed control principles apply to narrow rocky chutes and canyon-like sections. The obstacles are rock faces rather than trees, but the vision skill (look at the passage, not the walls), clutch control, and width awareness are identical.

## 9. EXPERT INSIGHTS & SOURCES

### Key Insights
- **Jonny Walker** (Hard Enduro World Championship): Walker is celebrated for his ability to navigate sections that appear physically impassable — tight rock sections, narrow forest chutes. His explanation consistently returns to vision: "I don't see the obstacles. I see the gaps. The gaps are the trail — the trees and rocks are just the edges."
- **Target fixation research:** Research on competitive cyclists (Causer and Williams, 2017, Journal of Sports Sciences) demonstrates that skilled riders fix their gaze on path/gap targets, while novices fix their gaze on obstacles. This is not natural — it is trained. The research confirms that explicit vision training (telling riders where to look) produces measurable improvement in obstacle avoidance.
- **Handlebar width pragmatics:** Many enduro riders riding tight woodland sections trim their handlebars by 2–5cm (both sides) to reduce effective width. This is a legitimate setup adjustment. However, the reduction affects leverage and control — a trade-off that must be assessed per rider and terrain type.
- **Trials riding and tight technical terrain:** British Trials riders navigate obstacle sections that are as narrow as 40–50cm between boulders at near-zero speed. The techniques — vision, clutch feathering, committed pre-planning — are identical to enduro tight tree technique at greater speed. Riders who cross-train in trials report significant improvement in tight technical enduro sections.
- **Wet leaf litter physics:** Wet deciduous leaves (autumn) on compacted ground have a friction coefficient of approximately 0.05–0.1 — comparable to polished ice. This is not exaggeration; it quantifies why autumn woodland sections are treated as the most dangerous common enduro surface condition.
