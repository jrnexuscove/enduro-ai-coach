# Rocky Terrain Navigation

## 1. OVERVIEW

Rocky terrain is a test of vision, commitment, and precise input timing. Unlike sand or mud, rock offers consistent grip — often excellent grip — but it punishes riders who deflect off obstacles rather than rolling over them. The fundamental shift in rocky terrain is that the rider must constantly be reading 5 or more rocks ahead, planning a "rolling line" that allows the wheel to roll over the surface of rocks rather than impact the face of them. Speed has a counter-intuitive relationship with difficulty in rocky terrain: below a threshold speed, rocks deflect the wheel violently; above that threshold, the suspension and tyre can absorb them more smoothly. Body position must allow the bike to move freely beneath the rider — active legs are not optional. Rocks can be square-edged (requiring precise timing) or rounded (requiring line selection). Understanding the difference and reacting to each is the core skill of rocky terrain navigation.

## 2. CORE PRINCIPLES

### Principle 1: The Rolling Line — Roll Over, Not Into
Every rock presents a choice: hit the face of it (an impact that deflects the wheel) or roll over the top of it (the tyre drapes over the rock and the wheel maintains forward momentum). The rolling line is the path through a rock section that strings together the roll-over opportunities — approaching rocks from angles and positions that allow the tyre to ride up and over rather than into their face. Finding this line requires reading the terrain well ahead and steering toward rocks at the correct approach angle. Skilled rocky terrain riders appear to float through sections that slower riders slam and deflect through.

### Principle 2: Speed Threshold — Above It, Rocks Absorb; Below It, They Deflect
There is a minimum effective speed in rocky terrain below which the suspension cannot react quickly enough to absorb impacts and the bike deflects off rocks rather than rolling over them. This speed varies with rock size and suspension setup but is generally higher than instinct suggests for beginners. The counter-intuitive reality is that slowing down in a rock section often makes it more difficult — the bike becomes harder to steer, the suspension works less effectively, and individual rock impacts become larger. Maintaining speed above this absorption threshold is as important as line selection.

### Principle 3: Vision 5+ Rocks Ahead — Planning, Not Reacting
Looking at the rock directly under the front wheel is too late to act on. Rocky terrain requires the rider to scan 5 to 10 rocks ahead, identifying the best rolling path and steering toward it while the current rocks are being absorbed unconsciously by the suspension. This is the same principle as target fixation in reverse: instead of fixating on the obstacle, the rider fixates on the line. The brain needs processing time to convert what the eyes see into steering inputs, so the further ahead vision is directed, the smoother the riding becomes.

### Principle 4: Throttle Pulses Between Rocks — Not Constant Throttle
Unlike sand (where constant throttle is the rule), rocky terrain benefits from rhythmic throttle pulses timed to the spaces between rocks. As the front wheel exits a rock, a brief throttle pulse drives the bike forward and slightly loads the rear — helping the front wheel clear the next obstacle. During rock contact, the throttle is neutral or slightly off to allow the bike to absorb without adding drive force that would increase impact loading. The rhythm of throttle on in the gaps and neutral on the rocks creates a cadence that experienced rocky terrain riders feel intuitively.

### Principle 5: Body Weight Neutral to Slightly Rearward
Rocky terrain weight distribution is different from sand. Weight should be central to neutral — enough rearward emphasis to allow the front wheel to roll over rocks rather than dig into them, but not so rearward that the front wheel is unable to track the terrain. On large or square-edged rocks, a brief deliberate weight shift rearward at the moment of front wheel contact allows the front to lift slightly and roll over rather than slamming into the face. On rounded rocks and in rock gardens, neutral position with active legs absorbing allows the suspension to do its job.

### Principle 6: Tyre Pressure and Compound for Rocky Terrain
Rocky terrain rewards appropriate tyre preparation. Lower pressures (within reason — too low risks pinch flats on sharp edges) allow the tyre carcass to deform over rocks, increasing contact patch and improving rolling. Harder compound tyres resist cutting and abrasion from sharp rock edges. Tyre inserts (mousse or foam inserts replacing the tube) are used by many hard enduro riders in rocky sections to eliminate the risk of pinch flats while allowing lower effective pressures. Tyre choice is a significant setup variable in rocky terrain racing.

## 3. TECHNIQUE BREAKDOWN BY LEVEL

### 3a. Beginner Level
**Goal:** Learn to read ahead, maintain enough speed to absorb rocks, and keep an active standing position with bent knees that absorb impacts.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Standing with active, bent knees — legs as shock absorbers
- Looking at least 3–4 rocks ahead (not the one under the wheel)
- Maintaining a minimum speed rather than slowing as it gets rocky
- Keeping arms bent and relaxed — not locking out against rock impacts

**What Good Looks Like:**
The rider approaches a moderate rock section standing with bent knees. They are looking ahead, not down. The bike deflects occasionally but the rider absorbs it through the legs. They maintain a consistent speed through the section rather than slowing dramatically. There are no violent arm jolts — the legs are taking the impacts.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Looking at the rock directly in front of the wheel:** Processing time is insufficient to steer around it. → Train eyes to look further ahead constantly.
- **Slowing down dramatically in rocks:** Makes each impact harder and steering less stable. → Hold the minimum effective speed or slightly above.
- **Locked arms absorbing rock impacts:** Impacts transfer directly to the torso and shoulders. → Bend the knees and let legs absorb — keep arms relaxed.
- **Straight legs while standing:** Removes the leg-suspension benefit. → Maintain athletic crouch throughout.

### 3b. Intermediate Level
**Goal:** Select the rolling line through rock sections, distinguish between square-edge and rounded rock technique, and time throttle pulses to maintain momentum.

**Progression Markers from Beginner:** Rider can traverse moderate rocky sections at a consistent speed without being deflected. Standing position is stable. Vision is extending forward.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Reading rock shape (square-edge vs rounded) and adjusting approach
- Finding the rolling path — approaching rocks from the side that allows roll-over
- Throttle pulse timing — drive between rocks, neutral on contact
- Weight shift timing — brief rearward shift as front contacts large rocks

**What Good Looks Like:**
The rider visibly chooses a line through the rocks — not the most direct path but the rolling path. Throttle pulses are rhythmic and visible (the bike accelerates slightly between rocks). On large rounded rocks, the front wheel visibly rolls up and over rather than impacting the face. The rider's torso remains relatively stable while the legs and bike move beneath them.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Approaching square-edge rocks straight-on at low speed:** The tyre hits the vertical face of the rock and deflects or stalls. → Approach at a slight angle or with more speed; the tyre needs to climb, not hit.
- **Constant throttle in rocky terrain:** Drive force during rock contact increases impact loading and reduces suspension performance. → Pulse the throttle; off during contact, on in the gaps.
- **Following the track centre in rock gardens regardless of rolling quality:** The worn centre line may not be the rolling line. → Look for the path with the best roll-over angles.

### 3c. Advanced Level
**Goal:** Navigate complex, large-rock terrain at pace — reading the macro line through rock gardens, managing square-edge hits precisely, and maintaining high speed through sections that beginners walk.

**Progression Markers from Intermediate:** Rider finds rolling lines automatically. Throttle pulses are rhythmic and unconscious. Can navigate large individual rocks correctly.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Macro line selection in rock gardens before entering — planning the full path
- Managing wrist angle during large rock absorption (preventing wrist buckle on impact)
- Rocky terrain cornering — maintaining grip on rock in corners
- Navigating wet rocks — dramatically reduced grip even on grippy surfaces

**What Good Looks Like:**
The rider pauses briefly before a complex rock garden, scans the full section, and identifies a macro line. They enter at committed speed, the line threads through the rolling path with minor micro-adjustments within it. Throttle rhythm is consistent. On individual large rocks, the front wheel climbs smoothly and the rear drives over without drama. Exit speed from the rock section is high.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Entering rock gardens without a macro line plan:** Making steering decisions under time pressure in a rock garden leads to poor choices. → Stop, look, plan, commit.
- **Wrist buckling on large square-edge impacts:** Impact force collapses the wrist and the handlebar deflects the front wheel. → Pre-tense wrists slightly before large impacts; keep grip firm but not rigid.
- **Riding wet rocks at the same pace as dry:** Wet rock is dramatically slicker than dry. → Reduce speed, increase smoothness of all inputs, eliminate aggressive braking.

## 4. DIAGNOSTIC CUES (AI COACHING ENGINE REFERENCE)

### Visual Cues — Correct Technique
- Front wheel visibly rolling up and over rocks rather than impacting the face and deflecting
- Rider's torso height relatively stable while legs and bike move beneath — active leg absorption visible
- Bike following a non-straight path through rocks — threading the rolling line rather than the shortest path
- Rhythmic, subtle speed variations visible — brief acceleration in gaps between rocks
- Weight distribution neutral to slightly rearward — rider not hanging over the bars
- Vision clearly directed ahead — head up, not looking down at the wheel

### Visual Cues — Error Indicators
- Front wheel repeatedly deflecting off rock faces — wrong approach angle, insufficient speed, or weight too far forward
- Rider's torso bobbing and jolting — legs not absorbing, impacts reaching upper body
- Rigid arms fully extended and locking against rock impacts — visible from chase camera
- Bike taking the straight-line path regardless of rock positions — no line selection occurring
- Speed dropping dramatically on entering rock section — throttle reduction from instinct
- Rider looking down at the wheel or the rock directly ahead — insufficient vision planning

### Audio Cues
- Sharp, violent impact sounds suggest the tyre is hitting rock faces rather than rolling over — approach angle or speed issue
- Rhythmic, moderate impacts with brief engine acceleration sounds between: correct technique pattern
- Consistent engine tone with smooth speed: rolling line found, throttle management working
- Metallic or frame-level impacts: very hard hits — may indicate square-edge contact with under-suspension or insufficient speed

### Sensor Cues
- High-frequency accelerometer spikes at handlebars: square-edge rock impacts, potentially with incorrect approach
- Lower-amplitude but broader-duration suspension data: rounded rock roll-over (suspension compressing and rebounding, not spiking)
- GPS speed showing consistent velocity through rock section: speed threshold maintained — correct
- GPS speed showing sharp deceleration mid-section: throttle lifted or rock impact exceeded absorption capability

## 5. COACHING CUES & LANGUAGE

### Positive Reinforcement
- "Your eyes are forward and your legs are working. The bike is moving underneath you and your head is staying level. That's exactly the rock technique."
- "I can see you finding the rolling path — you're not going in a straight line, you're going over the top of the rocks. Much smoother."
- "Good throttle rhythm — drive in the gaps, soft on the rocks. You're keeping the speed up and the suspension is doing its job."

### Corrective Feedback
- "You're hitting the face of every rock because you're going too slow and too straight. You need more speed — above a certain speed the rocks absorb, below it they deflect."
- "Look further ahead — you're watching the rock that's about to hit you, but by then it's too late. I need your eyes 5 rocks ahead minimum. Look where you want to be, not where you are."
- "Your arms are straight and you're getting thrown around by the rocks. Bend your knees — that's where your shock absorption is. Let the bike move, hold on loosely."
- "You're riding the centre line of the track but the rolling line is to the left of it. Look for the path that goes over the tops of the rocks, not into their faces."

### Progression Prompts
- "Next pass, choose your line before you enter. Stop at the start, look at the whole section, and point to the path you're going to take. Then ride exactly that."
- "I want you to count your rocks ahead as you ride — try to always see at least five rocks further than where you are. If you can't, you're looking too close."
- "Try this rhythm: as your front wheel comes off a rock, give a brief throttle push. Rock exit = throttle. Rock contact = neutral. Build that rhythm."

## 6. DRILLS & PRACTICE EXERCISES

### Drill 1: The Vision Walk
**Purpose:** Build the habit of scanning ahead by first practising vision on foot.
**Setup:** A rocky section of trail.
**Execution:**
1. Walk (on foot, not riding) through the rocky section at a normal pace.
2. Practice looking 5–8 metres ahead and identifying where you would ride.
3. Point to the rolling path as you walk it.
4. Repeat on the bike at slow walking pace — keep eyes forward, not down.
5. Gradually increase speed as forward vision becomes habit.
**Success Criteria:** Rider can describe the rolling line from a visual scan before entering. Vision consistently directed 5+ rocks ahead while riding.
**Duration/Reps:** 20-minute vision drill walking then riding.
**Progression:** Increase complexity of rock section. Reduce the planning stop — eventually identifying the line at approach speed without stopping.

### Drill 2: Speed Threshold Finder
**Purpose:** Identify the minimum effective speed for a given rock section.
**Setup:** A consistent, moderate rocky section, 20m long.
**Execution:**
1. First pass: walking pace — feel the deflections and impacts.
2. Second pass: slow jogging pace — note improvement.
3. Third pass: moderate enduro pace — note the point at which the bike begins to absorb rather than deflect.
4. Identify that speed as the minimum effective threshold.
5. Practice maintaining speed above that threshold consistently.
**Success Criteria:** Rider can identify and articulate the speed at which the section transitions from deflecting to absorbing. Maintains that speed or above consistently.
**Duration/Reps:** 10 passes at varying speeds.
**Progression:** Apply the same process to harder rock sections with larger or squarer rocks.

### Drill 3: Throttle Rhythm Practice
**Purpose:** Develop the throttle-on/throttle-neutral rock rhythm.
**Setup:** A moderate rocky section with clearly visible gaps between rocks.
**Execution:**
1. Ride the section consciously timing throttle to gaps: apply briefly as front exits each rock, neutral as front contacts next rock.
2. Exaggerate the rhythm initially — make it obvious to yourself.
3. Film the session if possible — the throttle rhythm should be visible in the bike's speed variations.
4. Gradually reduce the exaggeration until the rhythm is subtle but present.
**Success Criteria:** Rider can feel the throttle rhythm as automatic. Speed through section is more consistent than without the rhythm.
**Duration/Reps:** 20 minutes focused rhythm practice.
**Progression:** Apply the rhythm to larger rocks. Combine with the rolling line drill.

### Drill 4: Square-Edge Approach Angles
**Purpose:** Learn to approach square-edged rocks correctly to roll over rather than impact.
**Setup:** A single large square-edged rock, isolated for practice.
**Execution:**
1. First approach: dead straight, slow speed. Feel the impact and deflection.
2. Second approach: dead straight, more speed. Note improvement.
3. Third approach: slight angle (10–15 degrees) at moderate speed. The tyre begins to roll up the face.
4. Fourth approach: correct angle and speed — the front wheel rolls over cleanly.
5. Memorise the angle and speed combination that produced the cleanest roll-over.
**Success Criteria:** Rider can roll over a large square-edged rock consistently. Can describe the approach angle and speed that works.
**Duration/Reps:** 30 approaches on the same rock.
**Progression:** Add multiple square-edge rocks in sequence. Increase rock size.

## 8. TERRAIN & CONTEXT VARIATIONS

### Rounded River Rocks
Excellent grip when dry — the rounded shape allows roll-over with lower approach speeds. When wet, become very slick despite grippy appearance. Line selection focuses on avoiding the highest points (crowns) of individual rocks in corners, where lateral stability is reduced.

### Angular Quarried Rock / Square-Edge Rocks
Maximum impact potential — approach angle and speed are critical. The tyre must approach at an angle that allows the sidewall to contact and compress before the tread, enabling a rolling motion. Direct frontal impacts at low speed result in wheel deflection and potential pinch flats.

### Loose Rock Over Hard Base
The most unpredictable combination. Individual rocks move as the tyre loads them. The tyre must be cautious about putting weight on any single rock that might shift. Multiple contact points (loading several rocks simultaneously) is preferable to loading a single loose rock.

### Rocky Descents
Weight significantly rearward. Rear brake primary — but applied smoothly to avoid locking on rock. Front brake used progressively and carefully. Front wheel must remain free to deflect/absorb — do not brake hard with front while on rocks. Speed management before the descent, not during.

### Rocky Climbs
Weight forward to prevent front wheel lift. Throttle pulses timed to maintain drive without excessive wheel spin on rock. If wheel spin begins on a rocky climb, it immediately reduces grip and may cause the bike to stall. Momentum approach to steep rocky climbs is strongly preferred.

## 9. EXPERT INSIGHTS & SOURCES

### Key Insights
- **Wade Young** (multiple Red Bull Erzbergrodeo finalist, extreme rocky terrain specialist): Notes that the biggest difference between riders in extremely rocky terrain is vision — riders who look far enough ahead appear to float, riders looking close appear to fight. "The rock under your wheel is already too late. The rock five ahead is where your brain should be."
- **Alfredo Gomez** (Hard Enduro World Championship podium): Emphasises that commitment to speed in rocky terrain is counter-intuitive but essential — slowing in rocks does not make them easier, it makes them harder. Speed is working suspension.
- **Trials riding influence:** Trials technique for rock negotiation is built on the same rolling line principle — approach angles, timing, and roll-over versus impact are the core skills. Enduro riders who have trials training background typically excel in rocky terrain because these principles were drilled in a slow-speed, precision environment.
- **Tyre insert technology:** The adoption of tyre inserts (foam mousse replacement) in hard enduro has allowed riders to run lower effective pressures in rocky terrain without the risk of pinch flats. This has meaningfully changed what is possible — more tyre carcass deformation means better grip on irregular rock surfaces.
- **Wrist injury in rocky terrain:** The wrist buckle on square-edge impacts is one of the most common injury mechanisms in enduro. Riders are advised to maintain slight wrist flex tension before anticipated impacts — pre-loading rather than being caught relaxed. This distributes the impact force across the forearm rather than concentrating it at the wrist.
