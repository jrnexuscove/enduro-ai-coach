# Momentum Retention Score

## 1. OVERVIEW

The Momentum Retention Score (MRS) measures how effectively a rider maintains useful speed and forward progress through a section of terrain, relative to what is achievable given the terrain demands. High MRS riders enter sections with appropriate speed, carry it through with minimal unnecessary loss, and exit with strong speed that sets up the next section well. Low MRS riders lose speed repeatedly through over-braking, stalling, failed obstacles, or route choices that force additional deceleration. The MRS is distinct from raw speed — a slow but consistent rider who loses minimal momentum scores better than a fast rider who repeatedly brakes hard and then has to rebuild speed. In enduro racing, momentum is often described as the most important resource: once lost, it must be rebuilt using traction, engine power, and time — all of which are limited. The MRS quantifies the rider's success at managing this resource over a full session.

## 2. CORE PRINCIPLES

### Principle 1: Momentum Lost Is Energy That Must Be Rebuilt
Every time a rider brakes more than the terrain demands, they convert kinetic energy into heat at the brake discs and must then re-accelerate to rebuild speed. Re-acceleration costs traction budget, fuel, physical effort, and time. The cumulative cost of unnecessary momentum loss over a 20-minute enduro section is significant — it is often a more accurate predictor of section time than peak speed. A rider who never brakes unnecessarily will beat a faster rider who repeatedly kills and rebuilds momentum.

### Principle 2: Entry Speed Is the Most Influential Variable
Speed carried into a section — the entry speed — largely determines what is achievable through and out of the section. Entering a corner with good speed preserves momentum through the arc; entering too slow forces a re-acceleration that costs traction and time. The MRS captures this by measuring not just average speed but the entry-exit speed ratio per section: how much of the entry speed was successfully carried to the exit.

### Principle 3: Terrain-Required Braking Is Not a MRS Penalty
Braking for a legitimate obstacle, tight corner, or technical feature is correct technique — it is not a momentum retention failure. The MRS system must distinguish between terrain-demanded deceleration (which is scored neutrally or positively when correctly timed) and additional, unnecessary deceleration (which is penalised). This distinction requires terrain context from GPS, video analysis of corner geometry, or known section profiles. Without this distinction, the MRS would simply reward reckless speed.

### Principle 4: Stall Events Are the Highest-Impact MRS Penalties
A stall is a complete momentum loss event — the bike goes from moving to stopped with the engine off. Recovery requires restarting, re-engaging, and re-accelerating from zero — the maximum possible momentum cost. The MRS treats stall events as the most severe single-event penalty. A session with three stall events in a 10-minute section will score dramatically lower on MRS than a session with no stalls, even if overall speed is similar.

### Principle 5: Failed Obstacles Create Cascading Momentum Loss
When a rider fails an obstacle (stalls on a hill climb, gets stopped by a rock step, loses balance at a log crossing), the direct speed loss is compounded by the recovery: repositioning the bike, re-approaching, physically pushing or lifting, plus mental disruption. A single failed obstacle can cost 15-30 seconds of section time and reset momentum entirely. The MRS tracks failed obstacles from video (bike stopped, non-terrain-demanded) and sensor data (zero speed events not at designated stops).

### Principle 6: Momentum Is Track-Specific and Lap-Comparable
The most accurate MRS analysis compares a rider's own performance across multiple laps of the same section — not against an absolute speed benchmark. Lap 2 vs Lap 1 through the same 200m rocky section reveals whether the rider improved momentum management. This self-comparison removes terrain variability and isolates the rider's skill development. Multi-lap MRS trend (improving, stable, degrading) is a direct progression indicator.

## 3. TECHNIQUE BREAKDOWN BY LEVEL

### 3a. Beginner Level
**Goal:** Develop awareness that momentum loss has a cost, and identify the most obvious unnecessary braking events.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Recognising over-braking as a habit (braking when the terrain does not require it)
- Learning what "carrying speed" feels like versus what "rebuilding speed" feels like
- Avoiding stalls by managing engine speed and clutch use appropriately
- Understanding that going slower does not always mean going safer — sometimes it means losing stability

**What Good Looks Like:**
The rider approaches a moderate section and enters with a purposeful speed — not maximum, but not braked to near-stop. They pass through without slowing significantly and exit with a similar speed to entry. There are no stall events on moderate terrain. The rider does not brake repeatedly in a straight-line section without obstacle justification.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Braking at every uncertain moment:** Rider treats the brake as a comfort tool rather than a control tool. Why it happens: perceived safety through slowing. → Practice committing to an entry speed and holding it — discover that moderate speed is often more stable than very slow speed.
- **Chopping throttle instead of managing speed:** Rider closes throttle completely before obstacles, killing momentum. Why it happens: instinct to reduce power before something uncertain. → Learn to maintain trail throttle through moderate obstacles — keep the engine pulling.
- **Stalling on moderate hills from slow speed:** Rider enters hill climbs too slowly and stalls. Why it happens: fear of going fast on climbs. → Practice increasing hill climb entry speed in controlled progressions.

### 3b. Intermediate Level
**Goal:** Develop a conscious entry speed strategy per section type, and use self-comparison across laps to identify momentum loss patterns.

**Progression Markers from Beginner:** No stalls on terrain within current ability level. Braking events are identifiably terrain-linked rather than habitual. Rider can articulate when they lost momentum and approximately why.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Setting deliberate entry speed per section type (corner, climb, rock garden)
- Identifying the "stale speed" zone — where the rider slows below useful speed and then hesitates further
- Reading sections ahead to maintain speed rather than reacting and braking
- Comparing lap times on repeated sections to track MRS improvement

**What Good Looks Like:**
The rider looks ahead at a corner and adjusts speed smoothly 20-30m before the corner — no late heavy braking. They carry a purposeful speed through the corner and begin rolling the throttle on at the apex. Over a rock garden, the rider maintains trail throttle throughout — speed may vary slightly but never drops to near-zero unless the terrain genuinely demands it.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Speed hesitation loop:** Rider enters a section slightly too fast, panics and brakes hard, slows below optimal, then hesitates through the section very slowly. Why it happens: entry speed calibration error compounded by fear response. → Practice entry speed in small increments — 5 km/h more each run until the correct entry speed is found.
- **Inconsistent section performance:** MRS varies wildly between laps. Why it happens: lack of a consistent strategy — rider approaches each lap differently. → Develop a section-specific entry speed target and commit to it every lap.

### 3c. Advanced Level
**Goal:** Optimise entry-exit speed ratios across all section types, and maintain MRS consistency under fatigue and competition pressure.

**Progression Markers from Intermediate:** MRS consistently in the 75+ range across typical sessions. Entry speed strategy is deliberate and consistent. Stall events are rare and terrain-justified when they occur.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Fine-tuning entry speed to the exact terrain-specific optimum — not just "enough," but the maximum maintainable speed
- Momentum preservation through linking sections — the exit of one section is the entry of the next
- MRS management under fatigue — late-session momentum loss is an endurance metric
- Reading conditions (surface change, moisture, sun angle) that affect the optimal entry speed

**What Good Looks Like:**
An advanced rider's speed trace through a complex section shows smooth variation — speed adjusts precisely to terrain demands without large unexplained drops. The entry speed into each corner or feature is consistent across laps (±5 km/h). Section time standard deviation across laps is low. Late in the session, speed maintenance remains consistent — endurance keeps the MRS from degrading.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Optimising for single obstacles but losing section momentum:** Advanced rider manages every individual obstacle perfectly but decelerates too early or accelerates too late between features, losing section flow. → Think in sequences: the exit of obstacle A is the entry to obstacle B. Optimise the link.
- **Late-session MRS degradation:** Score drops significantly in the final 20% of the session. Why it happens: physical and cognitive fatigue reduce anticipation quality. → Track MRS by session segment — if the final segment consistently drops, this is the training priority.

## 4. DIAGNOSTIC CUES (AI COACHING ENGINE REFERENCE)

### Visual Cues — Correct Technique
- Consistent forward progress through sections — no stop events not demanded by terrain
- Speed entering and exiting a section is comparable — minimal unexplained reduction
- Rider's weight position shifts in anticipation of sections — not reacting to them with braking
- Bike continues to move forward on climbs — no stall events, no slow-down-and-restart patterns
- Corner exits show throttle application beginning at apex, not waiting until fully straight

### Visual Cues — Error Indicators
- Bike stops or near-stops in sections where terrain does not require it — unnecessary braking event
- Repeated approaches to the same obstacle followed by retreat — confidence and momentum failure
- Stall event visible: engine stops, rider puts foot down, restarts — maximum momentum loss penalty
- Late heavy braking visible as large front-end compression events in corners or before obstacles
- Rider visibly hesitates (decelerates, looks around, adjusts repeatedly) on features within their technical capability

### Audio Cues
- RPM drops to idle or near-idle in a section where the terrain does not demand deceleration: momentum loss event — throttle closed or clutch pulled in
- Engine note that remains consistent through a technical section: positive momentum indicator
- Stall event: engine sound stops abruptly without gradual deceleration — immediate high-penalty event
- Multiple restart attempts after stall: RPM rise-fall-rise-fall sequence before sustained idle — cascade momentum loss
- Consistent engine note that rises at section exit: good momentum carry-through into acceleration

### Sensor Cues
- GPS speed trace: unnecessary speed loss events defined as speed drop > 15% without GPS-confirmed terrain feature
- Zero-speed events on GPS not at designated stop points: stall or failed obstacle — immediate penalty
- Speed standard deviation within a section: high variance = choppy momentum; low variance = good carry
- Entry-exit speed ratio per section: calculated from GPS timestamps and speed at section entry/exit markers
- Time at zero speed (cumulative): total stall and stop time per session; directly subtracted from effective riding time

## 5. COACHING CUES & LANGUAGE

### Positive Reinforcement
- "Perfect momentum carry through that rock garden — you went in at 25 and came out at 22. That's nearly nothing lost. That's exactly what we're after."
- "No stalls all session. That means every piece of energy you put into the bike stayed in the bike. Clean riding."
- "Your entry speed is dialled — I can see you reading the section and setting up earlier. The exits are paying for it."

### Corrective Feedback
- "You braked there and the terrain didn't ask you to. What were you braking for? That's momentum you spent and now you have to buy it back with throttle and traction."
- "That's the second stall on that climb. You're entering too slow — you run out of momentum before the top. Go in 5 km/h faster and commit to it. Momentum is your friend on climbs, not your enemy."
- "You're treating every uncertain moment as a reason to brake. Instead, ask yourself: does the terrain actually require this? If not, trust your speed and keep the throttle open."
- "You're losing 3 seconds every lap between the first corner and the rock step. It's not the obstacles — it's the gap between them. You slow down when there's nothing to slow down for."

### Progression Prompts
- "Next lap, I want you to pick one section and focus only on the entry speed. Don't change anything else — just go in 5 km/h faster than you have been and see what happens."
- "Compare your lap 1 and lap 3 speed through the climb section. Which lap was faster? What did you do differently? That difference is your MRS lesson for today."
- "Count how many times you brake this lap when there is no obstacle or corner requiring it. That number is your target to reduce next week."

## 6. DRILLS & PRACTICE EXERCISES

### Drill 1: The Entry Speed Ladder
**Purpose:** Find the optimal entry speed for a specific section through progressive increments.
**Setup:** A 30-50m section with a clear start and end point — a corner, small climb, or rock garden.
**Execution:**
1. Run the section at 10 km/h entry speed. Note exit speed, feel, and any stops.
2. Run at 15 km/h entry. Note changes.
3. Continue increasing in 5 km/h increments until a speed that causes loss of control or forced stopping.
4. The optimal entry speed is 1-2 steps below the failure point — maximum maintainable momentum.
5. Run the section five times at the optimal speed to build habit memory.
**Success Criteria:** Rider identifies an entry speed that produces consistently clean section completion with no stalls or forced stops. Exit speed is within 10% of entry speed.
**Duration/Reps:** 30 minutes. Minimum five runs at each speed increment.
**Progression:** Apply the same process to a different section type. Then begin linking two sections with optimal entry speeds.

### Drill 2: The No-Brake Challenge
**Purpose:** Identify unnecessary braking habits by removing the option temporarily.
**Setup:** A 100m section of moderate difficulty trail — rough enough to be interesting but within the rider's capability.
**Execution:**
1. Run the section with normal riding — note naturally how many times you use the brakes.
2. Run the section again with a rule: no brakes unless genuinely required for safety or terrain.
3. Discover which braking events were habitual rather than necessary.
4. Now ride normally again — consciously choosing whether each brake application is terrain-justified.
**Success Criteria:** Rider can identify at least two habitual braking events per section pass that were not terrain-required.
**Duration/Reps:** 20 minutes, minimum six passes alternating normal and no-brake.
**Progression:** Apply to a more complex section. Then apply during a full trail loop as a mental exercise.

### Drill 3: Multi-Lap MRS Comparison
**Purpose:** Use self-comparison across laps to identify and target specific momentum loss points.
**Setup:** A consistent loop of 3-5 minutes with at least one climb, one corner sequence, and one technical feature.
**Execution:**
1. Run three laps, recording video from a fixed camera at two or three key points.
2. After riding, review video: at which point does speed appear to drop most between laps?
3. Identify the single highest momentum loss event across the laps.
4. Run two more laps with a specific focus on that one event — hold momentum through it.
5. Review again: did the targeted event improve?
**Success Criteria:** Section time reduces by at least 5% through momentum management improvement alone — no increase in risk.
**Duration/Reps:** Five laps plus 15-minute video review.
**Progression:** Target a second momentum loss point. Progressively eliminate loss events across the full loop.

### Drill 4: Stall Recovery Speed Drill
**Purpose:** Reduce the time cost of stall events when they do occur, and build resilience against cascade momentum loss.
**Setup:** A moderate hill or technical feature where stall practice is safe.
**Execution:**
1. Deliberately stall the bike on the feature.
2. Time the restart-to-moving sequence: foot down, neutral if needed, kick or button start, clutch out, moving.
3. Target recovery in under 5 seconds.
4. Practice the stall recovery sequence as a rapid, calm response — not a panic response.
5. Reflect: what is the fastest recovery order for your specific bike and situation?
**Success Criteria:** Rider achieves consistent stall recovery (bike moving again) within 5 seconds from stall event.
**Duration/Reps:** 10 deliberate stall recovery repetitions.
**Progression:** Practice on increasingly steep terrain. Then practice recovery mid-hill rather than at the bottom.

## 8. TERRAIN & CONTEXT VARIATIONS

### Rocky Technical Terrain
Momentum is most critical on rocky terrain — the minimum speed needed to roll over rocks is often higher than it feels. Under-speed on rocks causes the front wheel to deflect and stop rather than roll. Target higher entry speeds than feel intuitive, backed by commitment and correct body position.

### Mud
Momentum in mud is a different calculation — very high speed can cause control problems while very low speed causes bogging and stalling. Optimal MRS in mud is at the moderate speed range where the tyre can clear mud and maintain traction. Entry speed that creates excessive roost or loss of steering is too high; entry speed that stalls tyre rotation is too low.

### Loose-over-Hard Terrain
Entry speed must account for reduced traction — the bike can maintain speed but cannot accelerate as forcefully on a loose surface. Entry speed should be set slightly higher than on hardpack to compensate for the reduced traction budget available for re-acceleration if momentum is lost.

### Hill Climbs
Momentum is make-or-break on hill climbs — there is no option to partially complete a climb and recover. The MRS scoring for hill climbs weights stall events very heavily, and successful climb completion with maintained momentum (reaching the top without a speed-zero event) is scored as a high-value positive event.

### Competition
Under race conditions, the optimal MRS strategy shifts toward preserving momentum at all costs, accepting slightly wider lines or minor technical compromises to avoid momentum loss events. Time cost of a stall or failed obstacle in competition is always higher than the cost of a marginally suboptimal but clean pass.

### Key Insights
- **Jonny Walker** (multiple Hard Enduro World Championship podiums): Known for his smooth, momentum-preserving riding style on extreme terrain — commentators and coaches regularly note that he rarely stalls and maintains forward progress where others stop repeatedly.
- **Graham Jarvis** on momentum in hard enduro: "You can be the most technically skilled rider in the world, but if you're stopping every 20 metres to pick up your bike, you're not going to finish the race. Momentum is everything."
- **Enduro racing coaching principle** (across multiple schools): The phrase "momentum is your friend" is a near-universal coaching staple — it encapsulates the insight that riders perceive speed as danger when it is often the opposite: momentum provides stability, traction management options, and obstacle clearance.
- **Bret Tkacs / ADVMotoSkills**: Regularly demonstrates that riders who increase entry speed by 10-20% on a section they are struggling with will find the section easier, not harder — the stability and obstacle clearance benefits of momentum outweigh the perceived risk.
- **Rally Raid coaching** (Dakar Rally preparation): Momentum management across 500+ km stages is a primary race craft topic — unnecessary speed loss over the course of a day compounds into significant time and energy cost.
- **Motor racing parallels**: In circuit racing, "braking too early" is one of the most common beginner errors — the same principle applies in off-road riding, where over-braking is the primary momentum destroyer.
