# ERROR-07: Target Fixation

## 1. OVERVIEW

Target fixation is a neurological phenomenon, not a technique error. When a perceived threat appears in the rider's visual field — a tree, a drop, a boulder, another rider — the threat-detection system of the brain (the superior colliculus and amygdala acting together) redirects gaze toward the object and locks it there. The neuromuscular connection between gaze direction and steering direction — the visuomotor system — then causes the rider to steer toward the fixated object. The bike goes where the eyes go. This is not a metaphor or an oversimplification; it is a direct neural pathway. The fixation can happen faster than conscious awareness, and the steering input can be well underway before the rider realises they are looking at the hazard.

**CASCADE EFFECTS:** The cascade from target fixation is fast and often irreversible. The sequence is: hazard appears in field of view → gaze locks onto hazard → visuomotor system steers toward hazard → bike tracks toward hazard → impact. In most cases, this cascade runs to completion in under one second. The AI coaching engine should treat target fixation as a special-category error because its timeline is too short for corrective input to be given by a coach in real time — the intervention must be trained before the event, not delivered during it. Cross-reference with MENTAL-05 (high-pressure mental management) and INTEL-04 (vision and terrain reading) — both are prerequisites for effective fixation override training.

The correct override is a trained reflex: when a threat appears, immediately look at the desired path away from it. The word "immediately" is critical — a delay of even 300ms means the steering has already begun moving toward the hazard. The training goal is to make the look-away a faster automatic response than the original fixation. This requires deliberate practice under conditions that induce the threat-detection response — not just verbal repetition of the instruction.

Target fixation is most common in specific environments: tight woodland riding (trees are the most common fixation target), cliff-edge tracks (the drop is fixated), water crossings (uncertain surface induces fixation), and race situations with obstacles, rocks, or other riders in close proximity. Riders who have experienced target fixation without a crash (near misses) often report being aware of it happening but being unable to override it in the moment — this confirms that the reflex is faster than deliberate control and validates the training approach.

## 2. CORE PRINCIPLES

### Principle 1: The Visuomotor Connection Is Real and Fast
The connection between where the eyes point and where the steering goes is a genuine neural pathway, not a coaching abstraction. The visuomotor system evolved for hand-eye coordination tasks — catching, throwing, reaching — and it works at speeds far faster than conscious steering decisions. On a motorcycle, this means the handlebars begin moving toward the fixated point before the rider is consciously aware of looking at it. This is why verbal instruction ("don't look at the tree") is insufficient — the fixation has already happened and the steering has already begun by the time the instruction can be processed.

### Principle 2: The Survival Instinct Creates the Problem
The threat-detection response that causes fixation exists for good evolutionary reasons — tracking a threat is valuable in most survival situations. The problem is that on a motorcycle, the visuomotor result of threat-tracking (steering toward the threat) is opposite to the desired survival outcome (steering away from it). The override training is essentially retraining a survival instinct — teaching the threat-detection response to trigger look-away rather than look-at. This requires significant repetition in a context that activates the threat response.

### Principle 3: The Desired Path Is the Only Correct Focus Point
The trained fixation-override response is simple in concept: when a hazard appears, look at the desired path instead. Not at the space next to the hazard, not at the hazard briefly and then away — look at the desired path immediately. The desired path (the gap between trees, the open trail, the exit from the drop) becomes the focus point, and the visuomotor system then steers the bike toward the desired path. This is the reflex that must be built to replace the hazard-fixation reflex.

### Principle 4: Fixation Duration Determines Outcome
A very brief fixation — eyes pass over the hazard and immediately continue scanning — may produce only a minor steering perturbation that the rider can correct. Sustained fixation (more than 0.5 seconds) on a hazard while at speed is almost always unrecoverable because the steering input toward the hazard becomes too large to overcome within the available space. Training must target reducing fixation duration to zero — not reducing it to 0.3 seconds.

### Principle 5: Environmental Preview Reduces Fixation Risk
Target fixation is more likely when a hazard appears suddenly and unexpectedly than when it has been identified in advance and classified as a "managed hazard." A rider who has walked a section, identified the problematic tree, and decided their line past it before riding is much less likely to fixate on it — the threat-detection response is muted because the object has been pre-classified as known and managed. Environmental preview (walking technical sections) is therefore a direct fixation-prevention tool, not just a line-planning tool.

## 3. TECHNIQUE BREAKDOWN BY LEVEL

### 3a. Beginner Level
**Goal:** Understand that target fixation is neurological and non-obvious, and begin building the look-at-the-gap habit in controlled situations.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Understanding the visuomotor connection — why the bike goes where the eyes go
- Practising looking at the gap, not the obstacle, in low-threat drill environments
- Recognising what it felt like after a fixation event — the "I was looking at the tree" realisation

**What Good Looks Like:**
When approaching a cone slalom, the rider's eyes track the path between cones — not the cones themselves. When asked where they were looking during the drill, the rider can accurately report "the space between the cones, not the cones." This is the foundational look-at-the-path habit being established.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Looking at the obstacle while saying "I know not to look at it":** The intellectual knowledge has not transferred to the reflex. → More repetition of the look-at-gap drills under increasing stress.
- **Brief look at hazard then correction:** Rider fixates for 0.2–0.3 seconds before overriding. → The initial fixation is still present; the override needs to be faster.
- **Only applying the habit in drills, not on trail:** Rider looks at the gap in practice but fixates on trail hazards. → Trail application drills with coached hazards.

### 3b. Intermediate Level
**Goal:** Build the look-away reflex to the point where it begins to function automatically on trail — not just in drills.

**Progression Markers from Beginner:** Rider understands the neurological basis. Can explain the override. Can demonstrate look-at-gap in cone slalom without error. Has consciously practised the override on trail at least once.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Look-away reflex training under stress — drills that approach the threat-response activation threshold
- Identifying high-risk fixation environments (woodland, drops, water) and applying active scanning strategies
- Post-fixation near-miss debrief — learning from the event rather than suppressing it

**What Good Looks Like:**
In tight woodland, the rider's eyes are constantly scanning the open path between trees — not resting on any single tree. When an unexpected obstacle (log across the path) appears, the rider's eyes immediately go to the gap around it, not the obstacle. The steering follows the eyes to the clear path.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Fixation in woodland but not in other environments:** Rider has built the habit for trees but not for other hazard types. → Extend the training to each specific high-risk environment.
- **Looking at the drop on descent:** Rider on a steep, narrow descent looks at the drop edge repeatedly rather than the trail ahead. → Specific drop-edge drills with look-ahead training.

### 3c. Advanced Level
**Goal:** Automatic fixation override across all environments and at race pace — the look-at-desired-path reflex triggers before the fixation can begin to steer the bike toward the hazard.

**Progression Markers from Intermediate:** Rider rarely fixates in known environments. Can self-report fixation events accurately. Has experienced the override working automatically at least once without conscious effort.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Fixation override at race pace — the override must work faster as closing rate increases
- Group riding dynamics — other riders as fixation hazards in close-quarters competition
- Maintaining scanning discipline under fatigue — fixation risk increases as the session progresses

**What Good Looks Like:**
In a race situation, a fallen rider appears unexpectedly on the inside line of a corner. The racing rider's eyes immediately go to the outside gap, the bike steers around the fallen rider, and the section is completed without incident. The override operated within 150ms of the hazard appearing — faster than conscious decision-making.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Race-pressure fixation regression:** The override holds in training but the urgency of competition triggers the original reflex. → Race simulation training is required — not just technical practice.
- **Fatigue fixation:** Late in a long stage, scanning discipline degrades and fixation risk increases. → Recognise fatigue as a fixation risk factor; increase conscious scanning effort in the last third of stages.

## 4. DIAGNOSTIC CUES (AI COACHING ENGINE REFERENCE)

### Visual Cues — Correct Technique
- Rider's helmet direction visibly scanning ahead — not fixed on a single point
- Smooth steering trajectory through gaps — not sudden corrections near hazards
- Consistent line through tight sections — no last-moment deviations toward obstacles
- From forward-facing camera: rider's line of sight tracking the path ahead of obstacles, not the obstacles

### Visual Cues — Error Indicators
- Helmet direction fixed toward a hazard for more than 0.3 seconds — fixation signature
- Steering deviation toward a hazard followed by late correction — fixation steering in progress
- From forward-facing camera: rider's eyes appear directed at an obstacle rather than the path around it
- Bike tracking directly toward a hazard until a late correction — severe fixation with partial override
- Impact with an obstacle that was visible and avoidable — fixation that was not overridden

### Audio Cues
- Sudden crash sound without preceding technical difficulty — fixation-driven impact
- No audio cue of braking or course correction before impact — fixation override did not activate
- From rider-worn audio: "oh no" or similar before impact — conscious recognition of fixation but override too late

### Sensor Cues
- Handlebar direction sensor: steering angle moving toward a mapped obstacle — fixation steering signature
- IMU: unexpected steering input toward an obstacle followed by sudden large correction — fixation then override
- GPS track: deviation from intended line toward a known hazard location — fixation path signature
- Forward-facing camera with gaze tracking: fixation duration on hazard >300ms — AI vision analysis

## 5. COACHING CUES & LANGUAGE

### Positive Reinforcement
- "That's it — I could see your helmet tracking the gap between the trees, not the trees. Your line followed your eyes perfectly. That's the trained reflex working."
- "You had a log appear late in that section and I watched your eyes go to the gap immediately. That's the override. That's what months of practice builds."
- "Good trail scanning — you're always looking for the exit, not the hazard. That keeps the bike on the right path automatically."

### Corrective Feedback
- "You looked at the tree. I could see your helmet fixed on it — and the bike started to track toward it before you corrected. The override came too late. The reflex needs to be faster."
- "When something appears in your path, your first look must be away from it — not at it for a second and then away. The first look determines the first steering input."
- "In that tight woodland section, your eyes are resting on trees. They need to be finding the gaps. Look for the light between the trunks — that's your path."
- "After that near-miss, tell me what you were looking at. I want to hear it said out loud, because the first step to fixing it is knowing where the eyes went."

### Progression Prompts
- "The next time through that section, I want you to pick your look-at point before you enter. Pick the gap you're going to ride through. Look at that gap from the moment you enter to the moment you exit."
- "We're going to do the narrow gap drill with a helper who will randomly move one of the cones as you approach. Your override has to work on a surprise. That's the test."
- "Once the override is reliable in drills, I want you to identify the three highest-risk fixation points on your local trail. Walk them, pick your look-at point for each, and practise riding through them specifically."

## 6. DRILLS & PRACTICE EXERCISES

### Drill 1: Cone Slalom Look-at-Gap
**Purpose:** Build the look-at-desired-path habit in a low-threat environment where fixation on obstacles (cones) can be clearly identified.
**Setup:** 8–10 cones set in a slalom pattern, 4–5 metres apart. Open, flat surface.
**Execution:**
1. Walk the slalom first, identifying the "gaps" between cones — the path.
2. Ride the slalom at slow speed. Actively look at the gap ahead of your current position, not the cone you are currently passing.
3. After 3 passes, a coach asks: "What were you looking at?" Rider must accurately report looking at gaps, not cones.
4. Increase speed progressively. Maintain gap focus throughout.
5. Add a coach randomly placing a cone unexpectedly — look at the gap around it immediately.
**Success Criteria:** Rider accurately reports looking at gaps on 3 consecutive passes. Slalom line is smooth, not correcting near each cone. Response to unexpected cone is immediate look-at-gap.
**Duration/Reps:** 20 minutes. Multiple sessions.
**Progression:** Tighten cone spacing. Add a wider obstacle (board) that requires a genuine detour.

### Drill 2: Narrow Gap Threading
**Purpose:** Train the look-at-desired-path reflex under genuine spatial threat — the narrowness of the gap creates a mild threat response that starts to activate the fixation tendency.
**Setup:** Two posts, trees, or cones set with a gap just wide enough for the bike — approximately 1.2 metres.
**Execution:**
1. Approach the gap at slow speed. Fix eyes on the centre of the gap — not the posts.
2. If eyes drift to either post, stop and reset. Eyes must be on the gap.
3. Increase approach speed progressively. Maintain gap focus.
4. Add a coach who narrows the gap slightly with each session.
**Success Criteria:** Rider threads the gap 10 consecutive times with eyes consistently on the gap. No corrections near the posts. Reports looking at the gap, not the posts.
**Duration/Reps:** 15 passes per session. 3 sessions.
**Progression:** Reduce gap width. Add a curve approach before the gap so the gap appears later than on a straight approach.

### Drill 3: Surprise Hazard Override
**Purpose:** Train the fixation override specifically under surprise conditions — the scenario where fixation is most likely to occur on trail.
**Setup:** A short trail-like section with clear path. Coach positioned to introduce a surprise hazard (cone, flag, or foam obstacle) at random.
**Execution:**
1. Rider rides the section at moderate pace.
2. Coach places a hazard in the path at an unexpected point — rider must override and go around it.
3. After each pass: did the rider look at the hazard or the gap? Coach confirms by watching helmet direction.
4. Repeat with varying hazard positions and timings.
**Success Criteria:** Rider routes around the surprise hazard on 9 of 10 attempts without looking at the hazard. Helmet direction toward gap is confirmed by observer on each successful pass.
**Duration/Reps:** 15 surprise hazard introductions per session.
**Progression:** Increase approach speed. Add multiple surprise hazards in sequence.

### Drill 4: Woodland Scanning Protocol
**Purpose:** Build a specific scanning pattern for high-risk woodland riding that prevents fixation before it can begin.
**Setup:** A section of woodland trail — trees visible on both sides. Moderate complexity.
**Execution:**
1. Before riding the section, walk it and identify the "light" — the open path visible between trees ahead.
2. While riding, consciously scan for the light — where is the next gap visible?
3. The cue is "find the light" — the open space always visible ahead is the desired path.
4. At the end of the section, report: were there any trees that pulled the gaze? Which ones?
**Success Criteria:** Rider can ride the woodland section with no fixation events (confirmed by self-report and camera review). Can identify which tree positions are high-risk fixation targets for future sessions.
**Duration/Reps:** 3–5 passes per session. Debrief after each.
**Progression:** Add a fallen tree obstacle that appears mid-section. Apply the "find the light" cue immediately when the obstacle appears.

## 8. TERRAIN & CONTEXT VARIATIONS

### Tight Woodland
The highest fixation risk environment. Trees appear rapidly in peripheral vision, triggering threat response frequently. The override must be fast and reliable. The "find the light" scanning protocol is the specific intervention for woodland.

### Cliff and Drop-Edge Tracks
The drop at the edge of a narrow track is a powerful fixation target — height triggers a strong threat response. Eyes must be fixed on the track, not the drop edge. Specific training on narrow tracks with drops requires very progressive exposure — not riding near drops before the fixation override is reliable.

### Water Crossings
Uncertain surface (hidden rocks, depth variation) causes fixation on the near edge of the water rather than the exit point. The correct focus is the exit point of the water — where the track re-emerges on the far bank. Training the exit-point focus prevents the near-water-edge fixation.

### Race Situations with Other Riders
In competition, a fallen or slow rider appearing on the inside line is a fixation target. The urgency of competition increases the threat-response activation. Race simulation drills with human obstacles are required for this scenario.

## 9. EXPERT INSIGHTS & SOURCES

### Key Insights
- **David Hough (Proficient Motorcycling author, road safety researcher):** Describes target fixation as one of the most consistent contributing factors in motorcycle crashes where a hazard was visible and avoidable — the rider's path tracks directly toward the fixated object with no evidence of brake or steering override. The pattern appears across all types of motorcycle riding.
- **Neuroscience basis:** The superior colliculus reflex for threat-directed gaze has been studied in primate research. The pathway from gaze direction to manual/steering response is well established in visuomotor neuroscience. The specific application to motorcycle riding is documented in road safety research.
- **Off-road coaching experience:** Multiple enduro and trial coaches (including those at the ISDT/ISDE preparation camps) report that target fixation training is one of the most frequently taught topics at intermediate to advanced level, specifically because intellectual understanding of the problem does not translate to reflex override without specific drill work.
- **Race incident analysis:** Analysis of filmed enduro and hard enduro crashes consistently shows the bike tracking toward an identified hazard without braking or steering correction — the fixation signature. The hazard was visible and avoidable; the fixation prevented the override.
- **Fatigue amplifies fixation risk:** Sports psychology research and coaching observation both confirm that fixation override capacity degrades under fatigue. The prefrontal cortex — which houses the deliberate override capability — is among the first cognitive systems compromised by physical exhaustion. This means the most dangerous period in a long enduro stage is the final third, where both riding demands and fixation risk are elevated simultaneously. Coaches teaching advanced fixation override specifically practise the override at the end of hard training sessions, not only when the rider is fresh.
- **Peripheral versus central gaze in fixation:** One distinction useful for AI video analysis: target fixation typically manifests as a switch from broad, scanning gaze (peripheral vision active, head sweeping) to a narrowed, locked central gaze directed at a single point. On helmet-mounted camera footage, the camera stops sweeping and holds on the hazard — a clear visual signature distinguishable from normal scanning behaviour. Training riders to recognise this switch in their own footage is an effective self-coaching tool.
