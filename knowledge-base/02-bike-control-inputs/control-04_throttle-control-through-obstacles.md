## 2. OVERVIEW

Obstacle navigation is where throttle control becomes a timing discipline, not just a smoothness discipline. A log crossing, a rock ledge, a deep rut, or a step-up requires not only the right amount of throttle but the right timing — a burst too early lifts the front wheel before the obstacle, a burst too late buries it into the obstacle. The throttle must be coordinated with body movement, clutch action, and line selection to create a controlled, fluid crossing. This topic covers the specific throttle strategies for the most common enduro obstacles, and how to develop the split-second timing that makes technical terrain feel manageable.

---

## 3. CORE PRINCIPLES

### 3.1 The Throttle Is a Lifting and Driving Tool
In obstacle navigation, the throttle serves two functions simultaneously: it drives the bike forward (momentum) and it transfers weight rearward (which lightens the front wheel). A timed throttle burst can lift the front wheel over a ledge or log without the rider physically pulling the bars up. The throttle becomes the primary lifting mechanism when used with correct timing.

### 3.2 Timing Window: Before Contact, Not At Contact
The throttle burst for an obstacle must begin fractionally before the front wheel reaches the obstacle face — not at the moment of contact. This pre-contact input creates the weight transfer and front wheel lightening before the wheel hits the step. At contact, the input is already working. If the input starts at contact, it's already late.

### 3.3 Consistency Through, Not Just Over
A common mistake is thinking only about the front wheel — the rear wheel hits the obstacle half a second later. A throttle burst for the front must be maintained or followed by a second input to drive the rear wheel over the same obstacle. Single-wheel thinking leads to the rear wheel catching and washing the back end off.

### 3.4 Momentum Is the Safety Net
For most obstacles, entry momentum dramatically reduces the precision demanded of the throttle. A correctly fast entry over a log means the front wheel arcs over it with minimal throttle input required. Slow entry demands a perfectly timed burst. Teaching riders to maintain appropriate speed into obstacles reduces the precision required and increases success rates.

### 3.5 Ruts and Channels Are Different From Raised Obstacles
In a rut, the throttle drives through, not over. Over-throttling in a rut causes the rear to spin sideways and lose directional control. Smooth, consistent power through the rut length maintains directional stability. The exception is a rut exit — a timed burst helps drive the rear wheel out of the channel cleanly.

## 4. TECHNIQUE BREAKDOWN BY LEVEL

### 4a. Beginner Level

**Goal:** Develop safe, consistent approach to common obstacles — logs, rocks, deep ruts — using throttle and momentum correctly.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Log crossings: maintaining entry momentum and adding a brief throttle burst as front wheel approaches the log face
- Rock steps: approaching in a gear that provides power without a sharp power spike, then rolling on smoothly
- Deep ruts: smooth, constant throttle through the length; adding power only at exit
- Importance of not stopping mid-obstacle — commitment is required

**What Good Looks Like:**
- Log crossings: front wheel arcs over smoothly, rear follows without catching
- No stalling on rock steps or ledges
- Ruts: bike tracks the channel direction rather than washing sideways

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Rolling off at the obstacle face:** Instinctive fear response — the rider sees the obstacle and backs off. This is the worst response: front wheel ploughs in, momentum lost, crash or stall. Cue: "Throttle is your friend at the obstacle — back off before or after, not at."
- **Pulling the bars instead of using throttle:** Beginners try to physically lift the front wheel. This is ineffective above basic speeds. Teach the throttle-burst as the lifting mechanism.
- **Forgetting the rear wheel:** "I'm over it" thinking stops the throttle input after the front. Rear wheel catches. Teach through-obstacle, not to-obstacle.

---

### 4b. Intermediate Level

**Goal:** Develop timed throttle bursts for specific obstacles and begin combining clutch with throttle for larger features.

**Progression Markers from Beginner:** Handles normal-sized logs and rock steps consistently. Now working on larger obstacles, logs mid-turn, and rut exits.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Log crossing with a burst: clutch partially in, rev up slightly, then release clutch as front wheel approaches — the combined clutch-throttle creates a pop that lifts the front
- Rock ledge with step: body weight rearward at approach, throttle burst timed to front wheel contact with the face
- Rut exits: deliberate throttle burst at the exact exit point to drive the rear wheel out cleanly
- Off-camber obstacles: throttle only until the obstacle is cleared, then manage the off-camber with body position

**What Good Looks Like:**
- Larger logs crossed without the front wheel deflecting
- Rock ledge climbs done in one smooth motion — not stalling halfway up
- Rut exits clean without sideways rear wheel slide

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Early burst timing:** Throttle burst starts 1 second too early — front wheel goes up before the obstacle, then comes down on the face. "The burst starts when the wheel is at the obstacle face, not approaching it."
- **Excessive rev in clutch technique:** Over-revving before releasing causes a massive spike that flicks the front too high or causes wheel spin. The rev should be modest — just above idle to idle+50%.

---

### 4c. Advanced Level

**Goal:** Fluid, natural obstacle navigation with throttle as an unconscious tool; compounding techniques for obstacle combinations.

**Progression Markers from Intermediate:** Individual obstacles handled well. Now developing multi-obstacle sections, high-speed approach, and extreme terrain features.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Continuous obstacle sections: maintaining throttle management across 5–10 obstacles in quick succession without stopping the flow
- High ledges and step-ups: combining full clutch pop, body weight, and precise throttle timing
- Downhill obstacles: throttle on descents to control suspension loading and prevent front wheel wash
- Creek crossings: throttle burst to drive through the water entry and maintain speed through the section
- Fallen trees across hillside (angled obstacles): timing the throttle and line to drive the rear wheel around the angle

**What Good Looks Like:**
- Obstacle sections ridden without perceptible hesitation
- Rider appears relaxed — not bracing or lurching for each feature
- Front wheel lifts look effortless because timing is perfect
- Consistent pace maintained through varied obstacle density

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Cognitive overload in complex sections:** Rider reverts to reactive throttle under high obstacle density. Address with section-specific practice before race or long ride use.
- **Over-relying on the clutch pop:** Heavy clutch use for every log is fatiguing and imprecise at higher speeds. Throttle timing alone should be the primary tool above moderate speeds.

## 5. DIAGNOSTIC CUES (AI COACHING ENGINE REFERENCE)

### Visual Cues

**Correct Technique:**
- Front wheel visibly lightens or lifts at the obstacle face — not before, not after
- Bike flows over obstacles in a single motion — no stopping, no hesitation
- Rider's body shifts rearward slightly as throttle burst is applied — weight transfer is visible
- Rear wheel tracks cleanly over the same line as the front

**Error Indicators:**
- Front wheel ploughing into the face of a log or ledge = throttle too late or not applied
- Front wheel lifting excessively before the obstacle = throttle too early
- Rear wheel kicking sideways after crossing = throttle spike too large, rear spun on landing
- Rider physically pulling bars up = not using throttle as lifting mechanism
- Bike stopping mid-obstacle = momentum lost, throttle hesitation or wrong gear

### Audio Cues — CRITICAL

**Correct Technique:**
- Log crossing: a brief, clean RPM rise as front approaches the log face ("wrr-UP-wrr") then settles
- Rock ledge: smooth sustained RPM through the climb face — not a spike, a steady push
- Rut section: consistent, low, steady RPM through the channel — then a brief rise at exit
- Clutch-pop technique: a small rev build before release ("..wrr-BUP.."), then normal

**Error Indicators:**
- RPM spike before the obstacle = throttle input too early — front wheel lifted before face
- Stall sound (engine cut) = throttle not maintained through the obstacle, momentum lost
- Rear wheel spin sound at obstacle exit = too much throttle at exit or spin on landing
- "Clunk" of front wheel hitting the face hard = no throttle lift, front wheel ploughed in
- Repeated clutch-pop misjudgements: over-rev sound ("BRAP") then spin = too much rev before pop

### Sensor Cues (Future IMU Integration)
- Pitch angle: front wheel rise timing relative to obstacle detection = early/late throttle input
- Rear wheel slip at obstacle = excessive throttle during crossing
- Longitudinal deceleration spike before obstacle = rider rolling off at obstacle face (common beginner error)
- Suspension compression signature: smooth loading at obstacle vs. sharp spike = good vs. poor entry momentum

## 6. COACHING CUES & LANGUAGE

**Positive Reinforcement:**
- "Perfect timing on that log — front wheel lifted right at the face. That's exactly the burst timing we're working on."
- "You committed to that ledge and drove it cleanly. The throttle carried you over — well done."
- "Your rut exit was clean — I could hear the burst right at the exit point. That's the timing."

**Corrective Feedback:**
- "You're rolling off when you see the obstacle — that's the instinct to fight. At the face, you need more throttle, not less."
- "Your front went up too early — the burst started before the wheel was at the log. Push it later. Wait until you can almost see the wood under the tyre."
- "You're thinking 'front wheel over', then stopping your input. The rear hits the same log half a second later — drive through, not over."
- "In that rut, you were spinning — smooth constant power through the channel, save the burst for the exit."

**Progression Prompts:**
- "Now try the log but approach a gear higher and 5 km/h faster — feel how much easier the timing becomes with more entry momentum."
- "On that rock ledge, try applying the throttle one second earlier than you were. Not earlier than the obstacle — earlier in your approach so you're already building when you hit."

## 7. DRILLS & PRACTICE EXERCISES

### Drill 1: The Log Crossing Progression
**Purpose:** Build front wheel lift timing with increasing entry speed.
**Setup:** Single log, 20–30cm diameter, on flat ground.
**Execution:**
1. Walking pace: feel the front wheel reach the log, then add a brief throttle burst — watch the front arc over
2. Jogging pace: same timing — burst at log face contact
3. Moderate pace: the burst becomes smaller as momentum does more work
4. Increase pace until the log requires almost no burst — momentum carries the front over
**Success Criteria:** Consistent clean crossing at all speeds without front wheel catching.
**Duration:** 30 minutes
**Progression:** Add a second log 1 metre behind the first — rear-wheel timing.

### Drill 2: The Rock Step Approach
**Purpose:** Develop controlled momentum and throttle for ledge obstacles.
**Setup:** Rock ledge or simulated step (log on a gentle slope), 20–40cm high.
**Execution:**
1. Approach in a middle gear that provides power without a spike
2. Maintain consistent moderate throttle to the face
3. At the face, roll on smoothly — do not burst, do not back off
4. Drive through until both wheels are on top
5. Repeat with slightly less entry speed until finding the minimum speed that works
**Success Criteria:** Consistent clean ledge crossing without stall at various entry speeds.
**Duration:** 25 minutes

### Drill 3: The Rut Commitment Line
**Purpose:** Develop smooth throttle through ruts and clean exit timing.
**Setup:** Natural rut section or simulated with boards/rails, minimum 10 metres long.
**Execution:**
1. Enter at consistent speed — hold throttle steady through the entire length
2. At the exit, apply a deliberate burst (1/4 to 1/2 throttle increase) to drive the rear out
3. Count "hold… hold… hold… NOW" through the length, bursting on "NOW"
4. Vary exit timing to find the right burst point
**Success Criteria:** Consistent clean exit without rear wash, throttle steady through the channel.
**Duration:** 20 minutes

### Drill 4: The Clutch-Pop Log Lift
**Purpose:** Develop controlled clutch-pop technique for larger obstacles.
**Setup:** Log 40cm+ diameter, flat approach.
**Execution:**
1. Approach at slow speed in 2nd gear
2. 2 metres before log: pull clutch halfway in, rev slightly above idle
3. At 1 metre: release clutch smoothly while adding throttle — feel the front lift
4. Drive rear wheel over with maintained throttle
5. Practice the rev amount — start modest, increase until it's too large, find the correct level
**Success Criteria:** Front wheel lifts cleanly without excessive flight or catching.
**Duration:** 20 minutes

## 9. TERRAIN & CONTEXT VARIATIONS

### Logs on Flat Ground
Classic drill environment. Front wheel lifts with moderate burst, rear follows. Consistency drill — aim for the same entry speed and burst timing every crossing.

### Logs on Uphill
Gravity assists the rear wheel if the bike is already pointed uphill. Slightly less burst needed. The risk is stalling between the log and the continuation of the climb — drive through immediately.

### Logs on Downhill
The most challenging. Momentum carries the front over, but the rear can be thrown forward by gravity after crossing. Light rear brake while driving through with smooth throttle manages this.

### Rock Ledges and Steps
More abrupt than logs — no rounded top. Front wheel hits a vertical face. Throttle timing must be slightly earlier than with a log, and sustained longer to drive the front up the face rather than into it.

### Deep Ruts
Direction control, not obstacle navigation. Smooth throttle, no spinning. Exit is the obstacle — a clean burst at the right moment.

### Creek / Water Crossings
Entry momentum + throttle burst drives through the water resistance. Mid-crossing throttle maintains speed through the drag. Exit burst climbs the far bank. Water on brake rotors reduces braking after — be prepared.

## 10. EXPERT INSIGHTS & SOURCES

**Nic Garland (GNCC, Hard Enduro):** Describes obstacle throttle timing as "playing the rhythm of the terrain." Each obstacle has a beat — the throttle burst is timed to the beat, not guessed at. Repeated obstacle sections teach the rhythm automatically.

**Bobby Hewitt (Motocross Champion, Coaching):** Notes that the majority of front wheel catches on logs are caused by the rider looking at the obstacle rather than past it. "Look where you want to go — your body and throttle timing follow your eyes."

**Trials Influence:** In trials, almost every feature is negotiated with a precise throttle-timing burst — often called a "dab" of power rather than a sustained application. The precision of trials throttle timing transfers directly to enduro obstacle navigation. Many enduro riders train on trials bikes specifically to develop this skill.

**Adventure/Dual-Sport Note:** Heavier adventure bikes require more momentum and slightly more throttle to achieve the same front wheel lift on obstacles. The timing windows are the same; the required input is larger. Weight makes precision more important, not less.
