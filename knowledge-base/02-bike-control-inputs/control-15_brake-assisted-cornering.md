## 2. OVERVIEW

Brake-assisted cornering uses the brakes — primarily the rear brake — as active steering and rotation tools within a corner, not merely as deceleration devices. The rear brake in particular can be used to slide the rear wheel outward, rotate the bike onto a tighter line, control the exit angle, and manage chassis attitude through a corner. This is an advanced technique that expands the rider's corner toolkit beyond throttle and body position, and is particularly powerful on loose or slippery surfaces where conventional high-speed cornering grip is limited. Understanding when and how to use the brake as a cornering tool transforms the rear brake from a stopping device into a precision handling input.

---

## 3. CORE PRINCIPLES

### 3.1 Rear Brake Slides the Rear Outward — Rotating the Bike
When the rear brake is applied while the bike is cornering, it reduces rear wheel speed relative to the bike's forward motion. On loose terrain, this causes the rear tyre to slide outward from the corner — rotating the bike's nose toward the inside of the corner. This rotation effect is used intentionally to tighten the corner line, point the bike toward the exit, and set up a clean drive out. The throttle then stops or reverses the rotation.

### 3.2 Front Brake Loads the Front Tyre — Enhancing Turn-In
A brief front brake application at turn-in compresses the front fork, increases front tyre contact patch load, and creates sharper steering response. The front tyre "bites" the surface with more force at the moment of steering input. This technique (the basis of trail braking) can be used at corner entry specifically to sharpen the turn-in without a full trail braking strategy.

### 3.3 The Brake-Throttle Balance Controls Rear Slide Angle
Rear slide in a corner is controlled by the interplay of rear brake and throttle. Rear brake initiates and maintains the slide angle. Adding throttle reduces and stops the slide. Increasing rear brake increases the slide angle. The rider holds the rear slide at the desired angle by balancing these two inputs — then drives out of the slide with throttle as the bike points toward the exit.

### 3.4 Body Position Must Complement Brake-Assisted Cornering
For a brake-assisted rear slide, the rider's inside foot should be forward and the body slightly inside the corner — this helps the bike lean and rotate without the rider becoming unstable. The elbows remain up and the grip is light on the bars to allow the front wheel to steer naturally during the slide. Gripping the bars tightly during a rear slide causes the rider to fight against the bike's natural steering correction.

### 3.5 Entry Speed Enables Exit Speed
Brake-assisted cornering is often more about setting up the exit than about corner entry. By using the rear brake to rotate the bike during entry or mid-corner, the nose is pointed toward the exit line earlier than conventional technique allows. This means the throttle can be applied earlier and more aggressively, resulting in a faster exit even if the corner entry was not particularly fast.

## 4. TECHNIQUE BREAKDOWN BY LEVEL

### 4a. Intermediate Level (Entry for this topic)

**Goal:** Understand and begin using the rear brake as a rotation tool on loose terrain.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Light rear brake at corner entry on loose terrain — feel the rotation effect
- Maintaining the slide with balanced brake and throttle through a loose hairpin
- Understanding the difference between a controlled rotation and a lose-the-rear slide
- Body position for rear slides: weight balanced, inside foot forward, light bar grip

**What Good Looks Like:**
- Loose corners navigated with a controlled rear swing that tracks the exit line
- Rear slide is predictable and directional — not random
- Exit is set up by the rotation — throttle can come on before the bike is fully straight

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Holding rear brake too long:** Slide angle increases past exit pointing, bike goes wide. "Release the brake when the nose is on the exit line — not before, not after."
- **Adding too much rear brake in a corner:** Causes a large rear slide that exceeds the rider's ability to manage with throttle. "Start light — 20% rear brake in the corner. Feel the rotation before adding more."
- **Gripping the bars too tight during slide:** The front wheel cannot self-steer to correct the slide. "Light hands — let the bars move."

---

### 4b. Advanced Level

**Goal:** Dynamic, precise brake-assisted cornering at pace — using brakes to control corner radius, exit angle, and exit timing across all terrain types.

**Progression Markers from Intermediate:** Basic rear slide rotation is controlled. Now integrating at pace and across varied corner shapes.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Late corner rotation: brake applied late in the corner to rapidly rotate the bike when the entry line was wider than planned
- Front + rear combination: brief front brake load + rear slide for a complete corner management package
- Hairpins and tight turns: rear brake as primary steering for very tight turns on loose terrain where normal lean angle cannot create sufficient rotation
- Rutted corners: rear brake to control the rear wheel in a rut exit — preventing the rear from springing sideways uncontrolled
- Counter-throttle integration: rear slide stopped instantly with a precise throttle input — counter-throttle technique
- Speed of correction: managing different slide speeds — slow rotation vs. fast snap

**What Good Looks Like:**
- Corner entries of variable quality are consistently "saved" and converted to clean exits by mid-corner rear brake
- Very tight turns executed with rear slide rotation in place of high lean angle
- Rutted corners: rear wheel tracks cleanly out of ruts without sideways snap
- Rider appears loose and confident — slide corrections are small and immediate

**Common Mistakes:**
- **High-siding from counter-throttle too abrupt:** Rear is sliding wide; rider adds too much throttle too fast — rear grips suddenly and flicks the bike upright violently. "Counter-throttle smoothly — don't snap it. Roll it on."
- **Using brake-assisted cornering on grippy terrain:** On high-grip terrain, rear brake in a corner causes an abrupt rear slide that's much harder to manage. Technique is primarily for loose terrain.
- **Relying on brake rotation without body position:** The body must support the rotation — inside foot forward, upper body calm. Without body position, the rotation is unpredictable.

## 5. DIAGNOSTIC CUES (AI COACHING ENGINE REFERENCE)

### Visual Cues

**Correct Technique:**
- Rear of bike visibly swinging wider than front during corner — controlled oversteer
- Rear slide is continuous and smooth — not a snap then corrected
- Rider's inside foot is forward and positioned to put down if needed
- Bar grip appears light — bars steering themselves during slide
- Exit acceleration begins before bike is fully straight — rotation has set up the exit line early

**Error Indicators:**
- Rear slide going wider than the exit line = too much rear brake or too little counter-throttle
- Rear snapping sideways suddenly = rear brake stamped rather than progressively applied in corner
- Rider gripping bars tightly during slide = fighting the bike's natural steering, imprecise correction
- Front washing at corner entry = front brake trail carried too deep on loose surface
- High-side motion = counter-throttle applied too abruptly after rear slide

### Audio Cues — CRITICAL

**Correct Technique:**
- Rear tyre sliding: soft continuous "shushing" sound — smooth drag of the rubber on the dirt
- Balanced state (brake + throttle): slight engine note + continuous rear slide sound together
- Exit throttle: engine note rises as the shush diminishes — counter-throttle working, slide stopping
- Corner sequence: "…slide shush… wroooom…" as deceleration transitions to acceleration

**Error Indicators:**
- Loud sudden squeal of rear tyre = rear brake stamped suddenly in corner — not progressive
- Rear slide that suddenly stops with "BANG" of chassis snapping = abrupt grip return — high-side risk
- Long, uncontrolled scrub sound = rear slide going too far without throttle counter-control
- Front slide sound (higher pitched, from front) = front brake too heavy at lean — front washing

### Sensor Cues (Future IMU Integration)
- Rear wheel speed significantly lower than front wheel speed in corner = rear brake sliding rear wheel
- Yaw rate: controlled, consistent rotation into corner = brake-assisted rotation working
- Yaw rate sudden spike = rear snapping — abrupt rear brake input
- Lean angle + rear wheel slip: advanced riders show rear slip managed at desired lean angle
- Throttle sensor: smooth increase from zero after rear slide = correct counter-throttle; sudden spike = over-aggressive counter-throttle (high-side risk)

## 6. COACHING CUES & LANGUAGE

**Positive Reinforcement:**
- "The rear came around cleanly and the exit was set up early. That's what brake-assisted rotation looks like."
- "I could hear the rear sliding smoothly and then you picked it up with throttle — controlled and precise."
- "Your hands were light through that slide — you let the bars work and it tracked perfectly."

**Corrective Feedback:**
- "Your rear went too wide — hold the brake less long. Release when the nose is pointing at the exit, not later."
- "You're gripping the bars tightly during the slide — that's fighting the correction. Let your hands be soft and let the front wheel steer itself."
- "The counter-throttle was too sharp — the rear came back fast and the bike lurched. Roll it on smoothly next time."
- "That technique works on this loose surface. Don't take it onto the next hardpack corner — on grip, a rear slide is much harder to manage."

**Progression Prompts:**
- "Try a tighter hairpin — get the rear sliding deliberately, point the nose at the exit, then drive out. It should feel like you rotated the bike on a pivot."
- "Now try using just the rear brake — no front — and see how the rotation changes. Feel the difference in chassis attitude."

## 7. DRILLS & PRACTICE EXERCISES

### Drill 1: The Slow Loose Oval
**Purpose:** Experience controlled rear slide rotation in a safe, low-speed environment.
**Setup:** Marked oval on loose dirt. Approximately 20m diameter.
**Execution:**
1. Ride the oval at slow pace
2. At the apex of each corner: apply light rear brake — feel the rear step out
3. Counter with slight throttle to hold the slide angle — don't let it grow or stop
4. Hold the slide through the corner exit, then release rear brake and drive out
5. Build to doing the entire oval in a continuous controlled slide
**Success Criteria:** Can hold a controlled, consistent rear slide angle through each corner.
**Duration:** 20 minutes

### Drill 2: The Rotation Hairpin
**Purpose:** Use rear brake to rotate through a very tight turn that lean angle alone cannot navigate.
**Setup:** Very tight turn (3–4m radius) on loose dirt.
**Execution:**
1. Approach the hairpin at moderate speed
2. As the turn begins: apply rear brake to rotate the rear outward
3. The rotation should bring the nose through the turn
4. When nose points at exit: drive out with throttle
5. Repeat 15 times until the rotation feels natural and predictable
**Success Criteria:** Hairpin navigated consistently using rotation, not just lean.
**Duration:** 25 minutes

### Drill 3: Brake Hold Duration
**Purpose:** Develop precise timing of rear brake release at the correct exit angle.
**Setup:** Corner with a clearly defined exit line.
**Execution:**
1. Apply rear brake to initiate rotation
2. Mark the exact moment when the nose is on the exit line — this is the release point
3. Practise releasing the brake at exactly that point across 10 repetitions
4. Compare: too early (bike doesn't rotate enough), too late (bike rotates past exit)
**Success Criteria:** Rear brake released at exactly the correct exit angle on 8 out of 10 attempts.
**Duration:** 20 minutes

### Drill 4: Counter-Throttle Smoothness
**Purpose:** Develop smooth counter-throttle to stop rear slides without high-side risk.
**Setup:** Loose oval or corner.
**Execution:**
1. Initiate a controlled rear slide with rear brake
2. With rear at desired angle: smoothly add throttle over 0.5–1 second
3. Feel the slide stop gradually — no snap
4. Complete several attempts with the throttle applied faster each time
5. Find the fastest counter-throttle application that doesn't trigger a snap
**Success Criteria:** Rear slide stopped smoothly in all cases; no high-side events.
**Duration:** 20 minutes

## 9. TERRAIN & CONTEXT VARIATIONS

### Loose Dirt
Primary application. Rear brake rotation is most controllable and effective on loose dirt. The rear slides predictably and grip recovery is gradual enough to manage.

### Gravel
Works well — similar to loose dirt. Slide angle can be slightly larger before becoming unmanageable.

### Mud
High-rotation slides possible but very hard to predict. Grip recovery is often sudden. Use brake-assisted rotation with caution in deep mud.

### Hardpack
Avoid intentional rear slides on high-grip surfaces. Rear brake in a hardpack corner can cause very abrupt and hard-to-manage slides with high-side risk.

### Rutted Corners
Rear brake in a rutted corner helps prevent the rear from springing sideways at rut exit. Light rear brake through the rut and at the exit point controls the release.

### Sand
Rear slides in sand require significant rear brake — sand does not offer high friction resistance. Slides can be large and slow, which is manageable. Useful for slow sand hairpins.

## 10. EXPERT INSIGHTS & SOURCES

**Taddy Blazusiak:** His video analysis shows frequent, confident rear brake use through corners — particularly in tight rocky sections where rotation is needed without room for a wide sweep. His inside foot is always forward and his bar grip visibly light during slides.

**Motocross Cornering Evolution:** MX track riders developed brake-assisted cornering (rear brake drift exit) as a standard technique. The high-grip MX surface makes the slides more controllable and predictable. Transfer to enduro — where surfaces are more variable — requires recalibration but the fundamental technique is identical.

**Chris Birch (Adventure Bikes):** Notes that on heavy bikes, brake-assisted rear rotation is often the only practical way to negotiate very tight turns because lean angle is limited by ground clearance and weight. The rear brake becomes a steering tool of necessity for large-displacement enduro and adventure bikes.
