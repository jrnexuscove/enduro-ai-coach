# Line Choice Theory (Fast vs Safe vs Efficient)

## 1. OVERVIEW

Line choice is the foundational strategic skill of off-road riding — it determines the traction available, the exit speed possible, the obstacle difficulty encountered, and the physical effort required, all before the rider makes a single control input. Poor line choice forces the rider to compensate with superior technique; good line choice makes technique requirements minimal. There are three distinct categories of line available on any given section: the fastest line (optimised for maximum speed, often the most technically demanding and highest risk), the safest line (most forgiving surface and path, lowest risk, lower speed potential), and the most efficient line (optimal for the context — the line that produces the best outcome given the current session, conditions, and rider state). The critical insight is that the "best" line is context-dependent, not absolute. In competition late in a stage, the efficient line may be the same as the safe line. In training, the fast line may be prioritised for skill development. Understanding which category of line the situation demands — and then executing within that category — is what line choice theory provides.

## 2. CORE PRINCIPLES

### Principle 1: The Exit Velocity Principle — Lines Are Evaluated by Their Exit, Not Their Entry
A corner line that allows high entry speed but produces slow exit speed is inferior to a line that requires slower entry but produces maximum exit speed. In off-road riding, as in circuit racing, it is the speed carried out of a section that matters for overall pace — exit speed converts directly to time gained on the next straight or section. This means lines should be evaluated by their exit potential, not their visual appeal at entry. A tight, late entry that produces a clean, fast exit beats a wide, aggressive entry that requires heavy braking at the exit.

### Principle 2: The Off-Road Apex Is the Best Grip Point, Not the Geometric Centre
On asphalt, the apex is a geometric concept — the innermost point of the turn radius. On off-road terrain, the apex is the best traction point available within the turn. This may be a rock, a root crossing, a patch of firm soil, or the inner edge of a rut — wherever the surface provides the most grip at the turn's critical moment. Finding the off-road apex requires reading the surface, not just the geometry of the trail. Two riders may take lines that look similar from above but feel completely different because one is using the grip surface and the other is not.

### Principle 3: Context Determines Line Category — There Is No Universally "Best" Line
The fastest line is the correct choice when time is the priority and risk is acceptable. The safest line is the correct choice when consequence of error is high, fatigue is significant, or conditions are degraded. The efficient line is the correct choice when energy conservation is as important as speed — in long stages, late in a race, or in training focused on repeatability. Beginners benefit from riding the safest line until technique allows the efficient line. Intermediate riders develop the efficient line as their default. Advanced riders can select from all three based on reading the current context accurately.

### Principle 4: Line Quality vs Line Execution — A Good Line Poorly Executed Loses to a Decent Line Perfectly Executed
The best theoretical line on a section is irrelevant if the rider cannot execute it. A line that requires extreme precision at entry speed, perfect body position at the apex, and a very specific exit angle is the "best" line only if the rider can deliver those things consistently. For most riders in most situations, a slightly less optimal line executed with confidence and consistency produces better outcomes than an optimal line executed with hesitation or error. This is one of the reasons elite riders appear to choose simpler lines than expected — they have learned that clean execution of a good line beats imperfect execution of the perfect line.

### Principle 5: Session Context Modifies the Correct Line Category
The same section, ridden at the same time of day, may warrant different lines based on session context. In the first run of a training session (fresh, unfamiliar), the safe line is appropriate — building familiarity and confirming surface conditions. In the middle of a training session (confident, surface known), the efficient line becomes available. Late in a training session (fatigued), the safe or efficient line is again more appropriate — degraded technique and reaction time make the fast line higher risk for lower reward. Reading session context is as important as reading terrain when making line choices.

### Principle 6: Degraded Conditions Shift All Line Categories Toward the Safer End
Rain, mud, worn surfaces, and poor visibility all degrade traction and reduce the available speed at which any line can be executed. When conditions degrade, the fast line may become the efficient line, and the efficient line may become the safe line. Riders must recalibrate their line choices as conditions change, not carry over dry-condition line choices into wet conditions. The most common error in degraded conditions is attempting the same line at the same speed — the result is always a crash or a near-miss that the rider attributes to "bad luck" rather than the failure to recalibrate line selection.

## 3. TECHNIQUE BREAKDOWN BY LEVEL

### 3a. Beginner Level
**Goal:** Understand the three line categories, default to the safe line in unfamiliar terrain, and begin identifying traction surfaces within a section before committing to a line.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Reading sections before entry: identifying the highest-grip surface available
- Choosing the safe line deliberately — not just the line that appears most obvious
- Understanding that the entry line determines the exit possibility
- Noticing after each section: was that the right line? What happened at the exit?

**What Good Looks Like:**
Before entering a technical section, the rider slows slightly and visually scans the available surface. They choose a line that avoids the obviously loose, root-covered, or muddy sections, and commit to it. After the section, they reflect: was there more grip on that line? Did the exit feel right? The focus is on awareness and deliberate choice, not optimal execution.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Following the most-worn line reflexively:** Previous riders' tracks are the obvious visual choice but often the worst traction. → Look for the undisturbed surface alongside the track.
- **Making the line decision mid-section:** Changing the line mid-corner or mid-descent based on what appears as the bike is moving. → Decide before entry. Commit fully. Review after.
- **Equating "fast" with "good":** Beginners often choose aggressive lines because they look fast, even when execution cannot support them. → Safe line first. Fast line comes after technique allows it.

### 3b. Intermediate Level
**Goal:** Select efficient lines as the default, understand the exit velocity principle, and adapt line selection to conditions and session context.

**Progression Markers from Beginner:** Rider consistently chooses the highest-grip surface on familiar terrain. Can articulate why a line choice produced the outcome it did. Reflexive line following (copying other riders' tracks) is declining.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Exit velocity evaluation: choosing lines based on the exit speed they produce, not entry appearance
- The off-road apex: identifying the grip point within a corner, not just the geometric midpoint
- Session-context reading: recognising when fatigue or conditions require shifting line category
- Comparative testing: attempting the same section on two different lines and comparing exit quality

**What Good Looks Like:**
The rider approaches a corner and selects a line that looks slower at entry but produces a clean exit with full throttle earlier. They can explain: "I went wider at entry to set up a better exit angle, and the surface was firmer there too." After a degraded conditions run, they say: "I moved to the safer line in the second half because the mud made the fast line too risky." Line selection is deliberate and post-hoc reasoning is clear.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Optimising entry, not exit:** Rider takes the visually appealing entry line without thinking about where it leads at the exit. → Always visualise the exit before committing to the entry.
- **Static line selection — same line regardless of conditions:** Same line dry and wet, fresh and fatigued. → Context should change the line category. Build this as a conscious habit.

### 3c. Advanced Level
**Goal:** Immediately select the contextually optimal line from all three categories, including during competition, and assess line quality across full sections rather than single corners.

**Progression Markers from Intermediate:** Line selection is rapid and consistent. Exit quality is the primary evaluation metric. Conditions and context reliably shift line category choices.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Section-level line strategy: stringing the best sequence of individual corner lines into an optimal section line
- Competition line selection: rapid decision under race pressure with degraded information
- Line development: identifying on first pass where the optimal line is, then refining on subsequent passes
- Line reading from different positions: reading a line from altitude (walking above the section) vs ground level (approaching at speed)
- When to leave the obvious line entirely: recognising that the best line is sometimes significantly outside the worn track

**What Good Looks Like:**
On a first pass through a new section, the rider identifies 2-3 key line decisions and makes reasonable choices. On the second pass, they adjust based on what the exit quality of each choice produced. By the third pass, the section line is optimal. In competition, this condensed learning happens in a single pass. The rider can describe the section's optimal line sequence using consistent reference points.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Locked into one line after first pass:** First-pass line becomes the only line regardless of what the exit quality revealed. → Each pass is an opportunity to refine. Review exit quality actively.
- **Section-level line fragmentation:** Individual corners are optimised but the transitions between them are not considered. → Think about how each corner's exit sets up the next entry — the line is continuous, not a series of isolated choices.

## 4. DIAGNOSTIC CUES (AI COACHING ENGINE REFERENCE)

### Visual Cues — Correct Technique
- Rider chooses a line that is clearly different from the most worn/obvious track — demonstrates active line selection
- Entry point is not the widest or most direct approach — entry is set up for exit
- Mid-corner: rider's line passes through a visually distinct traction surface (rock, firm soil edge, rut wall)
- Exit: early throttle application possible — rider has set up for the exit, not fighting the bike at the exit
- Line is consistent across multiple passes — not varying randomly

### Visual Cues — Error Indicators
- Rider follows the most worn track through every section: no active line selection
- Mid-corner heavy braking or late throttle: exit not set up, recovering from entry commitment
- Line changes mid-section: no commitment, decision happening during the section
- Repeated same line with degraded conditions — no line category recalibration visible
- Exit line pushing wide or running out of road: entry angle set up the wrong exit trajectory

### Audio Cues
- Early throttle sound at exit: rider has set up for the exit correctly — power available early
- Late throttle at exit with heavy engine sound: fighting the bike at exit, line did not set up the exit
- Consistent, clean engine note through the section: line choice providing traction — no fighting

### Sensor Cues
- Early throttle position increase at exit combined with positive acceleration: exit velocity principle in action
- Late throttle with low acceleration: exit not set up, recovering from entry
- Lateral G-force spike mid-corner: line too tight at apex, rider fighting the turn rather than flowing through it
- Consistent lean angle data across multiple passes: line is repeatable and committed

## 5. COACHING CUES & LANGUAGE

### Positive Reinforcement
- "That's the right call — you chose the safe line in those conditions and you cleaned it. The fast line would have been a gamble today. Context-aware line selection."
- "Your exit on that corner was clean and you got on the throttle early. That means the line was correct — you'd set up the exit before you got there."
- "I can see you're not just following the track. You chose your own line and it produced better grip. That's active line reading."

### Corrective Feedback
- "You're optimising the entry and ignoring the exit. You go in fast and then have to brake hard to correct at the exit. The line that feels slower at entry but gives you a clean exit is faster overall. Think exit first, entry second."
- "You're following everyone else's track. Their track is where they rode — it's not necessarily where the grip is. Look for the undisturbed surface. It's often right next to the obvious line."
- "Conditions changed in the last section — it got wetter and muddier — but you kept the same line at the same speed. That's why you slid. You need to recalibrate the line when conditions change. Move to a safer line category in those conditions."
- "You changed your line mid-corner. That's the most dangerous thing you can do — you've committed to entry and then changed the plan. Decide before you enter and execute what you decided."

### Progression Prompts
- "This next section — before you ride it, walk it. Identify the three places where grip is highest. Tell me what line those points create. Then ride that line."
- "On the next pass, your only objective is to evaluate the exit quality. Was the throttle early? Did you need to correct at the exit? That answer tells you if the line was right."
- "Try this section on two completely different lines on consecutive passes. Compare the exit quality. Which line gave you earlier throttle and better exit speed? That's your answer."

## 6. DRILLS & PRACTICE EXERCISES

### Drill 1: Walk-First Line Planning
**Purpose:** Build the pre-entry analysis habit — deciding the line on foot before riding it, separating analysis from execution.
**Setup:** A section of 50-100m with multiple line options: corners, surface variations, obstacles.
**Execution:**
1. Walk the section before riding it.
2. Identify and mark mentally: entry point, the traction surface (off-road apex), and exit angle for each corner.
3. Return to the start and ride the planned line exactly as walked.
4. After the run, re-walk and compare: did the line match the plan? What did the exit quality reveal?
**Success Criteria:** Rider's ridden line matches the walked plan in the major choice points. Exit quality is evaluated explicitly after each pass.
**Duration/Reps:** 3 walk-plan-ride cycles on the same section.
**Progression:** Reduce walk time — compress the analysis into 60 seconds, then 30 seconds. Eventually the walk becomes a brief section scan before riding.

### Drill 2: Line Comparison Testing
**Purpose:** Directly experience the difference between two lines on the same section, building the exit-velocity evaluation habit.
**Setup:** A corner or section with two clearly distinct line options.
**Execution:**
1. Pass 1: ride the most obvious/conventional line. Note entry feel, apex feel, exit quality, and throttle timing.
2. Pass 2: ride an alternative line — wider, tighter, or on different surface. Note the same metrics.
3. Compare. Which produced earlier throttle at exit? Which felt more controlled at the apex?
4. On passes 3 and 4, ride the better line twice to confirm.
**Success Criteria:** Rider can articulate a clear reason why one line was better based on exit quality, not entry appearance.
**Duration/Reps:** 4 passes per section.
**Progression:** Apply the same testing approach to a more complex section with 3+ line variables.

### Drill 3: Condition-Adjusted Line Practice
**Purpose:** Build the habit of recalibrating line selection when conditions change.
**Setup:** The same section ridden in two different conditions — dry then wet, or at start of session then after 30+ minutes of riding.
**Execution:**
1. Ride the section in fresh, good conditions. Log the line — which category (fast, safe, efficient)?
2. Change the condition (wait for rain, or continue to fatigue point).
3. Re-ride the section. Consciously decide: does the line category need to change?
4. Implement the recalibrated line. Evaluate the result.
**Success Criteria:** Rider consciously recognises condition change and makes a deliberate line category shift. The adjusted line produces better or equal outcomes despite worse conditions.
**Duration/Reps:** 2 condition cycles if possible.
**Progression:** Apply the recalibration to race conditions — simulate degraded conditions mid-session and recalibrate without stopping.

### Drill 4: Exit Velocity Focus Runs
**Purpose:** Force exit-velocity thinking by making early throttle application the only performance metric for the drill.
**Setup:** A corner or section with a clear exit zone where throttle timing is measurable.
**Execution:**
1. Mark the throttle-on point on a clean run.
2. On the next run, attempt to get the throttle on 2 metres earlier than the marked point.
3. The only way to achieve this is to have the correct body position and line at the exit — braking or correcting at exit will prevent early throttle.
4. Assess: if throttle couldn't come on earlier, why? Line? Body position? Speed?
**Success Criteria:** Rider can move their throttle-on point earlier by adjusting entry line and body position — not by increasing entry speed.
**Duration/Reps:** 8 passes with progressive throttle-on-point improvement.
**Progression:** String two corners together with exit-velocity focus — the exit of the first corner must set up the entry of the second.

## 8. TERRAIN & CONTEXT VARIATIONS

### Forest/Woodland Trail
In dense woodland, line options are often constrained by tree positions, root patterns, and trail width. The choice is frequently between traction zones within a narrow corridor rather than between clearly distinct paths. The off-road apex principle is most important here — finding the highest-grip surface within the available width. The worn centre of the trail is often the worst traction option; the trail edge, where grass or firmer substrate exists, is often the best.

### Open Rocky Terrain
On rocky terrain, the fastest and safest lines often coincide — rocks provide the best grip. The challenge is identifying which rocks are stable (safe to weight) and which are loose (dangerous). Line choice becomes rock selection: choosing a sequence of stable rock surfaces from entry to exit. Speed carries between rocks, so the line also optimises for continuity — no sudden direction changes that require braking between rocks.

### Sand and Desert Terrain
In open terrain with minimal trail definition, line choice operates at a larger scale — choosing between sections of the course rather than within a corner. The primary variable is sand consistency: firmer sand (often found near vegetation, on the lee side of dunes, or on shaded north-facing slopes) provides better traction than loose sun-exposed sand. Line choice in desert terrain is route-finding as much as corner selection.

### Competition Racing
In race conditions, the line decision time compresses from seconds (in training) to a fraction of a second (at race speed). This is only possible because the decision is partially pre-loaded through pre-riding and mental rehearsal. Competitive riders also use other riders' lines as information — if a competitor takes a different line and appears faster, that is data. Race line choice also includes the concept of the "block pass" — taking a less optimal line to prevent overtaking — but this is a race strategy, not a pure technique choice.

## 9. EXPERT INSIGHTS & SOURCES

### Key Insights
- **Jeremy McGrath** (legendary motocross champion, now off-road coach): "The line doesn't care about your feelings. The fastest line is the fastest line whether it looks it or not. Your job is to find it and trust it."
- **Enduro coaching consensus:** Multiple elite coaches identify line choice as the primary separator between intermediate and advanced riders — "The techniques are similar, but the lines are different, and the lines determine everything."
- **Exit velocity principle — F1 origin:** The exit velocity principle was formalised in circuit racing by engineers who observed that corner entry speed had far less effect on lap time than exit speed. Off-road coaching borrowed and adapted this principle — in enduro, exit quality also determines the subsequent section quality, making it compound in effect.
- **Off-road apex research:** Analysis of professional enduro and motocross rider lines consistently shows that elite riders are using grip surfaces rather than geometric apices — their lines look "wrong" from a circuit perspective but are optimal for off-road traction physics.
- **Condition recalibration:** Coaching data shows that the majority of crash incidents in off-road riding occur when riders use dry-condition lines in wet conditions without recalibration — a systematic error that improved line choice awareness can reduce.
