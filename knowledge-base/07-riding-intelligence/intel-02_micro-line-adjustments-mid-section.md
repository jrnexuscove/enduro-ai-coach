# Micro Line Adjustments Mid-Section

## 1. OVERVIEW

Every committed line has a plan, and every plan encounters reality. Micro line adjustments are the small, precise modifications made to a committed line during execution — responding to unexpected obstacles, surface changes, or recovered traction without abandoning the committed framework. The distinction between a micro-adjustment and a major line change is critical: a micro-adjustment is a refinement of the committed line within its corridor of safety; a major change is a new commitment that resets the physics of the situation and creates a window of instability. Understanding which situation demands which response — and developing the skill to make micro-adjustments quickly, deliberately, and without triggering instability — is what separates riders who adapt gracefully from those who either freeze or overcorrect when reality diverges from the plan.

## 2. CORE PRINCIPLES

### Principle 1: Distinguish Between Planned Line and Micro-Adjustments
The planned line is the macro-decision made before entry: which side of the trail, where to apex, where to begin braking, where to open throttle. Micro-adjustments are small modifications within the planned line's framework — a half-metre shift inward to avoid a rock that appeared hidden under leaf litter, a brief throttle reduction to manage traction on a wet patch, a slight weight shift to manage a small rut crossing. These adjustments do not abandon the planned line — they refine it. The planned line remains the reference point; the micro-adjustment is a temporary deviation with immediate return.

### Principle 2: Micro-Adjustments Are Valid in Specific Circumstances Only
Three categories of situation warrant micro-adjustments: an unexpected obstacle that appeared after the line commitment (a rider fallen on the trail, a hidden rock); a surface that is different from what the approach suggested (dry-looking soil that is wet underneath, a firm-looking patch that is soft); or a traction recovery event (the rear slid and was recovered — brief adjustment to re-establish optimal position on the line). Outside these categories, a micro-adjustment is usually an error — a hesitation or a lack of commitment masquerading as adaptability.

### Principle 3: Micro-Adjustments Must Be Small — The Cost of Large Mid-Section Changes
A large mid-section line change — switching from one rut to another on a descent, dramatically changing direction mid-corner, making a heavy brake input mid-section — carries a disproportionately high risk relative to small adjustments. The reason is that the bike's physics are committed to the original line: weight distribution, lean angle, tyre loading, and suspension state are all aligned to the planned line. A large change imposes an incompatible set of demands simultaneously — braking while leaned, or turning while the suspension is loaded for straight-line running. Micro-adjustments are safe because they remain within the bike's committed physics. Large changes are dangerous because they impose a second commitment before the first is complete.

### Principle 4: The "Committed Line With Tactical Adjustments" Framework
The practical model is: commit fully to the macro-line, then make small tactical refinements as the section unfolds. This is not the same as riding reactively — the difference is that the macro-line commitment is maintained, and the adjustments are conscious, small, and deliberate. A rider using this framework is always "on plan" even when making adjustments, because the adjustments are anticipated as a normal part of executing within variable terrain. The alternative — riding without a plan and making all decisions reactively — produces much higher instability risk because every input is a new commitment with its own window of instability.

### Principle 5: The Specific Situations Where Adjustments Are Dangerous
Switching ruts mid-descent: the transition between ruts on a steep descent requires the wheel to cross a rib of soil between the ruts, which can deflect the front wheel — at descent speed, this deflection can be violent. Changing corner line at or after the apex: any direction change after the apex requires the bike to load from one lean angle and establish another — this is safe at low speed and very dangerous at high speed. Any lateral input when the rear is sliding: weight shift or bar input while the rear is in a slide adds a secondary force to an already dynamic situation and can convert a recoverable slide into a fall.

### Principle 6: Technique for Micro-Adjustments — Small, Controlled, Deliberate
The physical technique for a micro-adjustment: minimal handlebar input (1-3cm of bar deflection for a 0.5m lateral adjustment), coordinated weight shift in the same direction as the desired adjustment, and throttle modulation (reduce slightly before the adjustment, restore during). The adjustment is always proportional — a small obstacle requires a small adjustment. Using large control inputs for small adjustments creates the same instability risk as a large line change.

## 3. TECHNIQUE BREAKDOWN BY LEVEL

### 3a. Beginner Level
**Goal:** Not applicable for pure micro-adjustments — beginners should focus on committing to a planned line. The beginner-level content here is about understanding the commitment principle that makes micro-adjustments possible.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Commitment: once a line is chosen, ride it to completion. Do not change it mid-section.
- Why commitment matters: the bike's physics are built around the planned line. Changing it mid-section always has a cost.
- Post-section review: after each section, assess whether any mid-section change happened. Was it necessary? Was it safe?

**What Good Looks Like:**
The rider chooses a line before entry, enters, and rides the chosen line to completion even if it is not perfectly optimal. There are no mid-section steering changes except small handlebar corrections to maintain the planned line. The result is predictable and consistent.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Changing line mid-corner because a "better" option appears:** A seemingly better line appears mid-corner. The rider attempts to take it. → The original line is probably fine. The cost of mid-corner line change is usually higher than the imperfection of the original line.
- **Braking mid-section in response to a perceived hazard:** A rock or rough patch triggers a mid-section brake input. → Pre-read terrain and decide braking points before entry. Mid-section braking is only safe in genuine emergencies.

### 3b. Intermediate Level
**Goal:** Develop the ability to make small, valid micro-adjustments — responding to genuine unexpected obstacles or surface changes — without triggering instability or abandoning the committed framework.

**Progression Markers from Beginner:** Rider consistently commits to planned lines. Mid-section changes are rare and deliberate. Beginning to distinguish between valid and invalid adjustment triggers.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Identifying valid adjustment triggers: unexpected obstacle (hidden, not visible on approach), surface deviation (wetter or softer than appeared), genuine traction event
- Adjustment sizing: making the adjustment as small as possible while still clearing the obstacle or addressing the surface change
- The return to line: after a micro-adjustment, the rider returns to the planned line — the adjustment does not become a new committed line
- Throttle management during adjustment: the brief reduction and restoration that supports the adjustment without creating an independent stability issue

**What Good Looks Like:**
On a familiar section, a hidden rock appears under leaf litter at the planned apex. The rider makes a small handlebar input — perhaps 2-3cm — and shifts weight slightly to move 0.3-0.5m laterally, clearing the rock. The throttle reduces briefly during the adjustment and restores immediately after. The line resumes as planned beyond the obstacle. The rider does not brake, does not shift significantly from the planned line, and does not communicate surprise to the bike through tense arms.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Making a large adjustment for a small obstacle:** A small rock triggers a half-metre line change. The adjustment is disproportionate and creates instability. → Size the adjustment to the obstacle.
- **Not returning to the planned line after the adjustment:** The micro-adjustment becomes the new direction and the planned exit is missed. → Commit to returning to the planned line immediately after the adjustment.
- **Adjusting for obstacles that don't require it:** Rideable obstacles trigger line changes unnecessarily. → Develop surface reading to distinguish adjustable vs rideable obstacles.

### 3c. Advanced Level
**Goal:** Make micro-adjustments seamlessly and at full speed, within race-level commitments, with adjustments invisible from the outside — the line appears planned even when it has been refined mid-section.

**Progression Markers from Intermediate:** Valid adjustment triggers are reliably distinguished from invalid ones. Adjustment sizing is proportional. Return to planned line is automatic.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Speed of adjustment: the time window for a valid micro-adjustment at race speed is extremely short — the technique must be reflexive, not deliberate
- Chaining adjustments: making multiple small adjustments in sequence through a complex section without losing the macro-line reference
- Adjustment under braking: modifying a line slightly while a braking event is also in progress — requires independent arm inputs and precise weight management
- Knowing when a micro-adjustment is insufficient and a section abort is required: recognising that the obstacle or surface change exceeds what a micro-adjustment can manage

**What Good Looks Like:**
From a chase camera, the rider's line appears clean and planned even though several micro-adjustments were made mid-section. The adjustments are invisible because they are small and smooth — the line shifts by centimetres, not metres, and the body position adjustments are sub-perceptual. The rider can describe afterwards what adjustments were made, confirming they were deliberate, not accidental.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Adjustments becoming macro-changes under race pressure:** The urgency of competition causes small adjustments to become large overcorrections. → In competition, consciously size adjustments smaller, not larger. Urgency degrades adjustment precision.
- **Failing to abort when adjustment is insufficient:** The obstacle is too large for a micro-adjustment but the rider attempts it anyway, producing a crash. → If the required adjustment exceeds a half-metre lateral or requires braking, the section needs an abort plan, not a micro-adjustment.

## 4. DIAGNOSTIC CUES (AI COACHING ENGINE REFERENCE)

### Visual Cues — Correct Technique
- Line appears consistent and planned — no large mid-section direction changes visible
- Small lateral adjustments visible (0.3-0.5m): respond to specific obstacles or surface events, then return to line
- Throttle modulation: brief reduction at adjustment point, immediate restoration — visible as a minor speed change
- Body position remains stable during adjustment: weight shift is small and coordinated, not a panic response
- Return to planned line after adjustment: the adjustment is temporary, not a new direction

### Visual Cues — Error Indicators
- Large mid-section direction change: adjustment exceeds micro scale — or line change masquerading as adjustment
- Multiple adjustments in quick succession: reactive riding without a committed planned line
- Braking mid-corner: adjustment made too late, requiring emergency deceleration
- Body position collapsing during adjustment: panic response rather than controlled technique
- Adjustment in the wrong direction: body and bar input inconsistent — confusion between adjustment and correction

### Audio Cues
- Brief engine note reduction at adjustment point, immediately restoring: correct throttle management during micro-adjustment
- Sustained engine note drop mid-section: throttle management not restored after adjustment — momentum lost unnecessarily
- Tyre squeal during adjustment: adjustment made at a speed or angle exceeding traction limits — adjustment too large

### Sensor Cues
- Lateral G-force: small, brief spike corresponding to the adjustment, returning to baseline — correct
- Lateral G-force: large spike sustained for more than 0.5 seconds — adjustment exceeded micro-scale, line change instead
- Throttle position: brief dip corresponding to adjustment timing, immediate restoration — correct throttle management
- IMU lateral angle: brief change in lean angle corresponding to adjustment, then restoration — controlled micro-adjustment

## 5. COACHING CUES & LANGUAGE

### Positive Reinforcement
- "That was a clean adjustment. You moved around the hidden rock with a small input, kept the throttle working, and came back to line. I could barely see the adjustment from here."
- "Good awareness — you identified that surface as different from the approach and responded with a small adjustment rather than a big reaction. That's controlled adaptability."
- "You made three adjustments through that section and the line still looked like it was planned. That's advanced technique — adjusting without disrupting."

### Corrective Feedback
- "You changed your line mid-corner and it took you completely off your exit. That wasn't a micro-adjustment — that was a new commitment, and it cost you the exit. The original line was manageable. Next time, if the obstacle is rideable, ride it. Reserve adjustments for genuine hazards."
- "When you saw the obstacle you made a half-metre adjustment. That's too big. The obstacle only needed 20cm of clearance. Size your adjustment to the obstacle, not to your anxiety about it."
- "You adjusted and never came back to your planned line. The adjustment became your exit line, which wasn't set up for the exit. After any adjustment, your job is to return to the planned line immediately."
- "You braked mid-corner to make the adjustment. The braking created more instability than the adjustment resolved. Pre-read the terrain so adjustments don't require braking."

### Progression Prompts
- "This section again — same line, same commitment. But this time, if you see an obstacle, your adjustment limit is 30cm. If it needs more than that, you're going to ride over it or abort the section. No large mid-section changes."
- "Let's add an obstacle mid-section — a placed cone. Practice adjusting exactly 30cm around it, then returning to your planned line immediately. Make the adjustment size precise, not approximate."
- "Next time through, tell me afterwards: did you make any adjustments? What triggered each one? Was each one valid? This builds the conscious awareness before the adjustments become reflexive."

## 6. DRILLS & PRACTICE EXERCISES

### Drill 1: Cone Adjustment Precision Drill
**Purpose:** Train the ability to make a specific, sized adjustment precisely — developing proportional response rather than panic response.
**Setup:** A section of trail with a cone placed on the planned line at a mid-section point. Cone position requires exactly 30-40cm of lateral adjustment to clear.
**Execution:**
1. Ride the section at moderate speed.
2. Approach the cone on the planned line.
3. At the appropriate distance, make exactly the adjustment needed — no more — to clear the cone.
4. Immediately after the cone, return to the planned line.
5. Assess: was the adjustment the correct size? Was return to line immediate?
**Success Criteria:** Cone is cleared with minimal (30-40cm) adjustment. Line is resumed immediately after the cone. No mid-section braking.
**Duration/Reps:** 15 passes. Move the cone to different positions in the section.
**Progression:** Add a second cone at a different point in the section — practise chaining two adjustments in sequence.

### Drill 2: Commitment Line Without Adjustment
**Purpose:** Reinforce the committed line principle by practising riding a planned line without any adjustment — even over small rideable obstacles. Builds the distinction between rideable obstacles and genuine adjustment triggers.
**Setup:** A section with several small rideable obstacles (roots, small rocks) on the planned line.
**Execution:**
1. Plan the line and commit to it before entry.
2. Ride the section without making any adjustment — roll over every small obstacle.
3. Assess after: what actually happened to the bike when you rode over the obstacles? Was it manageable?
4. Compare to the instability of a mid-section large adjustment.
**Success Criteria:** Rider rides the committed line without adjustment over small rideable obstacles. Can articulate that the obstacles were manageable and that adjustment was not necessary.
**Duration/Reps:** 10 passes.
**Progression:** Increase the size of the rideable obstacles progressively. Identify the threshold at which adjustment becomes genuinely necessary.

### Drill 3: Valid Trigger Identification
**Purpose:** Develop the ability to rapidly classify an event as a valid or invalid adjustment trigger during riding.
**Setup:** A section where the coach or partner introduces unexpected events — a placed object, a wet patch spray from a water bottle, a vocal cue.
**Execution:**
1. Rider begins the section on planned line.
2. At unpredictable moments, an event is introduced (placed object, verbal "obstacle!" call).
3. Rider must rapidly decide: valid adjustment trigger (hidden obstacle) or not (rideable surface variation).
4. If valid: make a micro-adjustment. If not: hold the planned line.
5. Debrief after each pass: was the classification correct?
**Success Criteria:** Rider makes adjustment on genuine obstacles and holds line on rideable events. False positive rate (adjusting when not needed) reduces with repetition.
**Duration/Reps:** 10 events.
**Progression:** Increase speed. Increase event ambiguity — obstacles that are borderline between rideable and requiring adjustment.

### Drill 4: Chase Camera Review
**Purpose:** Use video review to develop objective awareness of what a micro-adjustment looks like vs a large line change.
**Setup:** A section ridden with a chase camera or GoPro following from behind.
**Execution:**
1. Ride the section with deliberate micro-adjustments at 2-3 planned points.
2. Review the footage: how large do the adjustments appear from behind? Are they visible as adjustments or invisible as part of a clean line?
3. Attempt the section again aiming to make the adjustments even less visible.
4. Review again and compare.
**Success Criteria:** Rider's adjustments are visible in the footage but small — centimetre-scale deviations, not metre-scale. Rider can identify their own adjustment size and deliberateness from footage.
**Duration/Reps:** 4 filmed passes.
**Progression:** Have an experienced rider review the footage and identify any adjustments they saw. Compare with rider's own identification.

## 8. TERRAIN & CONTEXT VARIATIONS

### High-Speed Open Terrain
At high speed, the adjustment window is extremely small — reaction time at 60 km/h is approximately 0.3 seconds, providing only 5m of decision and response distance. Adjustments at high speed must therefore be smaller and faster than at trail speed. The highest-speed sections of a stage are usually the lowest frequency of valid adjustment triggers — because high speed requires a wider line with more safety margin, and most surfaces are more consistent at high speed sections.

### Technical Slow Terrain
In technical slow sections — rock gardens, tight trees, root networks — the speed is low enough that adjustments can be more deliberate. However, the density of obstacles means multiple adjustments may be required in quick succession, and maintaining the macro-line reference while making sequential adjustments becomes the primary challenge. The committed macro-line is most important here as a reference — without it, sequential micro-adjustments become reactive riding.

### Wet and Slippery Conditions
In wet conditions, the valid trigger threshold for a micro-adjustment lowers — smaller surface variations can cause enough traction change to warrant adjustment. However, the physical cost of adjustments also increases — any lateral input in wet conditions has a higher traction demand, which means adjustments must be even smaller and more controlled than in dry conditions. This is the situation where many riders get into trouble: they make a slightly larger adjustment than the traction can support and the adjustment itself causes the slide.

### Competition Conditions
In competition, the pre-planned line is informed by pre-riding, which means the micro-adjustment triggers should be rare — the section is known. The primary valid triggers in competition are: a competitor's bike on the trail, a late-developing rut or surface change, or a dropped marker. The competition adjustment must be fast, small, and invisible — maintaining momentum while clearing the issue.

## 9. EXPERT INSIGHTS & SOURCES

### Key Insights
- **Enduro coaching principle:** "A good line ridden cleanly beats a great line ridden hesitantly. The adjustment should never be larger than required — because every incremental increase in adjustment size increases instability risk non-linearly."
- **MX coaching tradition:** In motocross, the concept of "running your line" is fundamental — the commitment to the planned line is the foundation, and any deviation from it is treated as a technical error unless clearly necessary. Enduro imports this principle into more complex and variable terrain.
- **Reaction time physics:** At 30 km/h (8.3 m/s), a 0.2-second reaction time means 1.7m of travel before any control input begins. At 60 km/h, this is 3.3m. Micro-adjustments at speed must be pre-recognitions — the rider sees the trigger and begins the adjustment as part of the scan process, not as a pure reaction.
- **Traction physics of adjustments:** Lateral inputs during a cornering event add a yaw component to the already-loaded tyres. The traction available for the yaw demand comes from the same traction budget as cornering — meaning adjustments in corners reduce the traction available for cornering load. This is the physical reason why mid-corner adjustments are dangerous.
- **Elite rider observation:** Video analysis of elite enduro riders shows that their lines appear planned even through very technical sections — the micro-adjustments are present but invisible, confirming that the adjustment technique is real but that it is refined to the point of imperceptibility.
