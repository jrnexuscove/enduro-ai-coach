# Adaptive Riding (Mid-Section Changes)

## 1. OVERVIEW

Section planning (INTEL-07) produces a pre-entry plan that covers the expected execution of a section. Adaptive riding covers what happens when the real section diverges from the plan — when an unexpected obstacle appears, the surface changes without warning, traction is lost and recovered, or a competitor blocks the planned line. The distinction between adaptive riding and reactive riding is the quality of the response: an adaptive rider updates their mental model and makes a deliberate modification to the plan, maintaining control authority throughout. A reactive rider responds to the triggering event alone, without updating the mental model, and is surprised again when the next deviation occurs. Adaptive riding is the higher-order skill that preserves plan-quality riding even when the section does not cooperate with the plan.

## 2. CORE PRINCIPLES

### Principle 1: Adaptation Is a Hierarchy — Minor, Moderate, or Abort
Adaptation events fall into a hierarchy based on the scale of the required response. Minor (micro-adjustment): the planned line requires a small adjustment — 0.3-0.5m lateral, brief throttle modulation — to manage an unexpected surface or obstacle. This is within the planned line's execution corridor and produces minimal instability risk. Moderate (modified line): the unexpected event requires a different line to the one planned — a significant change to the corner entry, a different rut choice, a surface avoidance manoeuvre. This has a higher instability cost but is manageable with deliberate, smooth inputs. Abort (section exit): the unexpected event exceeds what can be managed within the section — a competitor mid-section, a completely impassable surface change, or traction loss that cannot be recovered. The abort is a planned, deliberate procedure, not a panic response.

### Principle 2: The Mental Model Update — Not Just the Reaction
The difference between adaptive and reactive riding is that the adaptive rider updates their mental model — their understanding of the section's actual state — not just reacts to the triggering event. When a surface changes unexpectedly mid-section, the reactive rider responds to the change and then continues on the original plan. The adaptive rider responds to the change and then updates: "the surface is wet from here to the exit — my traction budget is reduced, my braking point moves earlier, my throttle is more conservative." This updated model governs all subsequent decisions in the section. Reactive riding produces a single correct response followed by continued plan mismatch. Adaptive riding produces a correct response and a correctly updated plan for the remainder of the section.

### Principle 3: When Mid-Section Changes Are Dangerous — Specific Situations
Some mid-section changes are too costly to attempt and require abort instead of adaptation. Switching ruts mid-descent: the rut wall between ruts deflects the front wheel violently at descent speed. Changing corner line at or after the apex: the bike's lean angle and tyre loading cannot accommodate a new direction simultaneously. Any lateral input while the rear is in active slide: adding a second force to a sliding situation creates cascade failure risk. Braking from high speed mid-section on loose terrain: the surface traction cannot support sudden deceleration without wheel lock. These situations require abort protocols, not adaptation — and the abort must be executed before the dangerous input is made.

### Principle 4: Aborting a Section — How to Safely Exit a Committed Attempt
A section abort is a deliberate procedure: recognise the abort trigger early (before the dangerous point is reached), release throttle smoothly, apply brakes progressively to reduce speed, find the safest available exit from the section (a wider section, a flat area, a natural break in the terrain), and control the bike to that point. The abort is not a panic stop — it is a controlled withdrawal to a safe position. Aborting late (when already in the dangerous zone) may require accepting the section through rather than attempting to stop — the abort trigger must be recognised early enough for the controlled exit to be possible.

### Principle 5: Why Beginners Struggle With Adaptation — Cognitive Load
Beginner and intermediate riders struggle with adaptation for a specific reason: their cognitive load during the section is already at capacity managing the planned execution. There is no spare capacity to simultaneously detect a plan deviation, assess its scale, formulate an adaptation response, and maintain vehicle control. Advanced riders have automated the planned execution through training, freeing cognitive resources that can be directed toward monitoring and adaptation. This is why adaptation skill is implicitly an intermediate-to-advanced topic — until the base techniques are significantly automated, the cognitive bandwidth for adaptation does not exist.

### Principle 6: Training for Adaptation — Deliberately Altered Familiar Sections
Adaptation can be trained by deliberately introducing unexpected events into familiar sections. Move a cone mid-section between passes. Change the line requirement by placing an obstacle on the planned apex. Add a new surface (wet patch from a water bottle, loose gravel placed on a corner) to a known dry section. These deliberate alterations practise the mental model update and the adaptation response in a controlled context where the consequence of failure is low — building the skill before it is needed under pressure.

## 3. TECHNIQUE BREAKDOWN BY LEVEL

### 3a. Beginner Level
**Goal:** Not applicable for true adaptive riding — beginners should focus on building and executing plans, and on understanding what constitutes a valid adaptation trigger vs what should be ignored. The beginner content here is about abort recognition and procedure.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Understanding that plan deviations are normal and expected — not evidence of poor planning
- Identifying abort triggers: situations that require section exit rather than adaptation
- Basic abort procedure: throttle off, brakes on progressively, find the exit
- Post-section analysis: was the deviation manageable? What caused it? Should the plan be updated?

**What Good Looks Like:**
When something unexpected happens mid-section, the beginner rider responds with the simplest available option: slow down, find a stable position, and exit if needed. They do not attempt complex adaptations that exceed their current cognitive capacity. After the section, they can identify what surprised them and begin to update the plan for the next attempt.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Attempting complex adaptation with insufficient cognitive capacity:** Beginner tries to change line mid-corner and crashes. → If adaptation feels overwhelming, abort. Adaptation is a skill that develops with experience.
- **Not recognising the abort trigger early enough:** Rider commits to an adaptation they can't complete. → The abort decision must be made early — as soon as the trigger is recognised.

### 3b. Intermediate Level
**Goal:** Execute deliberate minor and moderate adaptations to the planned line in response to valid triggers, update the mental model for the remainder of the section, and execute abort procedures cleanly.

**Progression Markers from Beginner:** Planned execution is partially automatic — some cognitive capacity is available for monitoring. Abort triggers are recognisable. Basic micro-adjustments are available.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Trigger classification: distinguishing minor (micro-adjustment), moderate (line change), and abort triggers
- Mental model update: after any adaptation, updating the traction and surface model for the section remainder
- Abort procedure refinement: clean, controlled, early — not panic stops
- Training adaptation: deliberately including unexpected elements in familiar sections during training

**What Good Looks Like:**
Mid-section, a wet patch appears on the planned apex. The rider makes a small lateral adjustment to avoid the worst of the wet surface, reduces throttle slightly through the wet zone, then restores the original line plan as the surface returns to dry. The mental model update is visible in the reduced throttle continuing briefly past the wet patch — the rider is using the updated model, not just reacting to the patch.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Adaptation without mental model update:** Rider adjusts for the wet patch but then continues at the same throttle level as the dry plan — does not adjust for the updated traction picture. → After any adaptation, ask: what does this change about the rest of the section?
- **Moderate adaptation attempted at micro-adjustment scale:** A significant line change is attempted with the inputs of a micro-adjustment — insufficient response. → Size the adaptation to the scale of the trigger.

### 3c. Advanced Level
**Goal:** Seamless adaptive execution — adaptations are invisible from the outside, mental model updates are continuous, and abort procedures are clean and fast.

**Progression Markers from Intermediate:** Trigger classification is fast and accurate. Mental model updates are automatic. Abort procedures are executed before the dangerous point. Adaptation training is incorporated into normal session structure.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Chained adaptations: multiple adaptations in quick succession through a complex section, without losing the macro-line reference
- High-speed adaptation: making valid adaptations at race speeds where the available time is extremely short
- Adaptation under fatigue: maintaining adaptation quality later in a stage when cognitive resources are depleted
- Post-section mental model carry-forward: using the updated model from one section to inform planning for the next
- Novel obstacle adaptation: quickly categorising an unfamiliar obstacle type and adapting the plan appropriately

**What Good Looks Like:**
On a race section, a competitor's bike is partially blocking the planned apex line. In 0.5 seconds, the rider classifies this as a moderate adaptation (line change required), selects an alternative apex, adjusts entry angle, and executes the modified line without hesitation. The exit quality is slightly lower than the optimal plan but significantly better than either aborting or attempting the planned line with the competitor present. The mental model is updated: the section will be slower than planned; the time buffer needs to be managed elsewhere.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Adaptation quality degrading under fatigue:** Late in a race, adaptations become reactions — smaller triggers are missed, responses are larger and less controlled. → Build adaptation quality under simulated fatigue conditions in training.
- **Chained adaptations losing macro-line reference:** After three micro-adaptations in quick succession, the rider has drifted significantly from the planned line without noticing. → Re-check macro-line reference after each adaptation chain.

## 4. DIAGNOSTIC CUES (AI COACHING ENGINE REFERENCE)

### Visual Cues — Correct Technique
- Adaptation is small and proportional: the line adjustment matches the scale of the trigger
- Mental model update visible: throttle modulation continues past the trigger point, not just at it
- Return to planned macro-line after adaptation: the deviation is temporary, with clear return
- Abort is early and controlled: exit begins before the section's most dangerous point
- Multiple adaptations in sequence without loss of overall direction: chaining is smooth

### Visual Cues — Error Indicators
- Large, sudden direction change mid-section: adaptation scaled incorrectly (too large) for the trigger
- No throttle change during adaptation: mental model not updated — reactive, not adaptive
- Adaptation in the wrong direction: response mismatched to trigger
- Late abort: exit attempt begins inside the dangerous zone — too late for a clean controlled exit
- Repeated similar adaptations without plan update: the plan is not being updated to prevent the repeated trigger

### Audio Cues
- Brief throttle reduction at adaptation point with immediate restoration: minor adaptation with correct throttle management
- Extended throttle reduction past the adaptation trigger: mental model update in progress — continuing to ride the updated model
- Sudden throttle cut with abrupt braking sound: late abort or panic response — not controlled adaptation

### Sensor Cues
- Lateral G-force: brief small spike at adaptation point, returning to baseline — correct minor adaptation
- Lateral G-force: large, sustained spike — moderate adaptation or incorrect input scale
- Throttle position: brief dip then sustained slight reduction past trigger point: mental model update present
- Throttle position: brief dip then immediate full restoration: reaction only, no mental model update

## 5. COACHING CUES & LANGUAGE

### Positive Reinforcement
- "That was clean adaptation. You saw the wet patch, adjusted the line, updated your throttle for it, and the throttle stayed conservative for the rest of the wet zone. Mental model update confirmed."
- "You recognised the abort trigger early and got out cleanly. That's what early abort looks like. You found the safe position before the dangerous zone, not during it."
- "Three adaptations through that section and you never lost the overall line. That's chained adaptation — each small adjustment kept the macro-line reference intact."

### Corrective Feedback
- "You made the adjustment for the wet patch but then went straight back to race throttle. The surface was still wet for another 5 metres. Your mental model needs to update beyond just the trigger point — what does this change about the next 10 metres?"
- "The adjustment you made was twice the size the trigger needed. A small patch of wet surface needed 20cm of adjustment, not a half-metre line change. Large adjustments at speed are as dangerous as the hazard you're avoiding."
- "You tried to adapt when you should have aborted. The competitor was on your planned line, you had no alternative that was safe, and you attempted a complex line change at speed. The abort trigger was there — the exit was 5 metres earlier. That's when the decision needed to be made."
- "You're still riding the pre-section plan even though the surface changed significantly at the first corner. The plan is stale from that point — everything after the change needs to be re-assessed. Update the model, don't run an outdated plan."

### Progression Prompts
- "Next pass: when you make any adaptation, immediately ask yourself 'what does this change about the rest of the section?' That question is your mental model update prompt."
- "I'm going to place an obstacle on your planned apex before the next pass — you won't know exactly where. Practise classifying it when you see it: micro-adjustment, line change, or abort? Then respond accordingly."
- "Find a section you've ridden 10 times and plan a deliberate adaptation: you must use a different line at the third corner. Execute the adaptation smoothly and return to the standard plan from corner four. This trains deliberate adaptation rather than reactive."

## 6. DRILLS & PRACTICE EXERCISES

### Drill 1: Deliberate Obstacle Introduction
**Purpose:** Train adaptive response in a familiar section by introducing unexpected obstacles, simulating mid-section surprises in a controlled context.
**Setup:** A familiar section the rider has ridden cleanly multiple times. Observer or coach places a cone/stick at a different position on each pass — unknown to the rider until they are in the section.
**Execution:**
1. Rider enters the section on the planned line.
2. The cone appears at an unknown position on the line.
3. Rider classifies the trigger (minor, moderate, abort) and responds.
4. After each pass, debrief: was the classification correct? Was the adaptation sized correctly?
**Success Criteria:** Rider correctly classifies each trigger and responds proportionally. Adaptations are smooth and do not cause instability.
**Duration/Reps:** 10 passes with varied cone positions.
**Progression:** Add two simultaneous obstacles. Increase section speed.

### Drill 2: Mental Model Update Drill
**Purpose:** Force the mental model update habit by requiring explicit verbal confirmation of the updated model after each trigger.
**Setup:** A section with a deliberately wet patch (water bottle poured on the surface), known to the rider.
**Execution:**
1. Ride the section approaching the wet patch.
2. When the wet patch is encountered, make the adaptation.
3. Immediately after the wet patch, say out loud: "Updated model — wet for 3 more metres, throttle conservative, exit on the right."
4. Execute the updated model through the section exit.
5. Review: did the verbal model match the adapted execution?
**Success Criteria:** Verbal model update happens at the trigger point. Execution matches the updated model, not the original plan.
**Duration/Reps:** 8 passes.
**Progression:** Remove the verbal requirement — the update becomes internal. Confirm it happened by post-section debrief.

### Drill 3: Abort Procedure Practice
**Purpose:** Build a clean, controlled abort procedure through deliberate repetition in low-consequence conditions.
**Setup:** A technical section with a defined abort exit point mid-section.
**Execution:**
1. Enter the section on the planned line.
2. At the designated abort trigger point (placed marker), execute the abort procedure: throttle off, progressive braking, steer to the designated exit point.
3. Control the bike to a stop at the exit point.
4. Note: was the abort early enough for a controlled exit? Was the exit smooth?
**Success Criteria:** Abort is executed at the designated trigger point without panic. Exit is controlled. Bike comes to rest at the designated exit point.
**Duration/Reps:** 10 abort procedures from different trigger points.
**Progression:** Move the trigger point progressively later in the section — testing how late a clean abort can be initiated and still produce a controlled exit.

### Drill 4: Chained Adaptation Sequence
**Purpose:** Train the ability to make multiple adaptations in sequence without losing the macro-line reference.
**Setup:** A familiar section with 4 placed obstacles at different points, requiring adaptations of varying scale.
**Execution:**
1. Enter the section. The obstacles require: a micro-adjustment at point 1, a line change at point 2, a brief abort decision at point 3 (the obstacle is in the safest abort zone — riders must recognise it as non-abortable and ride through), and a micro-adjustment at point 4.
2. Execute each adaptation in sequence.
3. After the section, review: was each adaptation classified correctly? Did the macro-line return after each adaptation?
**Success Criteria:** All four adaptations are executed with correct classification. Macro-line reference is maintained throughout. No drift from planned exit line.
**Duration/Reps:** 6 passes on the chained sequence.
**Progression:** Increase the speed of the section. Add a fifth obstacle that requires updating the mental model for the section remainder.

## 8. TERRAIN & CONTEXT VARIATIONS

### Woodland Technical Terrain
In woodland, adaptations are frequent because the terrain is dense and variable. The typical adaptive challenge is a fallen branch across the planned line, a new rut from a preceding rider, or a wet patch under leaf litter. The section plan must be treated as a framework rather than a script in woodland — adaptation is the norm, not the exception. The mental model is continuously updated as the woodland section unfolds.

### Open Desert/Sand
In open terrain, adaptations are less frequent but more consequential — because the available speed is higher, the time window for a mid-section change is shorter and the instability cost is higher. The primary adaptive trigger in desert terrain is a hidden hole, a soft sand patch, or a navigational deviation. Abort procedures in open terrain require more distance because speed is higher.

### Competition (Competitor on Line)
A competitor occupying the planned line is the most common competition adaptation trigger. The required response depends on the competitive scenario: if the competitor is moving and faster, follow and wait for a pass opportunity. If the competitor is stopped, execute a line change to avoid them. If the competitor's position blocks all viable lines, abort to a safe stop and wait. The adaptation must happen before the competitor contact zone — which requires recognising the trigger early from the approach.

### Night Riding
At night, adaptation triggers may not be visible until closer range than in daylight — the reading window is compressed. This means the adaptation must be executed faster (less time between trigger recognition and response) and with less information (the surface beyond the trigger may not be visible). Night riding requires a higher abort threshold than daytime — the cost of late-recognition adaptation is higher.

## 9. EXPERT INSIGHTS & SOURCES

### Key Insights
- **Cognitive load theory (Sweller):** Applied to adaptive riding: the adaptation capacity exists only when planned execution has been automated through training. The cognitive load required for adaptation is non-zero — it competes with the load of basic vehicle control. This explains why beginners cannot adapt and why training to automaticity is the prerequisite for adaptive capability.
- **Naturalistic decision making (Klein):** Studies of expert decision making in complex dynamic environments (firefighting, military operations) show that experts rarely use analytical decision processes — instead, they use pattern recognition followed by rapid mental simulation. Applied to adaptive riding: the trigger is pattern-matched to a known adaptation type, and the adaptation response is retrieved, not calculated. This is consistent with the observation that expert riders appear to "know" what to do instantly.
- **Enduro GP competitor analysis:** Video analysis of professional enduro riders in competition shows an average of 3-5 significant adaptations per minute in dense woodland sections — far more than casual observers perceive. The adaptations are invisible because they are small, smooth, and integrated into the overall riding pattern.
- **Abort decision timing:** Accident analysis in motorsport consistently shows that late aborts (initiated after the dangerous zone entry) produce worse outcomes than either completing the section or early aborting. The abort decision timing is the single most important element of abort procedure.
- **Mental model updating:** Cognitive science research on dynamic decision making shows that the quality of adaptation depends more on the quality of the updated mental model than on the quality of the initial response to the trigger. Riders who update their model produce better subsequent decisions; riders who only react to triggers continue to be surprised.
