# Risk vs Reward Decision Making

## 1. OVERVIEW

Every technical riding decision is implicitly a risk-reward calculation. The question is whether that calculation is made deliberately, with accurate inputs, or whether it is made by default — driven by ego, peer pressure, habit, or adrenaline rather than genuine assessment. In off-road riding, the consequence space is wide: rewards range from time gained, confidence built, and skills developed, to satisfaction and competitive position; risks range from minor time loss to permanent injury. The objective of risk-reward decision making as a trained skill is to bring accurate, calm assessment to decisions that are often made under pressure, in a fraction of a second, with partial information, and in an environment where social and emotional factors are distorting the inputs. Riders who develop this skill make better decisions more consistently — they attempt more of what they should attempt and walk more of what they should walk. Both outcomes improve performance.

## 2. CORE PRINCIPLES

### Principle 1: Risk Assessment Has Four Factors — Consequence, Probability, Energy Cost, and Failure Cost
Consequence severity: how bad is the worst-case outcome if the attempt fails? A fall on a soft section at low speed is low consequence. A fall at speed on rock is high consequence. Probability of success: based on current technique, conditions, bike state, and fatigue, how likely is success? Energy cost of attempt: how much physical and cognitive effort will this attempt cost regardless of outcome? Failure cost: beyond physical consequence, what does a failure cost — time lost, bike damage, morale impact, subsequent riding impairment? All four factors must feed into the assessment. Riders who assess only consequence (ignoring probability) or only probability (ignoring consequence) make systematically poor decisions.

### Principle 2: Reward Assessment Has Four Factors — Time, Position, Confidence, and Development
Time gained: in competition, attempting a difficult section rather than a safe alternative — how much time does the attempt save if successful? Position gained: does cleaning this section change competitive position meaningfully? Confidence built: successful completion of a challenging section has compound value — it expands the commitment range for similar sections. Skill developed: attempting the section develops the technical skill that makes future attempts easier. In training, skill development is the primary reward; in competition, time and position dominate.

### Principle 3: The Ego Filter Distorts Risk Assessment Systematically
Ego introduces a specific distortion: it inflates the probability of success estimate (because failure would be embarrassing) and deflates the consequence estimate (because acknowledging high consequence means accepting the legitimacy of not attempting). Peer pressure amplifies this distortion further — watching a peer attempt an obstacle makes the probability estimate of own success rise unrealistically (if they can, I can) regardless of the accuracy of that assessment. Experienced riders learn to apply an ego correction factor: when the assessment is being made in a group context or under social pressure, reduce the probability estimate by 20-30% to counteract the distortion.

### Principle 4: Consequence-Based Decision Weighting — High-Consequence Sections Require Higher Certainty
The decision threshold should scale with consequence severity. For a low-consequence section (soft landing, slow speed, easily recoverable), 60-70% estimated probability of success is often sufficient to attempt. For a high-consequence section (rocky face, large drop, exposed traverse), 90%+ estimated probability of success is required. This non-linear scaling reflects the asymmetry of consequence: losing a few seconds from a failed low-consequence attempt is manageable; a career-ending injury from a failed high-consequence attempt is not. The threshold scales with the worst-case outcome, not the average outcome.

### Principle 5: Dynamic Risk — Same Obstacle Varies by Conditions, Fatigue, and Bike Condition
The same obstacle section that was a clear "go" in the morning may be a clear "no" in the afternoon — if conditions have degraded (wet, rutted), if the rider is fatigued (reduced technique quality), or if the bike has a mechanical issue (reduced traction, compromised braking). Risk assessment is not a one-time evaluation of an obstacle's inherent difficulty — it is a continuous evaluation of the obstacle in the specific context of the current moment. Riders who carry over earlier-session assessments to later-session conditions make systematic errors.

### Principle 6: Walking Is Correct Risk Management — Not Failure
The cultural context of enduro and extreme off-road riding treats walking with a negative connotation. This is counterproductive and technically incorrect. Walking a section that exceeds current capability or current conditions is a correct risk management decision — it is not a failure of capability, courage, or technique. The failure is hesitating into an attempt that should have been walked. Elite riders walk sections routinely — including sections they could clean on a different day in different conditions. The decision to walk is as skilled as the decision to attempt.

## 3. TECHNIQUE BREAKDOWN BY LEVEL

### 3a. Beginner Level
**Goal:** Begin applying explicit risk assessment before technical obstacles, using the consequence and probability factors as the primary inputs.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Consequence identification: before every significant obstacle, identify the worst-case outcome
- Probability estimation: honest self-assessment — "given my current technique, how likely am I to clean this?"
- The 70% rule: if estimated probability of success is below 70%, the section requires either skill development (practise easier versions) or walking
- Walking without regret: practise the clean walk as a positive decision

**What Good Looks Like:**
Before a significant technical obstacle, the rider takes 5-10 seconds to explicitly assess: "worst case is a low-speed fall on soft ground — manageable. I've ridden similar things before and cleaned 8 of 10. I'll attempt it." Or: "worst case is a high-speed fall on rock — serious injury risk. I've never ridden anything this steep. I'll walk." The decision is made explicitly, not by default.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Consequence assessment defaults to "I'll probably be fine":** The worst-case is not genuinely assessed. → Force the explicit worst-case scenario. Describe it specifically, not generically.
- **Probability assessment is hope, not estimation:** "I might be able to do it" treated as a high probability estimate. → Probability assessment must be based on recent demonstrated technique on similar obstacles.
- **Walking treated as failure:** Rider walks but feels embarrassed or defeated. → Reframe walking as a correct risk management decision. The decision to walk correctly is a skill.

### 3b. Intermediate Level
**Goal:** Apply all four risk and four reward factors systematically, apply the ego correction, and calibrate assessment to conditions dynamically.

**Progression Markers from Beginner:** Consequence and probability assessment are explicit habits. Walking decisions are made without guilt. Assessment accuracy is improving.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Energy cost and failure cost: adding these factors to the assessment beyond consequence and probability
- Reward assessment: calculating time and position in competition, confidence and development in training
- Ego correction: recognising when group context or social pressure is inflating probability estimates
- Dynamic risk calibration: reassessing at session midpoint and end whether earlier assessments still apply

**What Good Looks Like:**
In competition, the rider approaches a difficult rocky section. The assessment happens in 3-5 seconds: "Consequence: rocky, high-speed — serious if it goes wrong. Probability: 70%, I've cleaned similar. Time gain: 20 seconds vs the walk. Failure cost: 60 seconds lost and possible bike damage. Net: slight negative on time, high on consequence. I'll walk the exposed section and attempt the rideable lower part." The decision is explicit, complete, and dynamic.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Assessment incomplete under race pressure:** Time pressure forces skipping factors. → Build the assessment so automatic that it takes 2-3 seconds, not 20.
- **Ego correction not applied:** Group context assessed as neutral when it is distorting. → In any group context, apply the correction as a default.
- **Dynamic risk not recalibrated:** Session-start assessment carried through the whole session. → Reassess at session midpoint and when conditions change.

### 3c. Advanced Level
**Goal:** Risk-reward assessment is fast, accurate, and automatic. Elite riders make these decisions in under 2 seconds with high accuracy. Competition assessment includes positional strategy, competitor behaviour, and terrain state.

**Progression Markers from Intermediate:** Full four-factor assessment is habitual. Ego correction is automatic. Dynamic calibration is accurate. Walking decisions are made without social friction.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Sub-2-second assessment: compressing the four-factor evaluation to near-instant with automatic weighting
- Competition strategy integration: risk-reward connects to stage position, energy budget, and stage remaining distance
- Assessing novel obstacles: accurate probability estimation on obstacles with no prior experience — generalising from similar obstacle types
- Post-race assessment review: comparing assessment decisions to outcomes — are the estimates accurate? Where are the systematic biases?
- Risk management across a stage: not just individual obstacle decisions but managing cumulative risk across the whole stage

**What Good Looks Like:**
The rider processes risk-reward automatically — the decision "appears" rather than being consciously constructed. After a race, the rider can reflect on 3-4 key decision points and explain the reasoning in detail — but during the race, the decisions were instinctive. Post-race review identifies that probability estimates were slightly over-optimistic late in the stage when fatigue was affecting technique — a systematic bias to correct in future events.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Assessment biases not identified over time:** Systematic over- or under-estimation of specific risk factors is not caught and corrected. → Regular post-session review of assessment accuracy is necessary for continued calibration.
- **Stage-level risk not managed:** Individual sections are assessed correctly but aggregate risk across the stage is not managed — too many high-risk attempts in the early stage leave no energy margin for the harder late-stage sections. → Think in stage-level risk budgets, not just section-level decisions.

## 4. DIAGNOSTIC CUES (AI COACHING ENGINE REFERENCE)

### Visual Cues — Correct Technique
- Brief but deliberate pause before significant obstacles: assessment time being taken
- Clean committed entry or clean walk: binary decision visible
- No hesitant entry with insufficient speed or incorrect body position: assessment was made and acted on
- Walk line is direct and efficient — no lingering or second-guessing
- After a clean walk, rider's posture and demeanour are neutral: no shame or frustration response

### Visual Cues — Error Indicators
- Hesitant entry: assessment was not made (or was made poorly)
- Attempting then stopping mid-section: assessment failure — commitment not completed
- Multiple false starts at the same obstacle: assessment not made, hesitation persisting
- Attempting a section that clearly exceeds current technique under group observation: ego filter active, assessment distorted
- Excessive post-walk frustration or self-criticism: walking is being treated as failure

### Audio Cues
- Verbal decision making (if audible): "I'm going" or "I'm walking" before entry — explicit decision
- No verbal: assess from entry behaviour — committed or hesitant

### Sensor Cues
- Entry speed consistent with planned approach: decision was made before entry
- Entry speed deceleration at section: decision not complete, hesitation at entry
- Multiple speed variations at section approach: repeated approach without entry — assessment loop not completing

## 5. COACHING CUES & LANGUAGE

### Positive Reinforcement
- "That was a correct risk call. The consequence was high, your probability wasn't there yet, and you walked it cleanly. That's accurate assessment."
- "In competition you kept your risk budget in check in the first half and had energy for the hard sections in the second half. That's stage-level risk management."
- "You walked that section and didn't look back. That's the correct response to a clean no decision. No shame, no second-guessing, move on."

### Corrective Feedback
- "You just attempted that section in front of your training group when you wouldn't have attempted it alone. That's the ego filter in action. Group context added 20% to your probability estimate that wasn't real. When you're assessing in front of others, apply that correction."
- "Your consequence assessment was 'I'll probably be fine.' That's not an assessment — that's wishful thinking. Tell me specifically: what is the worst-case if this goes wrong? Name it. Then we'll talk about whether the probability justifies the attempt."
- "You're assessing this section the same way you assessed it this morning. The surface is wetter, you're 90 minutes into the session. Your probability estimate needs to go down. Dynamic risk — the same obstacle changes throughout the session."
- "You attempted that section three times, barely started each time, and then walked it. The walking was the right call — but it should have been the first call, not the fourth. Make the assessment once, make the decision, execute it."

### Progression Prompts
- "Before the next obstacle, I want you to answer four questions out loud: worst case? Probability? Time if I clean it vs time if I don't? Energy cost? Then make the decision. Four questions, 10 seconds, then execute."
- "In your next training session: keep a note of three decisions — two attempts and one walk. After the session, tell me whether your probability estimates were accurate. Did the outcomes match the assessments?"
- "This climb: what's your probability estimate? Now reduce it by 20% for the group context. Is that number still above your threshold for this consequence level? Make the call from the adjusted number."

## 6. DRILLS & PRACTICE EXERCISES

### Drill 1: Four-Factor Verbal Assessment
**Purpose:** Build the habit of explicit four-factor assessment by making it verbal and conscious before making it internal and automatic.
**Setup:** A session with 6-8 significant technical obstacles.
**Execution:**
1. Before each obstacle, stop and answer out loud: consequence, probability, energy cost, failure cost.
2. Make the go/no-go decision out loud.
3. Execute the decision.
4. After the session, review: were the probability estimates accurate? What did outcomes reveal about assessment quality?
**Success Criteria:** All four factors are stated before every obstacle. Decision is explicit. Post-session accuracy review is meaningful.
**Duration/Reps:** One full session.
**Progression:** Compress the verbal assessment from 30 seconds to 10 seconds to 3 seconds. Eventually the assessment becomes internal and fast.

### Drill 2: Ego Correction Practice
**Purpose:** Train the ego correction factor in group context, preventing social pressure from inflating probability estimates.
**Setup:** Group session with 3-5 riders of varying skill levels.
**Execution:**
1. When a peer attempts an obstacle, note the group reaction (did everyone attempt it after seeing one person do it?).
2. Before own assessment, explicitly apply the group-context correction: reduce probability estimate by 20%.
3. Make the decision from the adjusted estimate.
4. After the session, compare decisions made in group context vs solo context on the same obstacle type.
**Success Criteria:** Rider makes decisions in group context that match solo-context decisions on equivalent risk assessments. Group observation does not inflate probability estimates.
**Duration/Reps:** Applied throughout a group session.
**Progression:** Repeat in competitive context where social pressure is highest (training race, timed section).

### Drill 3: Dynamic Risk Recalibration Points
**Purpose:** Train the habit of explicitly recalibrating risk assessments at defined points in the session, not just at the start.
**Setup:** A 2-3 hour training session with multiple technical obstacles.
**Execution:**
1. At the session start, assess overall readiness state: fresh, warmed up, conditions good.
2. At the 60-minute mark: reassess. How is fatigue? Have conditions changed? Adjust probability estimates downward as appropriate.
3. At the 120-minute mark: repeat. This is when most high-consequence decisions should be made more conservatively.
4. Log the adjustment: which obstacles moved from "yes" to "no" based on the recalibration?
**Success Criteria:** Rider makes explicit, logged adjustments at the recalibration points. Decisions in the later session are more conservative than the early session for equivalent obstacles.
**Duration/Reps:** One full training session.
**Progression:** Apply to a competition day: pre-race, mid-race, and late-race recalibration points.

### Drill 4: Outcome-Assessment Accuracy Review
**Purpose:** Close the feedback loop between assessment and outcome, identifying systematic biases in the rider's assessment process.
**Setup:** A training log for 4-6 sessions, recording assessments and outcomes.
**Execution:**
1. Before each significant attempt, record: probability estimate (0-100%), consequence level (low/medium/high).
2. After each attempt, record: outcome (clean, fall, stall, controlled failure).
3. After 4-6 sessions, review the data: are probability estimates accurate? Are high-probability estimates correlating with clean outcomes? Where are the mismatches?
4. Identify systematic biases: over-confident on certain obstacle types? Under-confident on others?
**Success Criteria:** Rider can identify 1-2 systematic biases in their assessment process. Can articulate a correction for each bias.
**Duration/Reps:** 4-6 sessions of data collection.
**Progression:** Apply the same review to a competition event — how did competition-context assessments compare to training-context assessments?

## 8. TERRAIN & CONTEXT VARIATIONS

### Competition Racing
In competition, the reward factors of time and position gain are explicit and calculable (relative to known alternatives). This makes competition risk-reward more structured than training — the reward side is more precisely known. However, competition introduces the ego filter and peer pressure at higher intensity, which distorts the risk side. The net effect is that competition often produces worse risk assessment quality than training, not better — despite the rewards being more clearly defined.

### Training Context
In training, the reward is primarily skill development and confidence building. The consequence is more conservatively assessed — there is no time pressure or competitive position to trade against consequence. Training should be the context where the most accurate risk assessment occurs, providing the calibrated probability estimates that inform competition decisions.

### Group Riding (Social Peer Pressure)
Group riding is the highest-risk context for assessment distortion. The ego filter and social observation pressure are both maximally active. Riders should approach group riding with the explicit awareness that their probability estimates are likely inflated by 15-30% compared to solo riding. The correction must be actively applied.

### Solo Riding
Solo riding provides the most accurate assessment context — no social pressure, no peer observation. However, it introduces a different risk consideration: consequence severity increases when riding alone because there is no assistance available if something goes wrong. This shifts the consequence factor upward and the required probability threshold higher than in group riding. Solo riders should use slightly higher probability thresholds before attempting high-consequence sections.

## 9. EXPERT INSIGHTS & SOURCES

### Key Insights
- **Graham Jarvis on walking:** "I've walked things at Erzberg that I could ride on a different day. The question is never can I ride this — the question is should I ride this, right now, in these conditions. Those are different questions."
- **Jonny Walker on competition strategy:** "In hard enduro racing, the riders who win aren't the ones who never walk. They're the ones who walk the right things and ride the right things. The decision is the skill."
- **Ego filter research:** Studies in extreme sport psychology consistently show that group observation elevates risk-taking behaviour beyond what individual assessment would produce. The effect is significant — up to 40% higher risk tolerance in observed vs unobserved conditions for similar skill levels.
- **Consequence weighting:** Research in decision theory (Kahneman & Tversky prospect theory) shows that humans are loss-averse — they weight potential losses more heavily than equivalent gains. In risk assessment, this means that consequence severity (the loss side) should genuinely outweigh time gain (the reward side) when they are equivalent in magnitude. The prospect theory framing validates the consequence-weighted decision threshold.
- **Stage-level risk budgeting:** Analysis of DNF (did not finish) patterns in extreme enduro races consistently shows that DNFs cluster in the second half of events — riders used their "risk budget" in the first half and had insufficient reserves for the harder later sections. Stage-level risk management is therefore a competitive skill, not just a safety principle.
