# Section Planning Before Entry

## 1. OVERVIEW

Section planning is the deliberate mental process of forming a complete picture of how a section will be ridden before the front wheel enters it. It reduces cognitive load during the section by converting real-time decisions into pre-made plans — the rider executes a prepared sequence rather than constructing technique responses on the fly. This distinction matters because cognitive load during a technical section directly degrades technique quality: a rider who is deciding where to go, what gear to use, when to brake, and what line to take simultaneously while controlling a motorcycle through rough terrain will make errors on all of them. A rider who has already made those decisions and is simply executing them makes far fewer errors. Section planning is the mechanism by which experienced riders appear to ride "automatically" — they are executing a mental plan, not processing raw terrain.

## 2. CORE PRINCIPLES

### Principle 1: The Mental Preview Reduces In-Section Cognitive Load
The mental preview is the internal sequence: "I will brake here, turn in there, apex on that surface, carry momentum through the exit, and accelerate from there." Formed in the seconds before entry — during the approach or during a deliberate stop — this preview converts four or five real-time decisions into a single executable plan. The cognitive bandwidth freed by pre-making these decisions can then be used for micro-adjustments, traction management, and terrain reading ahead of the current position. Pre-planned riders read further ahead because they are not spending cognitive resources on the current section's decisions.

### Principle 2: Active Pre-Planning Follows Key Points — Not Full Continuity
Section planning does not require a complete frame-by-frame mental preview of the entire section. The more effective technique is to identify the section's "key points" — the braking zone, the turn-in point, the apex (best grip surface), any surface transitions, and the exit. These 4-6 key points form the skeleton of the plan. Between key points, the rider trusts trained technique and body position to handle the terrain automatically. This makes the plan efficient to construct (seconds, not minutes) and robust to variation — minor deviations between key points are handled by trained automatic responses.

### Principle 3: Walk Before Ride for Critical Sections
For sections where the consequence of error is high or the terrain is genuinely unknown, walking the section before riding it is the correct preparation. Walking at 5-6 km/h gives the rider a view of the terrain that is impossible at riding speed — surface texture is visible from 1m away, rut depth can be assessed precisely, grip surfaces are identifiable by foot pressure, and the entry-apex-exit sequence can be walked physically. The walked plan is then converted to a ridden plan with appropriate speed scaling — obstacles that look large from 1m appear much smaller at speed, and terrain that feels gradual on foot feels much steeper on a bike.

### Principle 4: Pre-Plan the Failure Contingency
For high-consequence sections, the planning process should include not just the success path but the failure path: if point A doesn't work (the planned line fails at the first corner), what is the escape route? If the climb fails at the halfway point, where is the safe recovery position? Pre-planning the failure contingency removes the worst outcome of a failed section — the rider freezing because they have no plan. A rider with a pre-planned failure contingency can execute the recovery calmly because the decision was already made.

### Principle 5: Planning Time Compresses With Familiarity and Experience
A rider encountering a section for the first time needs 30-60 seconds of deliberate planning — possibly including a walk. A rider who has ridden the same section 10 times can form the complete plan in a 2-second approach scan. A professional rider who has ridden thousands of similar sections can plan a new section in a fraction of a second because the plan is assembled from pre-built plan components (corner type X requires inputs A, B, C — already packaged). This compression is the mechanism by which expert riders appear to "just know" what to do — they are still planning, but the plan is assembled from familiar components in milliseconds.

### Principle 6: Competition Pre-Riding Provides Plan Templates for Race Execution
In enduro competition, riders are permitted pre-riding of the course, which converts the competition day from first-pass unknown terrain to second-pass familiar territory. The purpose of pre-riding is not to memorise every tree and root — that level of detail cannot be retained. It is to identify the 5-6 key decision points per section, form a line strategy for each, and build a stage-level plan (where to push pace, where to conserve energy, where to walk). Competition pre-riding is section planning applied systematically across an entire race course.

## 3. TECHNIQUE BREAKDOWN BY LEVEL

### 3a. Beginner Level
**Goal:** Establish the habit of forming a basic plan before entering technical sections — even if the plan is simple (two or three key points) and imperfect.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Stop before difficult sections and look: identify at minimum the entry point and exit point
- Name the plan: "I'll go left at that rock, aim for the gap in the roots, and exit right"
- One key point is better than no plan: even identifying the braking point before entry is meaningful
- After each section, review: did the ride match the plan? Why or why not?

**What Good Looks Like:**
Before entering a technical section, the rider slows or stops, looks ahead, and can articulate (even briefly) a plan before entering. The section is then ridden with some reference to that plan — it may not be perfectly executed, but the plan guided the broad line. After the section, the rider can compare the ridden line to the planned line.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Entering with no plan and hoping:** Speed carries into the section with no preparation. → Stop before every technical section until planning is automatic.
- **Plan too detailed — paralysis by analysis:** Trying to plan every root and surface change, failing to complete the plan before entry. → Keep the plan to 3 key points. Key points only.
- **Plan abandoned at the first deviation:** An obstacle differs from the plan and the rider freezes. → The plan is a framework. Minor deviations from it are handled by trained technique. Major deviations trigger the failure contingency.

### 3b. Intermediate Level
**Goal:** Develop rapid, accurate section plans from the approach — without needing to stop — and incorporate failure contingency planning for high-consequence sections.

**Progression Markers from Beginner:** Basic 2-3 key-point planning is habitual. Plans are sometimes maintained through full sections. Post-section review is regular.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Rolling approach planning: building the plan during the approach rather than from a stop
- Identifying key points rapidly: braking zone, apex, surface transition, exit — in under 5 seconds from approach
- Failure contingency: identifying the safe recovery point for every high-consequence section before entry
- Plan quality evaluation: did the exit quality confirm the plan was correct? What would improve it?

**What Good Looks Like:**
The rider approaches a technical section at reduced speed, builds the plan during the approach, and enters without a full stop. The plan is complete at entry — key points identified, failure contingency noted. The section is ridden with consistent reference to the key points. After the section, the rider can describe each key point and evaluate whether the plan produced the desired exit quality.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Key points selected incorrectly:** Identifying cosmetically obvious features (big rock) rather than functionally important ones (braking point, surface transition). → Key points are defined by technique requirements, not obstacle size.
- **No failure contingency on high-consequence sections:** Only the success path is planned. → Always plan the failure path on high-consequence sections. The recovery plan is as important as the ride plan.
- **Plan not updated on second pass:** Rider runs the same plan on the second pass without incorporating first-pass learning. → After each pass, identify one plan improvement and implement it.

### 3c. Advanced Level
**Goal:** Plan full section sequences in seconds from a moving approach, update plans in real time as sections evolve, and use pre-riding strategy systematically for competition.

**Progression Markers from Intermediate:** Rolling approach planning is automatic. Failure contingencies are built into every high-consequence plan. Exit quality is the primary plan evaluation metric.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Sub-second plan construction on familiar terrain: plan assembled from pre-built component patterns
- Multi-section planning: planning 2-3 sections ahead while riding the current section
- Pre-riding strategy: identifying the 5-6 key decision points per section during pre-riding, building race-day plan templates
- Plan execution under pressure: maintaining plan execution under fatigue and competition stress
- Adaptive plan updates: recognising when conditions have changed mid-stage and updating section plans

**What Good Looks Like:**
On a pre-ridden competition section, the rider executes the pre-planned line with precision — the plan built during pre-riding is still accurate. Between sections, the rider mentally reviews upcoming sections and updates plans based on evolving conditions. After the race, the rider can describe specific key points for multiple sections and explain where the pre-race plan was executed vs where conditions forced a plan update.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Pre-race plan too rigid:** Conditions change on race day but rider executes the pre-ridden plan without adjustment. → Pre-race plans are templates, not scripts. Race-day conditions must override pre-ridden details.
- **Multi-section planning consuming current section attention:** Thinking about the next section while riding the current one degrades current section execution. → Multi-section planning is limited to flat or recovery sections — not during technical execution.

## 4. DIAGNOSTIC CUES (AI COACHING ENGINE REFERENCE)

### Visual Cues — Correct Technique
- Rider's pace reduces before difficult sections: planning time being taken during approach
- Brief head movement scanning the section from the approach: active plan construction visible
- Entry is smooth and directed — not tentative or searching: plan was in place before entry
- Technique preparation (body position, gear) happens before the section: key points triggered pre-section action
- Exit is clean with early throttle: exit key point was included in plan and executed

### Visual Cues — Error Indicators
- Rider enters technical section at full approach speed without scan or pause: no planning time taken
- Entering, then stopping or turning mid-section: plan was not formed — section arrived as a surprise
- Erratic line through section — not following a clear direction: no plan to follow
- Multiple approaches to the same section without variation: attempts are not informed by plan improvement

### Audio Cues
- Smooth, consistent engine note through section: plan executing — no mid-section decision making
- Sudden throttle changes mid-section: decision being made during the section, not before
- Gear change sound at section entry after approach: plan included gear selection, executed before entry

### Sensor Cues
- Speed reduction in approach zone followed by consistent speed through section: planning time taken, then executed
- No sudden throttle changes mid-section: plan included throttle points, executed as planned
- IMU shows consistent lean angle through corner: plan established the line, rider executing it — no mid-corner decision changes

## 5. COACHING CUES & LANGUAGE

### Positive Reinforcement
- "You built the plan on the approach and I could see you executing it. Braking point, apex, exit — each one happened where you planned it. That's pre-loaded decision making."
- "You planned the failure contingency before that section. When the first corner didn't work out, you went straight to the recovery point without hesitation. That's the value of pre-planning the failure path."
- "Your second pass was clearly planned from the first pass. You changed the apex position and the exit was earlier. That's plan updating working — each pass is better than the last."

### Corrective Feedback
- "You entered that section at full speed without scanning. I could see you searching for a line mid-section — the decisions were happening during the section, not before. Slow down on the approach. Use that time to build the plan."
- "Your plan had no exit point. You got to the end of the section and didn't know where to go. Always plan all the way to the exit — that's where the plan delivers its value. The exit is the payoff."
- "You planned the section but the plan was based on the big obvious rock, not on where the grip is. Key points are not the biggest obstacles — they're the braking point, the apex surface, and the exit angle. Re-walk and re-plan."
- "You rode the same line on every pass. There's no evidence you're learning from each pass. After each pass, identify one thing the exit quality told you about the plan. Change one key point on the next pass."

### Progression Prompts
- "Walk this section before the next attempt. Take 5 minutes. Walk it slowly. Tell me the five key points you identify — not the obstacles, the technique decision points."
- "On the next approach, build the plan while you're moving. Don't stop. Reduce speed to give yourself time, but keep moving. Build the plan, enter, execute. Post-section review."
- "Before pre-riding this section, set yourself a planning target: identify exactly five key points, including one failure recovery point. Then ride it and tell me how many key points you actually used during the section."

## 6. DRILLS & PRACTICE EXERCISES

### Drill 1: Walk-Plan-Ride Sequence
**Purpose:** Build the foundational planning habit by fully separating the planning phase (walking) from the execution phase (riding).
**Setup:** A 50-100m technical section with multiple line decisions.
**Execution:**
1. Walk the section slowly. Identify 5 key points: entry speed decision point, braking zone, turn-in, apex surface, exit angle.
2. Return to start. Verbalise the plan: "I'll be at speed here, braking there, turning there, aiming for that rock surface at the apex, exiting right."
3. Ride the section executing the plan.
4. After: what matched the plan? What didn't? Why?
**Success Criteria:** 3 of 5 key points are executed as planned on the first pass. Second pass is more complete than the first.
**Duration/Reps:** 3 walk-plan-ride cycles on the same section.
**Progression:** Reduce walking time on each cycle. Third cycle: 60-second walk only.

### Drill 2: Approach-Speed Planning
**Purpose:** Build rolling approach planning — forming the plan while moving rather than from a stop.
**Setup:** A familiar technical section with a defined 20m approach zone.
**Execution:**
1. Reduce speed to 50% of normal at the approach zone start.
2. During the 20m approach, build the complete plan — key points identified before entry.
3. Enter the section executing the pre-approach plan.
4. After: was the plan complete at entry? Did the section execute to the plan?
**Success Criteria:** Plan is complete before the entry point without a full stop. Section execution follows the plan at the key points.
**Duration/Reps:** 10 passes.
**Progression:** Reduce the approach zone to 15m, then 10m. Compress plan construction into less time.

### Drill 3: Key Point Identification Practice
**Purpose:** Train accurate identification of the five standard key points, building the correct framework for planning.
**Setup:** Three different sections of varying character.
**Execution:**
1. For each section, identify and name: braking zone, turn-in point, apex surface (best grip), surface transition, exit direction.
2. Write or verbally confirm the 5 points before riding.
3. Ride and compare: were the identified points actually the critical technique decision points, or were they the cosmetically obvious ones?
4. Revise identification on second pass.
**Success Criteria:** Rider's identified key points correspond to actual technique decision requirements. Cosmetic feature identification (big rock) is replaced by functional point identification (grip surface, braking zone).
**Duration/Reps:** 3 sections, 2 passes each.
**Progression:** Apply to a section without a walk — key points must be identified from the approach.

### Drill 4: Failure Contingency Planning
**Purpose:** Build the habit of planning the failure path alongside the success path for high-consequence sections.
**Setup:** A high-consequence section (steep rocky descent, exposed traverse, or technical climb).
**Execution:**
1. Plan the success path: entry, key points, exit.
2. Immediately plan the failure path: if the entry fails, the bike recovers here. If the mid-section fails, the safe recovery point is there.
3. Ride the section. Note: did the failure path need to be used? Was it useful even when not used?
4. After the section, evaluate: how did knowing the failure path affect confidence and commitment on the success path?
**Success Criteria:** Failure contingency is planned before every high-consequence section. When a failure occurs (planned or accidental), rider goes to the planned recovery point without improvising.
**Duration/Reps:** Applied to every high-consequence section in a session.
**Progression:** Plan failure contingencies for two successive sections — where does the failure path of section one connect to the success entry of section two?

## 8. TERRAIN & CONTEXT VARIATIONS

### Unfamiliar Technical Sections
Full walk-plan-ride sequence is appropriate. Walking gives surface information that visual scanning cannot provide at distance. Key point identification is less accurate (no experience with this specific terrain) but the walk dramatically improves it. First-pass plan is acknowledged as provisional — the priority is gathering information rather than perfect execution.

### Familiar Repeated Sections
Plan construction is fast (sub-5 seconds) and assembled from pattern-matched experience. The risk is over-confidence — a section ridden 20 times in dry conditions may have changed significantly after rain. Familiar sections require a brief conditions-check: "is today's surface the same as last time?" If not, plan revision is needed even for familiar terrain.

### Competition Pre-Ridden Sections
Pre-ridden sections have a plan template built during pre-riding. Race-day execution draws from this template, with real-time monitoring to check that conditions match the pre-ride. The primary competition planning skill is recognising when race-day conditions require a plan update vs when the pre-ridden plan remains valid.

### Night Riding
Night section planning is more challenging because visual information is compressed to the artificial light field. Key point identification requires closer approach than in daylight. The walk-before-ride is more important for night riding of technical sections than for daytime — the walking illumination from a head torch is closer and more detailed than the riding illumination.

## 9. EXPERT INSIGHTS & SOURCES

### Key Insights
- **Billy Bolt on pre-riding:** "Pre-riding is where the race is won or lost for me. By the time I'm racing, every key section already has a plan. I'm not making decisions on race day — I'm executing plans I made yesterday."
- **Cognitive load research:** Studies in sports psychology show that pre-planning decisions before complex motor tasks consistently improves execution quality compared to making the same decisions during the task. The improvement is larger for more complex tasks — which explains why the benefit of section planning is greatest on the most technical sections.
- **Chunking in expert performance:** Chess research (Chase & Simon, 1973) showed that expert players perceive board positions as familiar "chunks" rather than individual pieces. Applied to section planning: expert riders perceive sections as familiar pattern types (rocky switchback, loose traverse, etc.) and retrieve pre-built plan chunks for each — explaining the speed of expert planning.
- **Visualisation research:** Mental rehearsal (visualisation) of motor tasks produces measurable improvements in subsequent physical performance — the mental preview fires the same motor pathways as physical practice, with partially equivalent learning effects. Section planning is functional visualisation — it produces the same motor preparation benefit.
- **Dakar Rally team strategy:** Professional rally navigation includes a "road book" that provides terrain previews in advance. Elite riders describe mentally building the next section plan while riding the current one — demonstrating multi-level planning as a competitive skill.
