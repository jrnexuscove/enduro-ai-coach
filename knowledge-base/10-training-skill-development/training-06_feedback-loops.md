# Feedback Loops (Video vs Feel vs Data)

## 1. OVERVIEW

A feedback loop in skill development is the process of generating performance information, comparing it against the correct standard, identifying the gap, and adjusting. The quality and accuracy of the feedback determines how fast the loop improves performance: high-quality feedback produces fast improvement; low-quality or absent feedback produces slow or no improvement. Off-road motorcycle riding has three distinct feedback channels available to riders: feel (proprioceptive — what the rider senses in real time during riding), video (visual — what the camera recorded after the fact), and data (sensor — what the metrics measured). Each channel has specific strengths and limitations. Feel is immediate and intuitive but highly distorted under stress and fatigue. Video is objective and revealing but delayed and requires review discipline. Data is precise and quantitative but requires calibration to interpret correctly. The most effective coaching feedback loops combine all three channels, using data and video to recalibrate the rider's feel model — the internal sense of correct that guides real-time riding decisions.

## 2. CORE PRINCIPLES

### Principle 1: Feel Alone Is the Least Reliable Feedback Channel
Proprioceptive feedback — what the rider feels during riding — is the primary real-time guidance system. It is also the most distorted by stress, anxiety, fatigue, and self-belief. A rider who believes they are smooth with their throttle may feel smooth while objective data shows high variance. A rider who feels like they are standing may be partially sitting. The disconnect between perceived and actual technique is the fundamental coaching problem: if the rider's feel was accurate, they would self-correct without external feedback. Using feel as the sole feedback channel means improving only as fast as feel can self-correct — which in most riders is very slowly, because the distortions are persistent.

### Principle 2: Video Is the Most Accessible High-Quality Feedback Channel
Video provides objective visual evidence of what actually happened — not what it felt like happened. A rider who watches video of their own riding and compares it to a correct technique reference can identify deviations that they were completely unaware of during riding. The power of video as a feedback tool is in this gap revelation: "I thought I had bent elbows — video shows they were straight." This gap between felt and actual is the coaching payload. Video is accessible to all riders with a smartphone or action camera and does not require specialised sensor equipment.

### Principle 3: Data Is the Most Precise But Requires Interpretation Skill
Sensor data provides quantitative measurement — smoothness indices, section times, speed traces, heart rate — that is more precise than video for some questions. But raw data requires interpretation: a high throttle variance score is meaningless unless the rider understands what normal variance looks like, how terrain normalisation works, and what the score implies for technique. Without interpretation guidance, data can be misleading or ignored. The AI coaching engine's highest-value contribution is interpretation: translating raw sensor numbers into actionable technique feedback that the rider can act on.

### Principle 4: The Feedback Hierarchy — Data Corrects Video Corrects Feel
For objective accuracy, the information hierarchy is: data (most objective) → video (highly objective) → feel (least objective). When feel and video conflict, video is right. When video and data conflict, the truth is usually in the data with an explanation from the video. This hierarchy does not mean feel is unimportant — feel is what guides real-time riding and cannot be replaced by data or video in the moment. But feel must be recalibrated using data and video so that the rider's internal sense of correct more accurately reflects actual correct technique. Over time, the goal is to make feel accurate enough that it self-corrects without external reference.

### Principle 5: The Feedback Loop Must Be Closed — Riding Change Must Occur
A feedback loop that identifies a problem but does not produce a technique change is not a complete loop. The loop is: ride → review → identify gap → adjust → ride again. Many riders complete the first three steps (they watch video and identify problems) but never close the loop with a deliberate adjustment attempt. The review is valuable only if it changes the next session's riding. The coaching engine must ensure that feedback outputs include a specific, actionable adjustment — not just a diagnosis.

### Principle 6: Establishing a Post-Ride Review Habit Is the Highest-Leverage Coaching Behaviour
The single most impactful change most self-coached riders can make is to establish a regular post-ride video review habit. Most riders do not review their riding — they ride, rely entirely on feel for feedback, and improve slowly if at all. A rider who reviews 10 minutes of video after every session and identifies one specific improvement target will progress dramatically faster than a rider who rides twice as many hours without review. The review habit is the multiplier that makes all other coaching work more effective.

## 3. TECHNIQUE BREAKDOWN BY LEVEL

### 3a. Beginner Level
**Goal:** Establish the basic review habit; learn to watch their own video without self-criticism; identify the most obvious gaps between felt and actual technique.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Setting up a basic video capture system (GoPro or phone mount) — any video is better than no video
- Learning to watch own video constructively rather than reactively (cringe response is normal and must be overcome)
- Identifying one specific observable difference per review session — not trying to fix everything
- Connecting one video observation to one on-trail adjustment

**What Good Looks Like:**
After a session, the rider watches 10 minutes of their own video. They notice their elbows are straight in a technical section — something they did not feel during riding. They make one note: "elbows bent next session." In the next session, they consciously focus on bent elbows in the same type of terrain. After the next ride, they review again: were elbows better?

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Watching video without a target:** Rider watches the full session video passively without looking for anything specific. Why it happens: no review framework. → Define one question before pressing play: "Are my elbows bent on rough terrain?"
- **Self-critical paralysis:** Rider watches video, sees many problems, feels overwhelmed, and stops watching. Why it happens: perfectionism and self-comparison. → One improvement target per session. Not all problems at once.
- **Not acting on the video observation:** Rider watches video, notes the problem, forgets it by the next session. Why it happens: no action bridge between review and riding. → Write one sentence immediately after review: "Next session I will [specific adjustment]."

### 3b. Intermediate Level
**Goal:** Combine video and audio review with basic metric data; develop accurate feel recalibration from objective evidence; close the feedback loop consistently.

**Progression Markers from Beginner:** Post-ride video review is a consistent habit (every session or every second session). At least three feel vs reality gaps have been identified and partially corrected. One technique improvement is traceable directly to video feedback.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Adding audio review to video review — listening to RPM patterns reveals throttle quality that is not always visible
- Beginning to use basic metric data (session time, error frequency counts) to supplement video
- Developing the ability to predict what the video will show before watching — calibrating feel to reality
- Closing the loop deliberately: a specific written adjustment between review and the next session

**What Good Looks Like:**
After a session, the rider watches video with audio, listening specifically for RPM spikes. They hear three large spikes during a corner sequence — throttle snap events. They did not feel these as particularly notable during riding. They note the section and the approximately throttle position from the video. In the next session, they explicitly trail throttle through that section type. They review again: have the spikes reduced?

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Reviewing but not adjusting:** Rider watches video, identifies issues, and then rides the next session without consciously targeting the identified problem. Why it happens: review and riding feel like separate activities. → The review note must be visible during the pre-ride warm-up to be useful.
- **Using video to confirm feel rather than challenge it:** Rider selectively pays attention to the parts of the video that confirm what they felt and ignores parts that contradict it. Why it happens: confirmation bias. → Specifically look for instances where the video shows something different from what was felt — these are the highest-value observations.

### 3c. Advanced Level
**Goal:** Use all three feedback channels systematically; develop accurate real-time feel that rarely diverges from video or data; use the feedback loop for precision technique refinement rather than error identification.

**Progression Markers from Intermediate:** Feel is relatively accurate — predicted vs actual technique divergence is small and the rider can predict what the video will show. Basic metrics are actively tracked and compared across sessions.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Quantitative feel prediction: before watching video, predict specific metric scores (SI, SCS, approximate section times) — then compare to actual. The accuracy of predictions is itself a calibration metric.
- Using data to detect subtle improvements invisible to video — small SI changes or SCS improvements that are real but not camera-visible
- Designing feedback protocols for competition: how to extract meaningful feedback from a competition ride where video may be limited
- Mentoring others — explaining feel vs reality gaps to a training partner is one of the most effective ways to deepen the understanding

**What Good Looks Like:**
An advanced rider completes a session, makes predictions about their SI score and which sections will show the most errors before reviewing any data. They review video and data. Prediction accuracy is within 15% for most metrics. They identify two small, precise technique refinements from the data that are invisible on video. They design next session's drill prescription from these findings.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Feedback loop becoming a performance review, not a learning tool:** Advanced rider tracks metrics to assess performance rather than guide improvement. Why it happens: competition mindset replacing learning mindset. → Metrics should always answer the question "what should I change?" not "how did I do?"
- **Over-analysing to the point of paralysis:** Rider spends more time in review than on the bike. Why it happens: perfectionist response to access to data. → 15-20 minutes of targeted review per session is sufficient. More is diminishing returns.

## 4. DIAGNOSTIC CUES (AI COACHING ENGINE REFERENCE)

### Visual Cues — Correct Technique
- Rider can predict what video will show before watching — high feel-reality accuracy
- Rider identifies a specific technique element to review before pressing play — targeted review habit
- Post-review: rider writes or verbalises one specific technique adjustment — loop is closing
- On next session: the identified adjustment is visible in the riding — loop completion confirmed

### Visual Cues — Error Indicators
- Rider watches video passively without identifying any specific issue — no review framework
- Rider expresses surprise at what video shows — large feel-reality gap, uncalibrated feel
- Post-review adjustment is vague ("I'll try to be smoother") not specific ("I'll roll the throttle on over 2 seconds on corner exits")
- Next session shows no change in identified technique element — loop not closed

### Audio Cues
- Rider can predict RPM pattern for a section before listening — good audio feedback calibration
- Rider surprised by RPM spikes on audio playback — feel was not detecting throttle snap events
- Audio review reveals consistent patterns invisible to video (throttle quality) — audio adds value beyond video

### Sensor Cues
- SI prediction vs actual: difference > 20 points = poorly calibrated feel; < 10 points = well-calibrated feel
- Session-to-session SI trend: if feedback loop is working, SI should trend upward over weeks
- SCS trend across sessions: improving SCS = loop is closing on consistency-related improvements
- Rider-predicted vs actual section time: small gap = good feel calibration; large gap = feel recalibration needed
- RTAE trend: if feedback loop includes error recovery analysis, RTAE should trend downward

## 5. COACHING CUES & LANGUAGE

### Positive Reinforcement
- "You predicted your SI within 5 points before checking. That means your feel is calibrated — what you feel during riding closely matches what actually happened. That's a major advantage: your real-time guidance system is now reliable."
- "You identified the elbow issue from video, wrote a specific adjustment, and last session your elbows were visibly better. That's a complete feedback loop. This is how skills improve."
- "Your audio review this week was on point — you heard the RPM spikes and connected them to the throttle snap moments. Then you fixed it. That's exactly the process."

### Corrective Feedback
- "You said you were smooth on that section. Let's watch the video. What do you see? You were not smooth — look at that RPM trace and the chassis lurch. Your feel said smooth; your video says choppy. Your feel is miscalibrated. That gap is the most important thing to work on."
- "You watch your video, but then you ride the next session exactly the same as before. The review is wasted if nothing changes. What is the one specific thing you're going to do differently next session? Say it out loud. Write it down."
- "You're watching video but you're not asking it any questions. Before you press play, ask yourself: was my throttle smooth through the rock garden? Then watch to find out. Video without a question is entertainment, not feedback."
- "You've got data, you've got video — but you're not using either to change your riding. You're using them to confirm how good you were. That's the wrong question. Ask: what should I change? Then find the answer in the data."

### Progression Prompts
- "This week, after every session, write down one prediction: 'I think my throttle was [smooth/choppy] because I [felt/didn't feel] it.' Then check the video. How accurate was the prediction? Build that calibration muscle."
- "Before the next session, read your review note from the last session. Make sure you can remember the specific adjustment. Ride with that adjustment as your only focus for the first 15 minutes."
- "Experiment with audio review this week. Ride a section you know well, then listen back with headphones. Can you hear the throttle quality? Can you identify the moment when RPM spikes? That audio awareness is a skill in itself."

## 6. DRILLS & PRACTICE EXERCISES

### Drill 1: The Feel Prediction Test
**Purpose:** Calibrate feel accuracy by comparing pre-review predictions to actual video and data.
**Setup:** A session with a GoPro or phone camera mounted. A metric tracking device (GPS or phone app) if available.
**Execution:**
1. Immediately after the session (before reviewing), write down answers to three questions: "On a scale of 1-10, how smooth was my throttle this session? Which section was my best? Which section was my worst?"
2. Review video and data.
3. Compare predictions to actual evidence.
4. Gap analysis: where was feel most inaccurate? That is the area where feel recalibration is most needed.
5. In the next session, pay specific attention to the area where feel was least accurate.
**Success Criteria:** Prediction accuracy improves across three sessions. Rider can predict their SI score within 10 points and identify their worst section correctly from feel.
**Duration/Reps:** 10 minutes post-ride prediction + 15-minute review. Every session.
**Progression:** Add a third prediction category: "What one technique error will I see on video?" — then find it (or find out it was a different error). Builds diagnostic precision.

### Drill 2: The Targeted Review Session
**Purpose:** Build an effective, focused review habit rather than passive video watching.
**Setup:** 15 minutes. Video from the session. A specific review question decided before pressing play.
**Execution:**
1. Before pressing play: write one question the review must answer. Examples: "Were my elbows bent on rough terrain?" "Did I apex late on the main corner?" "Did my throttle cause any spin events?"
2. Watch only the segments relevant to the question — not the entire session.
3. Answer the question with a yes/no and one specific observation.
4. Write the specific adjustment for next session.
5. Total time: 15 minutes maximum.
**Success Criteria:** Every review session produces one specific, actionable, written adjustment. Review takes less than 15 minutes because it is targeted.
**Duration/Reps:** Applied to every review session.
**Progression:** Add a second review question per session. Then add an audio-specific question (listening for RPM patterns rather than watching video).

### Drill 3: The Three-Channel Comparison
**Purpose:** Experience all three feedback channels simultaneously to understand how they complement and differ.
**Setup:** A session with video, audio, and at least one metric (section time, or a smoothness app). A specific 30-60 second section to analyse.
**Execution:**
1. Ride the target section three times.
2. Post-session: analyse the section using all three channels separately.
3. Channel 1 — feel: write what the section felt like before reviewing anything else.
4. Channel 2 — video: watch the section. What does video add to or contradict from feel?
5. Channel 3 — audio: listen to the section. What does audio add to or contradict from video?
6. If available: Channel 4 — data: what does the metric score add to or contradict from the others?
7. Compare all four (or three) descriptions: where do they agree? Where do they conflict?
**Success Criteria:** Rider can articulate the specific information each channel added that the previous channel missed. Understands the hierarchy and complementarity of channels.
**Duration/Reps:** One detailed three-channel analysis per session. 20-minute review.
**Progression:** Apply to a different section each session. Eventually, three-channel analysis should happen automatically and quickly for any section of interest.

### Drill 4: The Improvement Loop Documentation
**Purpose:** Create a documented evidence trail of feedback loop completions — proving that the loop is closing and skill is improving.
**Setup:** A simple log or document tracking: observation → adjustment → outcome.
**Execution:**
1. After each review, log: "From [session date], I observed [specific technique element] was [specific problem]. Adjustment: [specific change] for next session."
2. After the next session: "Did the adjustment work? Evidence: [yes/no + what was seen on video or felt]."
3. After three sessions of the same adjustment: "Is this improvement sustained? SI score [before vs after]. Section time [before vs after]."
4. Review the log monthly: how many improvement loops have been completed? What is the pattern of what works and what does not?
**Success Criteria:** At least one complete improvement loop documented (observation → adjustment → confirmed improvement with evidence) per month.
**Duration/Reps:** 5 minutes per session to maintain the log. Monthly review of 20 minutes.
**Progression:** Share the log with a coach or training partner for external verification and additional insight.

## 8. TERRAIN & CONTEXT VARIATIONS

### Training Ground
Best feedback loop environment — familiar terrain removes terrain-novelty variables so the feedback signal is clearly about technique. The training ground is the most appropriate environment for consistent metric tracking and video comparison.

### Competition
Feedback loops in competition operate under constraints — often no video available, data collection limited, and post-event analysis is delayed. Developing a competition-specific review protocol (what can be assessed from feel and memory immediately after sections) is a specific skill for competitive riders.

### New Terrain
New terrain produces lower signal-to-noise ratio in feedback — errors may be terrain-novelty errors rather than technique errors. Feedback analysis on new terrain should weight video more heavily than feel, because feel on unfamiliar terrain is the least reliable channel.

### Group Riding
Training partners or coaches as live feedback providers is a legitimate fourth channel. Live observation feedback can be more immediate and specific than any post-session review. However, observer feedback quality depends on the observer's knowledge — untrained observer feedback can be misleading.

## 9. EXPERT INSIGHTS & SOURCES

### Key Insights
- **W. Timothy Gallwey — "The Inner Game of Tennis"**: The central insight — that performance is improved by accurate observation (clear, non-judgmental awareness of what is actually happening) rather than instruction or criticism — is the theoretical foundation for the video feedback approach. "Interference" (self-criticism, anxiety) reduces the feedback quality that feel provides.
- **Motor learning research — Augmented feedback**: Studies consistently show that augmented feedback (video, data, verbal coaching) accelerates skill learning significantly compared to intrinsic feedback (feel) alone. The effect size is largest for novel skills and reduces as the skill becomes expert-level — matching the observation that beginners benefit most from external feedback and advanced riders can rely more on calibrated feel.
- **Bret Tkacs / ADVMotoSkills**: Explicitly uses video review in coaching — having students watch their own riding and identify the specific problem that they felt was "fine" during the session. The gap revelation is a consistent coaching tool.
- **Professional motorsport data analysis**: Formula 1 teams provide drivers with immediate telemetry feedback — throttle traces, brake traces, GPS lines — within minutes of each session. The driver's recalibration of feel using data is a core part of the professional development cycle.
- **Golf's video coaching revolution**: Golf instruction changed fundamentally when video became accessible — coaches could show students that their "straight" arms were actually bent, or their "closed" face was actually open. The same revolution is available in off-road riding coaching and is a primary value driver of the AI coaching engine.
- **Learning without feedback research**: Studies show that skill learning without feedback produces a characteristic pattern: initial rapid improvement from incidental feedback (feel), then plateau — because feel alone cannot resolve persistent errors. External feedback breaks these plateaus consistently.
