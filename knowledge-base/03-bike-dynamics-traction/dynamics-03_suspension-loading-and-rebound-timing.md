# Suspension Loading and Rebound Timing

## 1. OVERVIEW

Suspension on an off-road motorcycle operates in two distinct phases: compression (absorbing an input) and rebound (returning to its natural length). Compression is reactive — the bike hits something and the spring and damper absorb the energy. Rebound is proactive — the damper controls how fast the energy is released back into the system. Of these two phases, rebound timing is the more critical and the less understood by most riders. If the suspension rebounds too fast before the next hit arrives, it is ready — spring fully extended, energy reset. If it is still rebounding when the next hit arrives, it packs down progressively — each subsequent hit finding less suspension travel available. This is the origin of "packing" — the suspension's cumulative failure to reset between inputs. Every experienced enduro rider has felt this: entering a sequence of bumps fine and exiting it with the bike slamming harshly on the last hit. The solution exists at three levels: suspension setup, rider body position, and the timing of inputs.

## 2. CORE PRINCIPLES

### Principle 1: Compression and Rebound — The Two-Phase Cycle
When a wheel hits an obstacle, the spring compresses and the damper resists the compression by pushing hydraulic fluid through calibrated ports. The spring stores energy; the damper converts some of it to heat. On the rebound stroke, the spring releases its stored energy and pushes the wheel back down. The rebound damper controls the rate of this extension. Too little rebound damping (fast rebound): the spring launches the wheel rapidly off the ground, potentially kicking the rider. Too much rebound damping (slow rebound): the suspension is still extending when the next hit arrives — progressive packing. The correct rebound setting allows the suspension to fully extend in the available time between obstacles, no faster and no slower.

### Principle 2: Rebound Timing Is Speed-Dependent
At slow speeds over widely-spaced obstacles, there is ample time between hits for rebound to complete. Rebound damping matters less. At high speeds through closely-spaced obstacles (whoops, rocks, roots), the time between hits may be less than the time required for full rebound extension. This is when packing occurs regardless of setup — the obstacles arrive faster than any rebound rate can handle. The rider's body becomes the additional rebound system at this point — by actively extending the legs to push the bike down between hits (body decoupling, see BIOMECH-09), the rider adds human-generated rebound force to supplement or replace the mechanical spring.

### Principle 3: Packing — The Cumulative Failure Mode
Suspension packing occurs when each compression event finds the fork or shock partially through its rebound stroke rather than fully extended. The effect is additive: the second hit finds 80% of travel available; the third hit finds 60%; the fifth hit may find 30%, causing a harsh bottom-out on what would otherwise be a manageable obstacle. Packing is speed-dependent — it worsens as speed and frequency of obstacles increase. Riders who increase speed through a rough section without understanding packing often report the section feeling "harder" at higher speed, which is counter-intuitive since momentum helps. The truth: momentum helps with individual obstacles but speeds can be reached where packing overrides the benefit.

### Principle 4: Pre-Loading — Multiplying Absorption Capacity
Before a significant obstacle or drop, the rider can deliberately pre-load the suspension by compressing it slightly with body weight — crouching into the bike. When the obstacle arrives, the suspension begins from a partially compressed state. Counterintuitively, this is beneficial in some scenarios: the suspension is already moving in the compression direction when the impact arrives, giving it more initial compliance. More importantly, a rapidly compressed spring generates more rebound force — the pre-loaded suspension snaps the bike over the obstacle more assertively. This is the physics behind the push-down technique used for log crossings and table-top obstacles.

### Principle 5: Rebound Speed Setting and Its Handling Consequences
The rebound adjuster (typically a small screw on the fork cap or shock body) changes the speed at which fluid passes through the rebound circuit. Faster rebound (adjuster opened) = wheel drops faster after compression = better at following rough terrain but risks kicking the bike on sharp edges. Slower rebound (adjuster closed) = wheel drops slower = less pack risk at speed but the bike "stacks" its response across multiple hits. For enduro, most modern fork and shock setups are calibrated for a moderate rebound rate — faster than cross-country motorcycles, slower than motocross bikes which run very fast rebound for whoop sections.

### Principle 6: Body Decoupling as the Rider-Controlled Rebound System
The rider's body, when decoupled from the bike (BIOMECH-09), acts as an additional suspension unit operating in parallel with the mechanical system. The legs compress as the bike pitches up over an obstacle and extend as the bike drops. By actively pushing the bike down with the legs during the rebound phase (rather than waiting for the spring), the rider supplements the mechanical rebound force and ensures the wheel tracks the ground more closely between hits. At very high speeds through rough terrain, this body-driven rebound assistance is essential — mechanical rebound alone is insufficient.

## 3. TECHNIQUE BREAKDOWN BY LEVEL

### 3a. Beginner Level
**Goal:** Understand the two phases of suspension action. Learn to recognise packing when it occurs and identify that riding slower through bumpy sections prevents it.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Feel the difference between a single bump (full suspension travel available) and a series of bumps (packing)
- Understand that higher speed increases packing risk through tight obstacle sequences
- Begin standing position with bent knees as the foundational body decoupling position
- Observe suspension travel visually — watch the fork leg position relative to the guard

**What Good Looks Like:**
The rider approaches a series of small bumps at moderate speed. The bike rides smoothly through the first several, then begins to feel harsher at the end of the sequence — recognising this as packing, the rider reduces speed on the next pass and finds the sequence is more manageable. They are beginning to understand speed-versus-packing as a trade-off.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Riding faster to "power through" rough sections:** More speed amplifies packing. → Understand that beyond a certain speed threshold, rough terrain gets harsher — find that threshold and manage to it.
- **Sitting through bumpy sections:** Removes the ability to use legs as additional suspension. → Stand and bend knees — even if the absorption is imperfect, it's better than seated.
- **Ignoring fork travel:** Rider has no sense of how much suspension is left at any given moment. → Look at the fork during drills; develop a feel for travel consumption.

### 3b. Intermediate Level
**Goal:** Actively manage body position to supplement mechanical rebound. Begin adjusting pre-loading technique for specific obstacles. Start to understand how suspension setup affects the handling character at different speeds.

**Progression Markers from Beginner:** Rider recognises packing as it occurs. Can identify a rough section as becoming worse at higher speeds. Standing position is established.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Active leg extension between bumps — pushing the bike down in the rebound phase
- Pre-loading technique before drops, logs, and steep compressions
- Reading the frequency of obstacles to estimate whether rebound timing will be adequate
- Understanding their own bike's rebound behaviour — do they need to supplement or does the bike manage itself?

**What Good Looks Like:**
Through a rocky section, the rider's knees visibly compress and extend — not statically but dynamically in response to each obstacle's compression and rebound. The torso remains level while the bike pitches beneath. Before a significant rock or log, the rider crouches slightly into the bike before contact, giving the suspension a primed start. The section is ridden faster than the beginner level without increased harshness.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Passive standing — legs absorbing but not contributing to rebound:** Rider stands but legs only compress with impacts — they do not push down in the rebound phase. → Think of it as cycling: compress (pedal down), extend (push down). Both phases are active.
- **Over-pre-loading before every obstacle:** Pre-loading all the time means the bike never has full travel available for unexpected impacts. → Pre-load deliberately for significant, identified obstacles — not as a default constant position.
- **Ignoring rebound speed as a variable:** Rider thinks suspension is fixed and only body position can change the result. → Learn what the rebound adjuster does; experiment with one click faster and one click slower across the same section.

### 3c. Advanced Level
**Goal:** Seamlessly integrate mechanical suspension management, body decoupling, and pre-loading into a continuous, dynamic system. Adjust technique in real-time based on section characteristics and speed.

**Progression Markers from Intermediate:** Body decoupling is automatic. Can pre-load instinctively before significant obstacles. Has experimented with suspension setup and understands what changes affect what.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- High-speed rough terrain: supplementing rebound with aggressive leg extension between hits at speed
- Section speed management: knowing where speed must be backed off to prevent packing, and where more speed is safe
- Suspension feedback interpretation: reading what the bike communicates about its remaining travel and rebound state
- Setup knowledge: diagnosing whether a handling problem is rider technique or suspension calibration

**What Good Looks Like:**
From a following chase camera, the rider's head and shoulders are rock-steady through a rough, fast section while the bike moves dramatically beneath them. The rear wheel tracks closely to the ground between rocks — the rider is actively pushing it down. Entry speed into the rough section is high but controlled, and it does not increase harshly through the section — packing is prevented by a combination of rebound rate, active legs, and appropriate speed.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Too-fast rebound causing kick:** The bike launches the rear off sharp-edged rocks or roots. The rider is thrown upward on each hit. → Slow the rebound by one or two clicks; also moderate entry speed.
- **Relying solely on suspension setup and ignoring body technique:** Rider adjusts setup to try to compensate for passive riding. → No setup can replace active body decoupling at high speeds.
- **Not adjusting technique between morning (cold) and afternoon (warmed) suspension:** Oil viscosity changes with temperature, affecting rebound rate. → Expect slightly different behaviour and be aware.

## 4. DIAGNOSTIC CUES (AI COACHING ENGINE REFERENCE)

### Visual Cues — Correct Technique
- From a side camera: rider's head height remains consistent across a rough section — minimal vertical bobbing despite significant terrain irregularity
- Knees visibly alternating between compression and extension as the bike passes over obstacles — active, not passive
- Fork leg position relative to guard shows suspension is not fully compressed at mid-section — adequate travel remaining
- Rear of the bike visibly drops down between bumps, tracking ground contour — rebound completing between hits
- Pre-load compression visible before significant obstacle: rider crouches slightly before log, rock, or drop

### Visual Cues — Error Indicators
- Rider's head and shoulders bobbing dramatically up and down through rough sections: not decoupled — body is moving with the bike
- Fork fully compressed mid-section and remaining compressed: packing has occurred, no rebound completing
- Bike visibly pitching forward and backward repeatedly — not tracking terrain but oscillating: either wrong rebound rate or excessive speed for the setting
- Rider's legs remain static (not cycling between compress and extend) while standing — passive standing, no active rebound contribution
- Bike kicking upward off each rock with the rear wheel leaving the ground: rebound too fast, kicking the bike skyward

### Audio Cues
- Distinct bottom-out sound (a solid thunk from the fork or shock): suspension fully compressed — packing or impact exceeds travel
- Rapid, repeated suspension movement sounds through a sequence: rebound is completing between hits (good) — the suspension is cycling audibly
- Single long compressed-sounding creak through a rough sequence: packing — the suspension compressed and did not return
- Metallic rattle from fork area: stanchion bottoming out against internal hardware — chronic over-loading

### Sensor Cues
- Accelerometer at handlebars showing spike amplitude increasing progressively through a rough sequence: packing confirmed — each successive hit transmits more force because travel is diminishing
- Consistent, oscillating acceleration pattern (spike-return-spike-return) at the same amplitude: rebound timing correct — suspension cycling between full and partial compression
- IMU pitch oscillation that matches terrain frequency: body and bike moving together — not decoupled
- Handlebar vibration frequency shifting to match obstacle frequency at high speed: packing zone entered — systematic suspension overload

## 5. COACHING CUES & LANGUAGE

### Positive Reinforcement
- "Your head barely moved through that whole rock section. The legs were working the whole time. That is exactly what body decoupling looks like — keep it."
- "I saw the pre-load before that log. You set the suspension up before the hit and the bike rolled straight over. That is advanced technique."
- "You found the right speed for that section — it was smooth all the way through. You understood where the packing threshold was and stayed under it."

### Corrective Feedback
- "You're standing but your legs aren't cycling. They compress on the hits but you're not pushing down in the gaps between rocks. Think of it like pumping — compress, extend, compress, extend. Both phases count."
- "It got harsh at the end of that rock sequence. That's packing — the suspension was still returning when the next hit came. Either back your speed off 10% or actively push the bike down between hits to help it rebound."
- "That bottom-out sound is telling you something. You're asking for more travel than the bike has. Either slow down, pre-load less aggressively, or we need to look at spring rate."
- "You pre-loaded before every single rock. The suspension spent the whole section already compressed — when a big one came, there was nothing left. Reserve the pre-load for the significant obstacles."

### Progression Prompts
- "Next pass, I want you actively pushing the bike down between each rock — not just compressing on them. Push the pegs away from you in the gaps. Exaggerate it."
- "Film yourself through that section. Watch where your head is relative to the horizon. If it's bobbing, you're not decoupled. That's what we're working on."
- "Try that section at 20% more speed. Feel where it starts getting harsher. That is your packing threshold. Now you know where the ceiling is."

## 6. DRILLS & PRACTICE EXERCISES

### Drill 1: Single-Obstacle Pre-Load Practice
**Purpose:** Train the pre-load motion as a deliberate, timed action before significant obstacles.
**Setup:** A single clear obstacle — a log, rock, or constructed roller approximately 150–200mm high. Clear approach and exit.
**Execution:**
1. Approach the obstacle at slow walking pace (5 km/h). Do not pre-load. Observe how the suspension handles it.
2. Approach at 10 km/h with a deliberate pre-load: 2 metres before the obstacle, squat into the bike by bending knees, compressing the suspension slightly.
3. As the front wheel contacts the obstacle, the suspension is already moving — it rolls over more smoothly.
4. Repeat 5 times, progressively increasing speed by 5 km/h each set.
5. On each set, note how the required pre-load timing changes as speed increases — it must occur further from the obstacle at higher speeds.
**Success Criteria:** Rider consistently pre-loads at the correct distance for each speed. The bike rolls over the obstacle without harsh impact. Pre-load timing is anticipatory, not reactive.
**Duration/Reps:** 20 minutes. 15–20 passes.
**Progression:** Chain two obstacles together, requiring two pre-loads in quick succession. Then try three.

### Drill 2: Active Rebound Push — Parallel Bars Exercise
**Purpose:** Build the habit of actively pushing the bike down during the rebound phase, supplementing the mechanical spring.
**Setup:** Two markers on flat ground, 2 metres apart, simulating the gap between two obstacles. No actual obstacles needed initially.
**Execution:**
1. Ride between the markers standing at 15–20 km/h.
2. Compress the legs as you pass the first marker (simulating an impact).
3. Actively push the feet down on the pegs between the markers (simulating the rebound push).
4. Compress again as you pass the second marker.
5. Exaggerate the motion — it should feel like deliberately pumping the bike down between markers.
**Success Criteria:** The bike visibly drops down between markers — compresses at marker one, rises between, presses down in the middle, rises at marker two. This should be visible from a side camera.
**Duration/Reps:** 15 minutes. Move to actual small obstacles once the motion is established.
**Progression:** Use two actual small obstacles spaced 2–3 metres apart. Then increase speed. Then add a third obstacle.

### Drill 3: Packing Threshold Speed Test
**Purpose:** Identify each rider's personal packing threshold for a given section — the speed at which packing begins to occur — so they can manage pace intelligently.
**Setup:** A consistent rough section — 20–30 metres of evenly-spaced rocks, roots, or constructed bumps.
**Execution:**
1. Pass 1: very slow speed (walking pace). Note how smooth the suspension feels — full rebound completing between every hit.
2. Pass 2: comfortable trail speed. Note whether the section becomes harsher toward the end.
3. Pass 3: 20% faster than pass 2. Note harshness level and where in the section it begins.
4. Pass 4: 40% faster than pass 2. Note the point at which packing is obvious.
5. Identify the "packing threshold" — the speed at which the last 30% of the section becomes noticeably harsher than the first 30%.
**Success Criteria:** Rider can identify their packing threshold for this section and can describe it. Recognises the threshold as a useful limit rather than just a "rough" feeling.
**Duration/Reps:** 30 minutes. 4 passes at each speed level.
**Progression:** After identifying the threshold, practice active rebound supplementation (Drill 2) at threshold speed — see if body technique can push the threshold higher.

### Drill 4: Rebound Setting Comparison
**Purpose:** Develop sensitivity to suspension rebound rate by deliberately testing different settings on the same section, building calibration feel.
**Setup:** A consistent rough trail section. Rebound adjuster accessible (typical fork cap or shock adjuster). Baseline setting noted.
**Execution:**
1. Ride the section at baseline rebound setting. Note the feel and the bike's behaviour.
2. Add 3 clicks of rebound damping (slower). Ride the same section. Note how the bike feels — does it pack sooner? Does it feel more controlled?
3. Remove 6 clicks from baseline (faster). Ride the section. Note whether the bike kicks more or whether it tracks better.
4. Return to baseline. Confirm the baseline feel is identifiable.
**Success Criteria:** Rider can describe the difference between the three settings in sensory terms. Can identify which setting was faster, which kicked more, and which packed sooner. Can return to a preferred setting by feel, not just by counting clicks.
**Duration/Reps:** 45 minutes. 3–4 passes at each setting.
**Progression:** Perform the same test at a higher speed. Note how the effect of each setting becomes more pronounced as speed increases.

## 8. TERRAIN & CONTEXT VARIATIONS

### Rocky Terrain
The highest-demand environment for rebound timing. Rocks are hard, closely-spaced, and irregularly sized. Each impact is sharp and the available time between impacts is short. The rider must supplement mechanical rebound with aggressive body decoupling. Speed management is critical — there is a speed beyond which no rebound technique can prevent packing on closely-spaced rocks. Trail vision must be used to identify individual rock clusters and manage speed accordingly.

### Whoops (Motocross-style repeated mounds)
Whoops are the definitive rebound-timing challenge. Perfectly spaced to be just below the ideal rebound interval at competitive speed, they force the rider to either pump through them (rhythmically supplementing rebound with body movement in sync with the whoop frequency) or absorb them at a speed where packing is inevitable. The pump technique — pushing down as the front exits each whoop to load the bike for the next one — converts what would be a harsh packing sequence into a rhythmic flow.

### Roots (Forest/Trail)
Roots present a variable-spacing challenge. Some sections have regular root intervals; others are chaotic. The chaotic sections are hardest because the rider cannot establish a rhythm. Each root impact must be treated individually. Slower speeds than rocks are often required because roots add a deflection risk (see DYNAMICS-07) that compounds the rebound timing challenge.

### High-Speed Desert/Hardpack
At high speeds on relatively flat hardpack with occasional sharp-edged rocks or deteriorated ruts, the rebound challenge is different: single sharp impacts at very high speed that demand very fast rebound rates so the wheel returns to ground contact immediately. The risk of a too-slow rebound is that the wheel is still extending when it hits the next obstacle and generates almost no cushioning force. Setup matters more at this speed range than body technique — the rebound rate must be calibrated for the terrain frequency at the riding speed.

## 9. EXPERT INSIGHTS & SOURCES

### Key Insights
- **Paul Whibley** (four-time AMA National Enduro Champion): describes suspension timing as "learning to speak the same language as the trail. The trail has a rhythm. If your rebound is on a different rhythm, you're always fighting it."
- **KTM Factory Suspension Setup (WP):** Factory enduro riders typically run rebound settings significantly faster than stock — prioritising wheel-to-ground tracking over comfort. This is possible because their body decoupling technique compensates for any kick the faster rebound generates.
- **Motocross whoop technique:** The "pumping" technique for whoops (actively pushing down in the trough between each whoop) is documented in all major MX coaching curricula. It is mechanically identical to the active rebound supplement technique described for enduro rocky terrain.
- **Cold vs warm oil viscosity:** Suspension fluid viscosity changes by approximately 15–25% between 5°C and 40°C operating temperature. This changes the effective rebound rate without the rider adjusting the adjuster. Morning rides (cold oil) will feel slower in rebound than afternoon rides on the same setting.
- **Progressive spring rate interaction:** Many modern enduro bikes use a progressive rear spring. The spring rate increases as travel is used — meaning the rebound force also increases in the middle of the travel range. Packing is therefore less linear than it appears — the bike may feel fine for the first compression events and then suddenly deteriorate as the higher-rate zone takes over.
