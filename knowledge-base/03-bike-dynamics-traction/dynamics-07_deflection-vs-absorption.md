# Deflection vs Absorption — Rocks and Roots

## 1. OVERVIEW

When a wheel meets an obstacle — a rock, a root, a rut edge — one of two things happens: the wheel absorbs the obstacle (rolls over it) or deflects off it (bounces sideways or backward). Absorption is the desired outcome. Deflection is the failure mode. The difference between these two outcomes is determined by the angle of approach, the speed of approach, the suspension state, the tyre contact patch, and the rider's body position. Understanding this distinction is practical and immediately actionable: a rider who knows that approaching a root at 30 degrees causes deflection and approaching at 80 degrees causes absorption can change their line on every obstacle, on every lap. It is one of the most transferable technical insights in enduro riding — a mental model that applies from a single rock to an entire rocky mountain face.

## 2. CORE PRINCIPLES

### Principle 1: The Deflection Mechanism
Deflection occurs when the lateral component of the impact force exceeds the tyre's ability to hold its line. Imagine a ball rolling into a wall at a shallow angle — it bounces sideways. A tyre hitting a rock at 30 degrees from the rock's face experiences a large lateral force component and a small forward force component. The tyre cannot generate enough lateral friction to resist the sideways push — it deflects. The result is a sudden, unintended direction change of the front wheel, which can cause the bike to track off line, hit another obstacle, or cause the front to wash. At shallow angles (0–30 degrees from parallel), deflection is almost certain. At steep angles (60–90 degrees from parallel), the lateral component is small and the forward component dominates — the wheel rides over rather than bouncing off.

### Principle 2: The Critical Approach Angle — 90 Degrees is Ideal
Approaching an obstacle perpendicularly (90 degrees to its face) eliminates the lateral component entirely. The force is entirely forward — the tyre compresses the obstacle or rolls over it. In practice, riding perfectly perpendicular to every obstacle in a rock garden is impossible — trails do not allow that kind of line selectivity. But the principle provides a clear direction: the closer to perpendicular, the less deflection risk. Many experienced enduro riders unconsciously angle their approach to rocks and roots more steeply than beginners, who tend to follow the trail direction regardless of obstacle angle. This single adjustment — actively seeking steeper approach angles through line selection — transforms obstacle negotiation without changing speed or technique.

### Principle 3: Speed and Momentum Affect Absorption Threshold
At very low speeds, even a good approach angle may result in deflection or stalling against the obstacle because the bike lacks momentum to ride over it. The tyre must have enough kinetic energy to deform the obstacle surface slightly (on compliant obstacles like roots) or to compress the suspension and vault the bike over (on rigid rocks). This is why "carrying speed" through technical terrain is not just about maintaining progress — it is about maintaining the physical conditions required for absorption rather than deflection. Each obstacle has a minimum speed threshold below which deflection or stop is the only outcome regardless of angle. However, very high speed can also reduce absorption — the suspension has less time to react, and a rigid impact at high speed can cause the tyre to skip sideways even from a good angle.

### Principle 4: Body Decoupling Allows Bike to Deflect While Rider Maintains Direction
Even when the wheel deflects, the crash is not inevitable — if the rider's body is decoupled from the bike (BIOMECH-09). A decoupled rider maintains their direction and momentum independent of what the bike is doing momentarily. The bike can kick sideways under them, and if the deflection is brief and the rider's body is stable, the bike often returns to the intended line. A rider who is rigidly coupled to the bike is deflected with it — their weight amplifies the lateral movement. Decoupling is the safety net for unavoidable deflection events. It does not prevent deflection; it prevents deflection from becoming a crash.

### Principle 5: Tyre Profile and Pressure Affect Deflection Tendency
A tyre with a rounded profile and large carcass (enduro tyre) deflects less than a narrow, stiff tyre because the rounded contact patch has more surface area to generate lateral friction on an angled impact. Tyre pressure matters too: an over-inflated tyre is rigid — it cannot deform around the obstacle and is therefore more prone to deflection. An under-inflated tyre deforms too much — it folds under sharp impact and can cause rim strike. Optimal enduro tyre pressure (typically 14–18 psi depending on terrain and tyre) maximises the contact patch deformation that enables absorption.

### Principle 6: Trail Braking Before Obstacle Contact — Softening the Impact
Applying a very light front brake in the metre before a rock or root impact slightly pre-loads the fork and slows the wheel's rotational speed. This has two effects: first, the fork is slightly compressed and has more effective travel to absorb the impact; second, the slightly slower wheel speed reduces the rotational inertia that would otherwise amplify the impact. This is a refined, advanced technique — the timing is critical (must be released before the wheel contacts the obstacle, or it will lock on the slick surface) and the pressure must be very light. When timed correctly, it is one of the most effective tools for reducing deflection risk on a specific identified obstacle.

## 3. TECHNIQUE BREAKDOWN BY LEVEL

### 3a. Beginner Level
**Goal:** Understand the approach angle concept and begin selecting lines that approach obstacles more perpendicularly. Maintain speed rather than decelerating into obstacles.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Read the angle of roots and rocks on the trail and actively adjust body heading before contact
- Resist the instinct to brake before every rock — moderate speed is necessary for absorption
- Stay loose through obstacle zones — rigid body amplifies deflection consequences
- Identify when deflection has occurred (front track changes suddenly) and understand why

**What Good Looks Like:**
The rider approaches a root section and subtly adjusts their line to cross roots more perpendicularly than the direct trail line. Speed is maintained or slightly increased before the root zone. When occasional deflection occurs, the rider's body remains balanced and the bike corrects its course without crashing.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Decelerating before every obstacle:** Loses the momentum needed for absorption. → Maintain speed into moderate obstacles. Only brake for significant obstacles where speed management is genuinely necessary.
- **Following the trail line regardless of obstacle angle:** The trail line often runs parallel to roots and rocks. → Look ahead and identify opportunity for line adjustment to improve approach angle.
- **Tensing up at the handlebar on impact:** Transfers the deflection force directly to the arms and makes the rider steer in the deflection direction. → Loosen the grip through obstacle zones; elbows slightly bent.

### 3b. Intermediate Level
**Goal:** Consistently select lines that minimise deflection through approach angle management. Begin using the light pre-contact trail brake technique. Active body decoupling through obstacle zones.

**Progression Markers from Beginner:** Rider consistently adjusts line for better approach angles. Speed management through obstacle zones is appropriate. Occasional deflections are recovered without crashes.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Line selection specifically for approach angle: identifying which part of the trail offers the steepest angle to each significant obstacle
- Pre-contact light trail brake technique: timing the brief brake touch before root contact
- Active body decoupling: legs working independently through obstacles, body tracking straight
- Reading deflection events in real time: feeling the direction change and using body position to counteract

**What Good Looks Like:**
In a root section, the rider's front wheel crosses each significant root at a clearly steeper angle than the trail direction. The trail brake is applied and released in a brief moment before each root. The body absorbs the impacts without transmitting them to the steering. When a root causes a small deflection, the rider's body and steering correct immediately and the bike returns to line within half a wheel length.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Trail brake held too long (into root contact):** The braked wheel on a wet root locks momentarily — maximum deflection risk. → Release the trail brake before the wheel reaches the obstacle.
- **Line selection for obstacle angle, neglecting the exit line:** Crossing a root at 90 degrees is correct — but if the 90-degree crossing leads into a worse line on the exit, it may not be worth it. → Line selection is a multi-variable decision: entry angle, exit line, and obstacle sequence.
- **Over-relying on body decoupling to compensate for bad approach angles:** Decoupling reduces the consequences of deflection — it does not eliminate deflection. Both angle selection and decoupling are needed.

### 3c. Advanced Level
**Goal:** Manage deflection risk across entire rocky sections with rapid line reading. Chain approach angle decisions through rock gardens. Use speed and momentum strategically to maintain absorption on the most difficult obstacles.

**Progression Markers from Intermediate:** Approach angle selection is automatic. Pre-contact trail brake is instinctive. Body decoupling through obstacle zones requires no conscious effort.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- High-speed rock garden management: reading 5–10 obstacles ahead, pre-selecting lines for each, executing at competitive speeds
- Speed calibration for obstacle size: knowing which obstacles require more momentum (larger rocks, unfavourable approach angles) and which require less
- Managing cumulative deflection: when one obstacle deflects the bike, the next obstacle's approach angle has changed — adjusting rapidly
- Obstacle sequencing: understanding that line selection for obstacle one determines the starting position for obstacle two — thinking in sequences, not individual events

**What Good Looks Like:**
Through a rocky section, the rider makes subtle steering corrections between each rock — continuously repositioning for the next obstacle while managing the current one. Speed is maintained or even slightly increased through the section because each obstacle is absorbed rather than deflected. From a following camera, the bike's line appears almost fluid through rock terrain that would cause significant deflection events for a less experienced rider.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Fixating on one obstacle and losing line for the next:** Focus narrows to a single rock and the exit line is not managed. → Constant vision push — as the current obstacle is cleared, vision moves to the next.
- **Speed creep in complex sections:** Advanced riders sometimes increase speed beyond the point where approach angle selection is possible — arriving at obstacles from whatever angle they arrive. → There is a maximum speed for each technical level of terrain at which deliberate angle selection remains possible. Know that limit.

## 4. DIAGNOSTIC CUES (AI COACHING ENGINE REFERENCE)

### Visual Cues — Correct Technique
- From a front/chase camera: front wheel crosses roots and rocks at steeper angles than the trail direction — rider is actively adjusting line for approach angle
- Bike tracks a slightly curved or angled path through straight sections containing diagonal obstacles — deliberate line selection visible
- Body remains upright and facing forward while bike makes minor lateral adjustments under it — decoupling visible
- Speed maintained or slightly increasing through rock gardens — momentum sustained for absorption
- No sudden lateral head or shoulder movement through rock zones — body decoupled from obstacle impacts

### Visual Cues — Error Indicators
- Front wheel following the trail line regardless of obstacle angle — no approach angle management
- Sudden lateral track deviation at a rock or root: deflection event — approach angle was too shallow
- Rider's head and shoulders deflecting in the same direction as the obstacle kick: coupled to bike, not decoupled
- Speed reduction visibly occurring mid-rock-garden: momentum lost, absorption threshold falling, deflection risk increasing
- Handlebar grab visible (white knuckles, arms extending and pulling): rider fighting deflection events with steering rather than allowing absorption

### Audio Cues
- Sharp metallic thunk followed by a scraping sound: wheel deflection — the initial contact sound and the tyre scrubbing sideways
- Clean, regular rolling sound over rocks: wheels absorbing and rolling over — absorption occurring
- Repeated rapid thumps from one side: tyre repeatedly deflecting off same obstacle type — approach angle problem
- Sudden tyre screech combined with handlebar impact: deflection into handlebar-level obstacle — significant deflection event

### Sensor Cues
- IMU lateral acceleration spike at front axle: deflection event — lateral force exceeded tyre's resistance
- IMU lateral acceleration event followed immediately by a counter-event: deflection then correction — rider recovering
- Wheel speed sensor: brief speed reduction at a consistent obstacle zone — wheel absorbing (compression loading) vs no speed reduction (deflection — wheel skipping over rather than rolling)
- Handlebar vibration frequency: high-frequency vibration at obstacle approach is scrubbing (pre-deflection); abrupt single spike is deflection impact

## 5. COACHING CUES & LANGUAGE

### Positive Reinforcement
- "I could see you angling your approach on that root section. The front crossed each root at a much steeper angle than the trail was running. That is exactly why you didn't deflect."
- "Your body stayed square through that whole rock section. The bike was moving under you but your heading never changed. Perfect decoupling."
- "You maintained speed right through that garden. That momentum is what keeps the wheel rolling over rather than bouncing off. Keep trusting that."

### Corrective Feedback
- "You deflected off that root because you were following the trail direction instead of the root angle. That root runs at 30 degrees to your path — you need to adjust your line so you're crossing it at 60 or 70 degrees. Look for the line, not the root."
- "You braked before that root and then deflected. The braking slowed you below the absorption threshold and the wheel didn't have enough momentum to roll over — it bounced off. Carry your speed into that root. Trust it."
- "When you deflected your arms stiffened and you steered in the direction of the deflection. That amplified it. Next time: loosen the arms. The bars will jerk, but the bike will correct itself if you don't fight it."
- "You held the trail brake right through root contact. That is the worst moment for a locked wheel on a wet root — maximum deflection risk. Touch the brake before the root, then release. Not through it."

### Progression Prompts
- "Next pass, I want you walking the section first. Look at each root and mentally draw the angle. Find the line through the section that gives you the best average approach angle across all of them."
- "Try that root crossing three times: once parallel to the trail, once at 45 degrees, and once at 90 degrees. Feel the difference in deflection force. Then choose your angle."
- "On the next rock garden, push your vision ahead by 3 obstacles instead of focusing on the one in front of you. See what that does to your line selection."

## 6. DRILLS & PRACTICE EXERCISES

### Drill 1: Approach Angle Contrast — Root Crossing
**Purpose:** Experience the physical difference between shallow-angle and steep-angle obstacle crossing, building the instinctive understanding of why angle matters.
**Setup:** A single root or constructed barrier 40–60mm high, running across the trail at a diagonal (approximately 30–45 degrees to the trail direction).
**Execution:**
1. Pass 1: cross the root following the trail direction exactly (approximately 30–45 degree approach). Feel the deflection force.
2. Pass 2: adjust your line to cross at 70–80 degrees to the root face. Feel the difference.
3. Pass 3: cross at 90 degrees (perpendicular). Notice the near-absence of deflection force.
4. Return to pass 1 approach — confirm the deflection feeling is clear by contrast.
5. Repeat the 90-degree crossing until it feels natural to seek it.
**Success Criteria:** Rider can clearly articulate the difference between each approach angle in physical terms. Can reproduce the perpendicular approach consistently.
**Duration/Reps:** 20 minutes. 5 passes per approach angle.
**Progression:** Add speed to each angle comparison. Then move to a section with multiple roots requiring angle management on each.

### Drill 2: Pre-Contact Trail Brake Timing
**Purpose:** Develop precise timing for the light trail brake technique — applying it before obstacle contact and releasing exactly before the wheel arrives.
**Setup:** A single rock or root with a clear 1-metre approach visible. Mark the obstacle with a strip of chalk or tape.
**Execution:**
1. Approach at 15 km/h. Apply very light front brake at 1 metre from the obstacle. Release the brake at 0.5 metres. Cross the obstacle.
2. Repeat 5 times, adjusting brake application point — note the effect on obstacle crossing smoothness.
3. Try with brake held through the obstacle contact (deliberately incorrect). Feel the increased deflection.
4. Return to releasing before contact. Confirm the technique is improving obstacle crossing.
**Success Criteria:** Rider can apply a light brake touch and release it before contact with the obstacle consistently. Can feel the difference between released and held brake on the obstacle crossing.
**Duration/Reps:** 20 minutes. 10–15 passes.
**Progression:** Increase speed. Move to a wet root where the difference is more pronounced.

### Drill 3: Line Selection Walk-and-Ride
**Purpose:** Build the habit of trail reading for approach angles before attempting a section at speed.
**Setup:** A 20–30 metre rocky or root-covered section. Walkable.
**Execution:**
1. Walk the section on foot. Identify each significant obstacle and its angle relative to the riding direction.
2. Plan a line through the section that optimises approach angles across all obstacles.
3. Ride the planned line slowly (slow enough to follow it precisely).
4. Ride at normal speed following the same line.
5. Compare the feel of the deliberately-lined section vs riding the first instinctive line.
**Success Criteria:** The planned line produces fewer deflection events than the instinctive first line. Rider can describe why their planned line was better.
**Duration/Reps:** 30 minutes per section. Walk and ride cycle repeated 3 times.
**Progression:** Reduce the walking phase — attempt to read the line from the saddle at slow speed rather than on foot.

### Drill 4: Decoupling in Obstacle Zones
**Purpose:** Train the body decoupling reflex specifically in rocky terrain — practicing the habit of keeping the body direction stable while the bike deflects.
**Setup:** A short, manageable rocky section where minor deflections are expected and safe.
**Execution:**
1. First pass: ride tensed, arms locked, body rigid. Note the deflection consequences — how the whole rider-bike system responds.
2. Second pass: consciously loosen the arms, elbows bent. Let the bars move. Focus on keeping the head and shoulders facing forward regardless of what the bike does.
3. Third pass: actively push the feet down into the pegs through obstacle zones — this grounds the body and helps decouple the upper body.
4. Film from behind: compare head/shoulder movement across the three passes.
**Success Criteria:** By pass three, the head and shoulders move significantly less than in pass one through the same obstacle zone. The rider can feel the difference in how impacts are absorbed vs transmitted.
**Duration/Reps:** 25 minutes. 3+ passes each style.
**Progression:** Increase obstacle size. Then increase speed. The decoupling habit must hold at higher speed and larger obstacles.

## 8. TERRAIN & CONTEXT VARIATIONS

### Rocky Terrain
Rocks are hard and rigid — there is no compliance in the obstacle. Absorption means the tyre and suspension must manage the full energy transfer. Approach angle is critical because rocks often present very irregular faces. At good approach angles, the suspension can compress and roll the tyre up and over. At shallow angles, the rigid rock face creates a horizontal reaction force that deflects the tyre with no energy absorption.

### Wet Roots
The highest deflection risk environment. A wet root at 30 degrees from the bike's path combines shallow approach angle with near-zero lateral friction (wet wood). The tyre has no ability to resist the sideways push even if the approach angle is moderate. The 90-degree rule becomes even more important in wet conditions. Any deviation from perpendicular approach on wet roots carries high deflection risk.

### Dry Roots
Dry roots have significantly higher friction than wet roots — deflection risk is lower at the same approach angle. However, the 90-degree target remains the correct technique even on dry roots because terrain wet-state changes rapidly and the habit of steep approach angles should not be condition-dependent.

### Mixed Rock and Root Sections
The combination section is the most cognitively demanding for approach angle management. Rocks and roots may have different face angles within the same metre of trail. The rider must process each obstacle's optimal approach angle rapidly and chain the line decisions together. Vision is the critical variable — looking ahead far enough to plan the approach angle for each individual obstacle.

### High-Speed Desert
At high speed on relatively flat, rocky terrain, approach angle selection is less critical because the speed itself provides absorption momentum for most obstacles. However, at extreme speed, even good approach angles can cause deflection because the suspension has insufficient time to compress and rebound between hits. This is the high-speed version of the packing problem — the obstacles arrive faster than absorption can occur regardless of angle.

## 9. EXPERT INSIGHTS & SOURCES

### Key Insights
- **Jimmy Lewis** (elite off-road instructor): emphasises the 90-degree rule as the single most useful concept for intermediate riders dealing with deflection. "If you change nothing else, change your approach angle to roots and rocks. That one habit will eliminate more crashes than any other technique change."
- **Trials riding tradition:** Trials sections over roots and rocks are an extended study in approach angle management. World-level trials riders consistently cross obstacles at near-perpendicular angles, even when doing so requires elaborate, seemingly-impractical approach lines. The geometry proves the principle.
- **Tyre deformation research:** Bridgestone and Michelin off-road tyre testing data confirms that lateral deflection force is proportional to the tangent of the approach angle — meaning a 30-degree approach produces roughly twice the lateral force of a 60-degree approach. This explains why even a modest improvement in approach angle creates a disproportionate improvement in deflection resistance.
- **Body decoupling as crash prevention:** Analysis of crash footage from enduro events consistently shows that riders who crash on rock deflections are rigidly coupling their upper body to the bike — the deflection carries the rider with it past the point of recovery. Riders who survive the same deflection events are visibly decoupled — their body stays centred while the bike kicks sideways.
- **Speed paradox:** Counter-intuitively, faster riders deflect less on rock gardens than slower riders — because they have more momentum for absorption. However, there is a speed ceiling beyond which even perfect approach angles cannot prevent deflection due to suspension packing. The optimal speed range is between "enough for absorption" and "packing threshold."
