# Chassis Pitch — Front and Rear Bias

## 1. OVERVIEW

Chassis pitch describes the nose-up or nose-down attitude of the motorcycle at any given moment. It is not a passive consequence of terrain — it is an active, continuously-changing state driven by every throttle and brake input the rider makes. A bike in neutral pitch has equal load on both axles, maximum steering precision at the front, and maximum drive potential at the rear. A nose-up (rear-biased) pitch reduces front tyre load and contact patch, degrading steering accuracy and front traction. A nose-down (front-biased) pitch from over-braking degrades rear traction and increases endo risk. Sustained pitch bias in either direction is always the symptom of an uncorrected input — either too much throttle, too much braking, or inappropriate body position for the terrain. Elite riders manage pitch continuously, treating it as a live variable that must remain close to neutral across all conditions. Understanding pitch as a controllable state — not just something that happens to the bike — is the step that separates reactive riders from proactive ones.

## 2. CORE PRINCIPLES

### Principle 1: Every Input Changes Pitch State
There is no neutral input that preserves pitch. Throttle creates rearward pitch (nose-up). Braking creates forward pitch (nose-down). Even no input has a pitch consequence: engine braking on a four-stroke creates mild nose-down pitch as the drivetrain decelerates the rear wheel. The only way to maintain neutral pitch is through a balanced, continuously-adjusted combination of throttle, brake, and body position that offsets these forces as terrain demands change. In practice, neutral pitch is not a static state — it is a dynamic balance that requires constant micro-correction.

### Principle 2: Sustained Rear-Biased Pitch (Nose-Up) — The Consequences
When the nose is consistently high — from aggressive throttle or body position too far rearward — three significant problems occur. First, front tyre load decreases, reducing the maximum front traction available. Second, the front contact patch area decreases as the tyre's weight on the ground reduces, further degrading grip. Third, steering precision degrades — the steering feels vague, requires more lock to turn, and the bike under-steers (runs wide). In corners, sustained nose-up pitch combined with the required lean angle means the front can wash even with modest cornering loads. On technical terrain with steering demands, sustained nose-up pitch is a direct route to front wash.

### Principle 3: Sustained Front-Biased Pitch (Nose-Down) — The Consequences
Nose-down pitch from sustained braking or from not picking up throttle after braking creates equally serious problems. Rear tyre load decreases — the rear becomes light and prone to sliding or locking under even light rear brake application. Endo risk increases as the front is heavily loaded and the rear is light. Fork compression limits become a risk — sustained nose-down pitch with any further braking force can cause the fork to bottom out. In corners, a front-biased bike over-steers — turns more sharply than intended — because the front is generating high grip with high load. On loose terrain, this can cause the front to "dig in" rather than track smoothly.

### Principle 4: Neutral Pitch Is the Goal — How to Achieve It
Neutral pitch is achieved by balancing the throttle and brake inputs so neither creates a dominant moment. The simplest practical cue: after braking, ensure full brake release and allow the fork to rebound (return to natural sag position) before applying throttle. After throttle, ensure the rear has settled (returned to natural sag) before braking hard. The transition between these phases — the neutral moment — is when pitch is closest to balanced. Body position adjustment also manages pitch directly: moving the rider's CoM forward counteracts rearward pitch from throttle; moving rearward counteracts forward pitch from braking.

### Principle 5: Suspension Setup Affects Resting Pitch
The bike has a resting pitch when stationary — defined by the front and rear sag settings. If front sag is too high (fork too soft or too much preload removed), the bike sits nose-down at rest and begins its dynamic pitch from a forward-biased baseline. If rear sag is excessive, the bike begins from a nose-up position. Correct sag setup — typically 30–35mm at the front and 95–105mm at the rear for enduro — establishes a neutral resting pitch that gives the suspension room to work in both directions. A poorly-set-up bike is perpetually fighting its own resting pitch state, making neutral pitch almost impossible under dynamic inputs.

### Principle 6: Corner Entry Pitch State Determines Available Traction Split
The pitch state of the bike at corner entry determines how traction is distributed between front and rear tyres at the moment the rider commits to the turn. Entering a corner with forward pitch (residual braking load) gives the front tyre more grip — which can be used for either cornering or additional braking (trail braking). Entering with rearward pitch (premature throttle) unloads the front — reducing available cornering traction at the most critical moment. The ideal corner entry for most enduro situations is slightly forward-neutral — braking complete, bike beginning to settle, throttle not yet opened — so that both tyres are reasonably loaded as the rider leans and turns.

## 3. TECHNIQUE BREAKDOWN BY LEVEL

### 3a. Beginner Level
**Goal:** Recognise that throttle and brakes cause the bike to pitch. Begin separating inputs to prevent extreme pitch in either direction.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Observing the fork dive under braking — it is the visible expression of forward pitch
- Observing the rear squat under throttle — the expression of rearward pitch
- Understand that braking through corners creates nose-down pitch while turning — a dangerous combination
- Begin separating braking and throttle: brake, then turn, then throttle — never all at once

**What Good Looks Like:**
The rider brakes on the approach to a corner, the fork compresses progressively and the bike settles to neutral as braking is released at the corner entry. The rider turns from a settled, near-neutral pitch state. As the corner exits, throttle is progressively applied and the rear squats slightly — the bike pitches rearward smoothly rather than suddenly.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Throttle and brake together in a panic:** Rider applies both simultaneously when startled. → One input at a time. If unsure, brake first, then reassess.
- **Ignoring fork dive as information:** The fork compresses severely but the rider doesn't change their approach. → Watch the fork. It's telling you how much forward pitch has occurred.
- **Over-riding with body rearward on acceleration:** Exaggerated rearward lean while opening throttle amplifies nose-up pitch. → Keep the body in athletic central position on moderate terrain.

### 3b. Intermediate Level
**Goal:** Actively manage pitch using both input timing and body position. Understand pitch as a tool rather than a consequence. Recognise when the bike's resting pitch (setup) is fighting the rider.

**Progression Markers from Beginner:** Rider consistently separates braking and throttle inputs. Can identify nose-up and nose-down pitch states by feel. Fork compression under braking is controlled.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Using body position to manage pitch — leaning forward into throttle to prevent nose-up, leaning back into brakes on descents to prevent severe nose-down
- Throttle-brake sequencing that maintains close-to-neutral pitch through technical sections
- Reading the pitch state of the bike before a corner — nose-up means front traction is compromised
- Checking sag settings and understanding how suspension setup affects the resting pitch baseline

**What Good Looks Like:**
On a loose, technical climb the rider moves their weight forward as the throttle opens, preventing the front from rising. On a descent, the rider sits their weight rearward before braking, maintaining neutral pitch through the braking event. At corner entry, the rider can feel whether they are entering with too much nose-down (braking too late) or too much nose-up (premature throttle) and adjusts for the next pass.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Static body position regardless of pitch state:** Rider uses correct input timing but doesn't move the body to compensate. → Body position is the equaliser. If the inputs create pitch, the body must counteract it.
- **Ignoring setup as a variable:** Rider assumes the bike is "right" and only adjusts technique. → Check sag. A nose-down resting pitch explains a lot of corner-entry problems.
- **Applying throttle too early after braking:** The bike is still in nose-down pitch from the braking event when throttle opens — the pitch transitions abruptly from nose-down to nose-up without the neutral phase. → Allow the fork to rebound fully before opening throttle.

### 3c. Advanced Level
**Goal:** Use pitch state as a precision tool in competition riding. Manage pitch in real time across multiple simultaneous inputs. Diagnose pitch-related handling problems accurately.

**Progression Markers from Intermediate:** Pitch management is automatic. Body position adjustments are unconscious. Has diagnosed and corrected a setup-related pitch problem.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Deliberate use of nose-up pitch for obstacle clearance (front wheel lift technique)
- Deliberate forward pitch (trail braking) to maximise front traction load in corner entry
- Reading mid-section pitch state and adjusting inputs in real time across a technical section
- Diagnosing whether a handling problem is pitch-related (input sequencing) or setup-related (sag, spring rate)

**What Good Looks Like:**
On a fast, rough section, the rider's pitch state oscillates around neutral — small corrections continuously applied. On a log crossing, the rider deliberately induces forward then rearward pitch to pop the front wheel over, then weight rearward to lift the rear. In a corner, the rider's trail braking keeps the front loaded to the entry point, then smoothly transfers to neutral as the throttle begins — the pitch never overcorrects in either direction.

**Common Mistakes:**
- **Over-managing pitch through small adjustments creating oscillation:** Rider makes too many corrections too quickly — the pitch oscillates in a feedback loop. → Larger, slower corrections are more stable than many small rapid ones.
- **Confusing pitch with traction loss:** A sudden rear step-out is sometimes blamed on pitch when it is actually a traction circle exceedance. → Distinguish cause from effect — pitch changes traction availability; traction loss is the result of exceeding that availability.

## 4. DIAGNOSTIC CUES (AI COACHING ENGINE REFERENCE)

### Visual Cues — Correct Technique
- From a side camera: fork compression and extension are smooth and cyclic — no sustained fully-compressed or fully-extended state
- Rear suspension cycles naturally between slight compression (throttle) and neutral — not constantly compressed or extended
- Bike attitude at corner entry is close to level — not visibly nose-down (over-braked) or nose-up (premature throttle)
- Rider's body adjustments are visible on severe inputs — leaning forward on sustained throttle climbs, rearward on sustained braking descents
- The bike's longitudinal axis is roughly parallel to the slope of the terrain it is riding on — not tilted significantly against the slope

### Visual Cues — Error Indicators
- Fork sustained at full compression through a section: persistent nose-down pitch from continuous braking
- Rear end persistently compressed (rear sag maxed out) through a section: persistent nose-up pitch from continuous throttle — or soft rear spring
- Bike visibly rocking fore-aft repeatedly through a straight section: pitch oscillation — over-correction cycle
- Front wheel visibly light (barely touching ground) through a mid-corner phase: nose-up pitch at the worst possible moment for front traction
- Rider sitting up suddenly mid-section: reactive adjustment to pitch state that should have been anticipated

### Audio Cues
- Fork bottom-out sound (solid mechanical thunk) on corner entry: severe nose-down pitch, fork overwhelmed
- Rear suspension top-out sound (clunk from extended shock hitting its limit): severe nose-up pitch, rear unloaded
- Oscillating suspension sounds through a straight: the bike is pitching back and forth — input over-correction
- Engine note rising without speed increase simultaneously with rear suspension compression sound: throttle-induced nose-up pitch occurring — rear squatting under drive

### Sensor Cues
- IMU pitch angle sustained above +5 degrees (nose-up) through a corner: dangerous loss of front traction potential — flag as high priority
- IMU pitch angle sustained below -5 degrees (nose-down) through a descent with braking: forward-biased pitch under heavy braking — endo risk indicator
- Oscillating IMU pitch angle in a regular cycle at constant speed: pitch instability — over-correction pattern
- IMU pitch acceleration (rate of pitch change) spike: sudden pitch event — abrupt input detected

## 5. COACHING CUES & LANGUAGE

### Positive Reinforcement
- "The bike was level going into that corner. You had your braking done, it settled, and then you turned. Perfect sequence — the front was loaded and ready."
- "Good forward lean on that climb. You kept the front wheel down despite a big throttle input. That is body position working with the physics, not against it."
- "I can see the bike pitching neutrally through that section — slight squat on throttle, slight dive on braking, always coming back to level. That is what managed pitch looks like."

### Corrective Feedback
- "You entered that corner with the front wheel light — I could see the fork barely compressed. That means you had too much throttle before the turn. Braking loads the front and gives you steering traction. You need that load at entry."
- "Every time you opened the throttle the front came up. Your body is too far back. Move your chest forward as you open — counteract the physics with your position."
- "The fork was fully compressed all the way through that descent. You were braking the entire time instead of braking then releasing. That nose-down pitch is endo territory on anything slippery. Brake — release — coast — brake again."
- "You're rocking back and forth — I can see the pitch going nose-up then nose-down repeatedly. You're over-correcting each time. Smaller adjustments. Give the correction time to take effect before making another one."

### Progression Prompts
- "Next pass, I want you deliberately checking the fork position at corner entry — if it's compressed, you've carried too much forward pitch into the corner. Aim to enter with the fork close to its natural position."
- "Try the climb again and this time consciously think about the neutral moment — the half-second between releasing the brake and opening the throttle. Let the bike find its level. Feel the difference."
- "For the descent, before you touch the brake, move your weight rearward and hold it there until you've finished braking. Don't return to neutral until the braking is complete. Then see how much more control you have."

## 6. DRILLS & PRACTICE EXERCISES

### Drill 1: Fork Level Check at Corner Entry
**Purpose:** Develop visual and tactile awareness of pitch state at the moment of corner entry — building the feedback loop between input timing and pitch outcome.
**Setup:** A single corner with a defined entry point marked by a cone or chalk mark. Straight approach of 30 metres.
**Execution:**
1. Approach at moderate speed and brake to corner entry speed.
2. At the entry marker, glance down at the fork position — is it compressed, normal, or extended?
3. If compressed, the braking was too sustained or too late — pitch is forward-biased at entry.
4. If extended, the throttle was opened too early — pitch is slightly rearward at entry.
5. Adjust the braking release point until the fork is close to natural position at the entry marker.
6. Repeat 10 times, noting the release point that gives the best pitch state.
**Success Criteria:** Rider can consistently achieve a near-neutral fork position at corner entry by adjusting braking release timing. Can identify whether they are entering nose-up or nose-down from feel alone after 10 passes.
**Duration/Reps:** 25 minutes. 10+ passes.
**Progression:** Add a second corner after the first. Maintain the neutral pitch state through both.

### Drill 2: Throttle-Pitch Body Compensation — Climb Exercise
**Purpose:** Train the forward body lean habit for throttle-induced nose-up pitch on climbs.
**Setup:** A consistent moderate climb, 40 metres, loose surface.
**Execution:**
1. First attempt: neutral body position, steady throttle. Observe how far the front rises.
2. Second attempt: aggressively move chest toward the bars before opening the throttle — then open throttle.
3. Third attempt: begin moving body at the exact moment of throttle opening — simultaneously, not before.
4. Compare front wheel height across the three attempts. The third should produce the lowest front lift.
**Success Criteria:** Rider can keep the front wheel on the ground without reducing throttle by using body position. The simultaneous body-and-throttle movement becomes instinctive within the drill.
**Duration/Reps:** 20 minutes. 5 attempts per body position style.
**Progression:** Increase slope. Add loose surface. Then combine with a direction change mid-climb.

### Drill 3: Neutral Moment Training — Brake, Settle, Throttle
**Purpose:** Ingrain the habit of allowing the fork to rebound (pitch to neutralise) between braking and throttle application.
**Setup:** A marked corner with braking zone, entry point, and throttle-on zone clearly marked. Flat or gentle surface.
**Execution:**
1. Brake hard from the braking zone marker to the entry marker.
2. Fully release all brake pressure at the entry marker. Count one deliberate second.
3. Feel the fork extending — the bike pitching back toward neutral.
4. Apply throttle at the throttle-on marker.
5. The sequence: brake — full release — one-second neutral — throttle.
6. On each subsequent pass, shorten the neutral pause by half a second until the pause is the minimum needed for the fork to rebound.
**Success Criteria:** Rider feels the fork rebound during the neutral phase. The throttle application begins when the fork has returned close to natural position. The transition from braking to throttle is smooth, not abrupt.
**Duration/Reps:** 20 minutes. 15+ repetitions.
**Progression:** Reduce the neutral phase deliberately below the fork rebound time — feel what happens (nose-down throttle application). Then restore the correct phase. This contrast builds sensitivity.

### Drill 4: Pitch Diagnosis Ride — Self-Assessment Loop
**Purpose:** Develop the ability to identify the bike's pitch state at any moment during a section, building ongoing pitch awareness for competition riding.
**Setup:** A 5–10 minute trail loop with varied terrain including at least one corner, one climb, and one descent.
**Execution:**
1. First lap: ride normally. At three specific points (corner entry, mid-climb, mid-descent), mentally note the fork position and pitch state.
2. Second lap: call out the pitch state at each point aloud — "nose-up," "neutral," "nose-down." Film if possible.
3. Third lap: attempt to keep pitch as close to neutral as possible at all three monitoring points.
4. Fourth lap: confirm whether the improvements from lap three were maintained.
**Success Criteria:** Rider can accurately describe the pitch state at each monitoring point by lap two. By lap four, the pitch deviations from neutral are smaller than in lap one. Self-assessment is accurate when confirmed by film.
**Duration/Reps:** 40 minutes. 4 laps.
**Progression:** Add more monitoring points. Then attempt the loop at a higher speed, maintaining the same pitch management.

## 8. TERRAIN & CONTEXT VARIATIONS

### Loose Uphill
Rearward pitch from throttle is amplified by the slope's natural rear-loading. Risk is front wheel lifting and losing steering control. Forward body position is essential. Pitch management becomes about preventing excessive nose-up while maintaining enough throttle for momentum. The two tools — throttle management and forward body position — must work together.

### Steep Descent
Forward pitch from gravity (permanent pre-load) amplified further by braking. The bike is almost always in nose-down territory. Rearward body position before and during braking is essential to counteract this. Manage braking in pulses rather than sustained application — allows the fork to rebound between pulses, preventing progressive nose-down accumulation.

### Rocky Technical Section
Pitch changes rapidly and repeatedly as individual rocks load front and rear wheels alternately. Maintaining a consistent pitch state through this terrain is difficult — the rider must accept more pitch variation and focus instead on the extremes — preventing sustained nose-up (loss of steering into rocks) and sustained nose-down (fork bottoming on successive impacts).

### High-Speed Open
At high speed, pitch changes occur very quickly in response to throttle and brake inputs. Small input errors create large pitch events. Body position adjustments must be proportionally more aggressive to counteract them. Smooth, precise inputs are the primary tool — body position corrections are supplementary.

## 9. EXPERT INSIGHTS & SOURCES

### Key Insights
- **Carlo Pernat** (former Aprilia MotoGP sporting director): documented that MotoGP riders operate with a constant mental model of pitch state, making dozens of micro-corrections per minute — "The rider is not steering the motorcycle. They are managing its attitude. Steering is a consequence of correct attitude."
- **Hard Enduro elite technique observation:** Analysis of top-ten Hard Enduro World Championship riders shows consistently lower pitch variance than mid-field riders on the same sections. The difference is not speed — the fastest riders maintain more neutral pitch at all speeds.
- **Suspension sag as pitch foundation:** Factory team mechanics report that incorrect sag is among the most common setup problems for privateer riders — particularly rear sag too high, creating a permanent nose-up baseline that fights the rider all day.
- **Pitch management as fatigue management:** Research from endurance motorsport shows that incorrect pitch management forces riders to make more corrective body movements throughout a stage, increasing upper body fatigue. A well-managed pitch state reduces the physical work the rider must do to maintain control.
- **Two-stroke vs four-stroke pitch characteristics:** Two-stroke engines, without significant engine braking, produce less nose-down pitch on throttle release than four-strokes. This means two-stroke riders can release the throttle mid-section without the nose-down pitch event that a four-stroke rider must manage — a genuine handling advantage in certain technical situations.
